# Chapter 11: Hallucination Harvesting

The word "hallucination" arrived in AI discourse as a term of reproach. A model that asserts false facts, invents citations, or fabricates structural elements not present in the source material is, in the ordinary view, broken — doing something it should not do, failing at the task for which it was deployed, generating noise in a system that needs signal. The practitioner's standard response to a hallucination is reflexive: discard the output, penalize the model mentally, and either regenerate or redesign the prompt to prevent recurrence. This response is understandable, occasionally correct, and systematically wasteful. It treats the hallucination as an embarrassment rather than as what it actually is: a message from the production system about a gap in its design. Among the original contributions of production theory to Cognition Systems Engineering, hallucination harvesting is the deepest and the most counterintuitive. It requires the practitioner to reverse a strong intuition — the intuition that failure is to be discarded — and replace it with a disciplined analytical posture: failure is information, and the highest-value information in the system is often the information carried by its failures.

The manufacturing parallel illuminates the principle clearly. In the Toyota Production System, a defective part is not merely scrapped. It is studied. Taiichi Ohno was explicit about this priority: in *Toyota Production System* (1988), he argued that defects are the most important data the production system generates, because a defect is evidence that the process failed — that something in the interaction between specification, materials, and process produced an output inconsistent with the specification. The defect pattern, analyzed systematically, reveals what the process is actually doing as opposed to what the engineer believes it is doing. A systematic pattern of dimensional errors in a particular direction reveals tool wear, fixture misalignment, or thermal expansion. A random pattern of defects suggests raw material variance or an unstable process parameter. The point is structural: the defect is not the problem; it is the symptom whose pattern diagnoses the problem. Ohno's Five Whys methodology — asking "why?" repeatedly until the root cause is reached — presupposes that defects carry causal information worth tracing. Discarding a defective part without analysis is discarding the most valuable signal the process has generated.

AI hallucinations are not random noise. This claim is central to hallucination harvesting and must be understood precisely. The outputs of a language model are not draws from a uniform random distribution over the space of possible tokens. They are draws from a learned probability distribution shaped by the model's training data and the specific input configuration. When a model generates information not present in the source material, it is generating information that its training distribution associates with the context it is in — information that the model has learned tends to appear alongside inputs of this type. The model is not making up information in the sense of generating arbitrary tokens. It is completing the distribution in the way its training has taught it to complete that type of context. This means that a systematic hallucination — one that appears repeatedly across different inputs of the same type — carries information about what the model's training distribution expects to find in that context. It is, in a precise sense, a recommendation from an advisor who has processed vastly more examples of this type of content than the practitioner has, but who is confused about what is in the current document versus what is expected in documents of this class. The advice may be wrong about the specific document. It may be correct about the general pattern. Harvesting requires distinguishing between these two possibilities.

The distinction between signal and noise in hallucination patterns is statistical. A single hallucination is an event — it may be common cause variation from the normal behavior of the model on this task type, or it may be a special cause signal. A single event cannot distinguish between these possibilities, which is why the first principle of hallucination harvesting is that single failures are not harvested; only patterns are. The **harvesting threshold** is the frequency at which a given hallucination type crosses from probable noise into probable signal. There is no universal threshold, but the practitioner's heuristic — one that can be formalized with sufficient data — is roughly this: a hallucination type that appears in fewer than 10 percent of outputs is likely common cause and should be noted but not acted upon immediately; one that appears in 20 to 40 percent of outputs is a probable signal and warrants analysis; one that appears in more than 50 percent of outputs is almost certainly signal, meaning the model's behavior is consistent and directional, and the question is whether the direction is appropriate.

The **harvesting process** unfolds in five steps, each with specific operational requirements. The first step is collection. The pipeline must retain and log all significant QC failures — not merely the pipeline-crashing format errors that prevent downstream processing, but the softer content failures that a loose QC criterion might pass. A failure log is a production artifact with the same status as a control chart: it is not a record of embarrassments but a data source for process improvement. The collection step requires that failure logs include, at minimum, the timestamp, the task type, the specific nature of the failure, and the portion of the output where the failure occurred. These metadata fields are essential for the second step.

The second step is analysis. The collected failures are categorized by type and frequency. The categorization should be fine-grained enough to distinguish meaningfully different failure modes — a model that adds a limitations section is doing something categorically different from a model that invents citations, even though both are technically adding information not requested. Frequency distributions by failure type reveal which patterns are statistically significant. The practitioner who finds that 40 percent of outputs from a particular pipeline stage contain an unrequested element — an additional section, a caveat, a structural reorganization — is looking at a pattern that demands the third step.

The third step is the critical analytical move: asking what the pattern of failures implies about what the system "wants" to do. This framing is anthropomorphic shorthand for a statistical inference: given that the model consistently produces this output element in the absence of an instruction to do so, what property of the model's training distribution associates this type of input context with this output element? The inference is usually available, and it is usually productive. A model that consistently adds a limitations section to analytical outputs has learned, from its training data, that high-quality analytical outputs of this type typically include a limitations section. The absence of a limitations section in the current outputs is a discrepancy between the model's learned expectation and the current specification. The analyst's question is whether the model's learned expectation is appropriate for this system.

The fourth step is incorporation. If the pattern represents a genuine improvement — if the element the model consistently adds is an element that, on reflection, the outputs should contain — then the specification is updated to include it, and the system is redesigned to produce it reliably. This step closes the loop between the production system's behavior and the practitioner's intent. It is a form of specification discovery: the model's training distribution is helping identify quality criteria that the practitioner did not know to specify. This is, when it works, a powerful form of collaborative design: the practitioner brings intent and domain knowledge; the model brings pattern recognition across a vast training distribution; the hallucination is the point of contact between what the practitioner intended and what the model has learned to expect.

The fifth step is verification. After the specification is updated and the pipeline is redesigned to incorporate the harvested improvement, the output quality is measured against the updated specification. The question is whether the explicit incorporation of the previously hallucinated element produces better outputs — as judged by the QC criteria — than the original pipeline. Verification closes the scientific loop: the harvesting process is not a speculation about what might be better but a testable hypothesis about a specific quality improvement. If the hypothesis is confirmed, the improvement is real and the specification has been correctly updated. If it is not confirmed — if explicitly specifying the new element produces outputs that are no better or worse than before — then the hallucination was noise, the pattern was coincidental, and the original specification was correct. The verification step is what distinguishes hallucination harvesting from uncritical accommodation of model behavior.

Three extended examples illustrate the harvesting process in sufficient detail to make it operational. The first concerns a model deployed in a pipeline for systematic analysis of competitive intelligence documents. The pipeline specification required the model to produce a structured analysis covering market positioning, pricing signals, and product differentiation. The QC pass rate was stable at approximately 85 percent, with the primary failure mode being a category labeled "unrequested content." Further investigation of the failure logs revealed that 38 percent of outputs contained an additional section, consistently appearing at the end, under headings that varied but semantically converged on "limitations of this analysis" or "caveats." The practitioner's initial instinct was to strengthen the instruction not to add unrequested sections. Before doing so, the practitioner applied the third step of the harvesting process: what does the model expect to be in this type of document? The answer, drawn from the training distribution's pattern, was clear: in the domain of high-quality analytical work, responsible analysis includes explicit acknowledgment of its own limitations. The model was not malfunctioning. It was applying a quality standard — epistemic responsibility — that the specification had failed to encode.

The practitioner's verification process confirmed this interpretation. Outputs with the limitations section, evaluated by a blind judge, were rated as more analytically credible and professionally appropriate than outputs without it. The specification was updated to require a limitations section, the pipeline was redesigned to produce one reliably as a named component, and the QC pass rate rose to 93 percent over the following evaluation period, because the model was no longer fighting against an absent specification in a domain where its training strongly associated limitations acknowledgment with quality output. The hallucination had been correct — not about the specific content of any particular limitations section, but about the class of quality criterion the specification was missing.

The second example involves a pipeline for synthesizing multi-source research into structured briefings. The output specification called for a flat list of key findings, each stated as a direct claim. The failure log showed that 45 percent of outputs restructured the flat list into a hierarchical outline — a primary finding with supporting sub-findings indented beneath it. The format violated the specification, but the frequency made it a candidate for harvesting analysis. Applying the third step: the model's training distribution associates research synthesis of this type with hierarchical structure, because hierarchical organization reflects the logical relationships between findings — a primary claim supported by evidence is a different thing from a list of co-equal observations. The restructuring was not arbitrary; it was logically motivated. The model was encoding logical relationships that the flat list specification was discarding.

Verification was direct. A sample of outputs was presented to domain experts in two formats — flat list and hierarchical outline — with identifying information stripped. The hierarchical format was rated as more informative and easier to use for decision-making in 73 percent of evaluations. The specification was updated to permit hierarchical structure when the logical relationships among findings warranted it, and a quality criterion was added requiring that the hierarchy accurately reflect evidential relationships. The pipeline was redesigned to produce hierarchical outputs with explicit logical connectors. Output quality, as measured by the updated QC criteria, improved substantially. The model had been more right about the structure than the practitioner had been.

The third example is subtler. A pipeline for producing technical documentation for a rapidly evolving software product was generating outputs that consistently introduced epistemic qualifiers — "as of the current version," "this behavior may change in future releases," "the following assumes a specific configuration" — at frequencies (52 percent of outputs) that far exceeded the specification. The specification required confident, direct statements of behavior. The hallucination looked, on first analysis, like a failure of decisiveness — the model hedging where the specification required commitment. The harvesting analysis reversed this interpretation. The domain was genuinely characterized by rapid change. The product changed every two to three weeks. Documentation that made confident, unqualified claims about behavior in a domain with that rate of change was not accurate; it was misleading. The model's training distribution, having processed large amounts of technical documentation across fast-changing domains, had learned that epistemic qualification is appropriate in these contexts. The confident-claim specification was not wrong in general; it was wrong for this domain. The hallucination was domain-appropriate behavior that the specification had failed to accommodate.

The verification process for this example was more complex. Epistemic qualifiers reduce the apparent authority of documentation, which affects user behavior in ways that are not captured by accuracy metrics alone. A user study was necessary to determine whether qualified documentation was more useful — in the sense of producing fewer incorrect user actions — than unqualified documentation in a rapidly changing environment. It was. The specification was updated to permit and encourage epistemic qualification where the domain warranted it, with a quality criterion requiring that qualifications be substantively meaningful rather than generic hedges. The pipeline was redesigned with a domain-context instruction that licensed appropriate epistemic humility. The model's behavior, previously a failure, became a feature.

The relationship between hallucination harvesting and specification theory deserves careful treatment, because harvesting is easily misunderstood as a form of specification abandonment — as the practitioner capitulating to model behavior rather than enforcing requirements. The misunderstanding arises from conflating two different activities: accepting whatever the model produces (which is not engineering) and revising specifications based on evidence that the model's behavior is correct in ways the specification failed to capture (which is exactly what engineering requires). Specifications are not sacred documents. They are engineering hypotheses about what quality means in a given domain. The practitioner who writes a quality specification for a cognitive pipeline is making predictions: this criterion, evaluated in this way, will select for outputs that serve the downstream user's actual needs. That prediction can be wrong. It can be incomplete. It can be systematically biased by the practitioner's preconceptions about what good output looks like in a domain they may know less well than the model's training distribution does.

**Specification incompleteness** is the technical name for what hallucination harvesting detects when it is working well. A specification is incomplete when the set of criteria it encodes does not fully capture all the quality dimensions that matter for the downstream use case. Specification incompleteness is nearly universal in the first version of any cognitive pipeline specification, because the practitioner writing the initial specification is drawing on their own understanding of the domain, which is limited by their own experience. The model's training distribution draws on a far larger sample of domain examples, and the gap between the model's learned expectations and the practitioner's specification is precisely the gap that hallucination patterns reveal. This is not a flaw in the model; it is information about the limits of the initial specification.

The connection to formal specification theory is productive. In requirements engineering, the distinction between **stated requirements** and **unstated requirements** is a classic challenge: stakeholders can articulate the requirements they are aware of, but they may not be aware of requirements they implicitly hold until they see an output that violates them. The same dynamic operates in cognitive pipeline specification, with the difference that the entity revealing the unstated requirements is not the stakeholder but the model — which, through its training distribution, has access to implicit domain norms that the practitioner may not have consciously articulated. Hallucination harvesting is a structured method for eliciting unstated requirements from the model's behavior. It is, in this sense, a form of requirements discovery — using the model's systematic deviations from the stated specification as a probe for requirements the specification does not yet capture.

This framing also clarifies when harvesting should not incorporate model behavior. If the model's hallucination violates a stated requirement for reasons that do not reflect any property of the domain — if it adds content because of a training bias toward verbose output rather than because verbose output is genuinely better in this domain — then harvesting should not incorporate it. The verification step is precisely the mechanism for making this determination: if explicit incorporation of the hallucinated element produces objectively better outputs as measured against downstream quality criteria, the element belongs in the specification. If it does not, the model's behavior is a bias to be corrected, not a recommendation to be adopted. The verification step is what makes hallucination harvesting a scientific process rather than an abdication of engineering judgment.

There is a meta-level observation about hallucination harvesting that deserves explicit treatment, because it illuminates the epistemology of the whole discipline. The framework of Tactical AI Orchestration — the precursor discipline from which Cognition Systems Engineering developed — was itself hallucination-harvested. The principles did not emerge from top-down design by practitioners who first constructed a theory and then applied it. They emerged from patterns in actual production work: the same structural decisions appearing in pipeline after pipeline, the same quality problems surfacing in domain after domain, the same solutions proving effective in contexts that were superficially different but structurally identical. The practitioner who builds enough pipelines begins to see the pattern — that context must be managed as a resource, that QC nodes should be independent of production nodes, that failure logs are more valuable than success logs, that the system's behavior at the margin reveals more about its design than its behavior at the center. These were the hallucinations of practice: outputs that exceeded the specification the practitioner had set, pointing toward a better specification they had not yet formulated. The discipline was discovered by harvesting the productive failures of its own early practice.

This observation has implications for how practitioners should relate to their own pipeline failures. A pipeline that never fails is not a high-quality pipeline. It may be an over-engineered pipeline whose specification has been written to accommodate whatever the model produces, or an under-ambitious pipeline whose tasks are too simple to reveal the model's behavior at the edges of competence. Productive failure requires that the pipeline be genuinely challenged — that the tasks be ambitious enough that the model's training distribution is engaged, that the specification be demanding enough that gaps in it produce visible failures. The pipeline that produces 100 percent pass rates on all metrics is either exceptionally well-designed or testing against criteria too weak to catch real problems. The history of quality improvement in physical manufacturing strongly suggests the latter is more common.

**Creativity within constraint** is the conceptual frame that makes hallucination harvesting coherent without making it an invitation to uncritical accommodation of model behavior. The harvesting discipline is not "accept whatever the model produces." It is "analyze what the model consistently produces against what you specified, and investigate the gap." The gap is the signal. The gap may reveal a specification that was too narrow — and the model's behavior should be incorporated. Or it may reveal that the model's training distribution is mismatched with the current domain — and the specification should be strengthened to override it. The harvesting process distinguishes between these outcomes through the verification step. What hallucination harvesting categorically rules out is the reflexive discard — the response that throws away the signal without reading it, that discards the most informative data the production system generates, that treats failure as an event to be suppressed rather than as information to be understood. A production system without a mechanism for productive surprise is one that can never exceed the practitioner's initial understanding of what quality means in the domain. It is, in the precise sense Ohno would have recognized, a system that cannot learn from its own operation. The harvesting discipline ensures that every failure the system generates is at minimum a question: does this failure reveal something about what this domain actually requires?
