The practitioner who has mastered every concept developed in the preceding fifteen chapters — information theory, production principles, pipeline architecture, quality control meshes, semantic contracts, statistical process control — remains professionally ineffective until they can do one thing: specify what they want with sufficient precision that a capable agent can build it without asking for clarification. This is the requirements mind, and it is the meta-skill that determines whether every other tool in the CSE practitioner's repertoire works or fails. An AI model is a powerful system for transforming a specification into an artifact. When the artifact is wrong, there are exactly two explanations: the model executed incorrectly, or the specification was insufficient. In practice, the first explanation is rare and shrinking; the second is pervasive and underappreciated. The field of Cognition Systems Engineering is built on probabilistic cognitive infrastructure, and probabilistic systems are extraordinarily sensitive to specification quality. Every ambiguity in the specification becomes a degree of freedom in the output space. Every missing constraint is an invitation to produce something technically correct but practically wrong. Developing the requirements mind — the discipline of knowing precisely what you want before you ask for it — is the highest-leverage skill available to the working CSE practitioner.

The problem is older than AI. **Requirements engineering** is the formal discipline of eliciting, analyzing, specifying, and validating the requirements for a system before that system is built. It emerged as a distinct field in software engineering during the 1970s, following a series of catastrophic failures in large government and defense software programs that were traced not to implementation errors but to requirements errors — systems that had been built exactly as specified, to specifications that failed to capture what was actually needed. The IEEE Standard 830, published first in 1984 and revised repeatedly through 1998, established formal criteria for requirements quality: completeness, consistency, unambiguity, verifiability, modifiability, traceability, and correctness. Decades of research and practice followed, producing structured approaches — use cases, user stories, formal specification languages, requirements traceability matrices — designed to bridge the gap between stakeholder intent and system specification. This body of knowledge represents one of software engineering's most mature subfields. It is also almost entirely absent from the discourse about AI-native development, which tends to treat the prompt as a casual, conversational artifact rather than as a requirements document. This treatment is a category error with significant consequences.

In AI-native work, requirements engineering collapses into the prompt. The **specification gap** is the distance between "I know what I want" and "I can specify what I want precisely enough that a capable agent could build it without clarifying questions." This gap is wider than most practitioners recognize. The experience of knowing what you want is subjective and often feels complete — you have a clear mental image of the desired output. But that mental image contains information that has not been made explicit, assumptions that have not been articulated, constraints that have not been stated. The prompt that flows from this experience captures only the surface: the nominal case, the happy path, the input-output relationship in general terms. What it omits — the edge cases, the boundary conditions, the error behaviors, the stylistic constraints, the architectural patterns that must be respected — is precisely the information that determines whether the output is genuinely useful or merely approximately correct.

The most important professional reorientation available to the CSE practitioner is this: when output is wrong, the first diagnostic question is not "what did the model do wrong?" but "what did the specification fail to constrain?" This reorientation is uncomfortable because it places responsibility squarely with the practitioner. The model built what you asked for. You asked wrong. This is not a model failure mode — it is a specifier failure mode, and CSE practitioners own the specification. The AI system cannot be blamed for failing to satisfy a constraint that was never stated. It cannot be blamed for resolving an ambiguity in the wrong direction when the correct direction was never specified. When the output does not match the intent, the prompt should be read as a forensic artifact — a document that reveals, by the nature of its failures, exactly what it failed to constrain. This diagnostic discipline, practiced consistently, builds the requirements mind through iteration: each failure teaches the specifier what class of constraint they habitually omit.

Requirements theory distinguishes among several levels of specification, and all of them apply to prompts. The first level comprises **functional requirements**: what the system should do — the inputs it receives, the outputs it produces, the behaviors it exhibits under nominal conditions. Most prompts operate entirely at this level. "Summarize this document." "Generate a report on Q3 performance." "Write a function that parses this JSON structure." These are functional requirements: they describe the transformation, the input, and the desired output in general terms. They are necessary but not sufficient. A prompt that specifies only functional requirements will produce outputs that are functionally plausible but potentially wrong in all the ways that matter — the edge cases, the error behaviors, the stylistic constraints.

The second level comprises **edge case requirements**: what the system should do when the nominal path breaks. Empty inputs. Inputs that fall at the boundary of the domain (a document with zero words, a dataset with one row, a JSON structure with no required fields). Conflicting information in the source material. Requests that cannot be satisfied given the available data. Error conditions. These are the requirements that reveal whether the specifier actually understands the problem. A practitioner who has specified edge case behaviors completely has been forced to think through the full problem space — they have asked themselves what the system should do not just when everything goes well but when it doesn't. This thinking almost always reveals assumptions that were invisible at the functional level. The act of specifying edge cases is itself a diagnostic practice: it exposes the parts of the problem space the specifier had not considered, and it forces resolution of those cases before the model is asked to resolve them. A model left to resolve edge cases without specification will resolve them using the prior distribution of its training — which may or may not match the practitioner's intent.

The third level comprises **constraint requirements**: what constraints the correct behavior must satisfy regardless of implementation. Performance constraints — the output must be generated in under two seconds, must not exceed five hundred words, must fit within a JSON structure of a specific schema. Stylistic constraints — the output must maintain third-person academic voice, must not use numbered lists, must use technical terminology consistently with the definitions established in the prior chapters of this document. Architectural constraints — the generated code must not use global state, must handle all errors through the result type rather than exceptions, must be compatible with the existing module structure. Standards compliance — the output must conform to citation style X, must reference only sources published after year Y, must not make claims that exceed what the cited evidence supports. A specification without constraints is a wish, not a requirement. Constraints are what transform a general description of desired output into a specification that uniquely identifies a class of acceptable outputs and rejects everything outside that class.

All three levels are required for a complete specification. Each level catches errors the previous level allows through. Functional requirements alone permit a vast space of technically correct but practically wrong outputs. Adding edge case requirements collapses that space by forcing resolution of non-nominal behaviors. Adding constraint requirements collapses it further by imposing the non-behavioral properties that the output must satisfy. The complete specification — functional, edge case, and constraint requirements together — defines a tight boundary around the acceptable output space. A capable model operating within that boundary will produce acceptable output reliably. A capable model operating without that boundary will produce technically plausible output that satisfies only the parts of the practitioner's intent that were made explicit.

The practical test for specification completeness is what practitioners call the **new team member test**: could a competent professional — one who knows the domain but has no context from this conversation, no access to the practitioner's internal assumptions, no knowledge of the specific decisions that have been made — build the correct thing from this specification alone, without asking any clarifying questions? If the answer is no, the specification is incomplete. The new team member test is the formal operationalization of the specification gap. It is ruthless because it eliminates the practitioner's ability to rely on shared context, implicit assumptions, and conversational disambiguation. The new team member has none of these. They have only what is written. If the specification is incomplete by this standard, the model is in exactly the new team member's position — it has only what is written, and it will fill in what is not written from its prior distribution, which is not this specification.

The new team member test is also diagnostic. To apply it, the practitioner must imaginatively inhabit the perspective of someone who does not know what they know. This imaginative shift is cognitively demanding precisely because it requires making tacit knowledge explicit — articulating the assumptions that feel so obvious that articulating them seems unnecessary. The assumptions that feel most unnecessary to articulate are exactly the ones most likely to be violated by a model operating without them. The practitioner who consistently applies the new team member test before prompting develops a growing awareness of the tacit knowledge their specifications habitually omit. Over time, this awareness produces specifications that are complete by habit rather than by effortful review.

Formal requirements theory provides four structural elements that every production-grade specification should contain: **preconditions**, **postconditions**, **invariants**, and **quality criteria**. Preconditions specify what must be true before the task begins — what the input space looks like, what data is available, what assumptions about the context hold. "The input document is in English." "The dataset contains at least one row." "The code being reviewed uses the language features available in Python 3.11." Preconditions make the scope of the specification explicit, and they define the conditions under which the specification's guarantees hold. Postconditions specify what must be true when the task completes — the properties the output must satisfy, the state the system must be in, the artifacts that must have been produced. "The summary must accurately represent all major claims in the source document." "The generated code must pass the existing test suite." "The report must contain a section for each of the five specified topics." Postconditions are what the evaluator checks to determine whether the task was completed successfully.

**Invariants** specify what must remain true throughout execution — the properties that must not be violated at any intermediate step. "The tone must remain professional throughout." "The naming conventions established in the first section must be applied consistently." "The output language must not switch from English to any other language." Invariants are the constraints that apply not just to the final output but to every intermediate artifact the task might produce. They are particularly important for multi-step tasks, where intermediate outputs are consumed by downstream steps — an invariant violation at step three can propagate through the entire pipeline. **Quality criteria** specify how the evaluator determines whether the output is good — the dimensions along which goodness is measured and the standards against which it is assessed. "The summary is good if a domain expert would assess it as accurate, complete, and free of distortions." "The code is good if it achieves the specified behavior with no more than fifty lines and no external dependencies." Quality criteria transform the binary question of correctness (did the output satisfy the specification?) into the graded question of quality (how well did the output satisfy the specification?). All four elements — preconditions, postconditions, invariants, quality criteria — are required for a specification that can be reliably evaluated, which means they are required for a specification that can be reliably improved.

The requirements mind, once developed, is not confined to AI interaction. It is a **transferable meta-skill** — the discipline of knowing what you want before you ask for it, of expressing intent with sufficient precision that a capable professional could execute against it without additional clarification. This discipline improves all communication: with human collaborators, with organizational systems, with clients and stakeholders. The manager who can write a precise task specification to an AI model can write a precise task specification to a human team member. The architect who can specify edge cases and invariants for a cognitive pipeline can specify edge cases and invariants for a business process. The consultant who has developed the requirements mind produces deliverables that align with client intent on the first delivery rather than the third, because they have learned to elicit and document client intent with the rigor that AI interaction demands. The requirements mind is a forcing function for clarity: it produces people who communicate more precisely, think more completely, and design more rigorously.

The connection between the requirements mind and Polanyi's concept of tacit knowledge deserves careful treatment, because it illuminates why the new team member test is so cognitively demanding and why the specification gap persists despite practitioners' best efforts. **Tacit knowledge**, as Michael Polanyi developed the concept in *The Tacit Dimension* (1966), is the knowledge we hold but cannot fully articulate — the knowledge embedded in practice, in skill, in professional judgment that exceeds what can be stated in rules or propositions. The experienced radiologist who sees the tumor the novice misses possesses tacit knowledge; the master craftsperson whose hands know the right pressure possesses tacit knowledge; the seasoned practitioner who recognizes a failing project in its early stages possesses tacit knowledge. Polanyi's famous formulation — "we can know more than we can tell" — describes the asymmetry between what an expert can do and what an expert can say they are doing. The specification gap is the instantiation of Polanyi's asymmetry in the domain of AI interaction. The practitioner knows more about what they want than they can tell the AI system. The requirements mind is the deliberate, disciplined reduction of that asymmetry — the practice of bringing tacit knowledge to the surface, making it explicit, encoding it in the specification where it can constrain the model's behavior. This reduction is never complete; some tacit knowledge resists explication by nature. But the practitioner who works at this reduction consistently — who asks, at each specification, "what do I know that I have not said?" — makes the specification progressively more complete and the output progressively more reliable. The requirements mind is, at bottom, the practice of encoding one's own expertise. It is the same obligation that Chapter 14 identified as the primary encoding obligation of the CSE practitioner, applied to the most immediate artifact of cognitive production: the prompt itself.

The requirements mind also requires getting the level of decomposition right. **Requirements decomposition** is the practice of breaking a large, complex specification into a hierarchy of smaller, more tractable specifications. The importance of decomposition level cannot be overstated: requirements at the wrong level of decomposition are impossible to specify precisely, regardless of the practitioner's skill. Too coarse: "build me an application" — this is not a specification; it is a project brief. No human and no AI can produce a correct implementation from a brief that has not been decomposed into the constituent decisions it implicitly contains. The specification would need to answer hundreds of questions — language, framework, architecture, data model, authentication, deployment target, performance requirements — before it could guide a capable professional to a correct implementation. Too fine: "implement this specific algorithm in exactly this way, in exactly these lines of code" — this is over-specified to the point of removing all creative latitude. It specifies the path rather than the destination, eliminating the model's ability to find a better path. The right level of decomposition is where the boundary between known and unknown falls cleanly: the practitioner knows the required output and the constraints the output must satisfy; the practitioner does not know (or does not need to constrain) the path by which the output is produced. At this level, the specification is complete enough to uniquely identify the acceptable output space, but unconstrained enough that the model's capabilities — its knowledge, its creativity, its ability to generalize — can operate freely within that space.

Finding the right decomposition level requires an accurate model of where one's own knowledge ends. The practitioner who knows the domain but not the implementation details should specify at the domain level and leave implementation choices to the model. The practitioner who knows exactly how the implementation should work should specify at the implementation level. Mismatches produce specifications that are either so vague that the model must guess at the practitioner's intent or so over-constrained that the model's superior knowledge cannot be brought to bear. The requirements mind includes the discipline of knowing the limits of one's own knowledge and specifying to those limits precisely.

The concrete behavioral change that builds the requirements mind most rapidly is what practitioners in the field call **spec before prompt**: write the specification before writing the prompt. Not alongside it; not as a mental exercise performed while typing; before. The specification is a separate artifact, produced through deliberate analytical work, that serves as the input to the prompting process. Writing the specification as a document — even a rough one — forces it to be complete enough to read back. The practitioner who writes the specification discovers what they don't know before they prompt rather than after. They discover ambiguities while reviewing the specification rather than after receiving output that exposes the ambiguities through its failures. They discover missing edge cases before they learn about them from a model that handled them wrong.

Reviewing the specification before prompting is a practice of applying the three levels and the four formal elements as a checklist: are functional requirements stated? Are edge cases addressed? Are constraints specified? Are preconditions, postconditions, invariants, and quality criteria present? Applying the new team member test as the final review — reading the specification as if encountering it for the first time, with no prior context — catches the class of errors that escapes formal checklists: the assumption so deeply embedded that it does not feel like an assumption, the constraint so obvious that it does not feel like a constraint. Then, and only then, the prompt is written.

This habit — specification first — is the single highest-leverage behavioral change available to the CSE practitioner. It is also the most resisted, because it requires slowing down before the reward arrives. The cognitive pull toward immediate action is powerful. The prompt box is available; the model is waiting; the desired output is vivid in the practitioner's mind. The impulse to ask immediately rather than to specify first is almost universal. The discipline of suppressing that impulse — of producing the specification, reviewing it, applying the tests, and only then prompting — feels slow when it is practiced and fast when it is compared to the alternative: the cycle of prompt, wrong output, revised prompt, still wrong output, further revision, iteration until convergence. Specification first collapses that cycle. It does not eliminate iteration; requirements always require refinement as understanding deepens. But it ensures that each iteration is an act of deliberate refinement rather than undirected exploration. The practitioner who specifies first converges on the right output faster, with fewer prompts, and with a deeper understanding of the problem at each step.

Part IV has established the full theoretical apparatus of cognitive pipeline engineering: the production principles that govern cognitive factories, the quality control mesh that ensures output integrity, the statistical process control methods that distinguish signal from noise, the semantic contracts that formalize component boundaries, the knowledge architecture that ensures organizational intelligence persists across time, and now the requirements mind that determines whether every element of that apparatus is directed toward the right end. The requirements mind is the closing discipline of Part IV because it is the discipline that holds everything else together. The pipeline can be perfectly designed, the quality control mesh perfectly implemented, the statistical process control perfectly calibrated — and all of it produces waste if the specifications that drive it are incomplete. Requirements quality is the upstream constraint. Everything else is downstream of it.
