# Chapter 10: The Stupid Company™, Defined

The Stupid Company™ is the company that uses AI to eliminate cognition.

The Wise Corporation is the company that uses AI to amplify it.

That is the entire distinction.

It is also, in the strip register, the same distinction Adams has been making for thirty-five years between *the company in the strip* and *every company Dilbert has ever wished he worked at instead.* The Stupid Company™ is the company in the strip. The Wise Corporation is what the strip never had a panel to draw, because the strip's joke was the Stupid Company™, and the joke required the strip to stay there. Adams stayed there for thirty-five years because the strip's job was to keep documenting the Stupid Company™ at three panels a day. The strip's job was not to imagine the Wise Corporation. Other people have to do that work.

This book is one of the places where the other work is happening.

That is the assignment.

You can write down everything else this book is going to teach you on the same index card.

If your CEO has decided that the goal of the AI rollout is *to reduce headcount,* you work at the Stupid Company™. If your CEO has decided that the goal of the AI rollout is *to let our current people get dangerously creative,* you work at the Wise Corporation. There is no third option. Every company that I have ever observed up close, including the forty-one I have casually evaluated over the previous six years, has been one or the other. Many of them have been transitioning from the second toward the first, which is the most common direction of corporate drift. None of them, in my experience, has been transitioning from the first toward the second, although I am told by people I respect that this transition is theoretically possible and has occurred at three companies in the historical record.

I have not personally seen the transition happen.

I do not expect to see it happen in the careers of most readers of this book.

If you work at the Stupid Company™, the book's advice is going to be to leave.

If you work at the Wise Corporation, the book's advice is going to be to stay, and to make sure your CEO never accidentally transitions the company.

That is the book.

The rest of the book is the detail.

---

I want to give you, for the record, the eleven warning signs that you work at the Stupid Company™. I have promised these throughout the book. Here they are. Read them carefully. Most readers will recognize their own employer by item six.

1. **Your CEO has, in the previous twelve months, used the phrase *AI Transformation* in writing, in a slide deck, or in a LinkedIn post.** The phrase is a tell. The phrase is what Stupid Company™ CEOs say when they are about to make labor-replacement decisions. Wise Corporation CEOs talk about specific tools, specific use cases, and specific bottlenecks. They do not talk about *transformation.*

2. **Your CEO has, in the previous twelve months, attended a conference at a resort in the Sonoran Desert.** I am being slightly facetious about the geography. But only slightly. The conferences that produce the Stupid Company™ playbook are held at resorts. The conferences that produce the Wise Corporation playbook are held at universities. The mailing list overlap between the two sets of conferences is approximately zero.

3. **You have a SOPHIE.** You may not call her SOPHIE. You may call her Copilot, or Cursor, or Devin, or one of the in-house wrappers that your CEO has had built around an OpenAI API for a license fee that is between three and twenty times what the underlying API would have cost the company directly. The tool is not the issue. The tool is fine. *The way the tool was rolled out* is the issue.

4. **There is an engagement dashboard.** Somebody at your company is tracking how often the engineers use the AI tool, by name, with their headshots on a wall. If this dashboard exists, you work at the Stupid Company™. The Wise Corporation does not measure tool usage. The Wise Corporation measures output. The difference is the entire chapter.

5. **The slack channels are quiet.** Track this empirically for two weeks. Compare the message volume in the engineering or design or sales channel to the volume from twelve months earlier. If the volume is down more than thirty percent, your company has begun moving its honest conversations off-channel. The diagnostic is that the off-channel conversations are still happening; they have just moved to phones and DMs and the parking lot. The channels are the public record. The public record is now empty. The actual conversation is happening privately.

6. **You can name three things about your company that your CEO does not know.** I asked you this in Chapter 8. I am asking it again. If the answer is yes, you work at a company in which the information flow from the people doing the work to the people making the decisions has been structurally degraded. The degradation has, almost always, been done by the people making the decisions, to themselves, by accident, through the cumulative effect of policies they put in place to make themselves feel decisive.

7. **A senior individual contributor has resigned, in writing, with an email that the rest of the engineering organization read seventeen times each.** If your company has had a Reagan-style resignation in the previous eighteen months, you work at the Stupid Company™. The Wise Corporation does not produce Reagan-style resignations because the Wise Corporation does not produce Reagans who feel they have to leave to be heard.

8. **The first round of layoffs has happened.** The first round is the most important diagnostic. The first round is the round in which the company makes the decision about what kind of company it is going to be. If the first round included senior individual contributors whose work was load-bearing and who had not been actively gaming the AI engagement dashboard, your company has chosen to be the Stupid Company™. If the first round was zero people, and the company is instead redeploying existing employees into more exploratory roles, your company has chosen to be the Wise Corporation.

9. **The parking lot is the most active social space in the building.** I told you about Marv's Method in Chapter 7. The lot is the dashboard. If the lot has, across the previous six months, become the place where the most candid conversations happen, your company has lost its internal trust infrastructure. The lot is the consequence.

10. **Your CEO has, in the previous six months, posted on LinkedIn about the AI Transformation more than four times.** The frequency is the tell. Wise Corporation CEOs post on LinkedIn approximately never about their internal AI rollout, because the internal AI rollout is, to the Wise Corporation, not the kind of thing you post on LinkedIn about. It is the work. The work does not post on LinkedIn. The work does the work.

11. **You have read this chapter and recognized your company in at least six of the previous ten items.** This is the diagnostic that closes the diagnostic. If you have recognized six or more, you work at the Stupid Company™. If you have recognized between three and five, you work at a company that is in transition, and the direction of the transition is, in my honest experience, almost always *toward* the Stupid Company™. If you have recognized two or fewer, you may work at the Wise Corporation, and you should read Part III with attention because Part III is going to teach you how to make sure your company does not accidentally transition.

That is the eleven.

I made the list eleven because eleven is funnier than ten.

There is no twelfth item.

If your company would have qualified on a twelfth item, it would also have qualified on at least three of the previous eleven, and you do not need the twelfth.

I want to spend a moment on the *trademark.*

The Stupid Company™ is, in this book, formatted with a small superscript trademark symbol. The symbol is not legally registered. The symbol is a joke. The joke is that the company is selling, internally, the idea that the AI Transformation is going to be the company's brand, and the company has trademarked the brand to itself, and the trademark is the company's most prized asset, and the trademark is what the company is going to be remembered for.

The trademark is the joke because the trademark is, in the historical record, the thing the Stupid Company™ defends most aggressively.

I have read three actual legal complaints filed by companies I would call Stupid Companies™ against either ex-employees, journalists, or competitors, in which the complaint centered on the company's *internal AI brand identity.* The complaints were, in each case, brought against entities who had described the company's AI rollout in publicly unflattering terms. The complaints did not survive motion to dismiss in any of the three cases. The complaints were filed anyway. The complaints were filed because the Stupid Company™'s brand identity is, to the Stupid Company™, more valuable than its underlying business — because the brand identity is what is on the dashboard, and the underlying business is in Marv's Window.

That is the joke.

I am giving the trademark symbol to the brand because the brand is, in operational terms, *the only thing the Stupid Company™ has invested more in than its own ability to fire people.*
