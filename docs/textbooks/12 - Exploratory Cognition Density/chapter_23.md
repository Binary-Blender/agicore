# Chapter 23: The Onboarding That Looks Like a Birthday Party

The first week at Hyperion did not have an orientation.

I want to be clear about what *did not have an orientation* means.

There was no HR onboarding. There was no IT training session. There was no benefits enrollment meeting in a conference room. There was no welcome email from a chief people officer. There was no slide deck on the company's history. There was no lunch with a designated buddy from a different team. There was no compliance training video. There was no review of the employee handbook.

There was, instead, a list of twenty-two names.

The list had been sent to me by Yumi at six fifteen PM the previous Friday, the day my offer was finalized, with a one-line note: *Lee, these are the other twenty-two of us. Get a coffee or a lunch with as many of them as you can in your first three weeks. — Y.*

That was the onboarding.

I want to walk you through what the onboarding actually accomplished, because I think it is the most consequential design choice the company has made.

---

I had coffee with three people on Monday.

I had coffee with two people on Tuesday.

I had lunch with four people on Wednesday.

I had coffee with three people on Thursday.

I had lunch with five people on Friday.

I had lunch with the remaining five people across the following Monday and Tuesday.

By the end of my second week, I had spent between fifteen minutes and ninety minutes with every other employee of the company.

I knew their names. I knew their roles. I knew, in most cases, why they had joined Hyperion. I knew, in many cases, what books they had read in the previous year. I knew, in a few cases, what they were currently working on and where they were stuck.

This was the onboarding.

The onboarding was not *processing me into a system.* The onboarding was *getting me into the social network the company runs on.*

The Wise Corporation does not run on a system. The Wise Corporation runs on the social network. The social network is the actual operating layer of the company. The system — Slack, the document store, the issue tracker — is a substrate the social network uses. The social network is the company.

Most companies have this backwards. Most companies treat the system as the operating layer, and they treat the social network as a side effect of the system. Most companies build their onboarding around getting new hires into the system. They give the new hire access to Slack and the document store and the issue tracker. They run them through trainings on how to use the company's particular flavor of each. They consider the onboarding complete when the new hire can find the right form in Workday.

The Stupid Company™'s onboarding produces a hire who can use the system.

The Wise Corporation's onboarding produces a hire who is in the social network.

The hire who is in the social network is the hire who can produce value.

The hire who can use the system is the hire who can submit a vacation request.

---

I want to give you a specific example of what *being in the social network* produced for me, on Wednesday of my first week.

I had lunch with an engineer named **Davian Park** (no relation to Sam) on Wednesday. Davian had been at Hyperion for ten months. Davian was working on a tooling project I will not describe in detail. The tooling project had a particular technical problem Davian was stuck on. The problem was, by Davian's description over the sandwich, *the kind of problem I have been turning over for four weeks and have not made progress on.*

I listened to Davian's description.

I had, in Year Eleven at Mendacium, written an internal tool that had handled approximately the same kind of problem.

I told Davian about the tool.

Davian asked me to describe how the tool had worked.

I described it across the next eleven minutes.

Davian, by the end of the eleven minutes, had the structural shape of the solution to her problem. She did not have the implementation. She did not have the specific code. She had the *what would happen if I tried this* in her head, and she could see that the answer was different from any of the four approaches she had been trying.

Davian shipped the solution the following Tuesday.

The solution unblocked her project.

The project was, by Theo's later description, *one of the projects we depended on for the next product release.*

The product release shipped on schedule.

The product release was, in revenue terms, worth somewhere between four and seven million dollars over its first year.

Three weeks after I joined Hyperion, my contribution to the company had been to know about a tool I had built six years earlier at a previous job, and to have lunch with the right engineer on the right Wednesday, and to talk about the tool for eleven minutes.

The contribution was, by any honest accounting, worth multiples of my annual salary.

The contribution was not, in any way, on a roadmap.

The contribution would not have happened if the onboarding had been *getting me into the system.*

The contribution happened because the onboarding was *getting me into the social network.*

---

I want to give you a sidebar on a related observation.

**Sidebar: The Lunch Hypothesis.**

The Lunch Hypothesis is the observation, made across approximately four years of personal data, that the most consequential conversations in any healthy company happen at lunch, and that the *frequency of lunches between people who would not otherwise have a reason to talk* is the single best leading indicator of the company's exploratory cognition density.

The Lunch Hypothesis has, in my experience, predicted whether a company is going to ship a breakthrough product in the next twelve months with approximately seventy-percent reliability, based on the frequency and diversity of its lunch table.

A company in which everyone eats lunch at their desk is, with probability approaching one, the Stupid Company™.

A company in which the lunch table has, on any given day, three to five people from three to five different teams talking about something that is not on anyone's quarterly plan is, with probability approaching one, the Wise Corporation.

The Lunch Hypothesis is not on any standard business dashboard.

The Lunch Hypothesis is, in my private estimation, the single most predictive metric in management science.

You can measure the Lunch Hypothesis at your own company in nine minutes by walking through the lunch room at twelve-thirty PM Tuesday.

If the lunch room is empty, your company has problems.

If the lunch room is half-full and the people in it are eating with their phones, your company has different problems.

If the lunch room is two-thirds full and the people in it are talking to each other about things that are not in their job descriptions, your company is one of the rare ones.

You will know which one in nine minutes.

I want to close this chapter with a description of Hyperion's three-hour internal seminar on Wednesday afternoon of my first week.

The seminar was given by Yumi.

The topic was: *why we do not have a conference room with a door that closes.*

The topic was not, by my expectations going in, a topic that warranted three hours of an internal seminar.

The topic, by the end of the three hours, was the most important seminar I have ever attended at any company.

Yumi's thesis was that *the door that closes is the most consequential piece of architectural infrastructure in any office.* The door that closes determines whether the conversation in the room is happening inside the social network or outside it. The closed door says *this conversation is not for the rest of you.* The closed door creates an in-group and an out-group inside the company. The in-group becomes a different entity from the rest of the company. The entity has its own information, its own incentives, its own narrative, and, eventually, its own interests. The interests, in many companies, diverge from the interests of the rest of the company. The divergence is invisible to the rest of the company because the rest of the company is on the wrong side of the door.

Hyperion did not have a conference room with a door that closes.

Hyperion's largest meeting space was an open area in the corner of the office, with a long table, a few hammock chairs, and a view of the rest of the office floor. Every conversation that happened in the meeting space was visible and audible to the rest of the company. Every conversation that happened in the meeting space was implicitly *in front of everyone.*

This had two effects.

The first effect was that nobody at Hyperion ever had a private conversation in the office. Private conversations happened outside the office, after hours, in cafes and bars and on walks. The private conversations were limited in number by the friction of moving them outside. The friction was, in Yumi's read, *a feature, not a bug.* The friction kept the number of private conversations low. The low number of private conversations kept the social network from fragmenting into in-groups and out-groups. The whole company stayed in the same social network.

The second effect was that the most important conversations were, by structural necessity, conducted in language that everyone in the company could understand. The language of the most important conversations was the company's *shared language.* The shared language was the substrate of the social network. The shared language was, across two years of intentional cultivation, what made the company able to think collectively about hard problems.

The closed door, Yumi argued, was the single most consequential thing Stupid Companies™ did to themselves. The closed door produced the structural conditions for the executive in-group's interests to diverge from the company's interests. The divergence produced the dashboards that lied. The dashboards produced the decisions that gutted the company. The decisions were all downstream of the door.

I wrote down four sentences from the seminar on a sticky note.

The four sentences were:

1. *The door is the asset.*

2. *Open doors produce shared language.*

3. *Shared language produces collective thinking.*

4. *Collective thinking produces the next product.*

I keep the sticky note in my wallet.

It is still in my wallet, four years later.

I have, several times, taken it out and shown it to people.

I am showing it to you now.
