# Chapter 7: The Slack Channel Got Quiet

Marv picked up on the second ring.

I want to give you the conversation in full, because the conversation contains a framework I have used in every job I have had since, and I want you to have the framework before you read the rest of the book.

The framework is called **Marv's Parking Lot Method.**

I will explain.

---

I said: "Marv."

Marv said: "Lee."

I said: "Marv, I need to ask you something."

Marv said: "Lee, before you ask, let me ask you something."

I said: "All right."

Marv said: "What time did you call me."

I said: "Three forty-six PM Pacific."

Marv said: "It's Thursday."

I said: "It's Thursday."

Marv said: "How was the parking lot this morning."

I said: "Marv."

Marv said: "Lee, just tell me how the parking lot looked."

I thought about it.

I said: "The parking lot was about a third full at eight-thirty. The west side was emptier than usual. The reserved spots up by the executive entrance had four cars. There was a moving van in the loading zone at the back."

Marv said: "Friday, last Friday, in the afternoon. The parking lot."

I said: "Last Friday afternoon. About four PM. The lot was almost empty. There were nine or ten cars."

Marv said: "Last Friday morning."

I said: "Last Friday morning the lot was about half full. Less than usual for a Friday."

Marv said: "The week before."

I said: "I don't remember the week before. Probably full. It was a normal week."

Marv said: "Lee."

I said: "Yes."

Marv said: "The lot tells you everything. The lot does not know it is telling you everything. The lot is the only honest dashboard in any company. I have been reading the lot at Mendacium Corp for nineteen years. The lot has predicted every major strategic decision Greg has made since he became CEO. The lot has predicted four out of five of Don's strategic decisions before him. The lot is, in my professional opinion, the single most reliable indicator of what is actually happening inside Mendacium, and it is freely available to anyone who is willing to walk through it on the way to the front door."

I said: "Marv."

Marv said: "Yes."

I said: "Walk me through it."

He walked me through it.

---

**Marv's Parking Lot Method** has seven rules. I am giving you the rules here, in numbered list form, because the rules are the entire framework, and the framework is something I want you to have.

1. **Read the lot at the same time every day.** Marv read the lot at eight-thirty AM, walking from his car to the front door, and at five-fifteen PM, walking from the front door back to his car. He did not skip days. He did not read the lot at random times. The same-time-every-day rule produces a baseline. Without a baseline you cannot see the deltas.

2. **Read the lot's geometry, not its volume.** The volume of cars in the lot at any given time is noise. Some days are full because of training sessions or visitors or a quarterly all-hands. The volume is not the signal. The signal is *which sections of the lot are full and which sections of the lot are empty.* The lot is, at most companies, geographically organized — the executives park near the entrance, the senior managers in the next ring, the senior individual contributors in the next, the juniors at the back. When the executive section is empty during work hours, something is happening at the executive level. When the executive section is full but the next ring is half-empty, something is happening to senior management. The geometry is the signal.

3. **Read the visitor section.** Mendacium had a small visitor section near the executive entrance, marked off with the eight reserved spots. The visitor section was, on any normal week, about half full — with consultants, sales prospects, and the occasional analyst. When the visitor section was empty for three consecutive days, the company was not under external scrutiny. When the visitor section was suddenly full with cars Marv did not recognize, *something was being scoped.* The Sophia AI vendor visits to scope the SOPHIE deal had populated the visitor section for two weeks in mid-October of Year Seven, three weeks before the conference. Marv had noted this at the time. He had been the first person at Mendacium to know SOPHIE was coming. He had not told anyone, because nobody had asked.

4. **Read the loading dock.** The loading dock at the back of the building was where physical goods came and went. Mendacium did not have many physical goods. The loading dock was, on a normal week, near-empty. When the loading dock was busy, *furniture or equipment was being moved.* When the loading dock was busy on a Friday afternoon, *furniture was being moved out.* When the loading dock had multiple moving vans, *somebody was leaving who took their furniture with them.* The lay-off Wednesday in late January had been preceded by three days of loading-dock activity that Marv had logged in his daily notebook. None of us had connected the activity to the impending layoff. Marv had. He had not warned us, because by that point he had calculated the cost of warning us, and the cost had been higher than the value of the warning to him personally. We will return to this.

5. **Read the time of day people arrive.** The senior engineers, the senior PMs, and the senior designers at Mendacium had, by their seniority, earned the right to arrive at the office between approximately nine and ten AM. When senior engineers began arriving at eleven, twelve, and one PM on a routine basis, *they were interviewing elsewhere.* The pattern would emerge for a particular person over the course of one to three weeks before that person resigned. Marv had predicted the resignation of six senior engineers across the previous three years by tracking arrival times in his notebook. He had been right on all six.

6. **Read the time of day the executives leave.** The reverse of rule 5. Greg left the building every weekday at five-thirty PM. This was Greg's pattern. When Greg started leaving at three or four PM, *Greg was going to a meeting somewhere else that was more important to him than running the company.* Marv tracked Greg's exit times in his notebook. In the six weeks before the conference, Greg had left early on twelve separate days. Marv had concluded, by mid-September, that Greg was scoping a major external initiative. The initiative turned out to be the AI procurement. Marv had been ahead of the rest of us by three months on this.

7. **Cross-reference the lot with internal Slack volume.** The lot is the leading indicator. Slack is the trailing indicator. When the lot tells you something is happening and Slack does not yet reflect it, *you have between two and six weeks before Slack catches up.* When the lot has been quiet for three weeks and then Slack suddenly explodes about an internal topic, *that topic has been the topic of executive-level conversations for the previous month.* Marv had been using the lot-Slack cross-reference for twelve years. He had been wrong twice. He had been right approximately forty-three times.

I was, by the end of Marv's explanation, twenty-eight minutes into a phone call I had thought would be three minutes.

I said: "Marv."

Marv said: "Yes."

I said: "Why did you tell nobody this for twelve years."

Marv was quiet for a beat.

Marv said: "Lee, I want you to think about that question."

I thought about the question.

I said: "Because the people who would have benefited from knowing it were not the people who could have done anything about it."

Marv said: "Closer."

I said: "Because the people who could have done something about it were the people the method was about."

Marv said: "Closer."

I said: "Because I would have been put on the second list."

Marv said: "You are catching up."

I said: "Marv."

Marv said: "Yes."

I said: "I should have called you four years ago."

Marv said: "Yes, you should have. But you are calling me today. Today is fine."

---

I want to draw the conclusion of this chapter explicitly, because the lesson is the lesson, and I do not want it to get lost in the joke about Wally typing question marks into SOPHIE.

**The Stupid Company™ produces, as one of its structural features, employees who know things they cannot say.**

The employees know what is happening before the executives do. The employees know the dashboards are lying before the dashboards stop lying. The employees know which colleagues are interviewing, which initiatives are being pre-decided, which products are quietly being put on the path to deprecation, which integrations are being held together by one quiet engineer with a *DO NOT TOUCH* Notion page.

The employees know.

They do not say.

They do not say because the cost of saying, at the Stupid Company™, exceeds the cost of not saying.

This is the central mechanism by which the Stupid Company™ destroys itself. The destruction is not done by the executives, who are too busy looking at the dashboards. The destruction is done by the *informational asymmetry between what is in the building and what is on the dashboard.* The asymmetry is, in the Stupid Company™, maintained by the executives' own behavior — by their indifference to walking the parking lot, by their hostility to honest internal communication, by the algorithm that fires the engineers who do not run SOPHIE queries.

The asymmetry destroys the company in slow motion.

By the time the destruction is visible on the dashboard, the people who could have surfaced it earlier are at Hyperion Studio.

This is what I want you to take from this chapter:

**The single most useful diagnostic for whether you work at the Stupid Company™ is to ask yourself: do I know things about this company that I cannot tell my CEO.**

If the answer is no, you may not work at the Stupid Company™.

If the answer is yes, write down the list.

The list is, for the rest of your career, your most important professional asset.

I am not joking.

---

Sam and I, in the weeks after my conversation with Marv, started reading the parking lot together.

We did not tell anyone.

We read it for the next six months.

The parking lot, by April of Year Eight, was telling us things the dashboards would not catch for nine more.

The parking lot was telling us, among other things, that the engineering Slack channels were getting quieter.

Sam pulled the actual data.

The engineering Slack message volume had dropped *eighty-four percent* across the four months since SOPHIE had arrived.

Eighty-four percent.

In four months.

The dashboard for *SOPHIE Engagement* was, in the same period, at an all-time high.

The dashboard for *Slack Engagement* was not on Greg's dashboard at all.

The Slack channel was empty because every conversation that had once happened there was now happening either:

1. In private DMs between two specific people who trusted each other.

2. In phone calls.

3. In the parking lot.

The parking lot, by April, was the most active social space at Mendacium Corp.

I learned more in the Mendacium parking lot in the spring of Year Eight than I had learned in the conference rooms in the previous fourteen years combined.

The conference rooms had become unsafe.

The parking lot had remained safe.

The lot is, again, the only honest dashboard.

If your company's parking lot is, today, the most active social space at your company, you have a diagnosis. You do not need to read further. You can skip to Chapter 20, the one about how to quit on Tuesday.

If your company's parking lot is, today, mostly used for parking, you are at a different kind of company, and we will get to what kind of company that is in Part III.

For now, in Chapter 8, I want to tell you about the day Marv called me back and told me he was right.
