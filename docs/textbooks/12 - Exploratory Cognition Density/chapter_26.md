# Chapter 26: Hiring for Discovery Probability

The Wise Corporation hires for *discovery probability.*

The Stupid Company™ hires for *predictable labor output.*

The two hiring philosophies produce, across a decade, two completely different companies.

I want to give you, in this chapter, the actual questions Hyperion uses in its interview loop. The questions are public. Yumi has talked about them on a few podcasts. Theo has written about them in a blog post. I am collecting them here in one place with the commentary I think is most useful.

The questions are not a list of behavioral prompts. The questions are not Leetcode. The questions are *conversation openers* designed to surface whether the candidate has the substrate that produces exploratory cognition.

There are seven questions.

---

**Question 1: What books have you read in the last twelve months that mattered to you, and why did they matter.**

The question is open-ended. The answer reveals four things.

First, *whether the candidate reads at all.* A surprising number of professional candidates have read approximately zero books in the previous twelve months. The candidates who have read zero books are not, by Hyperion's hiring criteria, candidates Hyperion is going to make offers to. The reading is the substrate. Without the substrate the exploratory cognition does not have anything to draw on.

Second, *the diversity of what they read.* A candidate who has read twelve books, all in their professional specialty, is less interesting than a candidate who has read eight books, four of which are outside their professional specialty. The cross-domain inputs are what produce the *what if* moments. The cross-domain inputs are the asset.

Third, *whether they can articulate why the books mattered.* A candidate who can name twelve titles but cannot articulate what they got from any of them is operating at the surface of their reading. A candidate who can name three titles and articulate, for each, the specific insight or shift or argument they took away is operating at the depth Hyperion is hiring for.

Fourth, *whether their reading taste suggests they will be interesting in the kitchen.* This is the most subjective criterion. The interviewer is essentially asking: *would I want to talk to this person at lunch.* The honest answer to this question, given Hyperion's culture, is decisive.

---

**Question 2: What side project have you built in the last year that you have not been paid for, and why did you build it.**

The question selects for candidates who *build things on their own time.* The candidates who build things on their own time are the candidates whose Skill Stack includes, by definition, the meta-skill of *initiating exploratory work without external direction.*

The Stupid Company™ does not value this meta-skill, because the Stupid Company™'s work is initiated by the executive team's roadmap, and the executive team's roadmap does not include the kind of exploratory work that side projects represent.

The Wise Corporation values this meta-skill above almost any other, because the Wise Corporation's most valuable work, in nine of ten cases, originates as someone's side project. Sam's tool from Chapter 24 is the obvious example. There are dozens of others at Hyperion alone.

A candidate who cannot name a single side project from the previous year is, in Hyperion's hiring frame, *probably not a Wise Corporation hire.*

This does not mean the candidate is a bad hire for some other company. The candidate may be an excellent hire at a Stupid Company™ that needs reliable execution. The candidate is just not the kind of hire that produces what Hyperion needs.

---

**Question 3: What was the last weird idea you had, and what did you do with it.**

The question selects for candidates whose internal monologue includes *weird ideas.* The candidates whose internal monologue includes weird ideas are the candidates who, in Bad Ideas Hour, will *contribute* rather than *silently observe.*

The follow-up — *what did you do with it* — selects for candidates who *act on weird ideas* rather than letting them die in their own heads. Acting is the differentiator. Most people have weird ideas. Few people act.

The acting can be small. A candidate who *wrote a one-page document about a weird idea and emailed it to a friend* has acted. A candidate who *built a small test rig* has acted. A candidate who *changed their behavior for a week to test the idea* has acted. The acting is the signal.

A candidate who has had no weird ideas is, in Hyperion's frame, *probably not in the substrate.* A candidate who has had weird ideas but never acts on them is, in Hyperion's frame, *probably not going to act on them at Hyperion either.*

---

**Question 4: When you have been wrong about a significant professional decision, what was the decision and how did you know you had been wrong.**

The question selects for *self-correction capacity.*

The Wise Corporation requires people who can change their minds when the evidence changes. The Stupid Company™ produces people who *cannot change their minds*, because changing one's mind at the Stupid Company™ is read as a sign of weakness and is punished.

The follow-up — *how did you know* — selects for candidates with *calibrated self-awareness.* A candidate who can articulate the specific signal that told them they were wrong is operating with the kind of metacognition the Wise Corporation needs. A candidate who cannot articulate the signal, or who claims they have never been wrong, is operating with the kind of metacognition the Wise Corporation cannot use.

This is one of the most predictive questions on the list. Hyperion has, in its hiring history, weighted this question more heavily than the others on close calls. The candidates whose answer to this question was strong have, on average, been the strongest hires. The candidates whose answer was weak have been, on average, the misfires.

---

**Question 5: Describe the worst job you have ever had, and what you learned from it.**

The question reveals two things.

First, *whether the candidate has a calibrated sense of dysfunction.* A candidate who describes a previous job as *terrible because of office politics* without specifics is operating with low calibration. A candidate who describes a previous job with specific architectural choices the company had made, specific decisions the executive team had taken, and specific patterns that produced the dysfunction is operating with the kind of calibration Hyperion needs.

Second, *whether the candidate carries grievance.* A candidate who describes a bad previous job with active anger toward specific people, four years after the fact, is carrying grievance. The grievance will arrive at Hyperion with the hire. The grievance will be projected onto future colleagues, who will not have done anything to deserve it. The grievance is a hiring risk.

A candidate who describes a bad job with curiosity rather than anger — *here is what I think was going on, here is what I learned, here is what I would do differently if I were running it* — is operating with the disposition Hyperion can work with.

---

**Question 6: What is the next field you want to learn about that is unrelated to your current job, and why.**

The question selects for the *cross-domain reading* skill from Chapter 13. The question reveals whether the candidate has the disposition to keep building the substrate.

A candidate who cannot name a single field of interest outside their current job is, in Hyperion's frame, *probably done growing.* The candidate may be a fine hire at a company that needs reliable execution in a fixed skill set. The candidate is not the hire Hyperion is making.

A candidate who can name a specific field, with a specific reason, has the substrate.

The reason matters more than the field. A reason like *I have been curious about how cities get built since I read a book about it last year* is a strong reason. A reason like *I want to learn about marketing because the data scientists I work with say it would be useful* is a weak reason — the weak reason is *instrumental*, and instrumental learning rarely produces the cross-domain insights that produce the discoveries.

---

**Question 7: If you joined us, what would you build in your first month that we would not have thought to ask you to build.**

This is the question Theo asked me at the end of my interview, in Chapter 21.

The question selects for *initiative.*

The question is, structurally, the most important question in the loop, because the question reveals whether the candidate has, by the close of the interview, *integrated enough of the company* to propose something specific.

A candidate who has been listening across the four hours of interview will be able to propose something concrete. The proposal will be informed by the conversations the candidate has had. The proposal will draw on the candidate's existing skill set and on the specific gaps the candidate has inferred from the four hours.

A candidate who has been *waiting to be told what to do* across the four hours will not be able to propose something. The candidate will give a generic answer about *being open to whatever the team needs.* The generic answer is the disqualifier.

The candidate who proposes something concrete is the candidate who will, in their first month, *bring exploratory cognition density to the company.* The candidate who gives the generic answer is the candidate who will, in their first month, be waiting to be told what to do, which is the Stupid Company™ disposition the Wise Corporation cannot afford to import.

---

That is the loop.

Seven questions.

No coding interview.

No architecture interview.

No behavioral interview in the conventional sense.

The questions take, in total, about four hours of interviewer time.

The questions produce, in Hyperion's history, a hire-success rate that I will not quote in specific numbers because the numbers are private, but which is, by Yumi's own description in a podcast last year, *substantially higher than the industry average.*

The interview is the diagnostic.

The diagnostic is, in itself, exploratory cognition.

The interview, in operational terms, is the company already being the company in front of the candidate.

The candidate, by the end of the interview, has either chosen the company or chosen against the company.

The company, by the end of the interview, has either chosen the candidate or chosen against the candidate.

The choices are usually aligned.

The misfires are, in Hyperion's history, approximately one in twenty.

The misfires are parted with cleanly inside ninety days.

The company continues.

The exploratory cognition density holds.
