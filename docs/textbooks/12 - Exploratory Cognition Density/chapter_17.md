# Chapter 17: Why Play Has Economic Value

Most readers, by this point in the book, are willing to accept that exploratory cognition is, in the abstract, valuable.

Most readers are not yet willing to accept that *play* — the actual experience of doing exploratory cognition, with colleagues, on company time — has direct economic value to the company.

The hesitation is reasonable.

The hesitation comes from a lifetime of corporate experience in which *play* was either nonexistent (Stupid Company™) or theatrical (the Stupid Company™'s attempt at *innovation culture*, which is play with a quarterly KPI attached, which is not play, which is theater).

I want to make the case here for *real* play. The case has three parts.

---

**Part one. The economic argument.**

In a labor market where execution is becoming cheap because AI does the execution, the bottleneck on company value is the supply of *useful new directions to point the execution at.*

The supply of useful new directions is the output of exploratory cognition.

The output of exploratory cognition is, as we established in Chapter 16, unstructured, exploratory, and unpredictable.

The output of unstructured exploratory unpredictable cognition is, in practice, *play.*

Play is the activity that produces the output. The output is the asset. The asset is what the company is worth in five years.

If your company is not producing the output, your company is, in five years, going to be worth what its current execution is worth.

In a world where execution is cheap, what current execution is worth is *the marginal value of one more execution*, which is approaching zero.

The companies that survive the AI era are the companies that have a sustained supply of useful new directions.

The supply of useful new directions is produced by play.

Therefore: *play produces the asset that determines whether the company is worth anything in five years.*

This is the economic argument. The argument is, in my honest experience, persuasive on first reading to about half of the executives I have presented it to. The other half nod politely and go back to their dashboards. The half that nod are going to be running Stupid Companies™ for the next decade. The half that are persuaded are the ones who will, in some cases, build Wise Corporations.

---

**Part two. The historical argument.**

Almost every major product that any of the companies you have heard of currently ships originated as somebody's weird side project.

Gmail was twenty-percent time at Google.

Slack was an internal tool a gaming company built for itself because its game was failing.

Post-it Notes were a failed adhesive at 3M.

The iPhone was Steve Jobs's reluctance to make a tablet first.

AWS was Amazon's internal infrastructure rented out to other companies as an experiment.

Twitter was a podcasting company's hackathon project.

The list goes on.

I am not the first person to make this observation. I am not the millionth. The observation is one of the most consistently repeated observations in the business literature. The observation is, in operational terms, one of the most consistently ignored observations in the business literature.

The reason it gets ignored is that it cannot be turned into a roadmap.

You cannot put *we are going to have a Gmail-shaped breakthrough in Q3* on a roadmap.

You can only put *we are going to give engineers twenty percent unstructured time and see what happens.* The first version has a clear outcome. The second version has an uncertain outcome. Executives in the Stupid Company™ tradition prefer the first version even though the first version, in the historical record, almost never happens.

The Wise Corporation prefers the second version because the Wise Corporation has internalized the historical argument and accepts that the breakthrough is what is on the other side of the unstructured time, even though you cannot predict when it will arrive or what it will look like.

---

**Part three. The diagnostic argument.**

You can identify whether your company has access to play by running a single test.

The test is: *can two engineers, both in good standing, spend an afternoon on something that neither of them has been asked to do, without having to clear it with their manager.*

If the answer is yes, your company has play.

If the answer is no, your company does not have play.

If the answer is *yes but they have to clear it*, your company has *theatrical play.*

Most companies are in the third category. They have a *hack day* once a quarter. They have *twenty percent time* in the employee handbook that nobody uses. They have an *innovation week* during which everyone is encouraged to be creative as long as the creativity is documented in advance and reviewed afterward.

This is not play.

This is the corporate immune system's response to the suggestion that play might be necessary.

Real play does not survive in companies that have a corporate immune system optimized to kill it.

The corporate immune system is, in most companies, run by people who genuinely believe they are doing the right thing. They are not malicious. They are working on the metric they have been given. The metric is *predictability.* The metric does not have a column for *exploratory cognition density.* The metric punishes the engineers who spend an afternoon on something not on the roadmap, because the metric reports the afternoon as *unallocated time.*

The metric is the cause.

The cause is upstream of the immune system.

The cause is on the dashboard.

I keep coming back to the dashboard. I do not apologize for this. The dashboard is the single most consequential management innovation of the previous forty years, and the dashboard is also, in its overuse, the single most consequential cause of the structural problems this book is about. The dashboard is a tool. The dashboard is a fine tool when used carefully. The dashboard has, across approximately ninety percent of the companies I have observed, been used uncarefully. The dashboard has been used to drive decisions in domains where the dashboard's coverage is incomplete. The dashboard's coverage is *always* incomplete. The decisions are *always* incomplete. The companies are, after ten years of incomplete decisions, hollow.

This is the structural argument for why play has economic value.

The structural argument is that play is the only thing that produces the value that the dashboard cannot measure.

---

I want to close this chapter with a short, important section.

**The play has to be voluntary.**

You cannot mandate play. You cannot calendarize play. You cannot reward play with a quarterly bonus tied to *quantity of play.*

You can mandate the *time and space* in which play could happen. You can create the *social safety* that allows weird ideas to be said. You can hire people who are inclined to play and not hire people who would rather not.

But the play itself has to be voluntary, or it is not play.

Mendacium Corp, in the Year Eight initiative I have not yet told you about — the *Innovation Wave* that Karina launched in Q3 of Year Eight in response to the cognitive drought she could not name — instituted a mandatory hack day every quarter, with required submissions, required participation, and required executive presentations of the outputs.

The hack day, by every honest measure, produced nothing.

The hack day produced approximately eighteen presentations per quarter, of which two were already-existing work product that the engineer had relabeled as a hack-day output and sixteen were defensive submissions of the bare minimum required to satisfy the participation requirement.

The hack day continued for six quarters.

Greg presented Innovation Wave at the Year Nine annual all-hands as one of his *defining cultural initiatives.*

Three months after the all-hands, Mendacium's first AI-replacement-driven customer non-renewals began appearing in the financial reports.

The hack day was not what was going to save the company.

The hack day was the *appearance* of what would have saved the company, performed for an executive team that wanted to be able to point to a slide that said *we tried.*

The Wise Corporation does not do hack days.

The Wise Corporation has Bad Ideas Hour, which has no formal structure, no required output, no executive presentation, no quarterly KPI, and no mandatory participation.

Bad Ideas Hour, at the companies I have observed where it exists, produces more shipped product value per engineer-hour than any other engineering ritual.

The Wise Corporation gets this for free, because the Wise Corporation does not try to capture it.

The Stupid Company™ cannot have this at any price, because the Stupid Company™ has, in trying to capture it, killed it.
