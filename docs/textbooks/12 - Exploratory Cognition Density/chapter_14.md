# Chapter 14: How to Tell If You Are Being Replaced

This is the diagnostic chapter.

I want you to read it slowly.

There are twelve questions. Each question is answered yes or no. There is no maybe. There is no *it depends.* If your honest answer is *it depends,* the answer is yes.

At the end of the twelve questions, I will give you the scoring. You will know, at the end of the scoring, whether you are being replaced.

If you are being replaced, you will know what to do.

If you are not being replaced, you will also know what to do.

You will not, at the end of this chapter, be in the position you are in right now, which is the position of *not being sure.* The Not-Being-Sure position is the worst position you can be in. The Not-Being-Sure position is what produces the paralysis that the Stupid Company™ depends on to keep you in your seat for the eighteen months it needs to fire you.

Let us get out of the Not-Being-Sure position.

Here are the twelve questions.

---

**Question 1.** In the previous twelve months, has your manager or any of your manager's manager mentioned, in writing or in a meeting, that your role might be *augmented* or *evolved* in light of AI capabilities?

**Question 2.** In the previous twelve months, has an AI tool — a specific named tool, with a specific named vendor — been introduced into your daily workflow as a *replacement* for any part of the work you used to do?

**Question 3.** In the previous twelve months, has the number of people doing your kind of work at your company gone down, stayed the same, or gone up? (Answer YES if it has gone down or stayed the same. Answer NO if it has gone up.)

**Question 4.** Is the dollar value of your annual compensation currently below the dollar value of the AI tool that has been introduced into your daily workflow? (You may have to ask. Most people who have asked have been surprised at the answer.)

**Question 5.** When you ask the AI tool a question that is directly inside your area of expertise, does the AI's answer match what you would have said, more than seventy percent of the time? (Answer YES if more than seventy percent. Answer NO if less.)

**Question 6.** Has anyone at your company, in the previous twelve months, used the phrase *productivity multiplier* in your presence in connection with the AI tool you have been given?

**Question 7.** Have you, in the previous twelve months, been asked to *train* the AI tool by feeding it documentation, examples, prompts, or other material from your past work product?

**Question 8.** Has your role been reorganized, in the previous twelve months, into a team or department whose name contains the phrase *AI*, *Automation*, *Transformation*, or *Innovation*?

**Question 9.** Has your direct manager, in the previous twelve months, taken on additional direct reports who were previously reporting to a different manager, as part of a *flattening* or *consolidation* of the organization?

**Question 10.** Has the cadence of feedback you receive from your manager changed, in the previous twelve months, from regular (weekly or biweekly) to either *more frequent and more detailed* or *less frequent and more vague*?

**Question 11.** Have you been asked, in the previous twelve months, to participate in any *upskilling*, *reskilling*, or *AI fluency* training initiative that was not optional?

**Question 12.** When you do the work you have always done, do you feel — in your honest estimation, not in your public performance for the company — that the work is going to be necessary in three years?

---

Score yourself.

Each YES is one point.

If you answered honestly, you have a score between zero and twelve.

Here is the scoring.

---

**Score 0-2.** You are not being replaced. You may be in a Wise Corporation, or you may be in a Stupid Company™ that has not yet reached your specific role with the AI rollout, or you may be in a role that the AI rollout is structurally unlikely to reach (most often: a hands-on physical-world skill, a senior leadership role, or a sales role with a personal customer book). The next eighteen months are an opportunity to skill-stack toward something more valuable. Do not waste them. Skip ahead to Chapter 19, which is the worksheet for your Skill Stack inventory. Come back for the rest of the book on a slower schedule.

**Score 3-5.** You are in a transition zone. The transition zone is the most dangerous zone. The transition zone is where most workers think they are not being replaced, but where in fact the early conditions for replacement are being put in place around them. The transition zone usually precedes the active replacement period by twelve to twenty-four months. Use the time. Do not panic. But do not be complacent. The actions in Chapter 15 through Chapter 20 are for you. Read them carefully. Adopt at least three of them in the next ninety days.

**Score 6-8.** You are being actively prepared for replacement. The AI tool has been introduced, the productivity language is in circulation, the reorgs are happening, and your role's *long-term necessity* is being questioned by the executive team in conversations you are not in. The active replacement period is usually six to fifteen months. You have, by my best honest estimation, between one and four quarters before the formal replacement decision is made. The actions in Chapter 20 — *how to quit on Tuesday* — are written for you. Read them. Quit. The cost of quitting now is much lower than the cost of being told to quit in eight months.

**Score 9-12.** You have already been replaced. The company has not yet told you, but the company has, in operational terms, decided. The dashboards are tracking your output for the formal review. The HR algorithm has tagged your role. The reorg has set up a structure in which your function can be cleanly subtracted. The active replacement period is in its final months. You will be told between now and roughly thirty weeks from now. The actions in Chapter 20 are urgent. Quit on Tuesday. Take what you have learned. Go to a Wise Corporation. The compensation you will negotiate at the Wise Corporation, in my honest experience with twelve engineers I have personally watched do this, has been roughly equivalent to the compensation at the Stupid Company™. The work satisfaction has been, on average, dramatically higher.

---

I want to flag, before we move to Chapter 15, the most common mistake people make on this diagnostic.

The most common mistake is to answer *no* to questions you should be answering *yes* to.

The mistake is structural. The mistake comes from the same place the *Not-Being-Sure* paralysis comes from. The mistake is the brain protecting the worker from acknowledging an uncomfortable truth that the worker does not yet feel ready to act on.

I am going to give you the test for the test.

The test for the test is: go back through the twelve questions, and for each question on which you answered *no*, ask yourself if a person who knew nothing about your situation would have answered *yes* based on the same evidence.

If the answer is yes, change your answer.

The brain that protected you from the truth was doing its job. Its job is to keep you functional in the short term. The brain's job is not to plan your career. The brain's job is to make sure you can show up for the next meeting. The career planning has to be done by a different part of the brain. The different part of the brain has to read the evidence the way a stranger would read it.

Do the test for the test.

Rescore yourself.

Most readers who do the test for the test increase their score by two to three points. A small number decrease it by one. A few do not change.

The new score is the score.

I want to close this chapter with a story.

In Year Eight at Mendacium, six weeks before I quit, I took this diagnostic myself.

I scored a five.

I told Sam I had scored a five.

Sam looked at me for a beat.

Sam said: "Lee."

I said: "Yes."

Sam said: "Take the diagnostic again. Pretend you do not know yourself. Pretend you are reading the evidence about a stranger."

I took the diagnostic again.

I scored a nine.

I told Sam I had scored a nine.

Sam said: "Yes you did."

I said: "Sam."

He said: "Yes."

I said: "I scored a five the first time."

He said: "I know you did."

I said: "Why."

He said: "Because if you had scored a nine the first time you would have had to quit on Tuesday. The five was the score that let you stay until you were ready to quit. The five did its job. The five protected you from quitting before you were ready. You are now ready. The nine is the score."

I said: "Sam."

He said: "Yes."

I said: "What did you score."

He said: "I scored a twelve."

I said: "When."

He said: "March."

I said: "March of this year."

He said: "March of this year."

I said: "Sam."

He said: "Yes."

I said: "It is now May."

He said: "I know it is."

I said: "Are you still here."

He said: "I am still here because I am closing out a project I want to take with me. I have been packing my desk for nine weeks. I am closing the project tomorrow. I am giving my notice on Wednesday. I am starting at Hyperion on the first of June."

I said: "Sam."

He said: "Yes."

I said: "You did not tell me."

He said: "I am telling you now. I would like you to come with me. I have already mentioned you to the Hyperion founders. They have asked me to ask you whether you would take an introductory call. The call is scheduled, on my recommendation, for tomorrow at three PM Pacific. The call is on your calendar already. It is on your calendar as *Hyperion Studio / intro discussion / 1 hour.* The slot is held. You do not have to take the call. You should take the call."

I said: "Sam."

He said: "Yes."

I said: "I will take the call."

He said: "Good."

I took the call.
