# Chapter 27: The Day the Two Companies Diverged

The text came in at four eleven PM Pacific on a Wednesday in March of Year Ten, twenty-two months after I had joined Hyperion.

The text was from Kai.

I had not heard from Kai in about a year.

The text said: *Mendacium just laid off forty percent.*

I read the text.

I read it twice.

I opened the news.

The news, on Bloomberg, was: *Mendacium Corp Announces Sweeping AI-Driven Restructuring, Cuts Workforce By 40%.*

The article said:

> *Mendacium Corp, the four-thousand-employee enterprise software firm, announced today a sweeping organizational restructuring that will reduce its workforce by approximately forty percent, eliminating roughly 1,680 positions across its engineering, product, design, customer-success, and operations divisions. The cuts, the company said in a press release, are the result of "the successful integration of AI-augmented operational tooling that has substantially increased per-employee productivity across the organization." CEO Greg Tannenbaum told reporters in a brief media call that "the new operational model positions Mendacium as the industry's most AI-native enterprise software company." The stock closed up four percent on the day.*

I read the article three times.

I sat at my desk in the Hyperion kitchen, where I had been for the previous nine months because the Hyperion kitchen had become, by some unspoken consensus, my preferred work location, and I thought about Greg.

I thought about the four percent up.

The four percent up was, by my honest read, the most informative single fact in the article.

The market had rewarded the layoff.

The market had not asked any of the questions the article had not answered.

The article had not answered: *what happens to the customers whose integrations are now being maintained by zero people.* The article had not answered: *what does the engineering organization's exploratory cognition density look like after a forty-percent reduction.* The article had not answered: *who, specifically, has been let go, and what institutional knowledge has gone with them.* The article had not answered: *what is the actual two-year operational risk of the restructuring.*

The market does not, in the AI era, ask those questions.

The market rewards the headline number.

The market will, in twelve to eighteen months, ask the questions.

The questions will, in twelve to eighteen months, be the questions the next *Wall Street Journal* article about Mendacium asks.

The next article will not have a positive headline.

---

I forwarded Kai's text to Sam.

Sam was at the long table at the back of the kitchen.

Sam read the forward.

Sam looked up.

Sam said: "Lee."

I said: "Yes."

Sam said: "I want to make a list."

I said: "Of what."

Sam said: "Of the people we should reach out to."

I said: "From Mendacium."

Sam said: "From Mendacium. We are going to lose some good people from that layoff. The good people are going to be the ones who are, today, sitting at their desks at Mendacium reading the same Bloomberg article you are reading, and thinking about whether to update their LinkedIn. We have a window of about ninety-six hours before the rest of the industry's recruiters start reaching out. I want our list ready by tonight. I am going to talk to Theo at five PM."

I said: "Sam."

Sam said: "Yes."

I said: "How many slots do we have."

Sam said: "I am going to ask Theo for three. I think he is going to give me three. If I make the case well, I might get five. We are not the only Wise Corporation that is going to be doing this in the next ninety-six hours. The other Wise Corporations are doing the same calculation in their own kitchens right now. Most of them have lists. We have a list, too. The list is in my head. I want it on paper."

I said: "Sam."

Sam said: "Yes."

I said: "I am going to help."

Sam said: "I know you are. That is why I told you about the list."

---

We made the list in seventy-eight minutes.

The list had twelve names.

The twelve names were:

1. Catalina Ortiz — the senior designer who, at Bad Ideas Hour in Year Seven, had proposed Dr. Seuss-style onboarding emails. The proposal had been killed. Catalina had stayed at Mendacium because she had a child with significant medical needs and the Mendacium health insurance was unusually good. The Mendacium health insurance had, in Year Nine, been reorganized in a way that was likely to make Catalina's situation less stable. Sam and I had been worried about her for six months. The layoff might be, in operational terms, *the event that lets her leave with a clean conscience.*

2. Yusuf Adekunle — the senior engineer who had proposed *Choose Your Own Adventure retry-logic* at Bad Ideas Hour in Year Six. Yusuf had been Sam's closest collaborator at Mendacium. Yusuf had stayed because his wife was finishing a PhD at the University of Washington and they could not move. Hyperion was, geographically, the right kind of move. Yusuf had been on Sam's list since the week Sam left Mendacium.

3. Devon Cheng — the database engineer who had proposed the idea about *the database telling us when it wants to be queried.* Devon had, by Sam's tracking, become increasingly disengaged at Mendacium across Year Eight and Year Nine. Devon was the kind of engineer whose disengagement, at the Stupid Company™, looked like *low productivity*, and whose re-engagement, at the Wise Corporation, would look like *one of the most prolific engineers on the team.*

4. Adira Eisenberg — the product manager who had proposed *pop-song-formatted error messages* at Bad Ideas Hour in Year Six. Adira had moved out of engineering and into product management at Mendacium in Year Eight, ostensibly as a promotion but in operational terms as a sideways move that had been read by the engineering organization as Karina's office quietly removing her from the engineering Slack channels. Adira was, by my honest read, *one of the highest-EQ product people in the industry.*

5-12. (Eight other names. I will not list them all. The pattern repeats. Each of them was, at Mendacium, the kind of person whose contribution had been visible to the engineering organization and invisible to the executive team. Each of them was, in operational terms, the kind of person the algorithm fires before the algorithm fires the person whose work the company actually depends on.)

Sam took the list to Theo at five oh-three PM.

Theo looked at the list for nine minutes.

Theo said: "Sam."

Sam said: "Yes."

Theo said: "We have three slots."

Sam said: "I expected three."

Theo said: "I am going to give you five."

Sam said: "Theo."

Theo said: "I have been waiting for this layoff for fourteen months. We have a budget line. The budget line was for *one-time hiring opportunities related to AI-restructuring at competitor firms.* I have been saving the line. The line is exactly five FTEs. The line is yours. Pick the five. Move fast. The window is, by your own estimation, ninety-six hours."

Sam said: "I have the five."

Theo said: "Tell me."

Sam told him.

Sam picked: Catalina, Yusuf, Devon, Adira, and one of the eight others — an engineer named Khari Williams who had been at Mendacium since Year Two and who Sam was confident would, at Hyperion, become a Distinguished Engineer inside thirty months.

Theo said: "Good list. Reach out tonight. I will personally call each of them on Friday morning if your introductions land. I want them in the kitchen by Wednesday of next week."

Sam said: "I will."

Sam reached out that night, by personal email, to all five.

All five responded within twenty-four hours.

Four of them accepted Hyperion offers within ninety-six hours.

The fifth — Catalina — accepted twelve days later, after she and her partner had thought through the health-insurance transition.

All five started at Hyperion within five weeks.

Two of the five — Yusuf and Khari — have, in the eighteen months since, become two of the most consequential engineers on Sam's team.

Catalina has, in the eighteen months since, shipped two product-design initiatives that together accounted for roughly nineteen percent of Hyperion's new customer onboarding growth.

Devon, in his second month at Hyperion, shipped a database tool he had been wanting to build for nine years.

Adira, in her fourth month at Hyperion, was promoted to head of product.

---

Mendacium, by Q4 of Year Ten, had lost an additional ninety-three engineers to voluntary attrition.

Mendacium, by Q1 of Year Eleven, announced a second restructuring, this one *streamlining* the company's go-to-market organization.

Mendacium, by Q3 of Year Eleven, announced that it was *exploring strategic options* with a major investment bank.

Mendacium, by Q1 of Year Twelve, announced its acquisition by a private equity firm for approximately one quarter of its peak valuation.

Greg, by Q2 of Year Twelve, was no longer the CEO.

Greg, by Q4 of Year Twelve, was on the board of two AI-first startups, on the speaking circuit, and was being interviewed for at least three other CEO roles that I am aware of.

Greg had landed.

Greg had taken with him a separation package that the board had approved at his hiring.

Greg's LinkedIn, at the time of this writing, lists him as a *strategic advisor on AI transformation initiatives.*

Greg's LinkedIn does not mention what happened at Mendacium.

The companies Greg is now advising will, on average, look like Mendacium did in Year Seven, three years after Greg arrives.

This is the pattern.

The pattern is the pattern.

The pattern is, on a long-enough time horizon, the most reliable signal of where the AI economy is going.

The second-to-last chapter is the compounding curve of the Wise Corporation.

The last chapter is the closer.

The closer is the question.

You already know the question.

You already know the answer.

We will get there.
