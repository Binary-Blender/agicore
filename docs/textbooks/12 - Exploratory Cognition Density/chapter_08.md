# Chapter 8: Marv Was Right

Marv called me back at four-twelve PM on a Thursday in late April of Year Eight, ten weeks after the layoffs.

He said: "Lee."

I said: "Marv."

He said: "Sit down."

I said: "I am sitting."

He said: "The cancellation just came in."

I said: "Which one."

He said: "Marv said the integrations were going to start failing inside six months. The integrations are starting to fail. Six months would have been the second week of July. The first integration failed yesterday afternoon."

I said: "Which integration."

He said: "The one with the second-largest VECTORLINE customer by ARR. The one I have been keeping alive on the deprecated endpoint that nobody at Mendacium other than me knew existed. The endpoint went down on Wednesday afternoon when someone — and I cannot prove this but I have a guess — touched one of the seventeen pieces of dead code on the platform team. The endpoint had been routing through the dead code in a way the original integration partner had set up in 2016. The customer started seeing data-sync failures at three-fifteen PM Wednesday. The customer-success manager opened an internal ticket at four-twenty PM. The platform team failed to reproduce. They closed the ticket Friday morning."

I said: "And."

He said: "The customer escalated to their CIO this morning. The CIO has notified Mendacium's account manager that they are putting the renewal on hold pending a root-cause analysis. The renewal is twelve point four million dollars a year. The contract is up for renewal in September. They are going to not renew, Lee. I have spoken to the CIO. He is a colleague from another life. He told me. He is not telling Mendacium."

I said: "Marv."

He said: "Yes."

I said: "How do you know about an internal Mendacium ticket from three weeks after you were fired."

He was quiet for a beat.

He said: "Lee. The *DO NOT TOUCH* page has a footer that you did not read. The footer is a Mailgun webhook. The webhook fires when one of the seventeen pieces of dead code is touched in a way that triggers an error. The webhook emails me. I have been getting the emails for six years. I have answered three of them across that time, in disguise, through a contractor email I set up under a different name. The other webhooks have just been my own internal record-keeping. The Wednesday webhook fired at three-eleven PM Wednesday. I knew about the production incident before the customer-success manager opened the ticket."

I said: "Marv."

He said: "Yes."

I said: "You set up a webhook to monitor Mendacium production from outside the company."

He said: "I set up the webhook in Year Sixteen, when I realized that anyone who touched the dead code without warning was going to take down a customer integration, and that nobody at Mendacium other than me would know what had happened. The webhook was my insurance. Insurance against being the only one in the building who knew what was about to break."

I said: "Marv."

He said: "Lee, I am telling you about the webhook because you are the person at Mendacium who is going to figure out how to use the *DO NOT TOUCH* page across the next six months. The webhook is going to keep firing. You are going to start getting the emails. I am forwarding them to you, starting tomorrow. You will know about the failures before Mendacium's own internal systems know. You will be able to choose, for each one, whether to surface it inside Mendacium or whether to let it ride."

I said: "Why are you giving this to me."

He said: "Because the *DO NOT TOUCH* page is going to save the customers, but it cannot save the company. The company has chosen to fire the person who knew the page. The company has chosen to put SOPHIE in the seat where the person sat. SOPHIE does not know about the page. SOPHIE will not know about the page until somebody types the page's content into SOPHIE's training data, which nobody is going to do because the page is in a personal Notion that has now been removed from Mendacium's tenancy. The company has, in operational terms, lost the institutional memory of its own product. The customers are going to leave. The revenue is going to follow. The dashboard is going to keep showing green for somewhere between nine and fifteen months. Then it is going to show red, and Greg is going to be told by the board that the company needs a *strategic review.* The strategic review is the press release in which the company announces that it is being acquired."

I said: "Marv."

He said: "Yes."

I said: "When does the dashboard turn red."

He said: "On Greg's dashboard, given the metrics he tracks, the red signal is not going to land until Q4 of Year Nine. That is eighteen months from this phone call. The actual underlying business is going to be in serious trouble by Q2. The gap is the gap between the dashboard and the reality. I have been calling this gap Marv's Window. I have been thinking about how to teach it to you for six months."

I said: "Marv's Window."

He said: "Marv's Window."

He explained it.

---

**Marv's Window** is the period between the moment the Stupid Company™'s underlying business begins to decline and the moment the Stupid Company™'s executive dashboard begins to register the decline.

Marv's Window has, in Marv's own twelve-year practice of measuring it, ranged from a low of seven months to a high of twenty-six months. The median, on his cases, was fourteen months.

The Window matters because *the people who know about the decline inside Marv's Window are the people who can make the most consequential personal-career decisions in the period immediately before the public market knows.*

Inside Marv's Window:

1. You can interview at other companies as a senior employee of an apparently-healthy company, which positions you better than interviewing as a senior employee of a publicly-troubled company.

2. You can sell stock at apparently-healthy-company prices, which is, in many cases, significantly above the price you will be able to sell at six months later.

3. You can advocate, internally, for changes that would actually save the company, *while the executives still believe the company does not need saving.* Most of these advocacy efforts fail. The ones that succeed are the only thing that has ever saved a Stupid Company™ from itself, in the historical record. They are rare.

4. You can write down the things you know that the company does not know, in a format that you can take with you when you leave. (Marv called this *the parallel filing cabinet.* I will not explain it here because it is the entire subject of Chapter 19.)

5. You can identify which of your colleagues are also inside the Window with you and form the small mutual-aid network that will get half of you out of the company alive.

Marv's Window is, in operational terms, the only thing that separates the smart employees of a Stupid Company™ from the unlucky ones.

The smart ones use the Window.

The unlucky ones find out about the trouble the same week the board does.

---

I want to be careful about how I am presenting this.

I am not telling you to be the rat that leaves the sinking ship.

I am telling you to be the navigator who recognizes that the ship is taking on water before the captain has noticed.

If you have the chance to bail water, you should bail water. Many of us do not have the chance. The captain has, as we discussed in Chapter 7, made it socially expensive to point out that water is coming in. The captain has built an algorithm that fires the people who notice the water coming in. The captain is going to keep doing this until the water is up to the captain's knees. By the time the water is up to the captain's knees, the captain is going to have a separation package that the board approved at his hiring. The captain is going to land at his next CEO role inside six months. The captain is going to take with him the lesson that he made the right calls but was sabotaged by *legacy culture* or *organizational resistance* or *the technology cycle.*

The captain is not the person this book is for.

The captain is the person this book is *about.*

The captain is not going to read this book. The captain reads books with the word *Disrupt* in the title.

You are the navigator. You are the person on the bridge who has the instruments. You are the person who can see the depth meter going up. You are the person who can read the parking lot. You are the person who, in Marv's Window, has the chance to make the calls that will determine where you are working in eighteen months.

I want you to use the Window.

I am not asking you to be the captain.

I am asking you to be the navigator.

The Window is the navigator's instrument.

---

>
> **LEE:** Yes.
>
> **MARV:** Marv's Window opens for a Stupid Company™ approximately the day they put SOPHIE in the building. You have, at Mendacium, somewhere between eight and fourteen months to use it. After that the Window closes and Mendacium becomes the story everyone already knows. Use the Window.
>
> **LEE:** Marv.
>
> **MARV:** Yes.
>
> **LEE:** How did you use yours.
>
> **MARV:** I started talking to Hyperion in October of Year Six. I had an offer letter in my desk drawer by March of Year Seven. The offer letter is what made it possible for me to not care about being on the layoff list. The offer letter is what I used to negotiate my severance package, which Bo in HR did not know about, because Bo is not paid to ask. I have been at Hyperion for ten weeks. I am happier than I have been at any point in the previous nine years. My SOPHIE engagement score at Hyperion is not measured because Hyperion does not believe in SOPHIE engagement scores. My output is, in my honest estimation, four times what it was at Mendacium. My pay is roughly equivalent. I will tell you about Hyperion in person sometime. For now, I want you to use the Window.
>
> **LEE:** Marv.
>
> **MARV:** Yes.
>
> **LEE:** Thank you.
>
> **MARV:** Lee. Goodnight.

He hung up.

I sat at my desk.

I opened a new document.

I titled it: *MY WINDOW.*

I began to write down the things I knew about Mendacium that Mendacium did not know about itself.

By Sunday night I had thirty-one pages.

By the following Wednesday I had forty-eight.

By the end of April of Year Eight, six weeks before I quit, I had a one-hundred-and-twelve-page document that contained, in operational form, every piece of institutional knowledge I had accumulated in fourteen years at Mendacium Corp.

I took the document with me when I left.

The document is, today, the single most professionally valuable artifact I own.

The document is what Chapter 19 is about.

The Window is what the next twelve chapters are about, indirectly, because the Window is the thing that produces the choices that the next twelve chapters teach you how to make.

You are in your own Window right now, almost certainly, although you may not know it. The diagnostic is whether you can name three things about your company that your CEO does not know.

If you can, you are in the Window.

If you cannot, you are not paying attention.

I would like you to start paying attention.

Marv would too.

He gave me his framework. I am giving it to you.

Reagan quits in Chapter 9.
