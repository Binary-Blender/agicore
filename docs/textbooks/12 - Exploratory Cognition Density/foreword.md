# Foreword

In June of 1995, a telecommunications company called Pacific Bell laid off a thirty-eight-year-old engineer named Scott Adams.

Pacific Bell, in laying him off, was doing what every Stupid Company™ does when it has decided that the engineering workforce is, in the current quarter's vocabulary, *bloated.* The company was rationalizing its labor costs. The company's executive team was looking at the dashboards. The dashboards said that Adams's department was full of expensive engineers whose specific contributions could not be easily tied to specific revenue lines. The dashboards recommended that the department be reduced. The dashboards were green. The executive team made the call.

The executive team did not know — and the dashboards did not have a column for this — that the engineer they were laying off had, on the side, for the previous six years, been drawing a comic strip about the engineering organization at Pacific Bell.

The strip had been syndicated since 1989. The strip had, by 1995, run in fifty-four newspapers. The strip's name was *Dilbert.*

In 1995, Pacific Bell laid Adams off and pushed him out the door, and Adams went home and went full-time on the strip, and the strip went from fifty-four newspapers to two thousand within four years, and Adams became the most-read corporate satirist in American history, and Pacific Bell became a footnote in the book the corporate world wrote about itself across the next thirty years.

The corporate world has, since 1995, been quietly aware that it lost Scott Adams.

The corporate world has not been aware that *the way it lost him is the same way it loses everyone like him.*

This book is about that loss.

---

I want to be honest, in this foreword, about what you are about to read.

The book you are holding is a *tribute.*

The book uses, by name, the characters Scott Adams created in *Dilbert*. The Pointy-Haired Boss is in this book. Dilbert is in this book. Wally is in this book. Alice is in this book. Asok is in this book. Catbert is in this book. Dogbert is in this book, although Dogbert is in this book as an AI vendor product, which is the joke. The characters appear in their canonical roles. The characters are referenced, used, and animated across all thirty chapters.

The book is not commercial.

The book is open-source. It will be published, free, on the AI WIN-WIN Institute's textbook shelf. Nobody will be charged for it. Nobody will earn royalties from it. The book is, in operational terms, a non-commercial homage to the engineer-turned-cartoonist who, in the thirty years since Pacific Bell laid him off, did more to document the structural dysfunction of corporate work than any other writer in American letters.

The legal frame for the book is the parody-law tradition. The artistic frame is the meta-comedy of it. *Dilbert* parodies corporate work. This book is a tribute to that parody. The tribute uses the characters that did the parody. The use is, in itself, the tribute. You can see how this is going to go.

Comedian Nathan Fielder once opened a coffee shop called *Dumb Starbucks.* The defense was that the word *Dumb* made it parody. The defense was never tested in court. The defense did not need to be tested in court because nobody seriously believed Fielder was a Starbucks competitor. Everyone understood the joke. The joke was the point.

The defense of this book is the same defense.

Nobody is going to confuse this book for a *Dilbert* product. The book is by an engineer named Christopher Bender, writing through the persona of an engineer named Lee Hauschild, in a register that anyone who has read Scott Adams will immediately recognize as both *inspired by* and *not actually written by* Adams. The book uses the characters because the characters are the most efficient way to land the lessons. The lessons are the lessons Adams has been teaching for thirty-five years. The lessons are still true. The characters still work.

The use is the tribute.

---

I want to say one more thing about Adams before the book begins.

In 2025, Scott Adams went public with a terminal cancer diagnosis. He has said he does not have long. He has continued to write, and to podcast, and to do the work that has, across his career, been the work he has been doing since Pacific Bell laid him off.

I do not know whether Scott Adams will read this book.

I do not know whether his estate will read it.

I do not know whether Adams will see it before he dies, or whether it will land in some other context, or whether it will land at all.

What I know is that the book exists because Adams existed. The book exists because the characters Adams drew, across thirty-five years, became part of the vocabulary the working professional class uses to think about corporate work. The book exists because the *Dilbert* strip taught me, when I read it in college in the late 1990s, what corporate dysfunction actually looked like, and the strip taught the same lesson to a generation of engineers and product people and designers and project managers who have, in the thirty years since, navigated their own corporate dysfunctions with Adams's vocabulary in their heads.

The vocabulary is the gift.

The book is the thank-you.

---

I want to close this foreword with the argument the book is actually making.

The argument is this.

In 1995, Pacific Bell laid off Scott Adams.

In 2026, every Stupid Company™ in the United States is, on a daily basis, laying off the next Scott Adams.

The Scott Adams of 2026 is not going to draw a comic strip. The Scott Adams of 2026 is going to write a Substack, or build a small studio, or join a Wise Corporation, or start their own. The Scott Adams of 2026 is going to do, in the AI era, what Adams did in the post-corporate era of 1995: build a body of work that documents the dysfunction they were just laid off from, and make a living doing it, and become a permanent part of the vocabulary of the next generation.

The corporate world, in laying them off, is creating its own future critics.

The corporate world has not yet realized this.

The corporate world will realize it in approximately five to ten years.

By the time the corporate world realizes it, the next Scott Adamses will already be at work. They will be writing the books and the strips and the substacks and the case studies that the corporate world will, twenty years from now, be using as the canonical literature of *what went wrong in the AI era.*

This book is the early entry in that literature.

This book is also, by its existence, a piece of evidence for its own thesis. The author of this book is a Stupid Company™ alumnus. The author of this book is, in operational terms, what Adams was in 1995 — an engineer who has been pushed out of the corporate world and has, instead of taking the next corporate job, gone home and started writing.

The corporate world lost the author of this book.

The corporate world will not get him back.

The corporate world also will not get back the eleven million next Scott Adamses it is, in 2026, in the process of losing.

That is the cost.

The cost is on no dashboard.

The cost will be measurable in the corporate world's deteriorating ability to think about itself, across the next twenty years, in the absence of the people who could have done the thinking.

Scott Adams is the case study.

Scott Adams is the proof.

The book begins on the next page.

I am grateful you are reading it.

I am more grateful that Scott Adams, in 1989, sat down and started drawing the strip.

— Christopher Bender (writing as Lee Hauschild)
