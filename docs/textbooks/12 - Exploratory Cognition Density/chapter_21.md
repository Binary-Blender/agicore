# Chapter 21: The Job Interview Where Nobody Asks About Your Job

The Hyperion Studio interview was four hours long.

I had been at Mendacium Corp for fourteen years at the time of the interview. I had interviewed at five other companies across those fourteen years. I knew, by the time the Hyperion interview was scheduled, what a normal senior-engineer interview loop looked like. The normal loop consisted of four to six rounds. The rounds were:

1. A recruiter screen (twenty-five minutes, mostly logistical).

2. A technical phone screen (one hour, coding problem on a shared screen).

3. An on-site or remote loop of four to five interviews, including: one architecture interview, one or two coding interviews, one behavioral interview, and one *culture fit* or *team match* interview.

4. An offer.

The Hyperion interview was not this.

The Hyperion interview was, in the order it happened:

1. A forty-five-minute conversation with one of the two founders, named **Yumi Ko**, about books I had read in the previous year. Yumi had a list of seventeen books she had pulled from my Goodreads account, which I had not realized was public, and she asked me about six of the seventeen. The conversation was not adversarial. Yumi was, by my honest read, genuinely curious about what I had thought of each of the six books. We disagreed about two of them in a way that surprised both of us. The conversation went a hundred and seventeen minutes over the scheduled forty-five. Yumi apologized for keeping me. I did not, by the end of the conversation, want to leave.

2. A thirty-minute conversation with the other founder, named **Theo Berlinski**, about a side project I had built in Year Six at Mendacium — a small internal tool that had monitored the data-event platform's tail latencies and surfaced anomalies in a way that the standard observability stack at the time had not handled well. The tool had been an unofficial Friday-afternoon project I had pursued for about six months. The tool had never shipped externally. The tool had been used inside Mendacium by about a dozen engineers for about three years before it was deprecated when SOPHIE arrived and the engineering organization stopped having unofficial tools. Theo had heard about the tool from Sam, who Theo had hired two weeks earlier. Theo asked me to walk him through how I had decided to build the tool, what I had learned in the building, what I had thrown away, and what I would do differently. The conversation was the most fun I had had talking about my own work in six years. Theo asked me, at the end, whether I had any other projects of similar shape that I had wanted to ship. I said I had three. Theo asked me to describe them in one sentence each. I described them. Theo took notes.

3. A one-hour lunch in the kitchen of the Hyperion offices, with a rotating cast of six employees who walked in, sat down, talked to me for between five and twenty minutes, and walked out. The kitchen was the office's primary social space. There was no formal conference room. The kitchen had a long oak table that seated approximately fourteen, an espresso machine that someone had clearly invested time in maintaining, and a small whiteboard on the wall that, when I looked at it, contained, in three different handwritings, the phrase *what if octopus social structures could be a meeting-design template.* I asked one of the rotating employees about the whiteboard. She said: *we had Bad Ideas Hour on Friday. Theo started it. He liked the octopus one.* I asked her if Bad Ideas Hour was a thing here. She said: *it has been a thing here every week since we opened. We did not call it that. Sam started calling it that. Sam is at the table at the back, having a coffee. You can ask him.* I asked Sam later. Sam said: *yes, I imported the name. I told them about Mendacium. Theo asked me to keep the name. The thing was already happening. We just named it.*

4. A ninety-minute conversation, in a hammock chair in a corner of the office, with an engineer named **Frances Okoye** who was, by Sam's later description, *one of the most exploratory thinkers Hyperion had hired in its first two years.* Frances and I talked about: distributed systems, the history of the Internet, the question of whether the personal computer revolution had been a net positive (Frances thought yes, I thought *yes with significant reservations*), a particular Octavia Butler novel we had both read, a tooling decision I had made at Mendacium in Year Four that Frances thought I had gotten right, and a tooling decision I had made in Year Eleven that Frances thought I had gotten wrong. Frances was not interviewing me in any conventional sense. Frances was, in retrospect, *playing with me* in the social sense that an engineer plays with a potential colleague. The play was the interview. I had not realized this until I was in the car driving home.

5. A twenty-minute closing conversation with Yumi and Theo together. They asked me one question. The question was: *Lee, if you joined Hyperion, what would you build in your first month that we would not have thought to ask you to build.* I sat with the question for about four seconds. I told them three things. The first thing was an internal-tooling project that would address a specific operational gap I had inferred from the four hours I had been on-site. The second thing was a piece of public technical writing on a topic I had been wanting to write about for two years. The third thing was *I would like to spend one full week before deciding what to build, walking around and asking everybody what they wished they had time to do.* They thanked me. They said they would be in touch.

There were no coding interviews.

There were no behavioral interviews.

There were no architecture interviews in the sense Mendacium used the term.

There was nobody asking me about my responsibilities at my prior job.

There was nobody asking me to describe a time I had handled a difficult colleague.

There was nobody asking me where I saw myself in five years.

I walked out of the Hyperion offices at five thirty-seven PM on a Friday afternoon, four hours and seven minutes after I had walked in, and I sat in my car in the parking lot for a full ten minutes before I started the engine, because I needed the ten minutes to process what had just happened.

What had happened was that I had been interviewed by people who were trying to figure out *who I was* rather than *what I had done.*

I had never been interviewed that way before.

Most people have not been interviewed that way.

If you are interviewing in the AI era and you encounter a process like the Hyperion process, *pay attention.* The process is the signal. The company that interviews you for *who you are* is, in nine cases out of ten, a Wise Corporation.

---

The offer came on Monday morning.

The offer was a one-page document.

The compensation was approximately equivalent to my Mendacium compensation, with one structural difference: the equity component was significantly larger and the cash component was slightly smaller. The equity vesting schedule was four years, the standard. The equity was in a company that I had, at the time of the offer, no public valuation for. The valuation, by Sam's read of the most recent funding round (Hyperion had raised twelve million dollars from a small group of seed investors eighteen months earlier), put my equity grant at a notional value somewhere between two hundred and fifty thousand and seven hundred thousand dollars depending on how you priced the equity.

I did not, at the time of the offer, know what the equity would be worth.

I did, at the time of the offer, know what the four hours of interview had been.

The four hours of interview had been worth more to me, as a signal of where I should work, than any compensation calculation I could have run.

I accepted the offer on Monday afternoon, by email.

I gave Greg my notice on Tuesday morning. (See Chapter 20 for the mechanics.)

I started at Hyperion three weeks later, on the first Monday of July of Year Eight.

The next chapter is my first day at Hyperion.

The first day is not what you think it is.

The first day is, by my honest accounting, the most important single day of my professional life.

I want to take you through it.
