# Chapter 2: Meet Mendacium Corp

Mendacium Corp was a four-thousand-two-hundred-person enterprise software company in a six-story building in the Pacific Northwest.

The building had six floors.

The elevator had five buttons.

I worked there for fourteen years. I never figured out what was on the sixth floor. I asked, periodically. The answer changed.

The first time I asked, in Year One, a senior IT director told me the sixth floor was "the network closet." I accepted this explanation because I was new.

The second time I asked, in Year Three, an HR manager told me the sixth floor was "the executive lounge, but it's not in use right now." I accepted this explanation because I was bored.

The third time I asked, in Year Eleven, a board member at a holiday party told me the sixth floor was "literally just storage, you guys are wild for thinking it's something else." I did not accept this explanation. By Year Eleven I had seen the freight elevator stop at the sixth floor with engineers in it on three separate occasions. The engineers came back down looking the way an engineer looks when he has been told something he is not allowed to repeat.

I never went up to the sixth floor.

Sam Park, my best friend at the company, claimed to have been up to the sixth floor exactly one time, in Year Seven, on a Saturday afternoon when the building was empty. He said the sixth floor was "fine." He would not elaborate. When pressed, he would change the subject to whether Haikus improved semantic retrieval.

The sixth floor of Mendacium Corp is not, technically, relevant to the rest of this book.

The reason I am telling you about it now is that **a company that cannot tell its employees what is on the sixth floor of its own building is a company that has, in operational terms, given up on transparency, and transparency is the precondition for most of the other things a company needs in order to not be the Stupid Company™.**

Write that one down.

---

The CEO of Mendacium Corp when I joined was a man named **Don Pillai**. Don was fifty-six years old in Year One. He was a serious person. He had founded the company in 1996 out of a sublet office in the Beacon Hill neighborhood with three employees, six laptops, and a single client he had poached from his previous employer. The company had grown across the next nineteen years into one of the larger enterprise software firms on the West Coast. Don had run it the entire time.

Don retired in Year Six.

In retrospect, this is the single most important fact about my time at Mendacium.

Don retired in Year Six, and the board, which had been chaired by Don and which had been populated by Don's friends and Don's investors and three Don-adjacent professors from the local universities, replaced Don with a man named **Greg Tannenbaum**.

The board did not look for a CEO outside the company.

The board did not interview candidates.

The board promoted Greg, who was already inside the company, because Greg had been Don's chief operating officer for the previous four years, and the board had been told that this was how succession was supposed to work.

I want to be careful here.

Don did not pick Greg. Don, who I have spoken to once since his retirement (over a glass of bourbon in his guest house in Boise, in Year Twelve, when he was seventy-three years old and writing a memoir nobody was going to read), told me he had recommended against Greg.

The board did not listen.

The board, the next time it should have been listening, did not listen either. We will get to that.

---

Here are the relevant facts about Greg Tannenbaum, in numbered list form, so that you can refer back to them when I describe his behavior later.

1. Greg was fifty-eight years old when he became CEO. He is now sixty-six. He still walks like he is fifty-eight. He has never appeared older than fifty-eight in any photograph I have ever seen of him. The hair points the way the hair points. I have not touched the hair up. The hair is real.

2. Greg has a master's degree in business administration from a school that is technically in the top fifty business schools in the United States. He mentions the school in every public-speaking engagement.

3. Greg has three pairs of identical horn-rimmed glasses that he rotates without telling anyone. The rotation is not perfectly random. I tracked it for two years. The rotation is correlated with the day of the week, in a pattern Sam and I called *Greg's Glasses Glide* and which I will not explain because it took us nine months of work to identify and we still have not published.

4. Greg owns a sailboat. He has, in the eight years I knew him, mentioned the sailboat in seventy-four separate meetings I have personal records of. The sailboat is, by his own account, "thirty-eight feet of trouble." The boat is twenty-six feet. I checked.

5. Greg's executive assistant, a woman named **Cindi**, has been working for him for nineteen years. She has the same energy as Greg's sailboat — small, well-cared-for, and chronically misrepresented in casual conversation.

6. Greg refers to his executive dashboard as "the dashboard," with the definite article. He refers to all other people's dashboards as "their dashboards," without it. This is a more revealing tic than it sounds.

7. Greg's email signature does not contain a phone number. It contains the URL of his LinkedIn profile. I have looked at the LinkedIn profile. The LinkedIn profile lists him as the founder of Mendacium Corp. He is not the founder of Mendacium Corp. Don was the founder.

8. Greg referred to Don exactly once in a public memo, in Year Six, on the day he took over. He referred to Don as "the prior leadership." The phrase is in the memo. I have a copy.

9. Greg has fired forty-three people across the eight years he has been CEO of Mendacium. Of those forty-three, thirty-nine have, on their LinkedIn, characterized the firing as a "layoff" — which is what the company called it — and four have characterized it as a "termination," which is what HR was internally instructed to call it. The four are the four who pursued unemployment claims. They all received the claims. I helped two of them. That is a separate book, and one I might actually write.

10. Greg has acquired six companies across the same eight years. Of the six, four have been wound down to skeleton operations within twenty-four months of acquisition, one has been spun back out to its original founders, and one is still operating but lost ninety-three percent of its workforce inside the first eighteen months under Greg's management. The acquisitions are not, strictly speaking, relevant to the AI thesis of this book. I mention them because they establish a pattern.

11. Greg first mentioned AI in a town-hall meeting in Year Six, eleven months into his tenure as CEO, in the context of *cost rationalization.*

It is item eleven that this book is about.

---

I want to draw a distinction here that I will refer back to throughout the book, because the distinction is the single most important diagnostic tool I am going to give you.

A CEO who mentions AI in the context of **products the company is going to ship** is a CEO who is, even if she is wrong about the products, thinking like a Wise Corporation operator.

A CEO who mentions AI in the context of **cost rationalization** is a CEO who is, even if he is right about the costs, thinking like a Stupid Company™ operator.

This is **Hauschild's First Law.**

I am calling it Hauschild's First Law because it is my book and I get to name things. You should feel free to call it whatever you want. Most of the people who have read the field note this book is based on call it **the Bender Test** because they are politer than I am.

Hauschild's First Law has been ninety-four percent reliable in my own application of it across forty-one companies I have casually evaluated over the last six years. Six percent of the time it is wrong. The six percent failures are usually CEOs who are saying *products* in public and *cost rationalization* in private. Those CEOs are also Stupid Company™ operators, but they are slightly more dangerous Stupid Company™ operators, because they are temporarily camouflaged.

That dialogue is not, technically, a transcript of an actual conversation. It is a composite of nine separate conversations Sam had with Cindi between August and November of Year Six. The composite captures the rhythm. The rhythm is the lesson.

The lesson is: **the Stupid Company™ does not arrive in a press release. It arrives in a fifteen-minute window at three-forty on a Tuesday.**

That is also worth writing down.

---

I should tell you what Mendacium Corp actually made.

Mendacium Corp made an enterprise customer-data-platform product called **VECTORLINE**, which had been Don's original product from 1996 (it had been a customer-database product for direct-mail companies at the time, ported across three platform generations as the internet ate the world). VECTORLINE, by Year Six, was a four-hundred-and-forty-million-dollar-a-year software business with about two thousand customers, most of them Fortune 1000.

VECTORLINE had four other Mendacium products bolted to its side over the years. All four were also called VECTOR-something. There was VECTORSCAN, which was an analytics layer. VECTORPOINT, which was a CRM integration suite. VECTORWAVE, which was a real-time-events product that nobody had wanted but that Greg's predecessor as COO had personally championed. And VECTORDRIVE, which was the AI-coding-assistant product Greg himself had championed in Year Seven, which is the product that will become relevant in Chapter 3.

There were, in total, five products called VECTOR-something at Mendacium Corp.

SOPHIE, when she arrived in Year Seven, would discover this fact within ninety seconds of being given access to the engineering codebase, and would flag it as a *product naming consolidation opportunity* in her first weekly report to Greg.

Greg would forward the report to the marketing team with a note that said *Let's brainstorm.*

The marketing team would brainstorm for fourteen months.

The five products would still be named VECTOR-something at the close of this book.

I have not been back to check, but I would bet you the price of the book that they still are.

---

I want to close this chapter with a brief portrait of the engineering organization at Mendacium Corp before Greg took over.

We had, in Year Five, four hundred and eleven engineers. The engineering organization was distributed across seven product teams and three platform teams. The team I had been running for four years at that point was a forty-person platform team that maintained the central data-event infrastructure underneath all five VECTOR products. We were, by internal accounting, the team that the rest of the engineering organization depended on most heavily and the team that Don Pillai, before he retired, had personally referred to as "the spine of the company."

Don used to say that.

Don used to come down to our floor on Friday afternoons and sit on the corner of someone's desk and ask whoever was around what they had broken that week.

Greg never came to our floor.

Not once.

In eight years.

That is also part of the diagnostic, but I will not formalize it until later.

For now, two facts to keep in mind as we move into Chapter 3:

1. Don Pillai, who founded the company, came to the engineering floor on Friday afternoons for nineteen years.

2. Greg Tannenbaum, who became CEO of the company in Year Six, did not come to the engineering floor for eight years.

The thing in between those two facts is the entire subject of the rest of Part I.

We move now to October of Year Seven, when Greg came back from a conference with a vision.
