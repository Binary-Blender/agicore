# Chapter 11: The Skill Stack, Explained for Adults

Scott Adams introduced the Skill Stack in 2014, in his book *How to Fail at Almost Everything and Still Win Big.* I would like to say I read the book in 2014. I read it in 2019. I am five years late on every important book in my life. I do not pretend otherwise. The Skill Stack, when I finally read it in 2019, was one of the four or five frameworks that has changed the way I think about my own career.

I want to teach it to you now, in this chapter, in case you have not read Adams's book. I want to teach it to you again, in a slightly different way, if you have. I want to do this because the rest of Part II depends on the Skill Stack, and because the Skill Stack is, in my own opinion, the single most important framework you can carry into the AI era.

I will be brief on the original framework. Adams said it better. You should still read his book.

---

The Skill Stack thesis goes like this.

There are two ways to become valuable to the labor market.

The first way is to be the best in the world at one thing. The first way is hard. The first way is, for most people, impossible, because there are seven billion people on earth, and one of them is better than you at any given thing. Even if you are very good — top one percent — there are eighty million people on earth who are at least as good as you are at that thing. The economics of being top one percent are not as good as the marketing brochures suggest. The economics are: a few people make a great living, a few hundred make a decent living, and the rest of the eighty million make whatever they would have made anyway.

The second way is to be in the top twenty-five percent at several things that, combined, become rare.

The math here is worth doing.

Suppose you are in the top twenty-five percent of the population at a skill. There are, statistically, one and a half billion people on earth who are also in the top twenty-five percent at that skill. You are not, on that one skill alone, valuable.

Now suppose you are in the top twenty-five percent at *two* skills. Statistically, only one in sixteen of the original population is also in the top twenty-five percent at both skills. Approximately five hundred million people. Still a lot of people, but the population has shrunk by a factor of fourteen.

Now suppose you are in the top twenty-five percent at *three* skills. Statistically, one in sixty-four of the original population. Approximately one hundred and twenty million people. Approaching a usefully small number.

Now suppose you are in the top twenty-five percent at *four* skills, and that the four skills are *not correlated* — meaning that being good at one of them does not statistically predict being good at the others. One in two hundred and fifty-six. Approximately thirty million people in the world. You are now, by Adams's argument, in a position to be valuable.

Now suppose five uncorrelated skills.

One in a thousand.

Approximately seven million people on earth.

You can now, on the math, be among the most useful people in your industry without being world-class at any single thing.

---

I want you to sit with this math for a moment.

The math is, on its surface, simple. The implication is not.

The implication is this:

**The labor market does not pay you for being the best at one thing. The labor market pays you for being in a rare combinatorial position.**

The combinatorial positions are where the money is. The combinatorial positions are also where the interesting work is. The combinatorial positions are also where you have leverage in negotiations, because there are not many other people who can do what you do.

Adams was not the first person to figure this out. Adams was the first person to name it cleanly, to write down the math, and to write it in a way that a person who had never read an economics paper could absorb in an hour.

This is, in itself, a Skill Stack move. Adams stacked *cartooning* with *business observation* with *plain-English explanation,* and the combination is what produced the book. Each of the three skills, on its own, was top twenty-five percent at best. Adams was not the best cartoonist in the world. He was not the best business observer in the world. He was not the best plain-English explainer in the world. He was, in 2014, the only person in the world who was top twenty-five percent at all three at the same time.

The book was a best-seller for a reason.

---

I want to give you my own Skill Stack, because the abstract argument is more credible if I show you what it looks like in a real life.

I am:

1. In the top twenty-five percent at software engineering. I am not in the top one percent. I am a competent senior engineer with a strong distributed-systems specialty and a good eye for production reliability. There are, in the United States alone, somewhere between sixty and a hundred thousand engineers who are at my level or higher on raw engineering ability. The labor market does not, on this skill alone, distinguish me.

2. In the top twenty-five percent at writing. I am not a great writer. I am a competent writer who has spent twenty-five years writing engineering documents, post-mortems, internal memos, RFCs, and the occasional trade-publication article. There are, in the United States, somewhere between one and three million people who can write at my level.

3. In the top twenty-five percent at teaching. I have taught somewhere between fifty and seventy junior engineers across my career. The juniors I have taught have, in disproportionate numbers, gone on to senior engineering roles inside seven years of working with me. This is not because I am a brilliant teacher. This is because I am a patient, structured teacher who has internalized a couple of basic mentoring frameworks. The number of engineers in the United States who can teach at my level is somewhere between two hundred thousand and a million.

4. In the top twenty-five percent at *recognizing institutional dysfunction.* This is a skill. The skill is the skill Marv was teaching me in Chapter 7. The skill is the ability to look at a company, watch the parking lot, read the dashboard, listen to the executives, and identify which of the patterns in the company are going to produce which outcomes inside the next eighteen months. The skill is not common. The number of people in the United States who can do this at my level is, in my honest estimation, in the low six figures.

5. In the top twenty-five percent at *managing my own anxiety about being in the middle of a career transition.* This sounds like a joke. It is not. The number of competent engineers who, given the diagnostic in Chapter 10, will be able to act on the diagnostic — quit on a Tuesday, take the Hyperion-equivalent offer, navigate the income gap, deal with their spouse's anxiety, deal with their own anxiety — is, by my honest estimation across the eleven engineers I have personally watched do it, no more than one in three. The other two-thirds, given the same diagnostic, stay at the Stupid Company™. The skill of being able to act on a diagnosis is, in itself, in the top twenty-five percent.

I am the intersection of those five skills.

There are, by my own honest math, somewhere between thirty and a hundred thousand people in the United States who occupy a similar intersection. Most of them have, at this moment, jobs they like.

The thirty-thousand-to-a-hundred-thousand-people market is small enough that I have leverage in it. The market is large enough that the work exists.

I am, by Adams's definition, in a usefully rare combinatorial position.

I did not engineer this on purpose. I am, in my own life, mostly an accumulator. I picked up the writing skill because I had to write a lot of engineering documents at my second job. I picked up the teaching skill because the third place I worked at threw juniors at me and I had no choice. I picked up the dysfunction-recognition skill because I worked for nine years at a company that had a slightly dysfunctional CEO before Greg, and the experience taught me what to look for. I picked up the anxiety-management skill because my second marriage required me to.

I did not stack the skills on purpose.

I stacked the skills because I lived a normal twenty-eight-year career and the skills accumulated.

The implication, which is the most important sentence in this chapter, is this:

**You are almost certainly already stacked. You have not been counting.**

That is what Part II is going to teach you to do.

I want to give you, before we move to Chapter 12, the four classic Adams examples of a Skill Stack, because the examples are useful for calibration.

**Adams himself.** Top-twenty-five-percent cartoonist + top-twenty-five-percent business observer + top-twenty-five-percent plain-English writer + top-twenty-five-percent at corporate-life observation. The combination produced Dilbert, *The Dilbert Principle*, and most of his subsequent career.

**The doctor who is also good at writing.** There are roughly nine hundred thousand physicians in the United States. There are several million decent writers. The intersection — physicians who are also in the top quarter of writers — is in the tens of thousands. The intersection makes you, on a good week, the medical correspondent for a major publication, or a doctor with a book contract, or the medical advisor on a TV show. The combination is leveraged. The individual skills are not.

**The accountant who is also good at presenting.** This one is closer to most people's lives. The number of accountants who can stand in front of a room and explain a financial concept to a non-accountant in language the non-accountant follows is, in my own experience, less than one in twenty. The accountant who can both do the math and explain the math is the partner. The one who can only do the math is the senior associate forever.

**The engineer who can also write a sales proposal.** This is the one I see most often in my own field. The engineer who can read a customer specification and write a credible response without three layers of pre-sales support is rare. The combination — engineering depth plus customer-language fluency — is the most reliably high-paying combination in enterprise software. Most engineers cannot do this. Most salespeople cannot do this. The intersection is, in any given company, no more than ten people. The intersection runs the company within ten years.

You can build your own examples.

You can find them in the people you have worked with. You can identify the people who have always seemed to land on their feet, and you can ask yourself what their combinatorial position looks like. The combinatorial position is, in nine cases out of ten, the explanation.
