# Chapter 15: The Thing Your Boss Calls Goofing Off

In Year Four of my tenure at Mendacium Corp — six years before SOPHIE arrived, four years before Greg became CEO, deep in what I now think of as Mendacium's golden era — the engineering organization had a Friday afternoon ritual that Sam had named **Bad Ideas Hour.**

Bad Ideas Hour was, in practice, one to three hours, starting at three PM on Fridays, in a conference room on the third floor that had a working whiteboard, a small projector, and a refrigerator full of bottled water that the company had stocked because somebody on the facilities team had decided the engineering organization should not be allowed to dehydrate.

Bad Ideas Hour was attended, on a typical Friday, by between four and twelve engineers. Attendance was voluntary. There was no agenda. There were no minutes. There was no calendar invite. There was an unwritten rule that you could not bring work into Bad Ideas Hour. There was an unwritten rule that you could not propose anything you actually intended to ship.

The unwritten rules were the entire point.

The point was to talk about ideas you would never propose in any other context — ideas too weird, too speculative, too half-formed, or too embarrassing to bring up in a real planning meeting.

I want to give you a list of ideas that were brought up at Bad Ideas Hour over the four years it ran.

I am not making any of these up.

1. *What if we wrote the entire VECTORLINE customer-facing query syntax as Haikus.* Sam, Year Five.

2. *What if our error messages were structured as pop-song choruses so customers could remember them.* Adira, Year Six.

3. *What if we built the VECTORWAVE retry-logic system as a Choose Your Own Adventure narrative.* Yusuf, Year Six.

4. *What if our customer onboarding emails were Dr. Seuss books.* Catalina, Year Seven.

5. *What if we let the database tell us when it wants to be queried instead of querying it on a schedule.* Devon, Year Seven.

6. *What if every product team had to write their roadmap as a movie pitch with three acts.* Reagan, Year Seven, before she became a senior PM.

7. *What if we trained a small model on our internal Slack and let it diagnose which projects were about to be cancelled.* Sam, Year Seven, three months before SOPHIE.

8. *What if the customer's data was the customer's data and we let them take it with them when they cancelled, with a one-click export, formatted for whatever competitor they were going to.* Lee (me), Year Seven, the most heretical thing I had said publicly in five years.

Of those eight ideas:

- Idea 1 became, eighteen months later, a presentation Sam gave at an internal architecture review that resulted in a real syntactic simplification of the VECTORLINE query language. The simplification produced an eight percent reduction in customer-onboarding time. The simplification was not, in the end, in Haiku form. The thinking that had produced it had started in Haiku form.

- Idea 4 became, twenty-two months later, the customer-onboarding email sequence that Mendacium used for its small-business tier. The emails were not Dr. Seuss books. The emails had Dr. Seuss-style rhythm. The conversion rate on the new emails was forty-one percent higher than the conversion rate on the old emails.

- Idea 5 became, three years later, the foundational thesis of the next-generation data-event platform that Sam was going to build at Hyperion Studio after he left Mendacium. The thesis is now the thesis of Hyperion's core product. The platform is, at the writing of this book, on track to be the second-largest product in its category.

- Idea 7 became, in Sam's spare time after he left Mendacium, a small tool that monitored Slack activity and predicted project-cancellation risk. The tool was the prototype of what Hyperion later open-sourced as one of its flagship community projects.

- Idea 8 became, in my own private journal, the thesis I never proposed but that, four years later, was the policy Hyperion adopted for every product it shipped.

Five of the eight Bad Ideas Hour ideas turned into commercially valuable work.

Three of the five turned into the foundation of Hyperion's product line.

This was the output of a one-to-three-hour conference room session that happened, on average, thirty-eight Fridays a year.

Bad Ideas Hour was, in operational terms, the single most productive use of engineer time at Mendacium Corp across the entire period from Year Four through Year Seven.

Greg, when he became CEO in Year Six, did not know Bad Ideas Hour existed.

Greg, when he found out about Bad Ideas Hour in Year Seven, called it *goofing off* in a weekly executive email.

Greg, after the email, formally suspended Bad Ideas Hour as part of the *engineering velocity initiative* that preceded the SOPHIE rollout.

The room remained empty on Fridays from then on.

The ideas continued to happen, but they happened in private DMs between two engineers who trusted each other. The shared exploration stopped. The cross-pollination stopped. The number of weird ideas that became real products dropped from approximately one per quarter to approximately zero per year.

By the time SOPHIE arrived, the cognitive infrastructure that had produced the original VECTORLINE product, the analytics layer, the customer-onboarding flow, and the data-event platform was, in operational terms, no longer in service.

The dashboards did not catch this.

The dashboards did not have a column for *cognitive infrastructure.*

I have thought about it.

Reagan was right.

*Being the kind of person who gets put on the list* is a skill. The skill is not on the sixteen-item list I gave you in Chapter 13. The skill is one of approximately fifty *uncounted skills* that the people who navigate the AI era well are going to bring into the negotiation room. I am not going to enumerate the other forty-nine in this book. The other forty-nine are your work to identify. The worksheet in Chapter 19 is going to help.

But I want you to notice what *being the kind of person who gets put on the list* is, in operational terms.

It is the byproduct of:

1. Showing up to Bad Ideas Hour for four years.

2. Being one of the people who could be trusted to read what someone else wrote and respond honestly.

3. Not having an obvious political agenda that the person on the institutional record list would have had to navigate around.

4. Being recognized, by someone with judgment, as a person who could carry information across time.

None of those four things is a skill that has a certification. None of them shows up in a job description. None of them is taught in a bootcamp. All four of them are the byproduct of *being present, sustainedly, in environments where exploratory thinking is happening, and being trusted not to weaponize what you hear.*

Bad Ideas Hour was not, technically, where I picked up the *institutional record* skill. Don did not attend Bad Ideas Hour. But Bad Ideas Hour was the *infrastructure* that produced the kind of engineering culture in which Don's institutional-record list could exist, and in which I could be on it.

When Greg shut down Bad Ideas Hour, he also shut down — not all at once, but cumulatively, across the next thirty-six months — the conditions that produced the institutional-record list and the conditions that produced the kind of engineer who could be on it.

The institutional-record list, by the close of this book, will have moved to Hyperion Studio.

The conditions that produce it have moved to Hyperion Studio.

The next twenty-three Sams, currently at the Stupid Company™ equivalents of Mendacium across the country, will, one by one, move to the Hyperion-equivalents across the next thirty months.

The Wise Corporation is being staffed by the Bad Ideas Hour alumni of the Stupid Company™.

This is the part I want you to write down.

---

I want to close this chapter with the lesson, plain.

**The thing your boss calls *goofing off* is, in eighty percent of cases, the most economically valuable thing your team does.**

Bad Ideas Hour, at Mendacium, produced more shipped value across four years than any other engineering ritual at the company. It produced more shipped value than the formal quarterly planning meetings. It produced more shipped value than the annual all-hands. It produced more shipped value than the formal product-design sprints. It produced more shipped value than the executive offsites.

It produced more shipped value than everything else combined.

The boss called it goofing off.

The boss shut it down.

The boss did not know what he had shut down.

The boss never knows what he has shut down.

This is the part where the title of the book lives.

The title is *Exploratory Cognition Density.*

The thing your boss calls goofing off is, in the language of this book, **exploratory cognition density.** It is the amount of unstructured, weird, half-formed, cross-domain, jokingly-proposed thinking that is happening, per employee, per day, in your organization.

It is the metric nobody puts on a dashboard.

It is the metric that, in the AI era, is going to determine which companies are still around in 2035.

The next chapter is the definition.

The next chapter is where the title finally lands.

The next chapter is going to be the most important sentence in this book up to this point.

I have been saving it for sixteen chapters.
