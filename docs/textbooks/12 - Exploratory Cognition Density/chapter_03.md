# Chapter 3: Greg Discovers AI

Greg went to a conference in October of Year Seven.

The conference was held at a resort in the Sonoran Desert that I will not name, although the resort's name is a homophone for the Spanish word for the act of giving up. I am told the irony was not appreciated by the conference organizers.

Greg attended the conference for three days. He came back on a Thursday afternoon. He went straight from the airport to the office. He did not stop at home to change clothes, which was a thing Greg had not done in eight years.

This was the first sign.

The second sign was the slide deck.

Greg had built a slide deck on the airplane. The slide deck was forty-seven slides long. The slides were in Greg's preferred deck template, which used a stock image of a stopwatch in the lower-right corner of every slide as a "branding element." (Greg had paid a consulting firm one hundred and twenty thousand dollars in Year Six to develop the deck template. The stopwatch was their primary recommendation.)

The slide deck was titled, in all capital letters, **THE AI TRANSFORMATION.**

It was the kind of title you give a slide deck when you have been on an airplane for four hours and you have just realized that the most important decision of your career is going to be the one you make this week.

Greg pulled fourteen senior leaders into the executive conference room on the fifth floor on Thursday evening. (I was not in the meeting. I learned about it from Reagan Cho, who was, and from Cindi, who had taken notes she was not supposed to be taking.)

He walked them through the deck.

The deck had three main sections.

The first section was titled **WHERE WE ARE.** It contained a series of slides showing Mendacium's headcount, payroll expense, engineering velocity, and operating margin trends across the previous six quarters. The slides were accurate. The slides looked like the slides of a company that was, by industry-standard measures, healthy.

The second section was titled **WHERE THE WORLD IS GOING.** It contained a series of slides about AI, most of which were screenshots of LinkedIn posts that Greg had taken on his phone during the conference. The screenshots showed influencer commentary about AI replacing software engineers. One of the screenshots was of a Tweet from a venture capitalist whose firm had three months earlier laid off twenty percent of its portfolio companies' engineering teams. The Tweet was: *the future of engineering is one human and a hundred agents.* Greg had circled the Tweet in red.

The third section was titled **WHAT WE'RE GOING TO DO.** It contained four slides. The four slides were, in order:

1. A slide with a graph going down and to the right. The graph was unlabeled.

2. A slide with a graph going up and to the right. The graph was also unlabeled.

3. A slide with the words **HUMAN-AI HYBRID OPERATING MODEL** in large font. The slide did not define the operating model.

4. A slide with a thirty-six-month timeline that included one milestone, labeled **TRANSFORMATION COMPLETE.** The milestone was at the eighteen-month mark, not the thirty-six-month mark. Nobody in the room pointed this out.

Greg walked the leaders through the deck across two hours.

The leaders nodded.

The leaders, by Reagan's account, did not ask a single substantive question. The leaders asked three procedural questions:

1. "When are we communicating this to the broader team?"

2. "What's the budget envelope for the AI procurement?"

3. "Do we want to coordinate with marketing on this?"

Greg answered each question with confidence.

The answers were:

1. "Two weeks. We'll do the all-hands the Tuesday after Veterans Day."

2. "Whatever it takes, we are talking about a multi-year ROI."

3. "Yes. I want a press release."

He closed the deck. He told the leaders the meeting was confidential. He sent them home. He went back to his office. He sat at his desk for forty minutes with the deck open on his laptop. Then he sent a calendar invite for the following Tuesday at nine AM to a man named **Bryce Kemmel**, the CEO of a four-person AI startup called **Sophia AI**, which Greg had met at the conference at a cocktail reception on Tuesday evening.

The Tuesday at nine AM meeting was on Greg's calendar as: *AI Strategy Discussion / Vendor Eval.*

Greg had decided, on the flight home, to buy Sophia AI's product, which was called **SOPHIE**, and to use SOPHIE to rationalize the engineering organization at Mendacium Corp by approximately thirty-five percent across the following eighteen months.

The decision had been made before the leadership meeting.

The leadership meeting was not the decision.

The leadership meeting was the ratification.

That is a distinction I want you to write down.

---

> **GREG'S NEW WHITEBOARD CAPTION (handwritten, blue marker):**
>
> *AI IS NOT A TOOL. AI IS A WORKFORCE.*

---

The whiteboard with that caption appeared in Greg's office on the Friday morning after the conference.

The whiteboard was a brand-new whiteboard. Greg had had Cindi order it on Thursday night.

Cindi, in the order to the corporate-supplies vendor, had specified that the whiteboard should be *the kind of whiteboard that suggests the writing on it is permanent without actually being permanent.*

The vendor delivered.

The whiteboard had been mounted by eight AM Friday by a man from the building's facilities team who, by Sam's later report, was overheard muttering *here we go* as he installed it.

Greg wrote the caption himself in blue marker at nine-twenty AM Friday. He photographed the whiteboard. He posted the photograph on his personal LinkedIn at nine-thirty-three AM Friday with the caption: *Reorienting around a new operating premise. More to come. #AItransformation #FutureOfWork #Leadership.*

The post received eleven hundred likes inside three hours.

Two hundred and forty-seven of the likes came from Mendacium employees. I checked.

The remaining eight hundred and fifty-three came from a roughly equal split between (a) AI vendors whose products Greg had not yet purchased, (b) executive coaches Greg had been LinkedIn-connected to since before he became CEO, and (c) middle managers at other companies who were about to write the same caption on their own whiteboards.

None of the likes came from anyone who knew anything about AI.

---

Sam Park sent me a screenshot of the LinkedIn post at nine-forty-six AM Friday.

The screenshot was accompanied by one message.

The message was: *we are cooked.*

I texted back: *we are cooked.*

We were.

---

I want to introduce you here to a concept I will use throughout the book.

**The Pre-Decided Decision.**

A Pre-Decided Decision is a decision that has been made before the meeting that ostensibly decides it. The meeting then ratifies the decision under the cover of process. Everyone in the room understands that the decision has been pre-decided. The decision is presented as a discussion. The discussion is a performance. The performance is required because the company has rules about how decisions are supposed to be made. The rules require the performance. The rules do not require the decision to be actually made in the meeting that is officially the decision-making meeting.

The Pre-Decided Decision is one of the most reliable diagnostic signals of the Stupid Company™.

The Wise Corporation has fewer Pre-Decided Decisions, because it has fewer decisions of the kind that are made in conference rooms with slide decks. The Wise Corporation makes most of its decisions in the kitchen, over coffee, in two-person conversations that are not on anybody's calendar. The decisions get made because somebody had to make them and there were no other people in the kitchen at the time.

This is one of the things you cannot tell from the outside.

You can tell from the inside in about three weeks.

By the third week of Year Seven, after the conference and the whiteboard and the LinkedIn post, every senior engineer at Mendacium Corp knew that the AI Transformation had been Pre-Decided.

The Tuesday-after-Veterans-Day all-hands was not the announcement.

The Tuesday-after-Veterans-Day all-hands was the ratification.

The thing Greg was going to announce that morning had already been signed for, paid for, and onboarded in test environments by the previous Friday afternoon. We knew this because Sam had access to the cloud-cost dashboard that the CFO's office reviewed weekly, and the dashboard showed a new line item of $397,000 for the month of October under the SaaS-procurement subcategory.

The line item was labeled: *Sophia AI / Annual License / Tier 4 / Multi-Tenant.*

The annual license, Sam later determined by digging through the procurement workflow, was for $4.8 million.

We had not been consulted.

The engineering leadership team — the people whose teams would be most directly affected by SOPHIE's arrival, the people who would have been able to tell Greg in nine minutes whether SOPHIE was going to do what Greg thought she was going to do, the people who had built and were running the product Greg was about to attempt to AI-augment — had not been consulted.

That is the Pre-Decided Decision in operational form.

---

I want to close this chapter with a question I have been turning over for six years.

Why did Greg not consult the engineering leadership team?

It would have been a fifteen-minute meeting. Sam could have walked him through the basic categories of what AI coding assistants could and could not do in October of Year Seven (which was, with apologies to the AI industry, about a quarter of what the LinkedIn discourse claimed they could do). I could have walked him through the operational risk profile of replacing a third of the engineering organization with an AI vendor whose product had been in market for fourteen months. Reagan could have walked him through the customer-impact model.

He did not consult us because consulting us was not part of the decision he had pre-decided.

He had pre-decided that we were the problem.

He had pre-decided this on the airplane home from the conference, somewhere over the Sierra Nevada, while reading LinkedIn.

I am not joking when I say *while reading LinkedIn.* Cindi later told me that Greg had spent five of the six hours of the flight scrolling LinkedIn. He had not opened his laptop until the descent into Sea-Tac. The slide deck had been built in forty-three minutes on his iPad.

This is a true sentence and I want you to sit with it.

**The single most consequential strategic decision in Mendacium Corp's history was made by a man scrolling LinkedIn at thirty-six thousand feet.**

The whiteboard caption was correct, in a sense Greg did not intend.

AI is not a tool. AI, in the hands of an executive who is making decisions on an iPad at thirty-six thousand feet on the way home from a conference, is a workforce.

A workforce that does not know it is replacing a workforce.

The workforce that does not know is the workforce Greg is about to bring into the building.

Her name is SOPHIE.

She arrives on Tuesday.
