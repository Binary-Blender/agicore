# Chapter 19: The Resume Item You Did Not Know You Had

This is the worksheet chapter.

I am going to give you the worksheet directly. The worksheet is the chapter. The accompanying text is the instructions.

Get a sheet of paper. Get a pen. Do this on paper rather than on a screen, because the work of writing each item down by hand will surface items the typing would not surface. I am serious about this. Use the pen.

Here is the worksheet.

---

**WORKSHEET: YOUR SKILL STACK INVENTORY**

(One page. Filled out by hand. Re-filled-out every twelve months for the rest of your career.)

---

**Section 1: The Counted Skills.**

List, in order of confidence in your own competence, the five to seven skills you would currently list on a resume.

Examples: *backend software engineering, project management, technical writing, public speaking, financial modeling.*

These are the skills you are aware of. These are the skills your professional identity is built on. These are the skills you would mention to a recruiter in the first thirty seconds of a screening call.

Write five to seven.

---

**Section 2: The Uncounted Operational Skills.**

List five skills that you do at work, regularly and competently, but that you have never put on a resume because they did not seem to count.

These are skills that you have stopped thinking of as skills because you do them so naturally. Look for them in the gaps.

Examples (use your own; these are illustrative):

- *I am the person my team comes to when they need someone to explain something to a customer who is angry.*

- *I can read a Jira board and tell, within ninety seconds, which projects are about to slip.*

- *I can write an internal memo that gets read.*

- *I can run a meeting that ends on time.*

- *I can mentor a junior engineer through a real architectural decision without telling them the answer.*

- *I can spot a feature that will not sell before it has shipped.*

- *I can identify, in a meeting with a vendor, which member of the vendor's team is lying.*

- *I can write a Slack message that takes a tense room and lowers the temperature.*

The skills in this section are, in my honest experience, the skills the AI labor market is most likely to pay you for in the next decade. They are not on your resume. They are on the worksheet. Put them on the worksheet.

Write five.

---

**Section 3: The Cross-Domain Inputs.**

List the three to five fields of knowledge, outside your professional domain, that you read or think about regularly.

These are the substrate for your *what if* moments.

Examples:

- *I read about urban planning for fun.*

- *I have spent a hundred hours playing competitive chess.*

- *I read history of medicine.*

- *I have a side interest in commercial fishing.*

- *I taught myself bookbinding.*

- *I have run a fantasy football league for nine years.*

- *I am fluent in a second language.*

- *I have raised a child through one full developmental stage.*

The cross-domain inputs are not, on their own, skills. The cross-domain inputs are the *pattern library* you draw on when your job presents you with a problem that resembles a pattern from outside your job.

The cross-domain inputs are the reason you, and not the candidate next to you, will be the one who notices that the company's customer-onboarding flow could be structured as Dr. Seuss-style rhyming emails.

Write three to five.

---

**Section 4: The Reputation Capital.**

List, by name, the five people in your professional life who would, if you asked them for a reference today, give you a reference that would noticeably help you.

For each person, write one sentence about *what they would specifically say about you* in the reference.

Examples:

- *Anjali Sharma. Would say I am the person she would hire to solve a problem she does not yet understand.*

- *David Lim. Would say I am the senior engineer he has personally seen mentor the most junior engineers into senior roles in his career.*

- *Maria Schmidt. Would say I am the one person on my old team she would have left her company to follow.*

The reputation capital is, in operational terms, the most valuable asset you have. The reputation capital is what gets you into the Wise Corporation interview loop in the first place. The reputation capital is what makes the rest of the worksheet matter.

If you cannot list five people, list as many as you can. If you can list fewer than three, your most urgent professional project for the next twelve months is to build the list. Build it deliberately. Build it by being the colleague to other people that you would want them to be to you.

Write five (or as many as you can).

---

**Section 5: The Combinatorial Stack.**

Look back at sections 1 and 2.

From the combined list of counted and uncounted skills, pick the three to five that, when you put them together, describe somebody *unusually rare.*

Then write, in one to three sentences, what kind of role that person is uniquely suited for.

Example (mine):

> *I am a competent backend engineer (S1) who is also a competent writer (S1), a competent teacher (S2), a person who can read institutional dysfunction (S2), and a person who can manage my own anxiety in a career transition (S2). The combination is somebody who can do the senior-engineer work at a small Wise Corporation while also writing the public technical content the company needs to recruit, mentoring the junior engineers the company is hiring, recognizing the early warning signs of organizational drift, and not panicking when the company hits its first cash-flow crisis.*

The combination is the rare position.

Write yours.

---

**Section 6: The Three-Year Plan.**

Given your combinatorial stack, write three sentences about where you want to be in three years.

The three sentences should be specific. They should name the kind of company, the kind of role, and the kind of compensation. They should not name a specific employer.

Example:

> *I want to be a senior-or-staff engineer at a Wise Corporation in the data-platform or AI-infrastructure category, with somewhere between fifty and one hundred and fifty employees, in a role where I am individually contributing to one product, mentoring two to four junior engineers, writing one piece of public technical content per quarter, and earning between thirty and fifty percent of my compensation in equity.*

The three sentences are the target.

The target does not have to be perfect. The target has to be specific enough that, the day you encounter a job posting that fits, you will recognize it as fitting.

The recognition is the thing.

Write your three sentences.

---

That is the worksheet.

It fits on one page.

I want you to fill it out today. Or tomorrow. Or this weekend. The longer you wait, the less likely you are to do it.

When you have filled it out, put it somewhere you will see it. The inside cover of your work notebook. The drawer of your desk at home. A folder on your laptop labeled *the worksheet.* Pinned to a board in your office, if you have an office.

Re-fill it out every twelve months.

The worksheet, across a decade, will become the most useful single artifact of your professional life.

---

I want to give you, before we move to Chapter 20, three observations from the people who have filled out the worksheet.

**Observation one.** Approximately seven out of ten people I have given the worksheet to have, in section 2, listed at least one skill that they had not previously thought of as a skill. The skill, in nine of ten of those cases, turned out to be the skill that ended up making the most material difference in their next career move.

**Observation two.** Approximately half of the people who have filled out the worksheet have, in section 4, listed fewer than three people. The half that listed fewer than three were, on average, six to twelve months from being asked to leave their current company. The half that listed five were, on average, in a position to negotiate their next role from strength. The reputation capital is the lever. Build the lever.

**Observation three.** Approximately one in three of the people who have filled out section 6 have written a three-year plan that, on careful inspection, describes a company that *is structurally a Stupid Company™ in the early phases of becoming visible as one.* The three-year plan is the place where the worksheet most often catches a hidden mistake. Pay specific attention to section 6. Read your own three sentences as if a stranger had written them. Ask yourself whether the company described is a Stupid Company™ or a Wise Corporation. If you cannot tell, rewrite the sentences until you can.
