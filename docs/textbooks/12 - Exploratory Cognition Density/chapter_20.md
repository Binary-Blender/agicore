# Chapter 20: How to Quit on Tuesday

I quit Mendacium Corp on a Tuesday in early June of Year Eight, six weeks after Reagan, four weeks after Sam, and seventeen weeks after Marv.

I want to walk you through it, because the mechanics matter, and because the people who have read drafts of this chapter have told me that the mechanics are the part they came to the book for.

Here is how to quit on Tuesday.

---

**Step 1. Have the next thing lined up before you give notice.**

This is, in nine of ten cases, the most important step.

The exception is the case in which the Stupid Company™ has become so toxic that staying is actively damaging your mental health, in which case quitting without a next thing is the correct move. I am not addressing that exception here. The exception is real and is, in some cases, the right call. But it is the exception. Most readers of this book will be in the nine-of-ten case, in which the responsible move is to line up the next thing first.

I had the Hyperion offer in my desk drawer for two and a half weeks before I gave Greg my notice.

The two and a half weeks were the most important two and a half weeks of my career.

The two and a half weeks were the weeks in which I had *the option to quit*, in writing, without yet having quit. The option is the most psychologically stabilizing artifact a senior professional can possess. The option means that every meeting you sit through is a meeting you are choosing to sit through. The option means that every demand your boss makes is a demand you have a baseline to compare against. The option means that you are no longer in the trap.

Get the option.

Sit with the option for a week or two before you exercise it.

The sitting is the work.

---

**Step 2. Pick the day deliberately.**

Tuesday is the correct day to quit.

The Stupid Company™ runs on Mondays. The CEO does his weekly all-staff Monday morning. The executive team meets Monday afternoon. The board calls happen Monday evening. The first half of the week is when the executive team's attention is at its peak. The second half of the week is when the executive team is preparing for the weekend.

If you quit on Monday, your resignation will be the lead item in the executive's Monday-afternoon meeting. The executive will take it personally. The executive will look for ways to use it against you in the news cycle of the week.

If you quit on Wednesday, your resignation will get buried in the mid-week chaos. The executive will not have time to process it cleanly. The executive will respond reactively. The reaction will be worse for you in the short term and better for the company's narrative in the long term.

If you quit on Thursday or Friday, your resignation will land at a moment when the executive is mentally checked out for the weekend. The executive will not respond at all until Monday. You will spend the weekend not knowing whether your resignation has even been read. The weekend is the wrong weekend to have.

Tuesday is the correct day to quit. Tuesday morning is the correct time. Eleven AM to noon is the correct window.

Quit Tuesday at eleven thirty AM.

The executive's calendar is, on Tuesday morning, mostly empty. The executive's attention is, on Tuesday morning, mostly available. The executive's emotional reserves are, on Tuesday morning, at their peak for the week. The executive will receive your resignation with the maximum availability they have to receive it.

You want that.

You want the cleanest possible exit.

The cleanest possible exit happens Tuesday morning.

---

**Step 3. Write the letter short.**

Reagan's letter, which I gave you in full in Chapter 9, was seven hundred and twenty-three words. Reagan's letter was the right letter for Reagan. Reagan was making a *public* exit. Reagan wanted the letter to be read by the entire engineering organization. Reagan wanted to leave a record.

Most readers of this book do not need to make a public exit.

Most readers of this book should write a short letter.

My letter was two sentences.

The letter was:

> *Greg,*
> 
> *I am resigning from Mendacium Corp, effective two weeks from today. My last day will be the eighteenth.*
> 
> *Thank you for the years.*
> 
> *— Lee*

That was the letter.

The letter is twenty-four words.

The letter contains no criticism. The letter contains no explanation. The letter contains no demands. The letter contains no narrative.

The letter contains a single specific fact (the date) and a single specific courtesy (the thank-you).

The letter does not give Greg anything to hold onto. The letter does not give Greg anything to be defensive about. The letter does not give Greg anything to leak. The letter does not give Greg anything to use against me.

The letter is, in operational terms, *frictionless.*

The letter is what the executive's lawyer will tell the executive he wishes every resignation letter looked like.

Greg responded to the letter at three forty-eight PM Tuesday with a one-sentence response: *Lee, thank you for the years. We will discuss transition next week. — G.*

Greg's response was eighteen words.

We discussed transition the following Monday in a forty-minute meeting that I will get to in step 6.

The transition was clean. The departure was clean. The press release that announced the departure (yes, there was a press release; Mendacium had begun publishing press releases for every senior departure during Year Seven, which was, in itself, a Stupid Company™ tell) was clean.

The cleanness was the gift of the short letter.

---

**Step 4. Do not negotiate counter-offers.**

The Stupid Company™, in nine of ten cases, will respond to a senior individual contributor's resignation with a counter-offer.

The counter-offer is, in nearly every case, a mistake to accept.

The counter-offer is a mistake because the counter-offer does not change the underlying conditions that produced the resignation. The counter-offer changes only the compensation. The conditions that produced the resignation are the conditions of working at the Stupid Company™. The conditions will be the same nine months after the counter-offer as they were the day before.

Workers who accept counter-offers, in the published research and in my own anecdotal experience across approximately forty people, leave the company within twelve to eighteen months anyway, on average. The compensation gain from the counter-offer is, in most cases, smaller than the compensation gain from the role they could have gone to in the first place. The dignity loss from accepting the counter-offer and then leaving twelve months later is real.

Do not accept the counter-offer.

If the counter-offer is offered, you can refuse it politely with a single sentence: *I appreciate the offer. My decision is final.*

That sentence ends the conversation.

The conversation ends because the executive has no escalation path on a final decision they have been given politely.

---

**Step 5. Document the assets you are taking with you.**

You are taking three things with you when you leave the Stupid Company™.

The first thing is the *worksheet from Chapter 19.* You have already done this work.

The second thing is the *parallel filing cabinet* — the personal documentation of the institutional knowledge you have accumulated. I will explain.

The parallel filing cabinet is the document Marv was telling me to keep in Chapter 8. The document contains, for the company you are leaving, every piece of institutional knowledge you have accumulated that is not formally documented in the company's official systems. The document includes:

- The names of the people who actually know how things work, by team.

- The undocumented dependencies between systems.

- The customers whose contracts depend on specific configurations.

- The decisions that were made in private and that nobody has written down.

- The reasons specific projects were killed.

- The reasons specific projects were greenlit.

- The political dynamics that determine which projects get funded next year.

The parallel filing cabinet is, by my honest count, the single most valuable artifact I took with me when I left Mendacium. The document is one hundred and forty-eight pages. The document is in my personal Notion. The document is in my personal email. The document is on a USB drive in a desk at home. The document is *never* on Mendacium's tenancy.

The document is what I draw on, four years later, when I evaluate other companies that are in the same category Mendacium was in. The document is what I draw on when junior engineers ask me how to navigate a situation that resembles a situation I navigated in Year Eight. The document is what I drew on when I wrote this book.

The document is the asset.

Build the document. Take it with you.

The third thing is the *reputation capital* — the people from section 4 of the worksheet. You are taking them with you in the sense that they are still going to be your references, your collaborators, your future colleagues. The reputation capital is a portable asset. You do not have to *do* anything to take it with you. You just have to *not damage it* in your exit.

This is part of why the short letter matters.

The short letter does not damage the reputation capital.

A long, public, settling-of-scores letter, on average, damages the reputation capital. The long letter feels good in the moment. The long letter is read by the rest of the industry. The rest of the industry remembers that you wrote a long, public, settling-of-scores letter. Some of the rest of the industry concludes, correctly or incorrectly, that you might write the same kind of letter about them in three years.

Reagan's letter was an exception to this rule because Reagan wanted to be a public figure in the AI-labor-market discourse and the letter was, in operational terms, a launch event for that public-figure career.

Most readers of this book do not want to be public figures.

Most readers should write the short letter.

---

**Step 6. Run the transition cleanly.**

After you give notice, you have a two-week transition period (in most American jobs; adjust for your specific contract and country).

The two-week transition period is, in many resignations, the period the worker treats as a punishment. The worker shows up disengaged, mails it in, and counts the days.

This is a mistake.

The two-week transition is the period in which you build your reputation capital with the company you are leaving.

The two-week transition is the period in which the colleagues who are staying behind decide whether to give you a *strong reference* or a *cordial reference.*

The difference between the two references will be measurable in your compensation across the next ten years.

Run the transition cleanly.

Hand off your projects. Document what you are leaving behind. Brief your successors. Stay until five PM every day of the two weeks. Show up at the going-away party (there will be a going-away party; somebody will organize it; do not skip it).

The two-week transition is the cheapest investment you will ever make in your reputation capital.

I ran my transition by:

1. Writing a thirty-page handoff document for the platform team that summarized everything I had been holding in my head. The document took me four days. The document was the most valuable single thing I produced in my entire tenure at Mendacium.

2. Holding three one-on-one meetings with each of my direct reports to walk them through the handoff and answer their questions.

3. Briefing the two engineers I had named as my succession (one for the technical lead role, one for the people-management role).

4. Attending the going-away party Sam organized at a Capitol Hill bar that I had been to with the team approximately eighty times across fourteen years.

5. Sending a one-paragraph farewell email to the engineering organization on my last day. The email was four sentences. The email said I was grateful for the years, that I would be at Hyperion Studio starting Monday, and that I was reachable at my personal email and on LinkedIn. The email said nothing about Greg.

The fifth item was, in operational terms, the most consequential.

The fifth item did not damage anyone's reputation, including Greg's.

The fifth item left Mendacium with a clean record of my departure.

The fifth item was, eighteen months later, the reason I was able to refer two former Mendacium colleagues to Hyperion without complications.

The fifth item was the gift I gave myself on the way out the door.

---

That is how to quit on Tuesday.

The mechanics are mechanics.

The mechanics are not the point.

The point is that you have been thinking about quitting for somewhere between three months and three years, and that you have, in your gut, known for at least six weeks that the next move is right, and that the only thing holding you in the seat is the *Not-Being-Sure* paralysis we identified in Chapter 14.

The mechanics get you past the paralysis.

The mechanics turn an emotional event into an operational procedure.

The operational procedure is doable.

You can do this.

You can do it on a Tuesday.

You can do it the Tuesday after next.

You can do it the Tuesday after you have lined up the next thing.

In Chapter 21, I am going to take you with me to the Hyperion Studio job interview.

The interview is going to be different from any job interview you have ever had.

The interview is the beginning of Part III.

We are now in the Wise Corporation.
