# Chapter 22: Welcome to the Wise Corporation

I arrived at the Hyperion Studio offices at eight forty-seven AM on the first Monday of July of Year Eight, fifteen minutes before my official start time, in the only suit I owned that still fit.

I had not been told what to wear.

I had assumed, based on fourteen years of professional habit, that *first day at a new job* meant a suit.

The office at eight forty-seven AM contained four people. None of them was in a suit. Two of them were in shorts. One was in jeans. One was in what I would later learn was Hyperion's de facto uniform for the operations lead: a button-down shirt, no tie, dark trousers, and a pair of Vans.

I stood inside the front door for approximately twelve seconds.

A woman walked up to me.

She said: "You're Lee."

I said: "I'm Lee."

She said: "I'm Imelda. I run operations. Welcome. The suit is going to be uncomfortable by lunch. We have a small storage closet where you can change. There's a t-shirt in the closet that should fit you. Theo bought a stack of them in your size when we hired you. He keeps them in the closet for situations like this. Do you want to change."

I said: "I would like to change."

She said: "Take this."

She handed me a Hyperion-logo t-shirt that fit and a pair of dark jeans that did not.

She said: "The jeans were a guess. You can return them. Wear what you came in if the jeans don't work."

I said: "Thank you."

I changed in the storage closet.

The jeans fit.

I walked out of the closet in the t-shirt and the jeans and the dress shoes I had worn from home.

I have, in the four years since, never worn a suit to the Hyperion offices again.

I want to flag, for the strip-canon reader, that Hyperion is the place Dilbert always wanted to work. The strip never had a panel for it because the strip's job was the Stupid Company™ joke. The strip's reader has been imagining Hyperion for thirty-five years, in the silences between the panels, in the moments where Dilbert looks at the camera with that face. The face has been Dilbert wondering what the other kind of company would look like.

This chapter is the other kind of company.

This chapter is the panel Adams did not get to draw.

I am, in Lee Hauschild's voice, going to take you through it for you.

You have been waiting for this since 1989.

---

Theo found me at nine-ten AM in the kitchen.

He poured me a coffee from the espresso machine.

He sat down across from me at the long oak table.

He said: "Lee."

I said: "Theo."

He said: "I am not going to give you any tasks today."

I said: "All right."

He said: "I am not going to give you any tasks this week."

I said: "All right."

He said: "I am not going to give you any tasks for as long as it takes you to figure out what you want to be working on."

I said: "Theo."

He said: "Yes."

I said: "What if it takes me three months."

He said: "If it takes you three months, it takes you three months. The people who have taken three months at Hyperion are the people who have, in their fourth month, started building something that we still rely on today. The people who have decided on day one have, on average, picked the wrong project. We have not had anyone take more than four months. We do not have a policy on what happens if someone takes more than four months. We will figure it out if it comes up. Most likely it will not come up."

I said: "Theo."

He said: "Yes."

I said: "What am I supposed to do today."

He said: "Have coffee. Have lunch with people. Walk around. Ask questions. Read the internal documentation if you want to. Read the public technical content we have put out. Take a long walk around the neighborhood. Take the afternoon off if you want to. The salary started today. The clock is on you. You can spend it however you spend it."

I said: "Theo."

He said: "Yes."

I said: "This is the strangest first day I have ever had."

He said: "I know it is. Most senior people who have joined Hyperion have said the same thing on their first day. I have started giving them a small piece of context that I think helps. Would you like me to give you the context."

I said: "Please."

---

Here is the context Theo gave me.

He said: "Lee. At your previous job — I am inferring this from the four hours of interview, not from anything specific Sam told me — your first day involved being assigned a project, being introduced to your team, being given a list of acronyms to learn, and being told to ramp up over a six-week period during which your value to the company was assumed to be zero. The ramp-up was tracked. The ramp-up had a calendar. The ramp-up ended at week six with you being expected to contribute at full velocity. Is that approximately right."

I said: "That is approximately right."

He said: "I want to tell you what we have noticed at Hyperion about that model."

I said: "Tell me."

He said: "The model assumes that what you are being hired for is *to fill a defined slot.* The defined slot has a job description. The defined slot has a set of skills it requires. The defined slot has a successor — if you leave in three years, somebody else fills the slot. The model is the model that ninety-five percent of companies use because the model is, in operational terms, a sensible model for a company that knows what it does and wants more of the same."

I said: "All right."

He said: "We do not, in our honest assessment, know what we do."

I said: "Theo."

He said: "We know what we do today. We have a product. The product makes money. The product is shipping. But the product we are going to be selling in three years is, we believe, not the product we are selling today. The product we are going to be selling in three years is the product that emerges from Hyperion's exploratory cognition density across the next thirty-six months. We do not know what that product looks like. We know it will be a product. We know it will be a product we are not currently building. We know it will be a product that exists because of the conversations that happen at this table, in this kitchen, in the Bad Ideas Hours, between people Yumi and I have hired because they had read books we found interesting and built tools we found interesting and asked questions we found interesting."

I said: "Theo."

He said: "Yes."

He said: "Lee, you are not being hired to fill a defined slot. You are being hired to participate in the production of the product we cannot yet describe. The production happens through the conversations. The conversations require you to bring your real self to the kitchen. Your real self does not arrive on a six-week ramp-up. Your real self arrives on the schedule your real self arrives on. We are not going to interrupt the schedule. We are going to let the schedule happen. If, in the meantime, you write some code, or you write some documentation, or you write some public content, or you do any of the things you have done at every other job you have ever had — that is fine. It is also fine if you do none of those things. The metric is *did Hyperion's exploratory cognition density go up when Lee joined.* The metric is not *how many lines of code did Lee write in his first month.*"

I said: "Theo."

He said: "Yes."

I said: "How do you know whether the exploratory cognition density has gone up."

He said: "We know because we are in the kitchen with you. We have lunch with you. We hear what you say. We feel the difference between a week in which the conversations are interesting and a week in which the conversations are flat. The difference is, in our honest experience, perceptible within two to four weeks of a hire. We have been wrong twice across twenty-three hires. We have, in those two cases, parted ways with the hires within ninety days, with reasonable severance, and no hard feelings on either side."

I said: "Theo."

He said: "Yes."

I said: "What if I am one of the two."

He said: "Lee. You are not one of the two. I do not say that flatteringly. I say it because Yumi and I and Frances and Imelda and the rotating cast at lunch had four hours of conversation with you on Friday, and we made the call on Friday afternoon between five thirty-seven and five forty-two PM. The call was a unanimous yes from the five of us. The five of us have been wrong twice. We are not, in our honest read, wrong about you. If we are wrong about you, we will tell you in ninety days, and we will part ways cleanly. In the meantime, drink the coffee. The coffee is the project."

I said: "Theo."

He said: "Yes."

I said: "Thank you."

He said: "Welcome to Hyperion."

He stood up.

He left the kitchen.

I sat at the long oak table for another forty minutes, drinking the coffee, watching the office come in.

---

I want to flag, before we move to Chapter 23, the specific thing Theo did in the conversation that I think is the most important thing.

Theo *told me what the model was.*

Most companies will not tell you what the model is.

Most companies operate on an unspoken model that you are supposed to infer from the behavior of the executives, the policies of HR, and the cultural rituals of the company. The unspoken model is, in most cases, the *defined-slot* model — you are being hired to fill a slot, the slot has a job description, the slot has a successor, the slot will be the same in three years.

Theo told me the unspoken model out loud.

The act of telling me the unspoken model out loud was, in itself, a Wise Corporation signal.

The Wise Corporation can tell you the model because the Wise Corporation has explicitly chosen its model.

The Stupid Company™ cannot tell you the model because the Stupid Company™ is operating on inherited assumptions that have never been examined.

The Stupid Company™'s model is also the *defined-slot* model. The model is everywhere. The model is what most workers have been living with for their entire careers. The model produces companies that are good at filling slots and bad at producing the products that will determine whether the company exists in a decade.

The Wise Corporation operates on a different model. The different model is what Theo described. The different model is: *we are hiring you to participate in the production of the product we cannot yet describe.*

The participation is the work.

The participation cannot be calendarized.

The participation produces the product.

The product produces the company's future.

The future depends on the participation, which depends on the hiring, which depends on the four hours of interview that determined whether Yumi and Theo wanted me in their kitchen.

The four hours of interview were the most important four hours of the company's last twelve months, in terms of its impact on the company's next five years.

The four hours of interview did not have a single coding question in them.

This is the Wise Corporation.

This is what it looks like on the inside.

The next chapter is the first week.

The first week is going to surprise you almost as much as the first day did.
