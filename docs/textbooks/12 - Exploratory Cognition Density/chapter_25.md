# Chapter 25: The Semantic Discovery Engine

This is the operating manual.

I am going to give you, in this chapter, the actual practices that produce a Wise Corporation. I am giving them to you as a numbered list. Each one is a practice I have observed, in person, at Hyperion Studio across the previous four years. Each one is a practice I am confident is *transferable* to any company that genuinely wants to adopt it.

The list is twelve items.

The list will look, on first reading, like a list of cultural quirks.

The list is, in operational terms, the system.

---

**1. No closed conference rooms.**

We covered this in Chapter 23. The closed door is the most consequential architectural choice a company makes. Hyperion has none. Every conversation that happens in the office happens in a space that the rest of the office can see and hear. Private conversations happen outside the office. The friction of moving them outside keeps the number low. The low number keeps the social network intact. The intact social network is the substrate on which the rest of the practices are built.

**2. The kitchen is the primary meeting space.**

Hyperion's primary meeting space is the kitchen, not a conference room. The kitchen has a long oak table. The kitchen has an espresso machine. The kitchen has snacks. The kitchen is, by deliberate design, the most comfortable space in the office. Comfortable spaces produce the most exploratory cognition. The conference-room model — uncomfortable chairs, sterile lighting, no food — was designed for *efficient transactional meetings.* The kitchen model is designed for *productive non-transactional conversation.* The Wise Corporation chooses the second.

**3. Open whiteboards everywhere.**

Hyperion has whiteboards on every wall that is not load-bearing. The whiteboards are open. The whiteboards have markers nearby. The whiteboards retain whatever is written on them until somebody erases it. The most productive conversations at Hyperion happen *standing at a whiteboard.* The standing matters. The whiteboard matters. The open access matters. A company that puts its whiteboards in conference rooms with closed doors has, in operational terms, made a choice about who is allowed to think out loud.

**4. Lunch is communal, by default, at the long table.**

Every employee at Hyperion eats lunch at the long table in the kitchen, every day, unless they are out of the office or working through lunch by deliberate choice. The default is communal. The default produces the *Lunch Hypothesis* effect from Chapter 23. Communal lunch is the single highest-leverage daily ritual a company can adopt. The cost is approximately fifty dollars per employee per day for the food. The return is, by my honest estimation, somewhere between two and ten times the cost in exploratory-cognition value.

**5. Friday afternoon Bad Ideas Hour, with no required attendance.**

Hyperion runs Bad Ideas Hour every Friday from three to five PM. Attendance is voluntary. There is no agenda. There are no minutes. There are no quarterly KPIs. There are no executive presentations. The hour is what it is. It produces what it produces. It has, across Hyperion's six-year history, produced approximately seventy percent of the company's shipped product features in some form, although the path from Bad Ideas Hour to shipped feature is, in most cases, indirect and long.

**6. The twenty-percent rule, applied loosely.**

Every Hyperion engineer is encouraged to spend approximately twenty percent of their time on work that is *not on the roadmap.* The twenty percent is not enforced. The twenty percent is not measured. The twenty percent is a cultural expectation. The expectation is that, if you are an engineer at Hyperion, *some of what you ship will not have been asked for in advance.* The expectation produces a culture in which exploratory work is normal. The exploratory work, across time, becomes a meaningful percentage of what Hyperion ships.

**7. Hiring conversations are about books, side projects, and play.**

We covered this in Chapter 21. Hyperion's interview process is structured around *who you are* rather than *what you have done.* The conversations are about books, side projects, and play. The conversations select for people who have read widely, built things on their own time, and play with ideas. The conversations do not select for people who have *executed within defined slots at other companies.* This is the most important hiring distinction in the Wise Corporation.

**8. The onboarding is the social network, not the system.**

We covered this in Chapter 23. New hires at Hyperion are not given tasks. They are given a list of names. They have coffee or lunch with every name across their first three weeks. The onboarding is complete when the new hire is in the social network. The system is irrelevant.

**9. There is no usage dashboard on any internal tool.**

Hyperion uses many internal tools. Hyperion does not measure tool usage. Hyperion measures *output.* The output is measured by what the company ships, not by how many keystrokes the engineers type into a particular IDE plugin. The absence of usage dashboards is, in itself, a deliberate cultural choice. The presence of usage dashboards, as we covered in Chapter 6, *changes what the engineers do* in ways that almost always degrade the underlying work.

**10. Every employee can talk to every other employee, directly, without going through a manager.**

Hyperion is twenty-three people. The structure is flat-ish. There are managers. The managers are not gatekeepers. Any employee can walk up to any other employee — including the founders — and ask them anything. The founders, in particular, are physically present in the office, in the kitchen, at lunch, at Bad Ideas Hour, every day. The access is the asset. The access produces the *information flow* that the closed-door companies have to engineer through formal mechanisms that mostly fail.

**11. The product roadmap is the substrate, not the constraint.**

Hyperion has a product roadmap. The roadmap is updated quarterly. The roadmap is *aspirational* rather than *binding.* What ships, on the actual ship date, is some mixture of (a) what was on the roadmap, (b) what emerged in the previous quarter from the engineering organization's exploratory work, and (c) what the founders decided to pivot toward in response to customer signal. The mixture is, on average, about forty percent roadmap, forty percent emergent work, and twenty percent pivot. The Stupid Company™ ships approximately ninety-five percent roadmap and five percent emergent work, with the five percent emergent work usually labeled as *bug fixes* on the executive dashboard.

**12. The company protects exploratory cognition density as the metric that does not appear on the dashboard.**

Yumi and Theo, at Hyperion, do not measure exploratory cognition density on a dashboard. They protect it. They protect it through hiring, through scheduling, through architectural choices about the office, through their own visible behavior, through the deliberate maintenance of the kitchen culture, through their own time spent in the kitchen, through the absence of closed conference rooms, through Bad Ideas Hour, through the twenty-percent rule, and through the operating manual you have just read.

That is the operating manual.

---

I want to flag, before we move to Chapter 26, the part of the operating manual that is hardest to copy.

The hardest part to copy is item 12.

Items 1 through 11 are practices. Practices are copyable. A Stupid Company™ that wanted to become a Wise Corporation could implement items 1 through 11 inside a year.

Item 12 is not a practice.

Item 12 is a *posture.*

The posture is the *founders' decision to protect a metric that does not appear on any dashboard, against the constant pressure from boards, investors, executives, and the company's own operating reality to optimize for the metrics that do appear on the dashboard.*

The posture is, in my honest experience, the rare thing.

Most founders cannot maintain the posture for more than a year or two. The pressure is enormous. The board has a quarterly meeting. The board wants metrics. The board does not want *we are protecting an unmeasurable cultural condition.* The board wants *Q3 revenue up forty percent year over year.* The board's pressure is, on average, irresistible.

Yumi and Theo have maintained the posture for six years.

They have done so, in my read across four years of close-up observation, through a combination of:

- Founder-controlled equity, which gives them the leverage to ignore board pressure when they need to.

- A small set of long-term investors who explicitly funded Hyperion on the *we are not going to be a normal company* thesis.

- A personal track record from their previous Google Brain work that gives them the credibility to make unusual claims about engineering culture without being dismissed.

- A genuine personal preference for the operating model, rooted in their own past experience at companies that had the model and at companies that did not.

- A willingness to lose specific investors, employees, and customers who require the conventional model. They have lost some of each across the previous six years. They have, in each case, judged the loss to be worth the maintenance of the posture.

The posture is, in operational terms, the *single most important thing* a founder of a Wise Corporation does.

The posture is also the thing that *cannot be transferred* if the founders ever leave.

This is the structural risk of the Wise Corporation.

The Stupid Company™'s structural risk is *that it dies of its own dashboards across thirty-six months.*

The Wise Corporation's structural risk is *that it dies when the founders leave, because the next CEO does not maintain the posture.*

Both risks are real.

The Wise Corporation, in the historical record, has had an easier time managing its risk than the Stupid Company™ has had managing its risk. The Wise Corporation's risk has, in many cases, been mitigated by deliberate founder-succession planning. The Stupid Company™'s risk has, in most cases, not been mitigated at all.

This is part of why Theo, in Chapter 24, gave Sam the comma.

The comma was the beginning of Theo's succession planning.

The comma is, in operational terms, *the title that says the company is going to be built around this person across the next decade.*

The comma was Theo's bet that Sam would, eventually, maintain the posture if and when Theo and Yumi left.

The bet, in my honest read across the previous two years, is paying off.

The bet is also, in itself, an exploratory cognition act.

The bet is one of the things that the Stupid Company™ cannot do, because the Stupid Company™ does not have anyone in it whose posture would be worth betting on.

The Wise Corporation has, structurally, more of those people, because the Wise Corporation hires for them.

We will talk about how to hire for them in the next chapter.
