# Chapter 1: How I Got Here

I am fifty-two years old. I have been wrong about most of the major decisions of my professional life. I am writing a business book because someone has to, and because most of the other people who are writing business books right now have never been fired by a man named Greg.

I have.

His name was actually Greg. I am not changing it for the book. He no longer works at the company we both used to work at. I am told he is now an executive coach, which I take to mean that he has at last located the career he was always going to be best suited for.

This book is about the company Greg used to run and the company I now work for. They make the same kind of thing. They use the same kind of technology. They hire from the same labor pool. They sell to the same customers. One of them, by the end of the book, is going to be acquired for parts. The other one is going to be on the cover of *Fast Company*.

You are going to be able to tell which is which by the end of Chapter 4.

If you cannot, the book is not for you, and I encourage you to return it for a refund. You are entitled to a refund. I have asked the publisher to honor it. The publisher has said no. But I have asked.

---

Here is the entire thesis of the book in nine sentences.

1. AI is two technologies.

2. The first technology is labor replacement.

3. The second technology is cognition amplification.

4. They look identical from the outside for about three years.

5. After three years they begin to diverge.

6. After five years one of them is producing more value per employee than at any point in industrial history.

7. The other one is being mentioned in a different section of the *Wall Street Journal.*

8. Most companies are currently buying the first technology and calling it the second technology.

9. The book is about how to recognize which one your company is buying, and what to do about it before the divergence reaches you personally.

That is the book. You can stop here. I would prefer that you do not, because the parts in the middle have most of the jokes.

---

I should explain why I am qualified to write this book.

I am not.

I am an engineer. I have a bachelor's degree from a state school that is good but not famous and a master's degree from a state school that is famous but not good. I have spent twenty-eight years writing code for a living. The code I have written has been, by my own honest accounting:

1. Mostly fine.

2. Occasionally important.

3. Once, briefly, on the cover of a trade publication that the rest of the industry treated as a minor scandal.

4. The rest of the time, code.

That is my qualification. I have written code for almost three decades, and during the last six years of that time I have watched, from inside two different companies, what AI does to organizations.

The two companies are the two companies in this book.

The first one is called **Mendacium Corp**. It is the Stupid Company™. It does not know it is the Stupid Company™ because nobody at the Stupid Company™ ever knows it is the Stupid Company™. That is a structural feature of being the Stupid Company™.

The second one is called **Hyperion Studio**. It is the Wise Corporation. Hyperion knows it is the Wise Corporation but does not say so because the Wise Corporation knows that saying you are the Wise Corporation is the first step toward becoming the Stupid Company™.

You can see how this is going to go.

---

The narrator of this book, by the way, is named Lee Hauschild. That is not the name on my driver's license. The name on my driver's license is a name I would prefer to keep on my driver's license. The publisher and I have an agreement about this. The agreement is in writing. I have read the agreement. The agreement does not, technically, prevent me from being identified, but it does require the reader to do the work of identifying me, and most readers will not. You are welcome to try. If you find me, please do not email me. I am not looking for additional correspondence.

Lee Hauschild is, in every other respect, me. The opinions in this book are Lee's opinions, which are my opinions, which are sometimes wrong. The anecdotes are real. The names of the other people in the anecdotes have been changed in some cases and not changed in others, depending on whether I am still afraid of them.

I am no longer afraid of Greg.

I have changed his name from Greg to Greg.

---

Here is what I want you to get out of this book.

1. A diagnostic that lets you tell, in nine minutes, whether you work at the Stupid Company™ or at the Wise Corporation. (Chapter 16.)

2. A framework — the Skill Stack — for thinking about your own career in the AI era. (Chapter 11 through 13.)

3. A list of skills that stack particularly well right now, paired with the AI capabilities they pair against. (Chapter 13.)

4. A second diagnostic, more brutal than the first, that lets you tell whether you personally are being replaced. (Chapter 14.)

5. A vocabulary. Named concepts, capitalized on first use, that you can carry into your next meeting and your next interview and your next dinner with a relative who works in tech. (Throughout. There are about twenty.)

6. Three examples of absurd ideas that produced commercially valuable discoveries because the people who had them worked at the Wise Corporation rather than at the Stupid Company™. (Chapter 18.)

7. Reagan Cho's resignation letter, in full, on the page. (Chapter 9.)

8. The two-sentence resignation letter that I personally used on the day I quit Mendacium Corp. (Chapter 20.)

9. The interview questions Hyperion Studio uses to hire. (Chapter 26.)

10. A worksheet you can use on yourself, the day after you finish the book, that will tell you which of your own skills are stack-eligible and which ones are not. (Chapter 19.)

11. The closer of the book, which is a question I am asking you, the answer to which you will have figured out by Chapter 4 but which will land with more weight in Chapter 30 because the intervening twenty-six chapters will have done their work. (Chapter 30. Do not skip.)

That is what you get.

You will notice the list is eleven items long. I think eleven is funnier than ten. I will explain in Chapter 13 why I think this matters.

---

The one thing I will not give you in this book is a confident prediction about your specific situation. I do not know your situation. I do not know your manager. I do not know whether the company you work for is being run by a Greg or by someone who is closer to one of the founders of Hyperion Studio.

I know what the patterns look like. I know what the patterns sound like. I know what the patterns smell like when you walk into the building.

But you have to do the diagnostic.

The reason you have to do the diagnostic is that I cannot fire your boss for you. I would if I could. The publisher will not let me.

---

A note on the title.

The book is called *Exploratory Cognition Density.* This is a phrase I did not invent. It comes from a field note I read in October of last year. The field note was an essay by a guy I have never met named Christopher Bender. The field note was titled *The Dual-Natured AI Labor Market*, and it was the clearest piece of writing I had read in five years on the subject of what AI was about to do to the labor market.

The field note made an argument I had been making for six months at Mendacium without anyone listening to me. I read it in one sitting. I emailed Bender the next morning. I asked him if I could turn the field note into a book. Bender said yes. He said he was not going to write the book himself because he was already writing eleven other books and one of them was a novel about a man who flies into outer space, and I should go ahead.

I went ahead.

This is the book.

---

The phrase *exploratory cognition density* is what it sounds like. It is the amount of unstructured exploratory thinking happening, per employee, per day, in your organization. It is the metric that nobody puts on a dashboard. It is the metric that, in the AI era, is going to determine which companies are still around in 2035.

The Stupid Company™ thinks the metric does not exist because they cannot put it on a dashboard.

The Wise Corporation does not put it on a dashboard for the same reason a good restaurant does not put its menu on Yelp. Once you put it on the dashboard, you are optimizing for the wrong thing.

The book is, in large part, about this.

---

One more thing before we start.

This book has a sense of humor. The humor is a teaching tool. I want to be clear about this because the previous business book I read — a book I will not name, but which has the word *Disrupt* in the title — also had a sense of humor, and the humor was not a teaching tool. The humor in that book was the disguise the author used to avoid being held accountable for the predictions in the book. Most of the predictions in that book have, in the two years since, turned out to be wrong. The author has not gone back and updated the book. The author is now writing a second book, which I assume will be funny in the same way.

I do not want to do that.

The humor in this book is the wrapper. The lesson is in the wrapper. If the lesson is wrong, the joke is also wrong, and you should email the publisher to tell them I was wrong. The publisher will not reply, but you will have done your part.

The joke and the lesson are the same thing. I want to be in business with you on that basis.

If you are willing to be in business with me on that basis, we can begin.

---

Mendacium Corp is the Stupid Company™. I worked there for fourteen years.

Hyperion Studio is the Wise Corporation. I have worked there for four.

The book starts in October of the year I joined Mendacium. I was thirty-eight years old. I had just left a smaller company where I had been the seventh engineer. Mendacium was the larger company my then-wife had wanted me to work at. We are no longer married. That is a separate book.

I will not write the separate book.

You are welcome.

Let's go.
