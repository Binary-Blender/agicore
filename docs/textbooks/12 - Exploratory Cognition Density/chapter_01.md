# Chapter 1: How I Got Here

I am fifty-two years old. I have been wrong about most of the major decisions of my professional life. I am writing a business book because someone has to, and because most of the other people who are writing business books right now have never been fired by a man with two pointy hairs.

I have.

You know him.

You have, in fact, known him for thirty-five years. He has been on the comics page since 1989. He has had the same haircut the entire time. He has, by now, fired roughly half of every engineering organization in the published canon of American corporate satire. He fired the engineers who became Scott Adams. He fired the engineers who became, in their own smaller ways, every engineer who has ever quietly resigned from a company that did not deserve them.

In this book, he fired me.

His name, in this book, is **Greg.** His title, in this book, is *Chief Operating Officer.* He was promoted to CEO in Year Six. He runs a company called Mendacium Corp, which is in the Pacific Northwest, which sells enterprise software, which is — and I want to be honest with you about this on the first page so the rest of the book makes sense — *the company you have been reading the comic strip about since 1989,* with the name changed so that I can publish this book without being sued by the company.

The company in the strip does not have a name.

I am calling it Mendacium Corp.

The name is mine. The company is the company you already know.

The Pointy-Haired Boss is in this book. He is also Greg. He is the same character. The fact that I am calling him *Greg* throughout is a tic of the narrator (which is to say, of me), because I worked for him for eight years, and across those eight years he was, to me, a specific man with three pairs of identical glasses and a twenty-six-foot sailboat that he insisted on calling "thirty-eight feet of trouble." To the rest of the world he is the Pointy-Haired Boss. To me, in this book, he is Greg.

Both names are correct.

I will be using both throughout.

---

I want to do this same introduction with the rest of the cast, because the meta-comedy of the book depends on you understanding the equivalences, and because I do not want you to spend three chapters wondering whether I am, in fact, talking about the Dilbert strip when I describe Sam, my best friend at the company, as *a hopeful engineer with rectangular glasses and a striped shirt that he has had pressed by his mother since he was twenty-two.*

I am talking about the Dilbert strip.

Sam Park, in this book, is the senior engineer on the platform team at Mendacium. He is Dilbert. He is the Dilbert. He is the engineer the comic strip is named after, given a working name and a present-tense life in the year 2026, doing what Dilbert always does, which is to say working competently at a company that does not deserve him. He goes by Sam in this book because I have known him for nine years and have called him Sam the whole time. The world calls him Dilbert. I call him Sam. He answers to both.

The same goes for everyone else.

**Reagan Cho**, senior product manager at Mendacium until she quits in Chapter 9, is Alice. She has Alice's rage. She has Alice's competence. She has Alice's hair, which is canonically the most aerodynamic hair in American syndicated comics. I call her Reagan because we have been colleagues for six years and I have always called her Reagan.

**Marv Donaldson**, the lifer, the principal engineer, the man who has been at Mendacium for twenty-one years and who has done the minimum for the last twelve of them, is Wally. He is *also* Wally with a twist that the strip never had room to show, because the strip's format is three panels, and the twist takes about nine years to develop, and the strip moved on too quickly to follow it. I will tell you the twist in Chapter 5. The twist is that Wally is, in operational terms, the most important employee in the company, and that the slacking — the legendary Wally slacking — is a calculated response to a real strategic problem that you, the reader, have not been told about. I am going to tell you about it.

**Kai Iverson**, the intern, twenty-three years old, has the new MacBook in the plastic case and the hopeful expression, is Asok. Of course he is Asok. He arrived on the same morning the AI did. He gets radicalized by Chapter 16. He goes to the Wise Corporation by Chapter 21. His arc is the most Adams-canonical arc in this book.

**Karina Whitford**, Mendacium's chief people officer, is Catbert. She has the title, the responsibilities, the malice, and — in a touch I will defend in Chapter 7 — the cat-shaped HR-conference-room artwork that everyone at Mendacium has been quietly noticing for nine years. She is not actually a cat. She just acts like one. The strip got there first. The strip was right.

**SOPHIE**, the AI product Mendacium buys in Chapter 4, is — and this is the part I want you to sit with — *Dogbert.* SOPHIE is the consumer-facing name. The internal vendor team at Sophia AI Corp calls the product something they have not made public. I will not tell you what they call it until Chapter 4. You can guess.

That is the cast.

That is the strip, given names and a present-tense year.

That is the book.

---

I want to tell you why I am writing the book this way.

I am writing the book this way because, in October of last year, I read a field note by a guy I have never met named Christopher Bender, titled *The Dual-Natured AI Labor Market.* The field note made the cleanest argument I had read in five years on what AI was actually about to do to the labor market. The argument was that AI is two technologies — labor replacement and cognition amplification — that look identical from the outside but produce two completely different kinds of company. The field note gave the two companies names. It called them *the Stupid Company™* and *the Wise Corporation.* The names landed. I wrote Bender the next morning and asked him if I could turn the field note into a book.

Bender said yes.

I started writing.

Three weeks into the writing I realized that the book I was writing was, at every paragraph, *trying not to be a Scott Adams book.* I was using generic names. I was inventing original characters. I was describing the dysfunctions of Mendacium Corp in language that, when I read it back, was a careful diluted version of the language Adams had been writing for thirty-five years.

I deleted the draft.

I started over.

The second draft is the book you are holding.

In the second draft, I let the Adams characters in. I let them be themselves. I let Greg be the Pointy-Haired Boss while also being Greg. I let Sam be Dilbert while also being Sam. I let Wally be Wally and let Marv be Marv and let the two names refer to the same person across two registers — the register of Adams's strip and the register of my own working memory.

The second draft is, in operational terms, *honest about its lineage.*

The first draft was, in operational terms, *plagiarizing Adams while pretending not to.*

I would rather pay the tribute openly.

That is why the book is written this way.

---

I should explain why I am qualified to write this book.

I am not.

I am an engineer. I have spent twenty-eight years writing code for a living. The code I have written has been, by my own honest accounting:

1. Mostly fine.

2. Occasionally important.

3. Once, briefly, on the cover of a trade publication that the rest of the industry treated as a minor scandal.

4. The rest of the time, code.

That is my qualification. I have written code for almost three decades, and during the last six years of that time I have watched, from inside two different companies, what AI does to organizations.

The two companies are the two companies in this book.

The first one is **Mendacium Corp.** It is the Stupid Company™. It is the company in the strip. It does not know it is the company in the strip because nobody at the company in the strip ever knows it is the company in the strip. That is a structural feature of being in the strip.

The second one is **Hyperion Studio.** It is the Wise Corporation. Hyperion knows it is the Wise Corporation but does not say so because the Wise Corporation knows that saying you are the Wise Corporation is the first step toward becoming the Stupid Company™.

You can see how this is going to go.

---

The narrator of this book, by the way, is named Lee Hauschild. That is not the name on my driver's license. The name on my driver's license is a name I would prefer to keep on my driver's license. The publisher and I have an agreement about this. The agreement is in writing.

Lee Hauschild is, in every other respect, me. The opinions in this book are Lee's opinions, which are my opinions, which are sometimes wrong. The anecdotes are real. The names of the other people in the anecdotes have been changed in some cases. The strip versions of their names — Dilbert, Wally, Alice, Asok, Catbert, Dogbert — have not been changed because Scott Adams already named them in 1989 and there is, in the open-source non-commercial parody-law tradition, no reason to pretend otherwise.

I am no longer afraid of Greg.

I am also, more importantly, no longer afraid of the Pointy-Haired Boss.

That is the same thing, in two different registers.

---

Here is what I want you to get out of this book.

1. A diagnostic that lets you tell, in nine minutes, whether you work at the Stupid Company™ or at the Wise Corporation. (Chapter 16.)

2. A framework — the Skill Stack — for thinking about your own career in the AI era, drawn directly from Scott Adams's 2014 book *How to Fail at Almost Everything and Still Win Big*, which is, in my honest view, the most important career book published in the previous twenty years. (Chapters 11 through 13.)

3. A list of sixteen skills that stack particularly well right now, each paired with the AI capability it pairs against. (Chapter 13.)

4. A second diagnostic, more brutal than the first, that lets you tell whether you personally are being replaced. (Chapter 14.)

5. A vocabulary of named, capitalized principles you can carry into your next meeting and your next interview and your next dinner with a relative who works in tech. Many of them are mine. Some of them are Adams's. The footnotes are honest about which is which.

6. Three examples of absurd ideas that produced commercially valuable discoveries because the people who had them worked at the Wise Corporation rather than at the Stupid Company™. (Chapter 18.) One of them involves Dr. Seuss. Adams would have approved.

7. Alice's resignation letter — sorry, *Reagan's* resignation letter — in full, on the page. (Chapter 9.) Alice would have written this letter if Adams had given her the room. The strip's format did not have the room. The book does. I am giving Alice the room.

8. My own two-sentence resignation letter, used on the day I quit Mendacium Corp. (Chapter 20.) This one is mine. Adams was not consulted.

9. The job-interview questions Hyperion Studio uses to hire. (Chapter 26.) None of these would have made it into a *Dilbert* strip because they are too earnest. The Wise Corporation is, in this book, the place where the earnestness lives.

10. A worksheet you can use on yourself, the day after you finish the book, that will tell you which of your own skills are stack-eligible and which ones are not. (Chapter 19.)

11. The closer of the book, which is the question the source field note ended on, which the book is going to ask you again at the end after thirty chapters of evidence. (Chapter 30.) The closer is also, this time, about Scott Adams.

That is what you get.

The list is eleven items long. I think eleven is funnier than ten. Adams thinks the same thing. He has said so in interviews. We can argue about this in Chapter 13.

---

One more thing before we start.

This book has a sense of humor. The humor is a teaching tool. The joke and the lesson are the same thing. If the lesson is wrong, the joke is also wrong, and you should email the publisher to tell them I was wrong.

This book is also, more importantly, a tribute.

The tribute is to Scott Adams.

The tribute is in the foreword, which I would like you to read if you have not already. The tribute is also in the characters, in the structural choices, in the named frameworks, in the cadence of the chapters, in the use of numbered lists, in the parenthetical asides, in the boxed dialogues, in the entire register of the book. I have stolen all of it from Adams. I have stolen it honestly. The theft is the tribute.

Adams, in 1995, was laid off by Pacific Bell. He went home and went full-time on a comic strip he had been drawing on the side since 1989. The strip became the most-read corporate satire in American history.

In 2026, the next Scott Adams is being laid off, on a Tuesday, by an algorithm that has decided his SOPHIE engagement score is too low.

The book is for him.

The book is also for her. There are now, by my honest count, multiple next-Scott-Adamses being laid off across the United States in 2026, and they are not all named Scott, and they are not all going to draw a comic strip. Some of them are going to write a Substack. Some of them are going to build a small studio. Some of them are going to join a Wise Corporation. Some of them are going to spend the next thirty years documenting, in their own register, what the corporate world has been doing to itself in the AI era.

The book is for them.

The book is also for Scott Adams himself, who is, at the time of this writing, ill. The book may not reach him. The book may not, if it does reach him, be the kind of thing he wants to read. But the book exists because he existed, and the book is going out into the world while he is still in it, and that is the right time for a tribute to land, which is *now,* before the tribute would become a eulogy.

The book is not a eulogy.

The book is a thank-you.

Mendacium Corp is the Stupid Company™. I worked there for fourteen years.

Hyperion Studio is the Wise Corporation. I have worked there for four.

The book starts in October of the year I joined Mendacium. I was thirty-eight years old. I had just left a smaller company where I had been the seventh engineer. Mendacium was the larger company my then-wife had wanted me to work at. We are no longer married. That is a separate book.

I will not write the separate book.

You are welcome.

Let's go.
