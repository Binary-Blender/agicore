# Chapter 28: How the Stupid Company™ Dies

The Stupid Company™ does not die in a press release.

The Stupid Company™ dies, in operational terms, through six phases, each of which lasts somewhere between four and eighteen months, and each of which is, at the time it is happening, invisible to the executive team.

I want to walk you through the six phases.

The phases are not unique to Mendacium. The phases are, in my honest experience across forty-one companies I have casually evaluated over six years, *the canonical failure curve of the AI-era Stupid Company™.* You can apply the phases to your own employer. If your employer is currently in phase three, you have between eighteen months and four years before the press release. If your employer is in phase five, you have between three and twelve.

If your employer is in phase six, the press release is coming inside ninety days.

---

**Phase One: The AI Transformation Announcement.**

The CEO returns from a conference. The slide deck is built on the airplane. The whiteboard is mounted by Friday morning. The LinkedIn post is up by ten AM. The procurement is signed within two weeks. The all-hands is held within four weeks. The welcome video is delivered by the AI vendor's marketing team. The engagement dashboard is built within six weeks.

This phase lasts about three months.

This phase ends when the first AI tool is fully deployed across the engineering organization.

The phase is, at the time it is happening, *the most exciting phase of the failure curve.* The dashboards are green. The executives are energized. The board is impressed. The press is positive. The stock is up. The LinkedIn engagement is up. The company believes it has made the right call.

The company has not, in operational terms, lost anything yet. The company has, in operational terms, *committed to a path* that, across the next thirty-six months, will produce the outcomes the company has not yet seen.

The Wise Corporations do not go through Phase One. The Wise Corporations either *do not deploy the AI tools in the same way*, or they deploy them with a different framing (tool, not colleague), or they deploy them without the engagement dashboard. The absence of Phase One is, in itself, a Wise Corporation tell.

---

**Phase Two: The First Layoff.**

Three to six months after the AI deployment, the first layoff happens. The layoff is framed as a *productivity-driven workforce optimization.* The layoff includes, by algorithm, the workers whose tool-engagement scores are in the bottom decile. The layoff includes, in many cases, the workers whose actual contribution was load-bearing but whose dashboard engagement was low.

The layoff is, on the executive dashboard, a positive event. The personnel cost line drops. The operating margin improves. The board is pleased.

The layoff is, in the engineering organization's reality, the event that marks the beginning of the company's *loss of institutional knowledge.* The people who are being let go are, in some specific cases, the people who knew where the bodies were buried. The bodies, in some cases, are still alive but are about to begin rotting.

This phase lasts about two months.

This phase ends when the laid-off employees have moved on to other companies.

---

**Phase Three: The Quiet Slack Channels.**

The phase that follows the first layoff is the slow death of the company's exploratory cognition density.

The engineering Slack channels go quiet, as we covered in Chapter 7. The conversations move to private DMs and the parking lot. The Bad Ideas Hour, if it existed, is suspended. The Friday afternoon ideation sessions, if they existed, become *quarterly hack days* with required output. The internal-tools projects, if they existed, are deprioritized in favor of *strategic initiatives* defined by the executive team.

The engineering organization, across the course of this phase, *stops generating exploratory work.*

The dashboards do not catch this.

The dashboards continue to show *engineering velocity up forty-one percent year over year*, by the inflated story-point metric we covered in Chapter 6.

The actual underlying output of the engineering organization is, in this phase, *declining.*

The decline is invisible.

The decline is, on the dashboard, *improvement.*

This phase lasts about six months.

This phase ends when the second wave of voluntary attrition begins.

---

**Phase Four: The Second Wave of Attrition.**

The engineers who were going to leave on principle begin leaving. The Reagan-style resignation letters begin appearing in the engineering inboxes. The recruiting calls from competitor companies — particularly from the Wise Corporations in the same geographic market — increase in frequency. The senior individual contributors start updating their LinkedIn.

The second wave of attrition is, by my honest count, where the company loses approximately fifteen to twenty percent of its remaining engineering organization over a six-to-twelve-month period.

The fifteen-to-twenty percent is, in nine cases out of ten, *the most consequential fifteen-to-twenty percent.*

The fifteen-to-twenty percent is the people who could see what was happening, who had options elsewhere, and who exercised the options.

The fifteen-to-twenty percent is the people the company most needed to keep.

The executive team does not, in most cases, notice this until the phase is almost over. The executive team sees the attrition numbers as *normal industry turnover.* The executive team does not, in most cases, ask which specific people left, what they did, and what is no longer being done.

This phase lasts about nine months.

This phase ends when the first customer non-renewal hits the financial reports.

---

**Phase Five: The First Customer Non-Renewals.**

The customer non-renewals begin somewhere between twelve and twenty-four months after the first layoff. The non-renewals are, in nearly every case, traceable to *the loss of a specific operational capability* that was carried by a specific person who was let go in Phase Two or who left in Phase Four.

In Mendacium's case, the first non-renewal was the twelve-point-four-million-dollar account that depended on the integration Marv had been quietly maintaining. The non-renewal happened in Q3 of Year Eight, eleven months after Marv was let go.

The customer-success team escalates the non-renewals to the executive team.

The executive team, in most cases, does not understand the root cause. The executive team understands the *event* (the non-renewal) but does not understand the *cause* (the operational capability the company no longer has).

The executive team, in most cases, responds by *adding more AI tooling to the customer-success organization* to *better predict and prevent churn.*

The additional AI tooling does not address the root cause.

The additional AI tooling addresses the *measurement* of the symptom.

The non-renewals continue.

This phase lasts about twelve months.

This phase ends when the first analyst downgrade hits the stock.

---

**Phase Six: The Analyst Downgrade.**

The first analyst downgrade happens approximately twenty-four to thirty-six months after the AI Transformation Announcement.

The downgrade is, in retrospect, the moment the public market begins to price in what the engineering organization has known for two years.

The downgrade triggers a stock decline of somewhere between fifteen and forty percent across the following ninety days.

The board, in response to the stock decline, *initiates a strategic review.*

The strategic review is, in operational terms, *the process by which the board decides whether the CEO should be replaced or the company should be sold.*

The strategic review takes between six and twelve months.

The outcome of the strategic review is, in nine of ten cases:

1. The CEO is replaced by a *turnaround specialist* from a private equity firm.

2. The company is, within six months of the new CEO arriving, sold to that private equity firm or to a strategic acquirer at a valuation roughly half of the peak.

3. The new owners begin the process of *cost rationalization*, which is the same process the original CEO began, except now under different ownership.

4. The company, across the following three to five years, is wound down to a smaller, more focused, more profitable, less interesting business.

5. The company, in the historical record, never returns to its previous scale or its previous market position.

This phase lasts between eighteen and thirty-six months.

The phase is the death.

The phase is, at the time it begins, *visible to the entire industry.*

The phase is, at the time it ends, *the press release everyone expected.*

---

I want to draw the lesson plain.

The Stupid Company™ does not die because AI is bad. The Stupid Company™ does not die because the technology was wrong. The Stupid Company™ does not die because the market changed.

The Stupid Company™ dies because the executive team made a series of choices, beginning with the AI Transformation Announcement, that systematically destroyed the company's *exploratory cognition density*, and the destruction was, for the first eighteen to twenty-four months, invisible on every metric the executive team was measuring.

By the time the destruction was visible, the company's most consequential employees were at other companies.

The destruction was self-inflicted.

The destruction was, in retrospect, predictable.

The destruction was, by anyone who had read the field note this book is based on, *predicted* in October of Year One.

The destruction is, in the historical record, *what most companies that go through Phase One end up looking like by Phase Six.*

The companies that avoid the destruction are the ones that did not start at Phase One.

The companies that did not start at Phase One are the Wise Corporations.

I want to flag, before we move on, that the six phases of the failure curve are the six phases that have been operating, in the strip's background, for thirty-five years. The strip's company has been in Phase Three for most of its run. Adams kept it in Phase Three on purpose, because Phase Three is the funniest phase. Phase Four and Phase Five have less material — by Phase Four, the most engaging employees have left, and by Phase Five, the customers have started leaving. The strip's joke depended on the company having a workforce of disgruntled engineers and a customer base that was still complaining. Adams stayed in Phase Three. The strip stayed funny.

The actual strip-canon company, if you read it as the long-run document Adams was unconsciously building, *was* moving through the phases. It is now, in the canonical timeline, somewhere in late Phase Five. The customer non-renewals have begun. The dashboards are still green because Adams stopped publishing dashboards. The Phase-Six press release is somewhere in the gap between when the strip went on permanent hiatus and now. The strip never had to show it because the strip's job was to make us laugh at Phase Three.

This book has the job the strip did not have, which is to walk the company through Phase Four, Five, and Six, and to tell you what happens at each one, and to give you the diagnostic to recognize them in your own company.

Adams did the Phase Three reporting for us.

I am doing the Phase Four through Six reporting for him.

This is the division of labor between the strip and the book.

The Wise Corporations are the subject of Chapter 29.

The Wise Corporations are not on the failure curve.

The Wise Corporations are on a different curve.

The different curve is, by my honest read of the next decade, *the curve that is going to define the AI economy.*

I will see you in Chapter 29.
