The three categories are:

**Category A: You are at the Wise Corporation, with a stack-eligible Skill Stack.** Your job is to *defend the Wise Corporation* against the constant pressure to drift toward the Stupid Company™ model. The defense is everyone's responsibility. Be in the kitchen. Attend Bad Ideas Hour. Refuse to participate in the construction of engagement dashboards if anyone proposes one. **Category B: You are at the Stupid Company™, with a stack-eligible Skill Stack.** Use the Window. The Window is closing. You are going to be fine.

**Category C: You are at either the Stupid Company™ or in transition, without a stack-eligible Skill Stack yet.** Build the stack. The building takes time. Pick one or two of the sixteen-and-a-half skill pairings from Chapter 13. Spend the next twelve months getting yourself into the top twenty-five percent on each. Now I want to talk about Scott Adams.

In June of 1995, an engineer named Scott Adams was laid off by Pacific Bell.

Adams was thirty-eight years old. He had been at Pacific Bell since 1986. He had spent nine years inside a Stupid Company™ that did not yet know it was a Stupid Company™, watching the dysfunctions, taking notes, drawing the comic strip on the side at six in the morning before his real workday started.

By June of 1995, the strip had been running for six years. The strip had grown from a few dozen newspapers to over a hundred. The strip was, to anyone paying attention, going to be Adams's full-time career within a couple of years.

Pacific Bell was not paying attention.

Pacific Bell's executives, in 1995, were operating on the dashboards that produced the same logic the dashboards in this book have been producing in Year Eight at Mendacium Corp. The dashboards said that Adams's department was bloated. The dashboards said that the engineering organization could be reduced by approximately the percentage the SOPHIE-equipped CEOs of 2026 are now using as their benchmark. The dashboards said that the specific engineer named Scott Adams was, in the metric Pacific Bell's HR algorithm was using, *not generating value commensurate with his compensation.*

This was wrong.

It was wrong in 1995 in exactly the same way the SOPHIE algorithm at Mendacium was wrong in Year Eight when it tagged Marv Donaldson for layoff.

The dashboards do not have a column for *secretly building the most influential corporate-satire body of work in American history while sitting at the workstation we are paying for.*

The dashboards never have that column.

The dashboards never had that column.

The dashboards never will have that column.

Pacific Bell laid Adams off.

The corporate world has, since 1995, been quietly aware that it made the wrong call. The trade press has written about it. The HR textbooks have referenced it. The MBA case studies have analyzed it. The case studies have, in every instance, concluded that *Pacific Bell was unlucky to have laid off a future cultural phenomenon,* which is the wrong conclusion, because the conclusion misses what actually happened.

What actually happened is that Pacific Bell's dashboards systematically destroyed the company's exploratory cognition density across the late 1980s and early 1990s, that Adams was one of the engineers whose contribution to that density was *invisible to the dashboards,* and that Pacific Bell's layoff of Adams was *exactly the call the dashboards would have made,* because the dashboards were measuring the wrong thing.

The dashboards have not changed.

The dashboards in 2026 are the same dashboards, refitted with SOPHIE telemetry instead of TQM telemetry.

The dashboards are, in 2026, in the process of doing to *eleven million* working professionals what Pacific Bell did to Scott Adams in 1995.

The eleven million are going to do, across the next thirty years, what Adams did across the previous thirty.

They are going to go home.

They are going to start writing.

They are going to start drawing.

They are going to start podcasting and substacking and building small studios and joining Wise Corporations and starting their own.

They are going to document, in their own registers, exactly what the Stupid Companies™ they were laid off from were actually doing.

And the corporate world is going to read the documentation.

And the corporate world is going to discover, in approximately the year 2055, that the documentation has produced the most extensive case-study literature on corporate dysfunction in the history of American business writing.

And the corporate world is going to wish, in 2055, that it had not laid off the people who produced the literature.

The corporate world will not get them back.

The corporate world also will not, by then, be the corporate world it was in 2026, because the literature will have shaped a generation of working professionals who will, having read the literature, build different kinds of companies.

That is the long game.

That is the trade.

The corporate world, in 2026, is making a trade. The trade is: *we will lower our personnel cost ratio by four points in this quarter's dashboard, in exchange for producing the next thirty years of our own critics.*

The trade is bad.

The trade is irreversible.

The corporate world does not yet know it has made the trade.

The corporate world has not yet read this book.

I want to draw, before I ask you the question, the conclusion plain.

Scott Adams was the proof of concept.

The Skill Stack he built — *engineering's eye for corporate dysfunction* plus *cartooning at the top of the twenty-fifth percentile* plus *plain-English explanation at the top of the same* — was the combinatorial position that, when Pacific Bell laid him off, gave him the launchpad to spend the next thirty years building one of the most-read corporate-commentary bodies of work in American letters.

There are, in 2026, by my honest estimation, between five hundred thousand and three million working professionals in the United States who have, at some level of consciousness, the same combinatorial position Adams had in 1995. They are engineers who write well. They are designers who think systematically. They are product managers who have, in their evenings, been doing the cross-domain reading. They are the workers whose SOPHIE engagement scores are in the bottom decile because they have been busy doing *the real work.*

The Stupid Companies™, in 2026, are in the process of laying off those between five hundred thousand and three million people.

The layoffs are going to produce, across the next decade, a creator class that the previous decade did not produce.

The creator class is going to be the most economically consequential creator class in American history, because the creator class is going to be writing the literature that the next generation of companies will read.

Some of the creator class will write books like this one.

Some of them will draw comic strips.

Some of them will, in 2030, do whatever it is that the AI tools have made possible by then, in a medium that has not yet been invented.

All of them will, in operational terms, *be doing what Adams started doing on a drafting table in his apartment in Dublin, California, in 1989, before Pacific Bell laid him off.*

The book is for them.

The book is also for Adams.

The book is also, in a smaller way, for Pacific Bell, who did not know what they did when they did it, who have never publicly apologized for it, who do not exist as a corporate entity anymore (Pacific Bell was absorbed into SBC Communications, which became AT&T, which is now a different company that no longer remembers it laid off Scott Adams), and who, in the disappearance of their corporate identity, are themselves the case study for what happens to the Stupid Company™ across thirty years.

Pacific Bell is gone.

Scott Adams is still here.

That is the trade in one sentence.
