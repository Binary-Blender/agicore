# Chapter 30: We'll Let You Decide

We are at the end of the book.

I want to spend a few pages on what to do next, because the question of *what to do next* is the question every reader of this kind of book has when they reach the last chapter, and because most authors of this kind of book fail to answer it.

I am going to try to answer it.

Then I am going to ask you the question that closes the book.

---

The thing to do next is, in operational order, the following.

**One.** Take the diagnostic from Chapter 10. Score your own company. Take it again as the stranger version. Score yourself.

**Two.** Take the diagnostic from Chapter 14. Score yourself. Take it again as the stranger version. Score yourself.

**Three.** Fill out the worksheet from Chapter 19. By hand, on paper, this weekend.

**Four.** Based on what the diagnostics and the worksheet tell you, decide which of the following three categories you are in.

The three categories are:

**Category A: You are at the Wise Corporation, with a stack-eligible Skill Stack.** Your job is to *defend the Wise Corporation* against the constant pressure to drift toward the Stupid Company™ model. The defense is everyone's responsibility. The defense includes: maintaining the kitchen, attending Bad Ideas Hour, being in the social network of the company, being a generous mentor to new hires, refusing to participate in the construction of engagement dashboards if anyone proposes one, and being the kind of person who, in three years when the founders are considering the strategic inflection, will be in the room arguing for the protection of the model. This is the highest-stakes category. The Wise Corporation is fragile. You are now responsible for it.

**Category B: You are at the Stupid Company™, with a stack-eligible Skill Stack.** Your job is to *use the Window.* The Window is closing. The Window has, in your specific company, between three months and three years left. Use it. Talk to recruiters at Wise Corporations. Update your worksheet. Build your parallel filing cabinet. Identify the four to seven names you would want to take with you, or who would want to take you with them. Quit on Tuesday, when the Tuesday arrives, with the short letter and the clean transition. You are going to be fine. You are going to be better than fine. The next ten years are going to be the most professionally rewarding years of your life if you act on what you now know.

**Category C: You are at either the Stupid Company™ or in transition, without a stack-eligible Skill Stack yet.** Your job is to *build the stack.* The building takes time. The building also produces, as a byproduct, the Skill Stack itself. Pick one or two of the sixteen skill pairings from Chapter 13. Spend the next twelve months getting yourself into the top twenty-five percent on each. Identify cross-domain reading you want to do. Build something on your own time. Get to a position, inside twelve to eighteen months, where you can credibly compete for a Wise Corporation hire. You do not have to be there today. You can be there in eighteen months.

That is the operational manual.

The operational manual fits on one page.

The rest of the book has been the explanation of why the operational manual is the right manual.

---

I want to give you one final framework before I ask you the question.

The framework is **the cognitive amplification asymmetry.**

The framework is the thesis the field note this book is based on opened with.

The field note was titled *The Dual-Natured AI Labor Market.* The author was Christopher Bender. The field note made the argument that AI is not one technology, but two — labor replacement and cognition amplification — and that the divergence between the two is going to define the AI economy across the next decade.

The book has, across thirty chapters, tried to teach you the operational shape of that divergence at the level of:

- the company (Stupid Company™ vs Wise Corporation),

- the executive (Greg vs Yumi-and-Theo),

- the worker (Mendacium engineer vs Hyperion engineer), and

- the career (your career, depending on which side of the divergence you choose).

The asymmetry is the central fact of the AI era.

The asymmetry is what most people in the industry are not seeing yet.

The asymmetry will, by my honest estimation, be the dominant narrative of business writing across the next decade. We are early. You are early. The fact that you have read this book puts you, in operational terms, in the small subset of professionals who are operating on the asymmetry intentionally rather than being operated on by it.

The asymmetry rewards intentionality.

The asymmetry punishes the absence of intentionality.

That is the entire game.

Now the question.

I have been promising you the question for thirty chapters.

You already know the question. The field note that started this whole project closed with the question. I have been opening every reference to the field note across this book by quoting the question. You have, by this point, read the question approximately fourteen times.

I am going to ask it one more time.
