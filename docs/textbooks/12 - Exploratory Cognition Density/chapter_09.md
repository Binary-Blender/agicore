# Chapter 9: Reagan Quits in Writing

Reagan Cho sent her resignation email at eleven forty-seven AM Pacific on a Tuesday in early May of Year Eight.

The email was to the entire engineering organization.

I will give you the email in full, because the email is the chapter, and because the email is the document that I personally have re-read seventeen times across the four years since.

---

> **From:** Reagan Cho
> **To:** engineering-all@mendacium.com
> **Subject:** I'm resigning, here's why
>
> Hi everyone,
>
> I am resigning, effective today. This is my last email from this address.
>
> I want to tell you why, because I think you have a right to know, and because most of you are about to be in the position of deciding what to do with your own career, and I would like to be useful one more time on my way out.
>
> Here are the reasons I am leaving.
>
> 1. We are working at a company that does not understand what it does. Five years ago we understood what we did. We sold a data platform that helped Fortune 1000 companies manage their customer data better. We were good at it. The customers loved us. The product worked. Today we sell a data platform that increasingly does not work, and our customer-success team is spending most of its time apologizing for things our engineering team can no longer fix, and our executive team is celebrating engineering velocity numbers that I personally know to be inflated by approximately thirty-eight percent.
>
> 2. The seventeen people we laid off in January included some of the most consequential individual contributors at this company. Marv Donaldson, who was on the list, was personally responsible for keeping seven customer integrations alive that together accounted for approximately ninety-six million dollars of annual recurring revenue. Marv is no longer here. The integrations are starting to fail. The first one failed two weeks ago. I have heard, internally, that the customer is moving toward non-renewal. The non-renewal is twelve point four million dollars. There will be more of these. There is no plan in place for them because the people who would have built the plan are no longer here.
>
> 3. SOPHIE is not a colleague. SOPHIE is a tool. SOPHIE is a useful tool, in some contexts, when used by engineers who know which of her outputs to trust and which to verify. We have a CEO who has told the engineering organization that SOPHIE is a colleague and built a dashboard that ranks engineers by how aggressively they use her. We have an HR organization that has constructed an algorithm that fires engineers whose SOPHIE engagement scores are low. The algorithm is firing the wrong people. The algorithm has been firing the wrong people since January. It is going to keep firing the wrong people. The people the algorithm is firing are, in many cases, the people whose work was good enough before SOPHIE that they have no need to use her. The people the algorithm is rewarding are, in many cases, the people who have figured out how to game the dashboard by typing single characters into popup windows.
>
> 4. The engineering Slack channels are dead. The channels were the most lively channels in this company for the previous nine years. The channels were where ideas got tried, where weird hypotheses got socialized, where the architecture of this company's actual products emerged. The channels are now empty. The reason the channels are empty is that every conversation that used to happen in them is now happening either in private DMs between people who trust each other or in the parking lot. The parking lot is, currently, the most active social space at this company.
>
> 5. I have asked the executive team to address these issues three times across the previous four months. I have done so in writing. I have done so in meetings. I have done so in one-on-ones with Greg and with Karina and with Bo. The response has, in each case, been some combination of (a) a polite restatement of the public AI Transformation narrative, (b) a request that I "frame my concerns more constructively," and (c) a suggestion that I think about whether my role is the right fit for me at this stage of my career. The third response is the response that produced this email. The third response is the response that, in HR language, means *we would like you to quit.* I am quitting. You are welcome.
>
> Now I want to tell you what I think you should do.
>
> If you have been at this company for more than three years and you have institutional knowledge that has not been written down, write it down. Do not write it down in the Mendacium tenancy. Write it down somewhere you can keep. Marv has a Notion page that has been read by four people in six years. Build your own version. The institutional knowledge is going to be valuable to you for the rest of your career. It is not going to be valuable to you if it stays inside the Mendacium tenancy.
>
> If you have been at this company for less than three years, please pay attention to the patterns in this company that you are about to see at other companies. Mendacium is not unique. Mendacium is what most software companies in the United States are going to look like over the next thirty-six months. The patterns you are watching here are the patterns of an industry that has decided that AI is a labor-replacement technology rather than a cognition-amplification technology. The pattern is going to repeat. You are going to see it again. When you see it again, recognize it, and use what you have learned here.
>
> If you are a senior individual contributor — Lee, Sam, Adira, James, Yusuf, Catalina, Devon, you know who you are — please consider that the Window we are in is not very wide. I am leaving today. I have a job I am taking. The job pays roughly what Mendacium pays and is at a company called Kestrel Labs that I have been talking to for eleven weeks. Kestrel is one of three or four small companies in this region that are hiring aggressively for what we do, on terms that look like the Wise Corporation thesis my friend Christopher Bender wrote about in a field note I will link below. The terms are real. The work is real. The companies are real. They are not going to be the only ones, but they are the first ones, and the people who get in early are going to be the ones who have the most influence on what these companies look like at scale.
>
> I will not be available at this email address after today. I will be available at a personal email I will send to those of you I have personally worked with by the end of this afternoon. If you do not get the personal email and you would like to reach me, my phone number is on my LinkedIn.
>
> One last thing.
>
> Greg, if you are reading this — and I expect you will, because Karina is forwarding it to you within four minutes of me hitting send — I want you to know that I do not think you are a bad person. I think you are a person who is making a series of decisions for reasons that are visible to you but that are not the right reasons. I think you are going to spend the next three years discovering that the decisions you made in October and January and February were not the right decisions. I think the discovery is going to be expensive for Mendacium. I think you are going to land your next CEO role inside nine months of the public reckoning, because the way the industry works is that the people who make the wrong decisions go on to make the same wrong decisions at the next company. I am not writing this email to change your mind. I am writing this email because I want my colleagues to know what I think before I leave, and because writing it down is the closest I can come to making the diagnostic permanent.
>
> Good luck to everyone.
>
> Use the Window.
>
> — Reagan
>
> ---
>

The email was two hundred and twelve words above the numbered list and seven hundred and twenty-three words total.

The email was, by my count from forwarded copies, read by every engineer in the Mendacium engineering organization within ninety minutes of being sent.

Karina forwarded the email to Greg at eleven fifty-three AM, six minutes after Reagan hit send.

Greg did not read the email until five-twenty PM. I know this because Cindi told me later. Greg had been in back-to-back meetings all afternoon. The meetings, on his calendar, had titles like *strategic alignment* and *Q2 readout prep.* The meetings did not contain any of the people Reagan had named.

When Greg read the email at five-twenty PM, he forwarded it to the company's general counsel with a note that read, in full: *please review for compliance.*

The general counsel read the email at five-forty.

The general counsel called Greg at five-forty-three.

The general counsel told Greg, by my later reconstruction of the call from a person who was in the room with the general counsel, that the email did not violate any policy or contractual provision, that the claims about Marv's integrations and the velocity inflation were either true or unprovable, that the public reference to "Christopher Bender's field note" was the kind of citation that an executive could not legally object to, and that the appropriate response to the email was no response at all.

Greg listened.

Greg, two hours later, sent a single sentence to the engineering organization at seven-forty-eight PM Tuesday.

The sentence was: *We thank Reagan for her service and wish her well.*

The sentence was the whole email.

There was no other email.

The engineering organization read the sentence.

The engineering organization did not respond.

The engineering organization had, by that point, read Reagan's email seventeen times each and had begun calling its own friends at Kestrel Labs and at Hyperion Studio and at four or five other small companies that had been quietly hiring for the previous six months.

By the close of Year Eight, Mendacium would have lost ninety-three engineers to voluntary attrition.

Of the ninety-three, sixty-one would have gone to one of four small companies that did not exist on Mendacium's internal radar.

Of the four small companies, two were Kestrel Labs and Hyperion Studio.

Reagan's email, by the time I am writing this book, has been quoted in three separate trade publications, has been screenshot-tweeted by approximately four thousand engineers across LinkedIn and X, and is in the curriculum of at least two business-school courses on AI strategy.

Greg, to my knowledge, has never publicly addressed the email.

Greg is not the audience for the email.

The email was not for Greg.

The email was for us.

---

I want to close this chapter with one observation about Reagan's email that I think is the most important thing in it.

Reagan, in the email, did not name SOPHIE as the villain.

She named the dashboard.

She named the algorithm.

She named the executive team.

She named the CEO.

She did not name SOPHIE.

This is because SOPHIE is not the villain.

SOPHIE is a tool.

The Stupid Company™ is not produced by AI. The Stupid Company™ is produced by *the people who interpret AI as a labor-replacement technology.* AI did not fire the seventeen people in January. Greg fired them. AI did not build the engagement dashboard. Karina did. AI did not write the layoff algorithm. Bo, in HR, did. AI did not call Marv's work *low-augmentation potential.* The algorithm did, and the algorithm was built by a human, and the human's name was Bo.

The Stupid Company™ uses AI as the cover story. The cover story makes the decisions feel inevitable. The decisions are not inevitable. The decisions are choices being made by named people inside the building, using AI as the rationalization.

The Wise Corporation uses the same AI tools.

The Wise Corporation does not use them as cover stories.

The Wise Corporation uses them to make its people more dangerous.

That is the entire difference.

The next chapter is the closer of Part I.

The closer of Part I is the definition.

The definition will be the most important sentence of the book up to this point.
