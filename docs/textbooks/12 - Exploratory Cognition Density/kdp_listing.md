## Book Description (Distribution copy)

**A tribute to Scott Adams. Your boss bought an AI. You can guess what happens next.**

In 1995, an engineer named Scott Adams was laid off by Pacific Bell. He went home and went full-time on a comic strip about an engineering organization run by a man with two pointy hairs. The strip ran for thirty-five years and became the most-read corporate satire in American history. The corporate world has, since 1995, been quietly aware that it lost Scott Adams. The corporate world has not been aware that *the way it lost him is the same way it loses everyone like him.*

This book is about that loss.

In the open-source non-commercial parody-law tradition, and as an explicit tribute to Adams while he is still able to receive it, **this book uses the Dilbert characters by name.** The Pointy-Haired Boss is in this book. Dilbert is in this book. Wally is in this book — with a twist Adams never had the panel count to show. Alice is in this book; the book gives her the resignation letter Adams never had the room to let her send. Asok is in this book. Catbert is in this book. Dogbert is in this book, although Dogbert is in this book as the internal vendor name for an AI product called SOPHIE, which is the joke. The characters appear in their canonical roles, in their canonical register, in a present-tense year (2026) on the assignment Adams was the first to identify, which is the documentation of corporate dysfunction by a person whose job it was, once, to live inside it.

The book teaches, in the Adams register:

- A diagnostic that lets you tell whether you work at the Stupid Company™ or at the Wise Corporation in nine minutes (Chapter 16)

- Scott Adams's Skill Stack framework, applied to the AI era, with sixteen-and-a-half specific skill pairings against the AI capabilities they pair against (Chapter 13)

- A twelve-question brutal diagnostic for whether you specifically are being replaced (Chapter 14)

- Marv's (Wally's) Parking Lot Method — seven rules for reading what your company is actually doing through the geometry of its parking lot (Chapter 7)

- Alice's resignation letter, in full, on the page (Chapter 9)

- The two-sentence resignation letter for quitting on a Tuesday (Chapter 20)

- The job interview questions Hyperion Studio uses to hire — the Wise Corporation interview Dilbert always wanted to take (Chapter 21, 26)

- The Pacific Bell case study, which is the closer the book has been building toward, which is the chapter Adams himself never got to write because the strip's job was the joke and the closer is, gently, the eulogy for the corporate world that made the wrong call

**The Stupid Company™** uses AI to fire people.

**The Wise Corporation** uses AI to make the people it already has dangerously creative.

The two companies look identical from the outside for about three years. After three years, one of them is on the cover of *Fast Company* and the other is in a different section of the *Wall Street Journal,* which is the section about bankruptcy filings.

**This book teaches you, in thirty short and unreasonably honest chapters, how to:**

- Diagnose whether you currently work at the Stupid Company™ or the Wise Corporation, using eleven warning signs that most readers will recognize in their own employer by chapter four.

- Apply Scott Adams's Skill Stack framework to the AI era — what skills stack well right now, what skills do not, and which uncounted skills in your own background you should be putting on your resume.

- Run the brutal twelve-question diagnostic in Chapter 14 that tells you whether you, specifically, are being replaced. (Then run it again as a stranger, because the first time you will protect yourself from the answer.)

- Read the parking lot the way Marv read it. Read the dashboard the way Sam read it. Read the Slack-channel pulse the way every honest engineer reads it.

- Quit on a Tuesday at eleven-thirty AM with a two-sentence letter, clean transition, and no damage to your reputation capital.

- Interview at a Wise Corporation, where the four-hour interview will contain zero coding questions, zero behavioral prompts, and one question — about a book you read last year — that you will not stop thinking about for years.

- Build your own *parallel filing cabinet* — the personal documentation of institutional knowledge that is, by the author's accounting, the single most valuable artifact he took with him when he left his previous job.

- Understand why play has economic value, why your boss has been wrong about *goofing off* for the previous ten years, and why the Friday afternoon Bad Ideas Hour at your old company produced more shipped value than every formal planning meeting combined.

**Featuring:**

- Greg Tannenbaum, the Pointy-Haired Boss with three pairs of identical glasses and a sailboat he calls "thirty-eight feet of trouble" that is actually twenty-six feet.

- SOPHIE, the AI tool with a Slack handle who may or may not be self-aware.

- Marv Donaldson, who has been predicting every strategic decision at his Stupid Company™ for nineteen years by watching the parking lot.

- Reagan Cho, whose two-hundred-and-twelve-word resignation letter has, by this writing, been screenshot-tweeted by approximately four thousand engineers.

- Sam Park, whose two-year spare-time side project became, eight weeks after he left the Stupid Company™, the foundational product of a Wise Corporation.

- Hyperion Studio, the company that does not have a conference room with a door that closes, because *the door is the asset.*

**For:**

Software engineers. Product managers. Designers. Senior individual contributors. Mid-career professionals who suspect their company is about to do something they have a bad feeling about. Anyone who has ever read a memo containing the phrase *productivity multiplier* and felt their stomach drop. Anyone who has ever been told that the Friday afternoon hack session was *goofing off.* Anyone who suspects they have been right about their boss for years.

**The companion book to *Carry: A Future Case Study* (Volume 11), which dramatized the platform pivot from the founder's side.** *Exploratory Cognition Density* dramatizes it from the worker's side. The two books are companion volumes. Either one stands alone.

The book closes on the question the source field note closed on:

*Which company made the better decision: the Stupid Company™ or the Wise Corporation?*

The book answers the question across thirty chapters, then gives the reader the satisfying privilege of saying it out loud themselves.

*We'll let you decide.*

## Cover concept (for cover designer brief)

A cinematic photograph of a whiteboard in an office, covered in lateral-thinking doodles, arrows, scribbled phrases, half-erased ideas, and a single small clean bar graph in the lower-right corner that is going up and to the right. The doodles include: a small octopus, a Haiku written in three lines, a pop-song verse-chorus structure diagram, the words *what if Dr. Seuss*, and the phrase *exploratory cognition density* underlined twice. The whiteboard is photographed at a slight angle from the side, with the office's window light catching the markers. The image evokes the cover of *How to Fail at Almost Everything and Still Win Big* in its texture and color palette while being clearly its own thing.
