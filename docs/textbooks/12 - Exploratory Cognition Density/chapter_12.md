# Chapter 12: Top Twenty-Five Percent Is Enough

There is a version of the AI era in which the labor market is ruled by a few hundred people who are world-class at one thing.

That version is the version the venture capitalists are betting on.

That version is wrong.

I want to explain why.

---

The argument for *world-class at one thing* goes like this. AI commoditizes everything that is in the middle of the distribution. The middle gets squeezed. The top of the distribution survives because the top is genuinely scarce. Therefore, the argument continues, the labor market of the future is going to consist of a small elite of world-class specialists, augmented by AI, while everyone else gets reduced to AI-supervised contract work or worse.

This argument has been published in approximately seven hundred Substacks across the previous twenty-four months.

The argument has one structural problem.

The problem is that *world-class at one thing* is a status that AI commoditizes faster than the middle of the distribution.

Let me show you why.

If you are world-class at one thing, you are by definition operating in a narrow specialty where the gap between you and the median practitioner is large enough to be observable. The gap is observable because the skill is well-defined. The skill is well-defined because there is a clear evaluation function for it. There is a clear evaluation function for it because the skill has been measured and ranked for a long time.

AI loves a clear evaluation function.

AI improves fastest on skills with clear evaluation functions, because the evaluation function is the training signal. The skills that have been measured and ranked for decades — chess, Go, code, certain kinds of writing, certain kinds of medical-image diagnosis, certain kinds of legal research — are the skills on which AI has already matched or exceeded the top of the human distribution.

The top of the human distribution, on those skills, is no longer the safest place to be.

The top of the human distribution, on those skills, is the place where AI has already arrived.

The middle of the distribution, on those same skills, is the place where AI is currently arriving.

The bottom of the distribution, on those same skills, will be reached by AI inside three to seven years.

If you are world-class at *one* thing, AI is at your level or above your level on that thing, depending on the year you are reading this book. AI is not going to be at the level of a *combination* of three or four uncorrelated skills for somewhere between fifteen and forty years, depending on how you count *combination* and how you count *level.* The combinatorial space is large enough that AI cannot, in the planning horizon of any career currently underway, fully cover it.

This is the argument.

The argument is the inverse of the *world-class at one thing* argument.

The argument is: **the rarest combinatorial positions are the safest positions in the AI era, because the combinatorial space is the part of the labor market AI is structurally bad at filling.**

---

Let me make this concrete.

Suppose you are world-class at radiology image interpretation. You are the best radiologist in the country. You earn, in 2026, somewhere between eight hundred thousand and two million dollars a year. You are at the absolute top of your field.

AI image-interpretation models, in 2026, are at approximately the level of a median radiologist on most cases and at the level of a top-five-percent radiologist on common cases. The models are improving at approximately twenty to thirty percent per year on benchmark accuracy. The models will, by 2030, be at the level of the best radiologist in the country on routine cases.

By 2032, the highest-leverage use case for an elite radiologist is going to be the small subset of cases the AI cannot handle. Those cases are going to be a small subset of the total volume. The total revenue available to the elite radiologist is going to drop.

This is not a prediction I am making with confidence. This is a prediction that is in the mid-range of the projections published in the actual medical and AI literature in 2025 and 2026.

Now suppose, instead, that you are a radiologist who is *also* in the top twenty-five percent at:

1. Patient communication.

2. Reading the operations side of a small medical practice.

3. Plain-English writing.

4. Designing and running clinical workflows.

You are not the best radiologist in the country. You are a competent radiologist who happens to also be a competent communicator, a competent operator, and a competent writer.

The AI model that replaces the world-class radiologist does not replace you. The AI model that replaces the world-class radiologist *makes you more valuable*, because the AI handles the routine reads, and your time gets redirected toward the four skills the AI does not do — the patient conversations, the practice operations, the patient-education materials you write, the workflow design that lets the practice handle three times the volume per physician.

You become, in operational terms, the person who runs the practice in the AI era.

The world-class radiologist becomes the person who reads the small subset of edge cases the AI cannot handle.

The compensation curves cross at some point between 2028 and 2033.

After they cross, the Skill Stack radiologist makes more money than the world-class radiologist. The Skill Stack radiologist also has more job satisfaction, more autonomy, more upside, and a more durable position.

This is the argument.

The argument is not new. The argument is the same argument Adams made in 2014. The only thing that has changed is that AI has dramatically sharpened the argument's edges. AI has not invalidated the Skill Stack thesis. AI has *vindicated* it.

---

I want to address a counter-argument I have heard from readers of the field-note version of this thesis.

The counter-argument is: *but what if AI also gets good at the combinations?*

AI will get better at the combinations across the next several decades. AI is going to compress the combinatorial space slowly across the long term. This is a real effect.

But two things blunt the effect.

The first thing is that the combinatorial space is *enormous*. There are, just within the skills covered in this book, approximately two hundred named skills that an individual professional could plausibly stack. The number of unique five-skill stacks across those two hundred skills is on the order of two hundred thousand. The number of *useful* five-skill stacks is smaller, but still in the tens of thousands. Each useful stack is its own little labor-market niche. AI compresses niches one at a time. The compression is slow.

The second thing is that the *number of stacks AI can simultaneously cover is bounded by training data.* Each unique skill stack requires its own training distribution. The training data does not exist for most stacks. The training data is expensive to generate. The training data is, in many cases, never going to be generated because the stacks are too narrow to justify the cost.

The Skill Stack survives the AI era for the same reason that *small restaurants* survive the era of food delivery apps. The apps can replace the chain restaurants because the chain restaurants are templated. The apps cannot replace the small family restaurant on the corner because the small family restaurant is *not templated* — it is the intersection of one specific family's specific recipes and the specific community they cook for. The intersection is too narrow to template profitably.

Your Skill Stack is the small family restaurant.

The world-class radiologist is the chain restaurant.

You can see how this is going to go.

---

I want to give you, in a brief sidebar, **Hauschild's Second Law.**

**Hauschild's Second Law:**

*The most durable position in any AI-affected labor market is the position that requires three or more uncorrelated skills, no one of which is at world-class level.*

This is the corollary to Hauschild's First Law from Chapter 2. The First Law was about how to identify the Stupid Company™. The Second Law is about how to be durable in any market, including the markets of companies that are not Stupid Companies™.

I am calling it Hauschild's Second Law because, again, this is my book.

---

I want to close this chapter with one observation that I think is, in operational terms, the most useful observation I can give you.

When you are listing your own skills, *you are going to underrate the boring ones.*

You are going to underrate them because they are boring. You are going to skip them because they do not feel like skills. You are going to think the things that count are the things that have certifications attached to them, or the things that pay extra in your salary band, or the things that show up on a resume in their own line.

This is wrong.

The most valuable skills in a Skill Stack are, almost always, the ones that nobody else thought to count. *Patient communication* does not have a certification. *Recognizing institutional dysfunction* does not have a certification. *Managing your own anxiety in a career transition* does not have a certification. None of these are taught in the bootcamps. None of these have a Coursera course. None of these will appear in a job description.

All three of them are part of my own Skill Stack.

All three of them are what the AI labor market is going to pay people like me to bring into the room.

When you do your own Skill Stack inventory — and I am going to give you the worksheet in Chapter 19 — pay specific attention to the skills you have not been counting. The uncounted skills are, in nine cases out of ten, the most valuable skills you have.

The Wise Corporation hires for the uncounted skills.

The Stupid Company™ hires for the certifications.

The two companies, again, will look identical for about three years.

After three years they begin to diverge.

We will get to the divergence in Part III.
