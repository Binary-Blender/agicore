# Carry: A Future Case Study

**Author:** Christopher Bender
**Publisher:** AI WIN-WIN Institute
**Volume:** 11 (Textbook Shelf)
**Genre:** Business thriller (Liar's Poker / Flash Boys / Bad Blood / Phoenix Project lineage — close third, past tense, kinetic register)
**Length target:** ~65,000 words, 14 chapters, 4 acts
**Companion to:** *Anchor* (Vol VI), *Multiplied* (Vol VII), *Untied* (Vol VIII). *Carry* completes the AI WIN-WIN business-fable quartet with the platform-pivot at industrial scale, rendered in the kinetic register the subject demanded.

---

## Concept

Cole Westerlund built Carrick, Inc. in his converted Capitol Hill townhouse in 1995, when he was twenty-nine years old. He had quit a New York hedge fund six weeks earlier with eleven thousand dollars in the bank, four employees signed on against verbal promises, and a thesis that books were the wedge into a category nobody else had named yet. Thirty-one years later the company does seven hundred and twenty billion dollars of revenue in seventy-three countries and Cole's net worth is, on the morning the book opens, two hundred and fourteen billion dollars, which makes him the third-richest person alive.

He is also, in October of Year 1, ten days away from writing the most consequential single document any commerce CEO has ever written.

The reason is what he saw on the deck of the *Coelacanth* on a Wednesday afternoon in late September, six hundred and twenty miles southwest of Guam, when his satellite phone rang and the friend on the other end of the line said the words that gave the book its first line.

The friend was a small-business owner. She had been selling on Carrick's marketplace for eight years. She was, that Wednesday, watching Carrick's own private-label brand undercut her bestselling product at fifty-seven percent of her cost, having used her sales data to time the launch within forty-eight hours of her seasonal peak. She was three weeks from closing the business. She had called Cole personally because the form letters she had received from his seller-support team for nine months had been, in her words, the kind of correspondence she had not been willing to believe a company Cole had built would put its name on.

Cole listened to her. He did not interrupt. He told her he would call her back inside forty-eight hours. He hung up. He stood on the deck of the research vessel in the long Pacific afternoon light and did not move for what the *Coelacanth*'s chief scientist later told her grandchildren was eleven minutes. Then he walked into the bridge and asked the captain to turn the ship around.

The flight from Guam to Seattle is fifteen hours. Cole used eight of them to read the Carrick marketplace's private-label competitive program from the inside, on the encrypted laptop he had carried onto the helicopter at the deck pad of the *Coelacanth*. He used three more to walk the financial model his CFO had been running in parallel for the previous four months. He used the last four to write the first draft of the document that, on the following Monday morning at six-fifteen Pacific, would land in one point six million Carrick inboxes simultaneously.

The document was eleven pages. The subject line was *The Piranhas Are Coming.* The opening sentence was: *I have been wrong about who our customer is.*

The book is what happens next.

## What the Microsoft analog gets you

This is the Bill Gates *Internet Tidal Wave* memo, but for AI commoditization at industrial commerce scale, written by a founder who saw the boil before it broke. Gates wrote the Tidal Wave memo on May 26, 1995, after one weekend of reading and one week of watching Netscape ship. He turned Microsoft on a dime. He bet the company on the new thesis. He was right.

Cole Westerlund, in October of Year 1, does the same thing on the same compressed timeline against a threat that was less obvious to the rest of the market — the AI-piranha threat, where AI tooling allows eleven million small sellers to leave the platform and run their own marketplaces, and where Carrick's retail business, sixty-four percent of the company's revenue, has roughly thirty months before the math turns terminal. Cole reads the boil before the boil breaks. He acts. The book is the consequences of acting.

The book is *not* a contemplative business fable. The book is what happens when a founder with two hundred billion dollars, a security detail, a deadlift max of four-twenty-five, and a thirty-one-year track record of being right about the math turns the largest commerce company in the world by writing one memo on a Friday night in his study at home.

## Voice and register

**Close third, past tense, kinetic.** Lewis (*Liar's Poker*, *Flash Boys*), Carreyrou (*Bad Blood*), Stone (*The Everything Store*) — the thriller register applied to corporate strategy. No interior rumination. No "He thought about Phillip Garber." Sensory and operational precision: brand names, model numbers, weights, distances, exact dollar figures, time stamps. Dialogue moves the plot. Action verbs. Short paragraphs.

**The reader Henry would have been at fourteen, reading this.** A Gen-X kid who watched *Top Gun* in a movie theater in 1986, played *Doom* on a Pentium in 1994, read Snow Crash the year it came out, and now reads Lewis at airport bookstores between consulting gigs. That is the reader. The voice serves him.

**No fabular beats.** No authorial-narrator paragraphs at chapter closes. The chapters end where the action ends. The book ends where the action ends.

**Set pieces over reflection.** Every chapter has at least one concrete operational set piece that drives the story forward. The set pieces are real — the memo, the boardroom vote, the stock crash, the steakhouse meet with the activist, the first major Carry customer signing, the warehouse conversion, the antitrust dinner with the senators, the leak crisis, the earnings call, the ESOP handover, the Coelacanth dive.

## Chapter-by-chapter

### Act 1: The Boil (Ch 1-4, ~17,000 words)

**Chapter 1: The Boil** (Cole + brief Iona POV, ~4,200 words)
The *Coelacanth*, six hundred and twenty miles southwest of Guam, late September Year 1. Cole on the deck with Bear in the morning, working a thirty-minute conditioning circuit on the helipad in the Pacific sun. The satphone in Mara's hand rings at eleven-forty local. Mara walks it to him. The caller is Iona Tran. She tells him about the Echo line, the Carrick Basics clone, the form letters, the three weeks she has before she closes the business. Cole listens. He tells her he will call back inside forty-eight hours. He hangs up. He stands at the rail. The *Coelacanth* is a research vessel; it does not turn around for one phone call. He asks the captain to turn around. The captain turns around.

Cole on the helicopter pad at sunset, the Sikorsky S-92 lifting him to Guam. Mara in the seat across from him. Cole reading on the encrypted laptop the entire transit. The boil. The first chapter ends with Cole on the Gulfstream out of Guam, the wheels lifting at 0140 local, fifteen hours to Boeing Field.

**Chapter 2: The Read** (Anjali POV, ~4,000 words)
The same Friday, Seattle time. Anjali Rao on the floor of the Kent fulfillment center at six twenty-two in the morning. Walking the inbound dock. The robots are at the speed they have been at for three years. The moat is filled in. Anjali knows it. She has known it for fourteen months. She has been waiting for Cole to know it. She gets a text from Mara at seven-eleven that reads *On approach to Boeing. ETA 0810. Cole would like you in his office at 0900.* She reads the text twice. She goes home. She showers. She drives to the office.

The scene in Cole's office at nine. Cole behind the desk. Anjali across from him. Cole tells her about Iona Tran. He tells her about the four months of work he has been doing alone. He tells her what he is going to do. He asks her if she can execute it on the ground. She says yes. They go to lunch at the Carrick cafeteria. Owen Friedlander joins them. The platform-pivot model is on the table.

**Chapter 3: The Walk** (Cole POV, ~4,500 words)
The following Tuesday. Cole undercover in Iona Tran's shop on NE Alberta Street in Portland — Carrick baseball cap, hoodie, a four-day stubble, contact lenses instead of glasses. Mara and one operator in a sedan a block south. Cole in the shop for forty minutes. Watches Iona run the studio. Watches a customer ask Iona about the Echo and watches Iona, without flinching, point the customer to the cheaper Carrick Basics version on the shelf at the front of the studio that Iona has been carrying as a way of converting the foot traffic Carrick has been taking from her into at least the eight percent margin of carrying the competitor's product. Cole watches Iona do this twice.

He buys an Echo bud vase. He pays in cash. He thanks her. He walks out. On the sidewalk on Alberta he stops for a moment. He has not done this — walked into a small business as the customer Cole had been when he was twenty-nine — in twenty-three years.

He calls Mara. *Mara. We are going back to Seattle. I want the encrypted laptop. I want the study cleared by the time I get to the house. I want nobody on the property tonight who is not security.* He gets in the car. They drive.

**Chapter 4: The Memo** (Cole POV, ~4,500 words)
Friday night at the Bainbridge house. Cole alone in the study from six-twenty PM Friday to four-ten AM Saturday. Annika in the house, working on the Bellevue model, passes the study door at one AM and does not knock. He is writing. The memo runs eleven pages. The set piece of the chapter is the writing of the memo — paragraph by paragraph, page by page, the document that is going to turn the company on a dime forming under his hand on the encrypted laptop in the small leather chair by the window. The reader sees the memo emerge. The reader reads the key paragraphs as Cole writes them. The opening sentence — *I have been wrong about who our customer is* — appears in italics in the chapter, alone on its own line, twice: once as Cole writes it for the first time and once at four-oh-six AM when he reads the finished document straight through and sees the sentence land.

The chapter closes with Cole sending the memo at six-fifteen AM Monday — three nights of work later, after he has shown it to Anjali, Owen, Margot Halloran, and to nobody else. The send is to the all-Carrick distribution list. One point six million inboxes in seventy-three countries. The send confirms. Cole stands up from the desk. He walks to the window. The Sound is the long horizontal pre-dawn light. He picks up his coffee. He drinks it.

He thinks: *now we find out who we are.*

End of Act 1.

### Act 2: The Strike (Ch 5-8, ~17,000 words)

**Chapter 5: The Boardroom** (Diana Marquez POV, ~4,200 words)
Tuesday morning, the day after the memo. Emergency board meeting on the eleventh floor of Carrick HQ. Nine directors in the room, two on a secure video link. Cole presents for thirty-eight minutes without slides. He lays out the AI-piranha framing, the ESOP transition, the full exit from retail, the timing. Diana Marquez, EVP Retail — whose division the memo is about to subordinate — sits across from him through the entire presentation. She does not move. She has read the memo eight times in the previous thirty-six hours. She has run her own math. She has called her father in San Diego the previous evening. Her father, who was put out of business by Walmart in 2003, has said one thing to her on the call: *Diana. You worked for the platform that crushed the small operator. Now you get to be the executive who hands the platform back. Do not be the one who fights the handing back. Be the one who runs it.*

Diana intervenes once, in the second hour, on operational execution language. She does not litigate the strategy. She knows what the strategy is. She is going to run the ESOP transition. Cole confirms her in the role at the close of the meeting. The vote is eight to two, with one abstention. Roland Vance votes no. Roland will resign within thirty days.

**Chapter 6: The Crash** (Owen Friedlander POV, ~4,200 words)
The two weeks following the announcement. The stock opens down twelve percent in the first three minutes of trading the morning the memo goes out and is down twenty-four by the close. By Day Six the stock is down twenty-eight. Owen Friedlander runs the institutional-shareholder outreach. The set piece of the chapter is the call Owen takes from Drew Sallinger on Day Five, the first conversation between the company and the activist who is about to take an $8B short position. Sallinger is polite. Sallinger is dangerous. Sallinger asks Owen one question: *Owen. Walk me through the founder-contribution mechanics on the ESOP.* Owen walks him through them. Sallinger thanks Owen. Sallinger hangs up. Sallinger files the 13D the following Monday.

Cole reads the 13D in the back of the Suburban on the way to the ferry. He calls Marcus — Owen — on the phone. *Owen. Set up the meeting. I want him in a room with me inside ten days. Off the books.* Owen sets up the meeting.

Roland Vance resigns from the board on Day Fourteen. The resignation letter is dignified. Cole accepts it. The board moves on with ten directors.

**Chapter 7: The Steakhouse** (Cole POV, ~4,500 words)
Second Friday in November. Cole on the Gulfstream from Boeing Field to Teterboro at eight-fifteen PM Pacific. Manifest sealed. Filed under a partner's name. Mara and a three-person detail. Wheels-down at Teterboro at midnight Eastern. Cars on the tarmac.

The meeting is in a private dining room above a steakhouse on East 47th Street that does not have a sign on the door. Drew Sallinger across the table. Sallinger's chief of staff in the room. Cole's general counsel, Priya Bhattacharya, in the room. No phones. The set piece of the chapter is the seventy-minute conversation, line by line. Cole lays out the antitrust carve-out brief from the subcommittee chair. Cole lays out the platform thesis. Cole lays out the consequence of Sallinger keeping the position open through the next four quarters. Sallinger listens. Sallinger drinks the water. Sallinger asks two questions. Cole answers both.

At the end of the meeting Sallinger says: *Cole. You used to be at Quine.* Cole says: *For eight years.* Sallinger says: *You learned this from Garber.* Cole says: *I learned everything from Garber.* Sallinger says: *Garber would have made this exact trip.* They shake hands.

Sallinger closes the position over the following six trading days. He never publicly explains why. The Wall Street Journal will describe the trade, eight months later, as the most disciplined retreat of Drew Sallinger's career. The Journal will not know about the room above the steakhouse.

Cole on the Gulfstream at one-forty AM Eastern. Wheels-down at Boeing Field at four-twenty AM Pacific. He is in the gym at five. Bear has the first set racked.

**Chapter 8: The First Customer** (Reese Okonkwo POV, ~4,000 words)
December through February. Reese building the Carrick Carry platform out from the inside. The seven products under one umbrella — Carry Fulfillment, Carry Logistics, Carry Customer Service, Carry AI Agents, Carry Identity, Carry Payments, Carry Returns. Reese hiring. Reese signing the first three small customers in January. Reese closing the first major contract in February — a 200-store regional department-store chain in the Midwest named Midcontinent Stores, $240M committed annual revenue across five years. Cole calls Reese at six-thirty PM Pacific the day the contract closes. *Reese. That was the proof.* Reese: *I know.* The stock moves up five percent on the announcement. The pivot turns the corner.

End of Act 2.

### Act 3: The Platform (Ch 9-12, ~17,000 words)

**Chapter 9: The Floor** (Anjali Rao POV with embedded Eric Salazar scenes, ~4,500 words)
February. The first wave of warehouse conversions. Anjali in Goodyear, Arizona, on the floor of the Goodyear fulfillment center, in her steel-toed boots and her fourteen-year-old vest, in the middle of the conversion week. Three hundred and twenty associates being laid off. Eight hundred being retained at a higher wage. The set piece is Anjali walking the floor at one AM with the night-shift general manager, then sitting with Eric Salazar — 28, married, two kids, six years at Carrick — in the break room at three AM and telling him that his wife (also at Carrick Goodyear, in a different role) is in the laid-off group and Eric is in the retained-at-higher-wage group. Eric absorbs it. He goes home at five. He sits at the kitchen table with his wife at six and they figure out the next year together. Anjali drives back to the hotel at six-thirty.

**Chapter 10: The Senator** (Cole POV, ~4,200 words)
March. Washington. Cole on the corporate jet to Dulles. Two senators and a Treasury undersecretary at a private dinner in Georgetown. Senator Lila Marsh runs the conversation. The breakup legislation has been moving in committee for two years. The carve-out for platform companies that maintain zero equity in marketplace tenants will be in the final draft. Carrick will be the first company through the safe harbor. The dinner runs ninety minutes. Cole flies back at one AM Eastern. He is at the Bainbridge house by five-thirty Pacific. The credible-threat section of the morning brief has grown by two entries.

**Chapter 11: The Crisis** (Cole POV, ~4,200 words)
April. The leak. An internal memo from Cole to Anjali about the Q2 ESOP-tranche pricing — a memo that contains material information that is not yet public — is leaked to Bloomberg by a junior member of the corporate-development team who has been positioning a personal trade. The leak is timed to crash the stock the day before the Q1 earnings call. The set piece is the forty-eight hours between the leak and the earnings call. Cole runs the entire response. Mara identifies the leaker by 2 PM Pacific the day the leak hits. Priya Bhattacharya brings the FBI in by 5 PM. The 8-K disclosure goes out at 6:30 PM. The stock recovers two percent in the after-hours. Cole does the earnings call the following morning. The stock closes up four percent on the day of the call.

The leaker is prosecuted. He cooperates. He gets two years' probation and a fine. The fund that took the position based on his tip pays a $94M penalty to the SEC. The Wall Street Journal calls Cole the cleanest CEO crisis response of the year.

**Chapter 12: The Earnings Call** (Reese Okonkwo POV, ~4,000 words)
Q1 of Year 3. The first earnings call since the pivot has started showing measurable results. Reese in the room with Cole, Anjali, Owen, Diana. Cole runs the call. Karen Yoshida from Morgan Stanley asks the second question — about the difference between the customer the 1997 letter named and the customer the pivot was actually for. Cole answers. Cole's answer is the chapter's set piece. The reader gets the full answer in dialogue. The stock closes up four percent on the day. The Wall Street Journal runs *The Pivot That Worked* on the Friday-morning front page.

End of Act 3.

### Act 4: The New World (Ch 13-14, ~10,000 words)

**Chapter 13: The Handover** (Diana Marquez POV, ~5,000 words)
Spring of Year 7. Diana Marquez at the headquarters of Carrick Retail Co. in Newark, New Jersey — the company has been headquartered separately from the parent since Year 2 — for the final ESOP-tranche close. The remaining twenty-two percent of the equity transfers to the trust at three-eighteen PM Eastern. Diana signs the certificate. She keeps the pen. The first worker-elected board takes its seats at the table at three-thirty. Diana presents the operating handover for two hours. She steps down as interim CEO at five PM. The new CEO is a former regional VP who has been at Carrick Retail for nineteen years. Diana hands her the keys. They embrace. Diana flies back to Seattle that night.

The set piece is the signing — twelve minutes in the boardroom with the trustee, the fiduciary committee, the new board, and Diana. The page is signed. The page is photographed. The photograph runs above the fold on the front page of the New York Times the following morning under the headline *The Largest Employee Ownership Transition in U.S. History Closed Yesterday.*

The chapter ends with Diana at Sea-Tac at midnight. She walks past the small ceramics shop in the C concourse that has a wheel-thrown bud vase in the window. She stops. The vase is from a studio called Lacquer & Cup, Portland, Oregon. She buys it. She takes it home.

**Chapter 14: The Coelacanth** (Cole POV with brief Iona Tran close, ~5,000 words)
Two weeks after the handover. Cole back on the *Coelacanth*, six hundred and twenty miles southwest of Guam, in the same waters he was in seven years earlier when Iona's call had come in. The vessel is ready for the fourth Mariana Trench dive. Bear is on board. Mara is on board. Annika is on board for the first dive of her life. The dive is scheduled for the following morning.

The set piece is the evening before the dive. Cole on the bridge with the captain at sunset. The Pacific is the same Pacific it was seven years ago. The light is the same light. The phone is on the bridge console. It does not ring.

Cole walks down to the aft deck. Annika is on the bench at the rail. He sits next to her. They do not talk for ten minutes. Then Annika says: *You did it.* Cole says: *We did it.* Annika says: *No, Cole. You did it. The we is for the rest of the world. I am telling you, alone, on this deck, on this night — you did it.*

He looks at her.

He says: *Thank you.*

They watch the light go out of the sky.

The chapter closes with a one-page scene in Iona Tran's studio in Portland on the same evening. Iona has just signed a wholesale contract with a boutique chain in eleven cities. The Echo line is in production again. Iona is closing the studio at six-fifty PM. She walks out onto Alberta Street into the May sunshine. She thinks about the call she made on the satphone seven years ago. She thinks about Cole on the deck. She does not know that he is on the deck again tonight. She locks the door. She goes home.

The book ends.

(End of novel.)
