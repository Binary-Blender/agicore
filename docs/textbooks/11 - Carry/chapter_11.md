# Chapter 11: The Earnings Call

Henry Castellan sat down behind the desk in his office on the eleventh floor at one minute before two o'clock Pacific time on a Thursday in late April of Year 3, looked across the room at the five people who were there with him, and waited for the operator to bring the analysts onto the line.

Daniela Park was on the couch on the south side of the room with the open laptop on her knees and the script flagged in three colors. Marcus Goldberg was next to her with the margin reconciliation in a binder he had been carrying for two days. Tomás Aguilar was in the chair by the window with his phone face-down on the side table the way Tomás always put his phone face-down before a serious meeting. Vivian Reyes was on the chair across from Tomás with the Argo Retail Inc. quarterly in her hand and her shoes off under the chair because Vivian, eighteen months into running the new subsidiary, had stopped pretending she was going to wear the heels through ninety minutes of sitting still. Frank Lattimore, the EVP of Argo Logistics, stood by the credenza with his arms folded. Frank had supported the pivot from the first board meeting and had been waiting, with a private and almost surgical patience, for this particular call to happen.

"Two minutes," Daniela said.

"All right."

"You have the numbers."

"I have the numbers."

"You have the framing."

"I have the framing."

"You have the Yoshida question."

"I have the Yoshida question."

Marcus looked up from his binder. "She's first in the queue."

"She would be."

"She would be."

The operator's voice came onto the line. The fifteen analysts and the institutional listeners were on. Daniela nodded at Henry. Henry nodded back. He pressed the unmute key on the console.

"Good afternoon," he said. "This is Henry Castellan. Thank you for joining the Q1 call for Argo Inc."

---

He read the prepared statement for eleven minutes.

He read it the way he had been reading prepared statements on quarterly calls for twenty-one years, which was to say flatly, without performance, with the cadence of a man who had been doing this long enough to trust the numbers to do the work. The numbers, in Q1 of Year 3, were the work.

Argo Cloud: revenue up forty-nine percent year over year.

Argo Carry: revenue up three hundred and eighteen percent year over year. He paused on that number, briefly, not for emphasis but because it was the number that mattered. Three hundred and eighteen percent off a base that two years earlier had been three hundred and forty million dollars and was now, on a trailing-twelve-month basis, a fundamentally different size.

Argo Retail Inc., the subsidiary: revenue down eleven percent year over year. Profitable for the first time in three years.

Total Argo revenue: up eight percent year over year. The first year of revenue growth since the announcement.

Operating margin: fourteen percent. Up from eight at the start of Year 1.

He did not editorialize. He did not narrate the pivot. He had spent the previous five quarterly calls narrating the pivot, in a tone that ranged from defensive in Q1 of Year 2 to plainly stubborn in Q3 of Year 2, and he had learned by Q4 that the analysts on these calls were not going to be persuaded by tone. They were going to be persuaded by Q1 of Year 3, or they were not going to be persuaded.

He turned the call over to questions at the eleven-minute mark.

The operator read the first name in the queue.

"Karen Yoshida, Morgan Stanley."

---

Karen Yoshida had covered Argo for eleven years.

She had been the senior tech-equity analyst at Morgan Stanley for the last seven of them. She was forty-six. She had a Princeton undergrad and a Stanford MBA and a reputation, inside the institutional community, for being the analyst whose models were the cleanest and whose downgrades were the rarest. When Karen Yoshida moved a rating, the rest of the Street paid attention.

She had moved her rating on Argo from BUY to HOLD eleven days after the pivot announcement.

She had moved it from HOLD to SELL six weeks later, on the morning the stock hit its low.

She had been quoted in the Wall Street Journal in March of Year 2, on a piece about the pivot's prospects, as saying that she did not see a path to revenue stability within the announced timeline. She had been specific. She had named the operational risk. She had named the customer-acquisition risk. She had named the competitive-platform risk. She had been, in retrospect, wrong about the timeline by approximately four quarters.

She had upgraded the stock from SELL to HOLD in October of Year 2.

She had upgraded it from HOLD to BUY in February of Year 3, three weeks before the Q4 earnings call.

Now she was first in the queue on Q1.

Henry knew all of this. He had read every Yoshida note for eleven years. He had a folder in his email called *Yoshida* in which he saved them. He had read her March-of-Year-2 piece three times, the second and third times with a pen, in the months when the pivot was hardest. He did not hold it against her. He had been, in the morning hours of those months, asking himself the same questions she had been asking in print.

Karen's voice came onto the line. It was a low, level, professional voice. She did not waste analyst questions on pleasantries and she was not going to now.

"Henry. Thank you. Two questions, if I may."

"Of course."

"The first is operational. On the Argo Carry growth — three hundred and eighteen percent year over year — I want to understand the customer-acquisition curve. You added, on my read, approximately nine hundred enterprise customers in the trailing twelve months. Of those, what percentage came through the unified-platform offering versus the legacy single-service offerings."

It was a working analyst's question. It was specific. It was answerable. Henry let Tomás take it. Tomás walked her through the numbers — seventy-three percent through the unified offering, twenty-one percent through single-service entry points that had then expanded to two or more services within nine months, six percent single-service standalone. Karen took notes. Henry could hear, on the line, the small dry sound of a pen moving on paper.

Then she asked the second question.

She asked it without changing her tone. She asked it the way she had asked the first one.

"Henry. The second question. The transition from a retail-dominant operating model to a platform-dominant operating model is a transition that, in the academic literature on platform economics, is typically completed by founders only with great difficulty. There are very few examples at scale. There are fewer examples at the scale Argo has executed it. I am asking this question as somebody who, in the second quarter of Year One, did not see how the timeline you announced was achievable. I would like to ask you, with the benefit of hindsight that all of us on this call now have, what you believe the decision was that made the announced timeline achievable."

The room was quiet.

Henry was looking at the console. He was not looking at any of the five people in the room with him. He could see, on his peripheral vision, Daniela's hand stop moving on her keyboard.

There was no gloating available in this room. There was no version of the answer that was about Karen having been wrong. Karen knew Karen had been wrong. She had said so, in a sentence that did not use the word, by acknowledging that she had not seen how the timeline was achievable. She had not apologized. She had not had to. She had asked a question that was, in operational terms, a question about what to learn from the eighteen months that had just happened, and that was, in dignified terms, the largest concession that an analyst of Karen Yoshida's standing could make on a public earnings call.

Henry took a breath.

"Karen. Thank you for the question. The decision was the decision to commit to the platform identity in the public communication on day one. We could have phased the announcement. We could have soft-launched the strategy. We could have positioned Argo Carry as a complement to Argo Retail and let the market figure out, over twenty-four months, that the complement was going to become the core. We considered all three of those paths. We chose not to take any of them. We chose to tell the market on day one what we were doing and why, and to take the stock movement that came with it. The stock movement was real. It was difficult. It was also, in retrospect, the price of the timeline being achievable. If we had phased the announcement we would have phased the internal execution. If we had phased the internal execution we would have given the organization permission to treat the pivot as optional. We did not have time for the pivot to be optional. We took the cost up front. We are now living on the other side of it."

He paused.

"That is the operational answer. The personal answer is that I was very fortunate to have a board chair, a chief operating officer, a chief financial officer, an EVP of Cloud, an EVP of Retail, an EVP of Logistics, and a number of other senior leaders in this company who, when they understood what the math required, did not relitigate the math. They did the work. I want to be on the record on this call as saying that the achievable timeline was achievable because of them. Not because of me."

Karen was quiet on the line.

Then she said: "Thank you, Henry."

"Thank you, Karen."

The operator went to the next name.

---

He answered fourteen more questions.

The questions were the questions a working analyst community asked on a quarter when the numbers had moved. Operating-margin sustainability. Capital-expenditure trajectory for Argo Carry. The Argo Retail Inc. subsidiary's path to revenue stability. The competitive response from Walmart's own platform-services initiative, which had been announced six weeks earlier and which the Street was still trying to model. The currency exposure on the seventeen-country Argo Carry international footprint. The implications of the Senate antitrust subcommittee's most recent staff report, which Priya Bhattacharya had spent four months engaging with and which had landed, in March, as a constructive document that read the pivot's structural separation between platform and tenant as an answer to several of the questions the subcommittee had been asking for six years.

Henry took the first nine questions. Tomás took two on Argo Carry. Marcus took two on the financial mechanics. Vivian took one on the subsidiary. Frank took one on logistics.

The call ran ninety minutes.

At the end of it Henry said, in the same flat voice he had opened with, *Thank you all for joining. We will see you next quarter.*

He pressed the mute button.

He pressed the disconnect button.

He sat in the chair for a moment.

Then he stood up and looked at the five people in the room.

"All right," he said.

Daniela closed her laptop. "All right."

Marcus closed his binder.

Tomás picked up his phone and turned it face up.

Vivian put her shoes back on.

Frank Lattimore, by the credenza, smiled. It was the first time Henry had seen Frank smile in a room since the announcement. Frank smiled rarely. When he smiled, it counted.

"Henry," Frank said.

"Yes."

"That was the call."

"Yes."

"Yoshida."

"I know."

"She was clean."

"She was."

"You were clean."

"I tried."

"You were clean."

The five of them filed out of the office over the next ninety seconds, one at a time, with the small ritual handshakes and shoulder squeezes that Henry had not seen in this office in three years. Daniela was the last out. At the door she turned.

"Henry."

"Yes."

"Stock is up two percent in the after-hours."

"All right."

"It'll keep moving."

"All right."

"Go home."

"I will."

She left.

---

The stock closed the next day four percent above the prior close.

The Wall Street Journal ran the story on the front page of the Friday-morning paper. The story was under the byline of a reporter named Adelaide Krug, who had covered Argo for fourteen years and who had written the most skeptical of the long pieces that ran in Year 1. Her piece was titled *The Pivot That Worked.*

The piece was fair.

It named the casualties. Three thousand five hundred households lost an income in the first conversion wave. Eighty-four thousand positions eliminated across the Argo Retail workforce reduction. The brand spinoff. Walter Brandt's resignation from the board. The institutional shareholders who had publicly demanded Henry's resignation and had, in the months since, quietly closed their positions. The activist hedge funds who had taken positions in November of Year 1 and had exited at a loss in Q3 of Year 2.

It named the result. Argo was healthier than it had been in five years. Margins were the highest they had been since 2019. The platform offering was the fastest-growing enterprise-software stack in the country. The customer roster included eleven of the top twenty American retailers. The subsidiary was profitable.

The piece closed with a sentence Henry read three times that morning at the kitchen table while Marina watched him without saying anything.

*Argo Inc., in the spring of Year 3, is more useful to American commerce than the old one was.*

He folded the paper.

He set it down on the counter.

He looked at Marina.

She was in the chair across the table in a sweater she had owned for a decade, drinking tea, looking at him in the way she had looked at him on the morning after the announcement and on the morning after Walter Brandt resigned and on the morning the stock had hit its low.

"Henry."

"Yes."

"You're going to want to go in."

"I'm going to go in."

"It's Friday."

"I'm going to go in."

She smiled.

"Go in. Come home for dinner."

"I will."

He went in.

---

He came home for dinner.

Marina had set the table on the deck. It was warm enough in late April, in Seattle on a clear evening, to eat outside, if you put a sweater on. The Sound was visible through the firs at the back of the property, the long slow horizontal evening light on the water the same light Henry had been looking at for seventeen years from this house. She had made the salmon. She had opened a bottle of the Russian River pinot they had been keeping for an occasion.

They sat down.

They ate.

They did not talk about the call.

They talked about Marina's project in Bellevue, a small civic library she had been designing for two years, which had just gotten the construction permit. They talked about their daughter, who had called from Boston in the morning to say that the dog had figured out how to open the back door. They talked about the trip to Sicily they had been postponing for two years. They talked about nothing in particular. The light moved on the water.

When they finished eating, Marina poured the rest of the pinot into Henry's glass. She did not pour any into hers. She set the bottle down. She looked at him across the table.

"Henry."

"Marina."

"What comes next."

He thought about it.

"The next thirty years," he said.

She laughed.

"Henry."

"Marina."

"You're sixty-two."

"I know."

"Promise me."

"Promise you what."

"Promise me that the next thirty years includes more than this."

He looked at her.

He reached across the table and took her hand.

"I promise," he said.

She held his hand for a moment.

Then she let go and picked up the plates and carried them inside, and Henry sat on the deck for another twenty minutes with the empty wineglass in front of him and the evening light on the Sound, and he thought about the call, and he thought about Karen Yoshida, and he thought about Walter Brandt, who had sent him a one-line note that afternoon — *Henry. You were right. I was wrong. Walt.* — and he thought about Marina, inside the house, doing the dishes.

He carried the wineglass inside.

He helped with the dishes.

They went to bed.

The Journal piece, by morning, had been picked up by every financial outlet in the world. By Saturday afternoon the stock was seven percent above pre-announcement levels for the first time since October of Year 1.

It was, in the institutional sense, the moment the pivot had been recognized.

It was also, in the sense Henry had been carrying for thirty months, the moment the carrying could be set down.

He set it down.

Then, on Monday morning, he picked it back up. Because that was the job. That had always been the job. The carrying did not end. It only changed shape. And the shape it was about to take was, for the first time in three years, a shape that was wider than his own.
