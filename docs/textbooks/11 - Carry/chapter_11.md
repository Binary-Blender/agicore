# Chapter 11: The Snatch

The Bloomberg piece hit at 1:14 PM Pacific on a Wednesday in early April of Year Two.

Marcus Drummond's byline. Eleven paragraphs. The fourth paragraph was the leaked memo, word-for-word — the Q2 ESOP-tranche pricing methodology that Carrick had not yet disclosed, now disclosed. The stock was down four percent in six minutes. The 8-K had been scheduled for two weeks from Friday. The leak had compressed the window to today.

Mara walked into the eleventh-floor conference room without knocking. It was the third time in four years she had done that. She set the encrypted tablet on the table. Cole read the piece in forty seconds. He turned to her.

"How many had access on Friday."

"Seventeen. The five executives, the three directors under Diana, the two corp-dev directors under Owen, the three engineers on the document store, Priya's three counsels. Seventeen."

"Run it."

"Running."

She walked out.

He picked up the encrypted phone and opened a LinkedIn profile he had been keeping in the third row of his bookmark bar for eleven months. Calvin Yates. Thirty-one. Junior associate, corp-dev, fourteen months at Carrick. Parked his Model 3 in B-47, a space assigned to a senior compliance analyst who had been on maternity leave since Year One Q3. Yates had parked in B-47 for fifty-one weeks. The only operational advantage over Yates's assigned space was speed of egress.

Cole had logged Yates's car in B-47 on forty-seven of the fifty-one Fridays.

The brother-in-law was Ethan Brock. Portfolio manager at Pemberley Capital, two point one billion AUM out of Greenwich. The Pemberley Carrick short had opened the previous Monday at 11:41 AM Pacific at forty-eight million notional. Cole had flagged the Pemberley position in the routine Tuesday sweep. The brother-in-law connection had been three layers deep in the LinkedIn graph and had not surfaced.

It surfaced in his head twelve minutes after Mara said the word *leak.*

He called her.

"Calvin Yates. Corp-dev. B-47. Brother-in-law named Brock at Pemberley. The Pemberley short is the position the leak was for. Your forensics will confirm at three PM Pacific. Have the operators ready at his desk by four. Let him have the afternoon."

"Cole."

"Yes."

"You have had this for twelve months."

"I have had it for twelve months without a triggering event. I should have flagged him at six."

"You watched him at the right cadence for the threat he presented. You flagged him in twelve minutes when the threat materialized. The cadence was the cadence. Run the day."

She hung up.

---

By 7:48 PM Pacific the 8-K was filed, the criminal referral was filed, the SEC emergency order was filed, the DOJ release was on the wire, the Carrick release was on the wire, the after-hours stock was recovering toward the Wednesday close, and Calvin Yates was in a federal holding facility in Tukwila.

Cole was alone in his office with the Q1 earnings deck on the desk in front of him when Mara knocked once at 7:53 and came in. She did not sit.

"Yates is in Tukwila. Brock is at Pemberley until ten AM Pacific tomorrow when the New York field office picks him up. Pemberley's positions are frozen as of four PM Pacific today. The fund is at two point one billion through the end of the week. Redemptions open Monday. Inside ninety days the fund is wound down."

"Threat posture."

She put a single sheet on the desk.

"Two new entries on the credible-threat board. Both downstream of the Pemberley exposure. The first is a small-dollar internet-forum aggregation that does not rise to credible on the protocol. The second is a name."

He picked the sheet up.

The name at the bottom of the sheet was *Sirocco Holdings, Limassol.*

He set the sheet down.

"Sirocco."

"Sirocco held the long convexity behind Pemberley's short. The structure is six layers — Cyprus to BVI to two Liechtenstein anstalten to a Cayman SPV to a Delaware LLC. The beneficial owner at the bottom of the structure is one of three Russian-Israeli financiers I have been tracking by reference since 2019. The most likely of the three is Anton Voskov. Voskov is down somewhere between one hundred forty and one hundred sixty on the Pemberley unwind, depending on how the fund liquidates the long side. Voskov has reached for leverage on losses in this range before."

"Twenty fifteen."

"Twenty fifteen. The Geneva commodities trader. The trader had his daughter taken from a parking garage in Vevey on a Friday afternoon, returned to him on the Sunday morning unharmed, with the renegotiation of the trader's open position on the table at the meeting Voskov took with him on the Monday. The Swiss police never opened a file. The trader settled the position on the Tuesday at the number Voskov wrote on a napkin. The trader retired to Lisbon in 2017. He has not given an interview."

"How long until Voskov knows the size of the Pemberley loss."

"He knows now. The Pemberley wind-down protocol has been public on Reuters for ninety minutes. The size of his long side is known to him because it is his long side. The size of his loss is known to him because he can do arithmetic. He has known the number since approximately five PM Pacific today."

"Posture."

"Perimeter check at Bainbridge to twenty-minute intervals through the night. Ferry-terminal protocol tightened. One operator moved to the inside-the-house slot. The detail at the office stays at the office until after-hours closes at five PM Pacific Friday. I am pulling two operators back from the Mid-Atlantic rotation to thicken the West Coast bench. The earliest I can have them on the ground is Thursday at oh-eight-hundred. The next twenty-eight hours are the gap."

"Routes."

"Tonight the ferry. The route does not change. The route changing reads as a response to the threat. We do not respond to the threat tonight. We respond to the threat starting Thursday at oh-eight-hundred when the bench is thick. Tonight you go home on the route you go home on. We close the gap with detail density inside the cabin and a follow car a quarter mile back instead of two."

"Mara."

"Yes."

"If Voskov is going to move, he is going to move inside the gap."

"He is not going to move inside the gap. The gap is twenty-eight hours. Voskov's prior operations have taken three to six weeks of pre-positioning. He cannot have pre-positioned a Bainbridge operation against an event that hit the wire ninety minutes ago. Even Voskov needs more than ninety minutes."

"Voskov pre-positions against contingencies. Voskov has had a Bainbridge contingency on a shelf since 2019. The Pemberley loss is the trigger for the contingency. The contingency is already pre-positioned. The gap is the window the contingency was pre-positioned for."

Mara was quiet for two seconds. The HVAC turned over in the ceiling. Somewhere down the hall, a vacuum started.

"Cole."

"Yes."

"You are correct."

"Tighten what you can in the next two hours. We move at nine."

"Tightening."

---

The Suburban pulled out of the underground garage at 9:04 PM Pacific in the rain.

Liu was in the lead vehicle with one operator and one driver. Cole was in the second Suburban — middle row, driver-side seat — with Mara across from him and one operator in the front passenger seat and one driver. The follow car had been pulled up to a quarter mile back. The ferry was canceled. The wind out of the south had been gusting to forty since six and the Edmonds-Kingston run was on a weather hold through the night.

They went around. The route was the back way — across the 520, north on I-405 to Bothell, west on 522 to the Tolt valley, north on 203 to Monroe, then the slow climb west across the Olympic foothills on State Route 2 to Stevens, the long descent through Snohomish to the Mukilteo terminal where the second ferry might be running by the time they got there.

It was a four-hour drive. It was a longer drive than the ferry. It was the same drive Cole had made eleven times in seven years.

Mara was on the phone with Liu the entire first hour. Liu's lead car was forty seconds ahead. The follow car ran dark behind. The operator in the front passenger seat ran the radio scan and watched the mirrors. The rain stayed steady through Bothell. The traffic thinned at Monroe. The Suburban climbed.

At 11:38 PM Pacific they were on a two-lane stretch of State Route 203 ten miles north of Carnation in a wooded valley with no shoulder and no cellphone reception on the carrier the Carrick devices were on. Liu was forty-three seconds ahead. The follow car was four hundred sixty meters back. The road bent left around a stand of cedar. The wipers ran on intermittent.

The headlights came around the bend going the wrong way.

The headlights came around the bend at speed. Cole had time to register the headlights and the angle and the size of the silhouette behind the headlights — a box truck, white, no markings, full-size cab — and to brace his hand against the seatback in front of him and lock his other arm across Mara's chest before the truck made contact with the lead Suburban at the apex of the bend.

The lead Suburban went sideways into the cedar.

The box truck kept going.

It hit Cole's Suburban on the front-left quarter at sixty-four miles an hour.

---

The world was very loud and then very quiet.

Cole's vehicle was on its side in the ditch on the west side of the road. The driver-side airbags had fired. The window on the high side — the passenger side — was gone. The cabin smelled like burned propellant and gasoline and wet cedar. Somewhere outside, a man was shouting in a language that was not English.

Cole was conscious.

Mara was conscious. She had her sidearm out. She had blood on the left side of her face from a cut above the eyebrow. She was looking up at the broken window above her head.

"Cole."

"Here."

"Operator?"

The operator in the front passenger seat was face down against the driver-side door. He was not moving. The driver was pinned by the steering column and was breathing in a way that meant the breathing was not going to keep going much longer.

Mara pulled herself up using the seatback. She braced her boots against the headliner. She came up to the broken window.

The first shot came through the window frame and hit the headrest two inches from Cole's left ear.

Mara fired three times through the window — controlled, paced, the way she had trained at Beltsville in 1998 and at the Rowley training center in 2003 and on the Carrick range at the Bainbridge property at four-thirty AM Pacific for nine of the last eleven Saturdays — and the shouting outside stopped on one and changed pitch on two and went away entirely on three.

She came down to Cole.

"Three were standing. There are more in the truck. The follow car is four hundred meters back. They are eighty seconds behind us. The first wave of the assault will hit the cabin in twenty. Cole. Get to the back. Out the rear glass. The follow car is the play. Twenty seconds."

"You go first."

"I am the perimeter. You are the package. Get to the back."

He moved. He had to move past the operator and over the center console. He was on his hands and knees against the inside of the driver-side door, which was now the floor. The rear cargo area was twelve feet behind him. The rear glass was intact. He pulled himself over the bench. He turned in the cargo space. He kicked the rear glass with both heels.

The glass spidered on the first kick.

It held on the second.

It blew out on the third.

He went through it head first onto the wet asphalt of the road.

He came up running.

The follow car was supposed to be eighty seconds behind.

The follow car was three hundred meters back and standing in the middle of the road with its hood up and its hazards on and two men standing next to it with rifles, and the third man was crossing the road from the cedar on the east side of the road with a black hood in his hand.

Cole understood, in the half-second it took him to register the staged follow car and the third man crossing the road, that the operation had not been three vehicles attacking two. The operation had been three vehicles attacking three. The follow car had been taken at the back of the column at the moment the box truck had taken the lead. The follow car had been compromised eighteen seconds earlier than the box-truck strike. The men running the follow car were not Carrick.

He turned the other way.

The third man had run forty meters in the time it took Cole to turn. The third man was twenty meters from him. The third man closed the twenty meters at full sprint. Cole had time to plant his back foot and turn his hip into the line. The third man hit him at the shoulder with the hood in his hand and the syringe in the other hand and the syringe went into the side of Cole's neck below the carotid and the plunger went down before Cole's hand could get to the wrist that was holding the plunger and Cole's last clear thought before the chemical hit the brainstem was that the third man had run the closing speed of a man who was twenty-six years old and that there were going to be more like the third man inside the truck.

He went down.

The cedar above the road was the last thing he saw.

The cedar was very tall and very still.

The world went out.

---

He came up on the steel floor of a transport van doing what felt like sixty miles an hour on what felt like a paved road.

His wrists were zip-tied behind his back. His ankles were zip-tied. There was a strip of cloth across his eyes. His mouth was free. He was lying on his right side with his cheek against the corrugated steel of the cargo floor. The van smelled like diesel and rubber and a faint chemical residue he placed, with some effort, as the same residue that had been on the cloth of the hood the third man had brought across his face in the second before the syringe.

His head hurt in a specific place behind his right ear where the impact of going down had caught him on the asphalt.

His left shoulder hurt in a specific way that meant the rotator cuff had taken something it had not been ready to take.

The four ribs on his right side hurt in a way that meant two of them were probably cracked.

He was alive.

He listened.

Two voices in the front cab. Both male. Both speaking what he placed, after thirty seconds of listening, as a dialect of Russian he had heard at three of the four contract-security dinners Voskov's operations had been on the periphery of in the previous nine years. The drivers were not Voskov's own. Voskov did not use his own. The drivers were Belarusian contractors out of the Vilnius network, which Voskov had used for the Vevey operation in 2015 and for two operations against German industrialists in 2019 and 2021. The Vilnius network worked on a flat fee plus completion bonus and did not, by reputation, take losses well.

The conversation in the cab was about a road junction. The driver was asking the navigator about whether to take the next left. The navigator was telling him to wait. There was a third voice — a woman's voice — coming through a speaker on the dashboard. The woman was telling the navigator the GPS had them sixteen minutes out from a transfer point.

Sixteen minutes was longer than Cole had.

He worked the zip ties.

The zip ties were the heavy-duty plastic kind. They were doubled. The doubling was the tell of a crew that had taken kidnappings to completion before. The doubling defeated the cuff break that a single tie would not have defeated. Cole had pulled three-eighty for triples on the trap bar at five AM that morning. He was sixty-two years old. He was not pulling out of doubled heavy-duty zip ties at the wrists in the back of a moving van.

He worked his belt.

The belt was a calfskin Sant'Eustachio he had bought in Rome in 2019 and that had a custom Bear Doerksen brass buckle on the front, a one-piece machined buckle Bear had given him for his sixtieth birthday with the inscription *The Bar Goes Up* on the inside flat. The buckle was four ounces of machined brass with a single tongue and a tongue post. The tongue post was an inch and a half of polished brass that came to a point. Bear had ground the point himself.

The belt was on him.

The kidnap crew, in the rush of the snatch on the road, had not searched him.

He rolled onto his back. He worked his bound hands down past his hips. He bent his knees up against his chest — the cracked ribs registered the bend at a level of pain he set aside in the way Bear had taught him to set things aside in the squat rack in 2017 — and pulled the bound hands forward under his legs and got them in front of him.

He pulled the blindfold down.

The interior of the van was empty cargo space. Steel walls. A bulkhead with a small sliding window into the cab, closed. A single dome light in the ceiling, off. Two emergency reflectors on the inside of the back doors. A roll of duct tape and a coil of nylon line in the corner. No tools. No weapons. No second restraint kit.

He worked the belt buckle.

The buckle came off the belt in eleven seconds.

He held the buckle in his right hand. The tongue post was an inch and a half. The point was sharp. He went after the zip tie on his ankles first because the ankles were easier and because the ankles were what he needed for the floor.

The post went between the strap and the doubled tie. He worked the post against the locking pawl of the upper tie. The pawl was nylon. The point of the post was brass. The brass was harder than the nylon. He worked the post against the pawl for ninety seconds. The pawl gave. The tie released. He freed the second ankle in forty seconds.

He sat up.

The wrist ties were harder. The wrist ties were behind the angle his hands could comfortably work the buckle at, even with the hands in front. He pressed his back against the bulkhead. He worked the buckle in his right fist, point out, against the pawl of the first tie. The first pawl gave at three minutes.

The second tie was harder still. He worked it for two minutes. The second pawl gave. The wrist ties came off.

He had two zip-tie loops in his hand and a brass belt buckle and a calfskin belt. He had four cracked ribs and a torn left rotator and a head injury he was not going to know the size of until somebody who was not him had a chance to look at it.

He was alive. He was unbound. He was in a van. The van was sixteen minutes from a transfer point. The conversation in the cab was about whether the next left was the left.

He had ten minutes before the navigator opened the bulkhead window to check the package.

He did the math.

The van was a transport van. The transport van had a sliding bulkhead window between the cab and the cargo. The bulkhead window opened from the cab side. The bulkhead window opened toward the cargo side. The bulkhead window was the only line of communication between the cab and the cargo while the van was moving. The bulkhead window was twenty inches wide and eight inches tall. The bulkhead window was at the height a man's head would be at if he was sitting on the cargo floor with his back against the bulkhead and his legs out in front of him.

The bulkhead window was the firing slot.

The bulkhead window was also the kill point.

He moved.

He took the coil of nylon line from the corner. He cut a four-foot length with the point of the buckle. He looped one end around the buckle, two wraps, square knot, square knot, doubled. The buckle was now a four-foot brass-and-nylon impact weapon weighing four ounces at the working end. He had used a similar improvised weapon in a tactical scenario Bear had run him through in the Bainbridge barn in 2019.

He took the duct tape. He wrapped four turns of tape around his right fist over the four knuckles. He wrapped two turns around his left fist. The left shoulder would not throw at full extension. The left was the brace hand. The right was the strike hand.

He sat down against the bulkhead on the cargo side.

He waited.

---

The navigator opened the bulkhead window at minute fourteen.

The window slid open on a steel track. The navigator's face was on the other side. The navigator's face was lit by the dashboard. The navigator was a man of approximately forty, beard, gray eyes, the face of a man who had done this work seventeen times and was carrying himself the way a man carries himself on the eighteenth.

The navigator looked through the window for half a second.

In that half-second he registered: a man sitting on the cargo floor with the blindfold down and the zip ties off and a length of nylon line in his right hand.

In the next quarter-second Cole's right hand came up and through the window slot and the brass buckle on the end of the nylon line caught the navigator on the bridge of the nose with the full power of Cole's hip rotation behind it, and the navigator went sideways against the driver, and the driver — who had been doing sixty on a wet road at one AM Pacific in a stand of cedar twenty miles inland from a Puget Sound shoreline he did not know — lost the wheel.

The van went off the road.

It went off on the right side. It went down a six-foot embankment. It rolled onto its right side and slid through underbrush for thirty feet and stopped against a stump.

The dome light came on with the impact.

Cole was thrown against the right wall of the cargo. He absorbed the impact on the right shoulder, which was the good shoulder. He came up on his knees. He had the belt and the buckle. He moved to the bulkhead.

The bulkhead window was now horizontal. The navigator was unconscious against what was now the floor of the cab. The driver was conscious. The driver was reaching for a pistol in a shoulder holster under his left arm.

Cole had the buckle through the window slot before the pistol cleared the holster.

The buckle caught the driver above the right ear. The driver went down. The pistol went down with him.

Cole came through the window.

He came through the window head first, because the rotated geometry of the van made head first the only available approach. He landed on the navigator's chest. He came off the navigator's chest onto the driver. He took the pistol from the driver's hand. He cleared the chamber. The chamber was loaded. He checked the safety. The safety was off.

He had a pistol. He had two unconscious men. He had a van on its side in a wood. He did not know how far from the road he was. He did not know whether the woman on the dashboard speaker was on the way. He did not know whether the assault team that had taken him on State Route 203 was running a follow vehicle behind the transport van.

He found out by listening for ten seconds.

The wood was silent except for the sound of the van's engine, which had stalled. The wood smelled like wet cedar. The night was very dark. He was high in the foothills. He was not on a highway. He was somewhere in the deep stand of state-forest cedar that ran north of Stevens Pass and that he had hiked through with Bear in the summer of 2021.

He climbed out through the cab door, which was now the upper door of the cab. He came up onto the side of the van. He scanned. The road was forty feet up the embankment. There were no other headlights.

He came down the embankment to the road.

He turned south.

He walked.

---

A logging truck found him at three fourteen AM Pacific six miles down the road at the junction with Forest Service Road 6212.

The driver was sixty-one, named Earl, had been driving the route for thirty-one years, was on his second cup of coffee out of the thermos his wife had filled at midnight, and had pulled over because a man was standing in the middle of the road in a black wool overcoat with blood down the left side of his face and one hand raised.

The man got into the cab without speaking.

Earl drove him to the Stevens Pass ranger station, which was the closest building with a landline that worked at three in the morning.

The man asked Earl, in the second mile, for the loan of a phone. Earl handed him a flip phone. The man dialed a number from memory. The number was answered on the first ring. The man spoke four words into the phone.

*Mara. I am up.*

He listened to the answer for eleven seconds.

He said: *Forest Service Road 6212. South of Stevens. Logging truck. Driver named Earl. ETA the ranger station in nine minutes. I have a pistol. I have two down in a van approximately six miles north on 203, off the east embankment in the cedar. I am ambulatory.*

He listened for four more seconds.

He said: *I know you are. Get here.*

He handed the phone back to Earl.

He did not speak again until the Suburban came up the ranger-station access road at four oh seven AM Pacific with Bear at the wheel and Mara in the front passenger seat with the side of her face bandaged from her right temple to her jaw.

The Suburban stopped six feet from the ranger station porch.

Cole walked down the porch steps.

He got in the back.

Bear turned the Suburban around.

They went home.
