# Chapter 10: The Senator

The Gulfstream G700 broke ground at Boeing Field at 6:58 PM Pacific with the wing strobes punching white holes into the rain. Cole watched the runway lights slide under the trailing edge and disappear. Priya Bhattacharya sat across from him at the conference table in the back of the cabin, a four-inch binder open between them, tabbed in nine colors. She'd been building the binder for sixty-three days with Whit Mendelson, chief counsel to the Senate antitrust subcommittee. Tonight it would either be a useful document or wastepaper.

"Read me the language as it stands," Cole said.

Priya read. Cole listened with his eyes closed.

The plane crossed into Montana airspace at altitude thirty-nine thousand.

---

Wheels down at Dulles at 2:41 AM Eastern.

Two Suburbans waited on the apron with their parking lights on and the rear hatches up. Mara had pre-positioned them out of Tysons two days earlier, swept by the Carrick Mid-Atlantic team Tuesday afternoon. The drivers were ex-DSS, vetted through Mara's old State Department network, paid in cash through a Carrick-controlled LLC that did not appear on any organization chart anyone outside Cole's office had ever seen.

Cole walked off the airstairs and into the back of the lead vehicle without breaking stride.

The Hay-Adams was two blocks north of the White House. The night manager opened the side door himself. Cole took the freight elevator to the eighth floor. He was in the suite at 3:22 AM.

He slept three hours.

At 6:35 AM he was in the basement gym with Bear, who had flown out a day ahead on a commercial ticket under his own name. The gym was empty. Bear had asked the overnight manager to clear it for the morning, and the overnight manager — having watched Bear's shoulders move under his jacket on the way down in the elevator — had cleared it.

Cole stripped his hoodie, chalked his hands, and loaded the trap bar to three sixty. Pulled it for triples. Worked through thirty minutes of conditioning while Bear ran the timer.

In the shower, the hot water hit his neck and Cole replayed Whit's three problems in his head. The third was disposable. The second was already won; Whit just didn't know it yet. The first was the one.

He'd been carrying the answer to the first one for four months.

He toweled off, put on the dark gray suit, and went downstairs.

---

The senator's row house sat on R Street between Twenty-Eighth and Twenty-Ninth, brick, four windows wide, ivy on the south wall. Mara had walked the block at 6:00 PM. Two operators sat in vehicles a block off in each direction. The lead Suburban idled at the corner with its lights off.

Cole rang the bell at 8:27 PM Eastern.

The senator opened the door herself. Sixty years old, charcoal cashmere sweater, dark jeans, barefoot. Tortoiseshell readers on a chain at her throat. The hallway behind her smelled like braised short ribs and pinot.

"Cole."

"Senator."

"Lila. There are no senators in this house tonight."

"Lila, then."

"Come in. Take the coat off. Hang it there."

He did. He set a bottle on the side table — a 2014 DRC Échezeaux he'd pulled from the Bainbridge cellar Tuesday afternoon. The label caught the hall light.

Marsh picked it up and held it against her chest like a small dog.

"I'm not opening this tonight, Cole."

"I didn't think you would."

"It goes in the closet. We open it when the carve-out passes."

"That's the trade."

"That's the trade."

She walked it to the closet at the end of the hall, opened the door, and set the bottle on the second shelf, label out. She closed the door with the soft *snick* of a well-hung interior latch. When she turned back, her face had gone neutral in a way Cole had seen in courtrooms.

"Whit and Tom are in the dining room. Tom is Tom Becker, Treasury, domestic finance. He's been working the platform side of the carve-out since November. He's here because I asked him to be. He's not writing anything down. He's not taking anything back to the secretary tonight." She let the sentence land. "The four of us are going to spend ninety minutes either landing the language or not. If we land it, the markup is in May with the carve-out in. If we don't, the markup is in May without it, and Carrick isn't in the safe harbor when the law passes."

"I came because I knew it was either-or."

"I asked you because I knew you knew."

She turned and walked toward the dining room. Cole followed. Bear stayed at the front door, jacket buttoned, hands clasped low.

---

The room was the back of the house — French doors open a finger's width onto a small brick garden where a single pear tree had begun to bud. The table was set for four under a brass chandelier turned low. The short ribs sat in a Le Creuset between two carafes of decanted Bordeaux.

Mendelson was already standing. Forty-six. Wiry. Wire-frame glasses he pushed up with the back of one knuckle. Eleven years on the Hill, two years with Marsh on antitrust, in his second draft of a book on platform-monopoly doctrine he was never going to finish because he wanted to be a federal judge more than he wanted to be an author.

Becker was the unknown. Mid-fifties. Lawyer hands. A wedding ring that had been resized at least once. The kind of Treasury career civil servant who had outlasted three administrations and would outlast a fourth. He nodded once at Cole and did not extend his hand. He had already decided how the night would go.

Marsh served the food herself. She did not let anyone clear plates.

"Whit," she said, and Whit started.

---

"Three problems with the language as it stands," Mendelson said. He pulled a single folded sheet from inside his jacket — handwritten, in pencil, blocky engineer's letters. He did not look at it.

"The definition of *material conflict of interest in real time.* As written, the FTC will spend six years in district court teaching the platform companies what *real time* means. Carrick needs language it can comply with on day one."

"Two."

"Five-year sunset review. Current language requires the platform company to make a positive case at each cycle that the safe harbor should be extended. You'll want the burden flipped."

"Three."

"Equity-equals-zero. The current text treats any executive equity in a tenant entity as automatic forfeiture. Carrick has no exposure on the operating language. Carrick has structural exposure on Cole's four-point-two-billion contribution to the Retail Co. ESOP trust on the morning of the memo. As drafted, a hostile reading of the text would treat the contribution as an indirect equity holding in a tenant entity."

Mendelson refolded the paper and slid it back inside his jacket.

"That's the table."

Marsh looked at Cole. So did Becker. Cole could hear the brass pendulum of a clock down the hall and, somewhere out on R, a single car passing through standing water.

He picked up the water glass, drank half of it, set it down.

"I'll take them backwards."

He took them backwards.

---

On three, he said, "The four-point-two is in an irrevocable trust. Independent trustee. Six hundred forty thousand beneficiaries — every hourly Retail Co. employee in the country. I'm not a beneficiary. I take no cash flow from the trust, ever. The trust documents are on Page Sixty-Eight of the 8-K. Anyone with a Bloomberg terminal can read them tonight."

He let that sit one beat.

"I'll support bright-line language. A contributor to an irrevocable ESOP trust with a beneficiary class above fifty thousand workers and a contribution above five hundred million is conclusively presumed to hold no indirect equity in a tenant entity for purposes of the safe-harbor test. That line excludes a Carrick-style contribution. It excludes maybe two more like it that anyone could ever build. It does not paper over shell-trust gaming. It's defensible on the floor."

Mendelson's mouth had gone tight at the corner — a tell Cole had read once before, in a deposition in a different room. Mendelson liked the language. Mendelson liked the language a lot.

"Tom?"

"Treasury is comfortable."

"Cole. On three we are aligned. Move."

---

On two, he said, "An unflipped burden is an annual political renegotiation. A flipped burden is a safe harbor."

He pushed his plate forward an inch.

"Carrick will commit, in the legislative text, to a quarterly public transparency report — audited by the company's outside auditor, filed on the same schedule as the 10-Q, available on the SEC's EDGAR system at zero cost to the FTC. The FTC builds its removal case from the data in the public report. The Commission's burden is met by reading the filing. The platform company carries the cost of transparency. The Commission carries the cost of nothing. That's the trade for the burden flip."

"Whit?"

Mendelson exhaled a sound that was almost a laugh. He pushed his glasses up.

"I've been turning the burden flip in my hands for nine weeks. I couldn't find the trade that paid for it. *That's* the trade. I'm comfortable if Cole is committing in the legislative text."

"He's committing."

"I'm committing."

"Tom?"

Becker considered the ceiling.

"Treasury is comfortable."

"Cole. Move."

---

On one, he set the water glass aside and put both hands flat on the table.

"The reason *real time* has been *real time* across five drafts," he said, "is that nobody has wanted to define the operational meaning in front of the working group. Because once it's defined, the platform companies will either comply with it or fight it, and the working group has not wanted to find out which."

He waited two seconds.

"I'm going to define it. In language Carrick will commit to comply with."

Becker leaned forward by maybe half an inch. Marsh did not move.

"*Real time*, for purposes of the safe harbor, is the platform company's standard internal latency on competitive-intelligence reporting in the calendar quarter in which the disclosure event occurs. Carrick's standard internal latency today is twenty-four hours. In Q3 last year it was thirty-six. AWS, from the public technical literature, runs somewhere between twelve and twenty. Whatever the platform's standard internal latency is in the quarter — measured by the FTC, auditable, on the platform's own production telemetry — *that* is *real time* for purposes of the safe harbor."

He picked the water glass back up.

"Platform companies cannot game the definition by lengthening their internal latency, because the audit catches a deliberate lengthening inside one quarter. The definition is operational. The definition is auditable. The definition will hold."

The clock down the hall ticked.

Marsh looked at Mendelson.

Mendelson was smiling. It was not a public-record smile. It was the smile of a man who had just been handed the rest of his career on a porcelain plate.

"Lila. He's been holding that definition for two months."

"I've been holding it for four," Cole said.

Mendelson's smile widened a quarter inch.

"Tom?"

Becker took his time. He laid his fork down. He tented his fingers. He looked at the chandelier, then at Cole.

"Treasury is comfortable. Treasury asks for one technical amendment. The latency should be calculated on a rolling four-quarter average rather than the in-quarter number. The in-quarter calculation creates a perverse incentive to bunch high-latency events at quarter boundaries."

"Tom is right."

"Rolling four-quarter average."

"Whit?"

"Rolling four-quarter average."

"Cole?"

"Rolling four-quarter average."

Marsh exhaled. She picked up the second carafe and poured the last of the Bordeaux into the four glasses. The wine clicked twice against the rim of Cole's glass — the senator's hand was not entirely steady. She set the carafe down and lifted her own glass.

"To the carve-out."

Four glasses up.

They drank.

---

The dinner ended at 10:23 PM Eastern. Cole was in the Suburban at 10:31.

Inside the vehicle, Priya was in the second row. Mara up front. Bear behind the wheel. The doors thumped closed. Bear pulled away from the curb at a speed that did not look hurried.

Cole drew the satphone from his coat and dialed Seattle. Anjali picked up on the first ring. She had been at her desk through the dinner.

"Cole."

"Three problems. We took all three."

A pause on the line — the kind of pause that, in someone else, would have been an exhale.

"Walk me through the first," Anjali said.

He walked her through the first. Rolling four-quarter, standard internal latency, audited on production telemetry, the FTC catches a lengthening inside one quarter. He could hear Anjali writing.

"You've been carrying that definition for four months," she said.

"Yes."

"You didn't tell me."

"I didn't tell anyone. If the language had been in the working-group drafts at any point in the last nine weeks, the working group would have edited it into something the working group could agree to. The working group would have arrived at a definition that did not work. I needed it to be *the* definition. So it had to walk into Lila Marsh's dining room with me and nobody else."

"Cole."

"Yes."

"You ran the table."

"Lila ran the table. Whit ran the table. Tom ran the table. The four of us did the work the working group had been avoiding for nine weeks. We did it across ninety-six minutes at a dining room table on R Street."

"The closet?"

"Closet."

"Nine months."

"Nine months."

Down the line, a chair creaked. Anjali shifting her weight.

"Cole. Get some sleep."

"I'll sleep on the plane. Wheels-up at oh-four-hundred Eastern."

"Run me through the floor before you go."

He ran her through the floor. Goodyear status. The second-wave conversions starting Monday. The third wave queued for June, the fourth for September. Sixty-four facilities by year-end. He listened to her ratify each number with a single syllable on the other end of the line.

"Cole."

"Yes."

"The pivot is the work."

He smiled at the back of Bear's head.

"The pivot is the work."

He killed the call. The Suburban turned onto Massachusetts Avenue. The wipers cleared the windshield twice.

---

The Gulfstream lifted off Dulles at 4:03 AM Eastern.

Cole slept three of the four hours and fifty minutes of the flight.

He woke over Montana. He read the redline Whit Mendelson had sent to Priya at 3:47 AM Eastern — by which point Whit had been back at his Russell Building office for an hour and a half, drafting the language the four of them had agreed to over the short ribs.

The redline was clean. He sent it back at 7:14 AM Eastern with two pencil marks — both inside square brackets, both numerical, neither substantive.

The G700 banked over Puget Sound at 5:38 AM Pacific. The water was the color of pewter. The Olympics were a black ridge against gray cloud.

Boeing Field, 5:44 AM Pacific. Rain.

Mara opened the door. Cole walked down the airstairs into the rain.

It was the second time in five months he had walked down the airstairs of the Gulfstream in the rain at Boeing Field on the work of the pivot. The first had been the Friday after the *Coelacanth* turned around. This time he carried a brief from a senator's dining room and a redline in the bracketed pencil of two governments coming into alignment with a private company across four hours of one night.

The Suburban took him to Bainbridge.

At 7:00 AM he was in the home gym with Bear. Three eighty on the trap bar. He pulled it for triples. The bar caught the morning light through the rain on the window glass.

Bear racked the next set.

Cole bent down for the bar.

Outside, somewhere across the water, a ferry horn answered another ferry horn.

He went to work.
