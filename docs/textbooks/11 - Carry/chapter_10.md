# Chapter 10: The Senator

The Carrick corporate Gulfstream G700 lifted off Boeing Field at six fifty-eight PM Pacific on a Tuesday in March of Year Two, with Cole in the seat at the conference table in the back, Priya Bhattacharya across from him, and a four-inch-thick briefing binder on the table between them that Priya had been building with Whit Mendelson of the Senate antitrust subcommittee for the previous nine weeks.

Wheels-down at Dulles was at two forty-one AM Eastern.

The cars on the apron were two Suburbans Mara had sent ahead two days earlier with two Carrick operators who had driven the vehicles cross-country from Seattle in advance of the trip. The drivers were former Diplomatic Security operators Mara had recruited from her own former network at State. The vehicles had been swept on Tuesday afternoon at the Carrick Mid-Atlantic regional office in Tysons Corner. Cole walked off the airstairs in the dark and into the back of the lead Suburban without breaking stride.

The hotel was the Hay-Adams, two blocks north of the White House.

Cole was in the suite at three twenty-two AM Eastern.

He slept for three hours.

He was in the hotel's basement gym at six thirty-five AM with Bear, who had flown out a day earlier on a commercial flight under his own name. The gym was empty. Bear had asked the night manager to clear it for the morning. Cole pulled three-sixty for triples on the trap bar. He worked through a thirty-minute conditioning circuit. He showered in the suite at seven forty. He was in the dark gray suit at eight.

The dinner was at the home of Senator Lila Marsh.

Senator Marsh was fifty-nine years old. She was the senior senator from Wisconsin. She had chaired the Senate Subcommittee on Antitrust for the previous four years. She had been a federal prosecutor in the Eastern District of Wisconsin from 1996 to 2008. She had been the attorney general of Wisconsin from 2010 to 2018. She had been elected to the Senate in the 2018 cycle and reelected in 2024. She lived in a row house on R Street in Georgetown that she had bought with her wife in 2019.

The dinner had been on the senator's calendar for fourteen weeks.

The dinner was Carrick on the senator's home turf.

The dinner was not on her public schedule.

Cole arrived at R Street at eight twenty-seven PM Eastern.

Mara had walked the block at six PM. The other operator was a block south in the lead Suburban. The follow car was a block east. The Hay-Adams car was at the corner.

Cole rang the bell at the front door.

The senator opened the door herself.

She was in a charcoal cashmere sweater and dark jeans and tortoiseshell reading glasses on a chain around her neck. She was barefoot. The hallway behind her smelled like braised short ribs.

She said: "Cole."

He said: "Senator."

She said: "Lila. There are no senators inside this house."

He said: "Lila."

She said: "Come in."

He walked in. He took his coat off. He hung it on the rack to the right of the door. He had brought one bottle of wine, a 2014 Domaine de la Romanée-Conti Échezeaux that he had pulled from the Bainbridge cellar on Tuesday afternoon. He set it on the side table in the hallway.

The senator said: "Cole."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "I am not opening that tonight. I am putting it in the closet. We will open it in nine months."

He said: "When the carve-out passes."

She said: "When the carve-out passes."

She picked up the bottle. She walked it to the closet at the end of the hallway. She put it on the second shelf. She closed the door.

She came back.

She said: "The others are in the dining room."

He walked through.

The dining room was the back of the row house, with French doors that opened onto a small brick garden. The table was set for four.

The two other people at the table were Whit Mendelson, the subcommittee's chief counsel, and a man Cole did not know.

The senator said: "Cole, this is Tom Becker. Tom is the undersecretary for domestic finance at Treasury. He has been working on the platform-companies side of the carve-out since November. He is here at my request. He is not going to write any of this down. He is not going to take this back to the secretary tonight. Tom and Whit and I have been the three people in the room on the platform-companies provision of the legislation for sixteen weeks. You are the fourth. The four of us are going to spend ninety minutes tonight either landing the language of the carve-out or not landing it. If we land it, the legislation is in front of the full subcommittee in May. If we do not land it, the legislation is in front of the full subcommittee in May without the carve-out, and Carrick is not in the safe harbor when the law passes. You came to this dinner because you knew the dinner was either-or. I came to this dinner because I knew you knew. We are now sitting down."

Cole said: "Lila."

She said: "Yes."

He said: "Thank you for the directness."

She said: "Sit down."

He sat down.

---

The dinner ran one hour and fifty-six minutes.

The senator served the short ribs herself. She poured the wine herself. She did not allow anyone to clear plates.

The conversation was the conversation.

Senator Marsh laid out the language of the carve-out as it stood in the current draft. The carve-out applied to platform companies that maintained zero equity in marketplace tenants, that operated their platform services on terms identical to those offered to every other tenant, that disclosed every material conflict of interest in real time, and that submitted to a five-year sunset review under which the safe-harbor designation would be revisited every five years on the basis of the company's actual operating conduct rather than its stated commitments.

The current draft had three problems.

Whit Mendelson named the three problems.

The first problem was the definition of *material conflict of interest in real time.* The current language was, in Whit's words, *the kind of language that the Federal Trade Commission was going to litigate for six years* before the meaning of *real time* became settled. Carrick needed the language to be specific enough that the company could comply with it on Day One.

The second problem was the five-year sunset review. The current language required the platform company to make a positive case at each review that the safe harbor should be extended. Carrick wanted the language flipped — the FTC should have to make a positive case to *remove* the safe harbor, with the platform company entitled to a presumption of continued compliance in the absence of demonstrable conduct violations.

The third problem was the equity-equals-zero provision. The current language did not address the question of executive equity in tenant entities. If a platform-company CEO held personal equity in any tenant on the platform — even at de minimis levels — the safe harbor was automatically forfeit. Carrick had no exposure on this provision on the day of the dinner. But Cole's personal contribution to the Carrick Retail Co. ESOP trust on the morning of the memo had created, in the language of the current draft, a structural question about whether Cole's identity as a contributor to the ESOP trust constituted an indirect equity holding in a tenant entity for purposes of the safe-harbor test.

Cole listened to all three problems.

He drank the water.

He set the glass down.

He said: "Lila. I will take the three problems in reverse order."

He took the three problems in reverse order.

On the third problem he said: "The four point two billion is in an irrevocable trust. The trustee is independent. The beneficiaries are six hundred and forty thousand Carrick Retail Co. employees. I am not a beneficiary. I will not receive any cash flow from the trust at any point in my lifetime or after. The trust documents are public on Page Sixty-Eight of the 8-K. I am willing to support language in the carve-out that establishes a bright line — a contributor to an irrevocable ESOP trust whose beneficiary class includes more than fifty thousand individual workers and whose contribution exceeds five hundred million dollars is conclusively presumed to hold no indirect equity in the tenant for purposes of the safe harbor. The line excludes Cole-Westerlund-style contributions from triggering the loss of safe harbor. The line does not excuse less rigorous arrangements. The line is good for Carrick and good for the public-interest framing of the carve-out."

Senator Marsh looked at Whit.

Whit said: "Lila. That language works. It draws a hard line. The line is defensible. The line is conservative enough that we are not opening up a hole for shell-trust gaming."

The senator said: "Tom."

Tom Becker said: "Treasury is comfortable."

The senator said: "Cole. On the third problem we are aligned. Move to the second."

On the second problem Cole said: "Lila. The flipped burden makes the safe harbor real. The unflipped burden makes the safe harbor an annual political renegotiation. Carrick will commit, in the legislation, to a quarterly transparency report — a public document, audited by the company's outside auditor, that the FTC can build a removal case on if the conduct violates the safe-harbor conditions. The FTC has the data. The data is in the public report. The FTC's burden is met by reading the report. The flipped burden does not insulate the platform company from accountability. The flipped burden moves the accountability into the daylight where it belongs."

The senator said: "Whit."

Whit said: "I have been going back and forth on the burden flip for nine weeks. The quarterly-transparency report is the answer. The FTC gets the data. The data is the case. The presumption flips because the data is in daylight. I am comfortable with the flip if Cole is committing to the quarterly transparency report in the legislative text."

Cole said: "I am committing to it in the legislative text."

The senator said: "Tom."

Tom said: "Treasury is comfortable."

The senator said: "Cole. On the second problem we are aligned. Move to the first."

On the first problem Cole said: "Lila. The definition of *material conflict of interest in real time.* The reason the language has been the language across the previous five drafts is that nobody has wanted to define the operational meaning of *real time* in front of the working group because once it is defined the platform companies will either comply with it or fight it, and the working group has not wanted to find out which. I am going to define it. I am going to define it in the operational language Carrick will commit to comply with. The definition is this. *Real time*, for purposes of the safe harbor, is the platform company's standard internal latency on competitive-intelligence reporting in the calendar quarter in which the event being disclosed occurs. The standard internal latency at Carrick today is twenty-four hours. The standard internal latency in the third quarter of last year was thirty-six hours. The standard internal latency at AWS, by my understanding from the public technical literature, is between twelve and twenty hours. Whatever the platform company's standard internal latency is in the quarter, that latency is what *real time* means. The FTC can audit it. The platform companies cannot game it by lengthening their own internal latency, because the audit will catch a deliberate lengthening within one quarter. The definition is operational. The definition is auditable. The definition will hold."

The senator was quiet for a beat.

She looked at Whit.

Whit was smiling.

Whit said: "Lila. He has been holding that definition for two months."

Cole said: "I have been holding it for four."

The senator said: "Tom."

Tom said: "Treasury is comfortable. Treasury is going to ask for one technical amendment. The latency calculation should be on a rolling four-quarter average rather than the in-quarter latency. The in-quarter latency creates a perverse incentive to bunch high-latency events at quarter boundaries."

Cole said: "Tom is right. Rolling four-quarter average."

The senator said: "Whit."

Whit said: "Rolling four-quarter average."

The senator said: "We are aligned on the first problem. The three problems are solved. The language drafts on Friday. Whit and I will have the redline to the platform-companies working group by Monday morning. The subcommittee markup is in May. The carve-out is in the final draft."

She poured the last of the second bottle of wine into the four glasses. She set the bottle down.

She raised her glass.

She said: "To the carve-out."

The other three raised theirs.

They drank.

The dinner ended at ten twenty-three PM Eastern.

Cole was in the Suburban at ten thirty-one.

The Suburban drove him back to the Hay-Adams.

The Gulfstream lifted off Dulles at four oh-three AM Eastern.

Cole slept for three hours of the four hours and fifty minutes of the flight.

He woke up over Montana.

He read the Whit Mendelson redline that had been sent to Priya at three forty-seven AM Eastern, by which point Whit had already gone back to his office and had begun drafting the language he and Cole had agreed to at the dining table.

The redline was the redline.

The language was the language.

The carve-out was the carve-out.

The Gulfstream landed at Boeing Field at five forty-four AM Pacific.

Mara opened the door.

The Suburban was on the apron.

Cole walked down the airstairs into the rain.

It was the second time he had walked down the airstairs of the Gulfstream in the rain at Boeing Field on the work of the pivot.

The first time had been five months earlier on the Friday after the *Coelacanth* turned around.

This time he was carrying a brief from a U.S. senator's dining room.

The carve-out was going to be in the law.

The pivot was going to be in the safe harbor.

The work was the work.
