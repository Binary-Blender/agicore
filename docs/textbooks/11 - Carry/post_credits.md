# Post-Credits Scene

A warehouse in central Washington, three miles east of the Vector Aerospace balloon launch facility, on a Tuesday night in June of Year Eight, four weeks after Diana Marquez signed the final tranche of the Argo Retail ESOP in Newark and one week after Cole Westerlund returned from the fourth Mariana Trench dive.

The warehouse was not on any Carrick property registry.

The warehouse was not on any Vector Aerospace property registry.

The warehouse was not on any Westerlund-family-trust registry.

The warehouse was, on the Yakima County tax rolls, owned by a Delaware limited-liability company named *Lacerta Holdings LLC*, which had been incorporated in October of 2008, which had no listed officers other than a registered agent in Wilmington, and which had paid sixteen consecutive years of property taxes in cash through a wire-transfer chain that ran, by Priya Bhattacharya's own private mapping, through eleven jurisdictions on four continents.

The warehouse was eighty-four thousand square feet under one roof. The roof was forty-two feet at the peak. The interior was lit, in the off-hours, by two emergency safety lamps that pulsed every nine seconds in a red wavelength that the original electrical contractor in 2009 had assumed was a regulatory artifact and had not, at any point in the sixteen years since, asked about.

Mara Quinn was at the front door.

Liu was at the perimeter.

A six-operator detail from Mara's deep-rotation team — operators Cole had not, in eight years, used for any working trip — was at the road junction nine hundred meters down the unpaved access road, in two unmarked vehicles, in radio silence.

There was no other security.

Cole walked into the warehouse alone.

He pressed a single button on the control panel by the door.

A row of halogen lamps came on across the length of the warehouse, one bank at a time, two seconds apart, eleven banks total.

The thing in the warehouse was visible by the time the seventh bank came on.

It was approximately one hundred and forty feet long along its principal axis. It was approximately fifty-two feet across at its widest beam. It was the color of seawater in conditions that did not have any seawater in them. The hull was, at the surface, neither metallic nor ceramic nor polymeric in any classification system the two former Sandia National Laboratories materials scientists who had spent four months in 2018 cataloguing it had been able to confirm against any element on the periodic table or any synthesized compound in the United States chemical-inventory database. The metallurgy was its own metallurgy.

Cole had been the only person to enter the warehouse in those sixteen years except for the two Sandia scientists, who had signed personal non-disclosure agreements Priya had drafted from scratch and who had each, by 2019, retired to coastal towns in countries with no extradition treaty with the United States.

The thing had been in the warehouse since the fourteenth of October, 2008, which was forty-one days after Cole had completed his first private deep-sea dive in a chartered submersible to a survey site one hundred and ninety nautical miles east of the recovery coordinates that had been published, in 2007, by the U.S. Navy, in the inadvertent declassification of a single page of a sonar log that should not have been declassified and that had remained available on the Navy's public records portal for eleven days before it was quietly removed.

The page was, today, in Cole's safe on Bainbridge Island.

Cole walked across the warehouse to the thing.

He stopped at the access port on the lower starboard quarter.

He pressed his right hand, palm flat, against the access port.

The access port did what it had done every Tuesday and Saturday night for sixteen years.

The thing recognized him.

The thing began, with the small specific sound it always made, to power up.

Cole looked at the thing for a long minute.

He took his jacket off.

He hung it on the hook by the door.

He walked back to the access port.

He said, to the thing, in the warehouse, on the Tuesday night in June of Year Eight, in a voice that was not loud and that the thing nevertheless registered:

"All right. Now that I do not have to worry about retail."

The thing made the small specific sound a second time.

Cole stepped through the access port.

The port closed behind him.

The eleven banks of halogen lamps remained on.

Mara, at the front door, checked her watch. The watch read eleven seventeen PM Pacific. Cole had, on the previous one hundred and forty-eight visits Mara had logged across the eight years she had been chief of executive protection, spent an average of three hours and forty-one minutes in the warehouse, with the longest visit at nine hours and twelve minutes (on Christmas Eve of Year Three, which Mara had assumed was an outlier) and the shortest at twenty-six minutes (on the morning Iona Tran had called him on the *Coelacanth* satphone, which Cole had not, until that day, known he was going to take inside of a year on the same day as a warehouse visit).

Mara settled in at the door.

The pulse of the red safety lamps continued, every nine seconds, at the back of the warehouse where the lamps were not necessary because the eleven banks were on.

The thing, inside the warehouse, behind the access port, in the small specific sound it had been making at increasing rates for the previous eleven minutes, continued to do whatever it was that it did when Cole was inside it.

In Yakima County, Washington, on a Tuesday night in June of Year Eight, at eleven seventeen PM Pacific time, the second-richest man in the United States was no longer in the United States.

He was, by every honest accounting of the rules of physics that the United States chemical-inventory database had been built on, also not, at that moment, in the year 2026.

He would be back by morning.

He always was.
