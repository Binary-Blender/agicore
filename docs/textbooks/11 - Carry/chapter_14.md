# Chapter 14: The Coelacanth

Cole was on the deck of the *Coelacanth* at oh-five-twelve on the Thursday morning after the closing, in the gray pre-dawn that had been the same gray pre-dawn six years before, six hundred and twenty miles southwest of Guam, three hundred and forty miles west of the Mariana Trench, in the same waters he had been in when Iona's call had come in on the satphone seven years earlier.

The vessel was at the dive station.

The dive was at oh-six-hundred.

The submersible — a custom-built three-person pressure capsule named the *Phantom*, which Cole and the foundation had commissioned in Year Three and which had been delivered in Year Five and had completed twelve qualification dives in the trench in the previous nineteen months — was on the deck, in its cradle, with the topside crew running pre-dive checks under the launch lights.

Bear was in the pressure suit.

Cole was in the pressure suit.

Annika was in the pressure suit.

The three of them were going to be the three in the *Phantom.*

The dive was Annika's first.

She had not asked to dive before.

She had asked to dive on this one.

Cole had said yes.

She was standing at the rail of the upper deck with the captain at five fifteen. The light off the Pacific was the same light off the Pacific. The freighter to the north of the *Coelacanth* on the radar was not the freighter that had been on the radar in Year One, but the freighter was, in operational terms, the same freighter.

Cole walked up the ladder to the upper deck.

He stood next to Annika.

The captain was on the other side of her.

The captain said: "Cole. We are at thirty-eight minutes to launch. Bear is in the launch tunnel. Dr. Mayfield is on the topside console. We have the descent line cleared to the trench floor. The weather window holds until oh-nine-hundred. We will be back on the deck at oh-eight-fifteen."

Cole said: "Henrik."

The captain said: "Yes."

Cole said: "Thank you for the seven years."

The captain said: "Cole. The seven years are not over."

Cole said: "The seven years that started on the deck of this ship at eleven-forty on a Wednesday in September of Year One. Those seven years are over. The next seven start at oh-six-hundred."

The captain said: "All right."

The captain walked down the ladder to the bridge.

Cole and Annika were at the rail.

Annika said: "Cole."

Cole said: "Annika."

She said: "The closing closed on Wednesday at two fifty-nine and twenty-eight seconds Eastern."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "Diana called you at five forty-eight."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "You called Iona at twelve oh-one Pacific."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "What did Iona say."

He said: "She said the studio shipped one hundred and ninety-six orders between eleven forty-eight Pacific and twelve oh-one Pacific. She said the inbound order log was the largest single thirteen-minute window the studio had ever recorded. She said she was going to put the order log on the wall of the studio next to the photograph of the original studio team from Year minus four. She said she was going to be at her wheel at six AM Pacific tomorrow morning, which is two AM Thursday her time, in the same Brent CXR Maria had been using when I walked into the studio in the Carrick baseball cap and the hoodie in October of Year One. She said she was going to throw an Echo at six oh-three. She asked me what time it was going to be on the *Coelacanth* when she threw it. I told her it would be eleven oh-three PM Wednesday night by the ship's time, which is fourteen hours later than her wheel time. She said she was going to think about that when she threw it."

Annika said: "Cole."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "Throw a memo every seven years. The math is moving in the direction of a memo every seven years."

He said: "Annika."

She said: "Yes."

He said: "I am sixty-nine years old. The next memo is going to be Reese's memo. I am going to be on the deck of this ship reading the next memo. I am going to be reading it on the satphone Mara is going to bring me. The next memo is going to be the memo Reese is going to write the morning he realizes the customer has changed again. I am going to call him after I have read it. I am going to say to him the words Phillip Garber said to me in 1995 when I told him I was leaving the hedge fund to sell books. The words are *then go do it.* I am going to say them on the satphone. Reese is going to do it."

Annika said: "Cole."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "You are going to be sixty-nine when Reese sends the memo."

He said: "I am going to be sixty-nine."

She said: "You are going to be reading the memo on the deck of this ship."

He said: "On the deck of this ship."

She said: "I am going to be sixty-eight."

He said: "You are going to be sixty-eight."

She said: "We are going to be the same people standing at the same rail."

He said: "We are going to be the same people standing at the same rail."

She said: "Cole."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "The dive is at oh-six-hundred."

He said: "The dive is at oh-six-hundred."

She said: "Take me down."

He said: "I am going to take you down."

---

The *Phantom* launched at oh-five-fifty-eight Pacific time on the deck of the *Coelacanth* at the dive station six hundred and twenty miles southwest of Guam in the gray pre-dawn of the Thursday morning after the closing of Carrick Retail Co. into full employee ownership.

The submersible descended on the cleared descent line.

The descent rate was thirty-eight meters per minute.

The descent to the Challenger Deep was four hours and forty-two minutes.

The submersible touched the floor of the Mariana Trench at ten forty-two Pacific.

Cole, Annika, and Bear walked the trench floor in the submersible for one hour and seventeen minutes.

The submersible ascended on the cleared descent line at twelve PM Pacific.

The ascent was four hours and twenty-eight minutes.

The submersible broke the surface at four twenty-eight PM Pacific.

The topside crew recovered the *Phantom* into the cradle at four forty-three.

Cole stepped out of the *Phantom* at four fifty-six.

He stood on the deck of the *Coelacanth* in the late-afternoon Pacific sunlight in the pressure suit with the helmet under his arm.

Annika stepped out at four fifty-seven.

Bear at four fifty-eight.

The three of them stood on the deck.

Annika looked at the Pacific.

She looked at the sky.

She looked at Cole.

She said: "Cole."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "That is the deepest place on earth."

He said: "It is."

She said: "I am glad we went."

He said: "I am too."

She said: "Cole."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "What is for dinner."

He laughed.

He said: "Henrik has the salmon."

She said: "Good."

They walked across the deck toward the bulkhead door into the upper deck of the *Coelacanth.*

The sun was setting on the Pacific.

The waves were the same waves they had been six years before.

The light was the same light.

The ship was the same ship.

The phone in the captain's bridge was on the console.

It did not ring.

---

In Portland, Oregon, on Wednesday evening at twelve oh-eight PM Pacific time — which was Thursday evening at oh-five-oh-eight ship time on the *Coelacanth* — Iona Tran was at the wheel at the back of the Lacquer & Cup studio on NE Alberta Street, with the kiln on, with Maria at the wheel beside her, with Iris at the design table in the front, with Beck at the wedge table in the back, with the studio at twenty-three employees, with the studio at four times the size it had been seven years earlier on the Tuesday afternoon Cole had walked through the front door in the cap and the hoodie.

She was throwing an Echo.

The Echo was on the wheel.

The clay was the porcelain from the supplier in Sheridan, Oregon, that the studio had been using for thirteen years.

She finished throwing the Echo at twelve fifteen.

She lifted it off the wheel.

She set it on the drying rack at the back of the studio.

She wiped her hands.

She walked to the front of the studio.

The front of the studio had, on the wall, a small framed printout of the email Cole had sent her at 6:14:59 Pacific on the morning of the memo seven years and seven months earlier, which read: *Iona. This is what your call did.*

She looked at the email.

She looked at the door.

She walked outside onto NE Alberta Street into the May sunshine.

The street was the same street.

The bakery two doors down had a line.

The coffee roastery across the street had its garage door open to the sidewalk.

Two women in their twenties were laughing at the curb.

Iona stood on the sidewalk for a moment.

She thought about the call on the satphone seven years earlier.

She thought about Cole on the deck of the *Coelacanth* fourteen hours into the future of her own evening.

She did not know that he was on the deck of the *Coelacanth* with Annika and Bear at four fifty-eight PM Pacific.

She did not know that he had just stepped out of the *Phantom* at the surface of the Pacific Ocean after a dive to the deepest place on earth.

She did not know that, in two minutes, he was going to walk down the corridor to the small private dining room on the bridge deck, and that he was going to pour himself a glass of bourbon from the bottle Henrik had set out, and that he was going to look at the Pacific through the window for a moment, and that he was going to pick up the encrypted satphone, and that he was going to call her.

She walked back into the studio.

She closed the door behind her.

The bell rang once.

The studio was the studio.

The kiln was on.

The wheel was at rest.

She walked to the back of the studio.

She picked the wet Echo off the drying rack.

She carried it to the small worktable by the window.

She set it down.

She picked up a small porcelain stamp the studio had been using for three years.

She pressed the stamp into the wet clay at the base of the foot ring.

The stamp left a small mark.

The mark read: *Lacquer & Cup — Echo — May, Year Seven — Iona.*

She set the Echo back on the drying rack.

She washed her hands.

The phone in the front of the studio rang.

She walked to the front.

She picked up.

The caller ID read *WESTERLUND.*

She answered.

She said: "Cole."

He said: "Iona."

She said: "You dove."

He said: "I dove."

She said: "How was it."

He said: "It was the same as the dive in Year minus two. The trench is the trench. The light at the bottom is the same light. The pressure is the same pressure. The walk on the floor was an hour and seventeen minutes."

She said: "Annika."

He said: "Annika is here. She is at dinner with Bear and Henrik. I am in the dining room on the bridge deck. I am calling you from the satphone."

She said: "I threw an Echo at twelve oh-three Pacific. I stamped it at twelve oh-eight Pacific. It is on the drying rack at the back of the studio."

He said: "Iona."

She said: "Yes."

He said: "When the Echo is fired and glazed, ship it to the Bainbridge house."

She said: "I will."

He said: "Iona."

She said: "Yes."

He said: "The memo was what your call did. The closing was what the memo did. The platform is what the closing did. The platform is going to be what the platform does next. The next thirty years are the next thirty years. I am sixty-two years old. I am going to be sixty-nine when Reese sends the next memo. I am going to be eighty-one when Reese hands the company to the person who is going to send the memo after that. You are going to be sixty-three when Reese sends the next memo. You are going to be the customer the next memo is going to be about. The next customer is going to call you on a satphone. The next customer is going to call you because you are going to be the founder who is forty-two years into the work and who is, in the moment of the call, the founder the next customer can reach. You are going to take the call. You are going to call back inside forty-eight hours."

She said: "Cole."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "I will take the call."

He said: "I know you will."

She said: "Cole."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "Land the rest of the dinner."

He laughed.

He said: "I will land the rest of the dinner."

She said: "Goodnight, Cole."

He said: "Goodnight, Iona."

She hung up.

She set the phone down.

She walked back to the wheel at the back of the studio.

Maria was still at her wheel.

Maria looked up.

She said: "Cole."

Iona said: "Cole."

Maria nodded.

She went back to the wheel.

Iona stood at the back of the studio for a moment.

The light through the front windows was the long horizontal late-afternoon light Alberta Street had on Wednesday afternoons in May.

She walked to her wheel.

She wedged a new ball of porcelain.

She set it on the wheel.

She started the wheel.

She threw a second Echo.

The Echo grew under her hands the way the first one had grown.

She finished it at twelve thirty-nine PM Pacific.

She set it on the drying rack next to the first one.

She washed her hands.

She walked to the front of the studio.

She locked the door for the day.

The bell did not ring.

The light was the light.

The studio was the studio.

She walked out the back door of the studio.

She walked to her car.

She drove home.

The Echo on the drying rack at the back of the studio was, in twenty-eight days, going to be fired in the kiln, glazed in moss-green, cured, packaged, and shipped to the Bainbridge house.

It was going to be on the corner of the desk in Cole's study on the Monday after it arrived.

It was going to be on the desk for the rest of Cole's professional life.

The second Echo on the rack was going to be the Echo Iona kept for herself.

It was going to be on the windowsill of her kitchen at home.

It was going to be there for the rest of her life.

The waves on the Pacific were the waves on the Pacific.

The hunt continued.
