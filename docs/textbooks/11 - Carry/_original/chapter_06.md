# Chapter 6: The Communication

Daniela Park walked into the small conference room on the eleventh floor at seven fifty-five on the Wednesday morning after the board meeting carrying a laptop, a printed copy of the meeting minutes, three different drafts of an opening paragraph she had been working on since five-forty, and the strong sense that the next forty-eight hours were going to be the most consequential forty-eight hours of her career.

The conference room was called Conference Room B. It was twenty feet by fourteen. It had a wood table that seated eight, a screen on one wall, a whiteboard on the opposite wall, and a window that looked north toward Lake Union. Daniela had asked for it specifically. The larger conference rooms had glass walls. The small ones did not.

Vivian Reyes was already there. She was at the far end of the table with her own laptop open and a yellow pad in front of her. The pad had writing on it. The writing was not in Vivian's normal hand. The writing was the smaller, faster hand of someone who had been writing for two hours before sunrise.

"Vivian."

"Daniela."

"How long have you been here."

"Since six."

"Did you sleep."

"Some."

Daniela set her laptop down at the seat next to Vivian.

"We're going to do this together," she said.

"I know we are."

"All right."

Marcus Goldberg came in at eight-oh-two with a coffee in one hand and a printout of the disclosure schedule in the other. Anika Bhattacharya, the SVP of Communications, came in at eight-oh-four with two laptops, a binder, and a thermos that Daniela had seen her carry to every crisis meeting across the past four years. Henry came in at eight-oh-six. He closed the door behind him.

"Everyone here."

"Cassandra is dialing in at eight-thirty."

"Then we have twenty-four minutes. Let's get started."

He sat down at the head of the table.

He looked around the table.

"Here is the order of work," he said. "Wednesday is the internal communication. Thursday is the external communication and the press materials and the analyst briefings. Friday at eight a.m. Pacific the internal goes out. Friday at eight-fifteen the external goes out. Friday at nine I do CNBC. Friday at ten Marcus does the analyst call. Friday at one we do an all-hands video at the Seattle campus that will be streamed to every site globally. Anika has the schedule."

Anika nodded.

"The internal communication," Henry said, "is the hard one. It is the one we have to get right. It goes to one point six million people in seventy-three countries. It has to be true. It has to be readable. It has to acknowledge that some people's jobs are going to change and that some people's jobs are going to end. It has to give people a future to work toward. If we get the internal wrong, the external does not matter. So we are spending today on the internal."

He looked at Vivian.

"Vivian and I are writing it together," he said. "Vivian's name will be on it next to mine. Anika is the editor. Daniela owns the operational accuracy. Marcus owns the financial accuracy. Cassandra is reviewing every draft. The document will be six pages. We have eight hours. Let's start."

Anika opened her laptop.

"Henry," she said. "Read me the opening you have."

---

Henry read the opening he had written the night before. It was four paragraphs long. It was, Daniela thought as she listened, the worst writing she had heard out of Henry in a decade. It was over-formal. It buried the lead. It used the phrase *strategic transformation* twice in the first paragraph. It sounded like a press release.

When Henry finished reading, the room was quiet.

Vivian was the one who spoke.

"Henry."

"Yes."

"That's not it."

"I know."

"I have a draft."

"Read it."

She picked up the yellow pad.

She read.

*"To my colleagues at Argo:*

*Today I am writing to tell you about a change we are going to make in how this company operates. The change is large. The change will affect every business unit, every region, and many of you personally. I want you to hear it from me, in writing, before you hear it from anyone else.*

*Argo was founded thirty-one years ago to sell books online. Over thirty-one years it has become the largest commerce company in human history. We have, in that time, served the retail buyer well. A working family in any town in any country we operate in can today get nearly any product they need in two days, at prices that did not exist before this company existed. That promise — the one we wrote down in 1997 — we kept.*

*What this letter is about is something different. A different customer walked into our store about fifteen years ago. The small seller. Eleven million of them, by 2026, building real businesses on top of our infrastructure. We have not been serving that customer well. We have been competing with that customer, using infrastructure she helped fund, with private-label products timed to her sales data, and we have been telling her in form letters that her only recourse is software we also sell. That is not the company we wrote down in 1997 because the customer in 1997 was a different person. We did not notice the new customer walk in. We are noticing now. Starting today, the company is going to turn around and face her."*

She set the pad down.

The room was quiet.

Henry was looking at her.

"Vivian," he said.

"Yes."

"That is the opening."

"I know."

"How long did it take you."

"Three hours."

"Vivian."

"Yes."

"Thank you."

She did not say anything.

Anika was already typing it into her laptop.

---

They worked through the morning.

Cassandra dialed in at eight-thirty and stayed on the line for two and a half hours. Her camera was on. She sat in her apartment in Belltown in the same gray cashmere sweater she had worn the previous Friday. Her contribution to the morning was, in Daniela's experience, the contribution of someone who had drafted a lot of letters of this kind across the second half of her career: she pushed for specific language about why now. She pushed against soft language about the future. She said, at one point, with the calm authority of someone who had seen the cost of the other approach: *People can carry hard truths. They cannot carry vague ones. Tell them what is happening. Tell them why. Tell them what they need to do. The vagueness is what destroys trust.*

By eleven they had three pages.

The first page was Vivian's opening, lightly edited.

The second page was a one-page summary of the strategic decision: Argo, Inc. would exit retail entirely over five to seven years; the retail business would be reconstituted as Argo Retail Co. and transferred to its 640,000 employees through a chartered ESOP, the largest employee-ownership transition in U.S. corporate history, financed by the retail business's own pre-tax earnings under standard ESOP-leverage mechanics, a debt facility the parent would retire across the transition window, and a one-time $4.2 billion founder contribution from Henry personally; by the end of Year 7 the parent company would hold zero equity in any retail business; Argo Carry would become the strategic priority and would receive the bulk of new investment; the cloud business would continue on its current trajectory but its operating principles — open platform, never compete with the customer, never own a tenant — would become the operating principles of the company as a whole.

The third page was the part that contained the workforce language.

Daniela had drafted the third page across the previous three days with three different versions. She had been awake at four-fifteen that morning rewriting the third version. She read the current draft to the room at eleven-twelve.

It contained two sentences that Vivian asked her to change.

"Daniela."

"Yes."

"The sentence about 'role transitions.'"

"Yes."

"It is too soft. People are going to read it twice and not be sure what it means. They are going to forward it to their spouses and their spouses are going to ask them what 'role transition' means and they are not going to have an answer."

"What do you want it to say."

"I want it to say what it means."

"It means some jobs are going to end."

"Then say that."

Daniela looked at the page on the screen.

"Some jobs will end," she read aloud, slowly. "Over the next twelve to twenty-four months, the retail business will reduce its global workforce by approximately eighty thousand positions. The reductions will be conducted in phases. Affected employees will receive separation benefits, transition support, and re-employment assistance at industry-leading levels. We will publish the timeline and the criteria for the first phase within two weeks of this announcement."

The room was quiet.

Anika typed it in.

Marcus, from the other side of the table, said quietly: "Vivian. That is the right number."

Vivian nodded.

"It is the right number," she said. "I would rather have it in the announcement than have it leak in three weeks."

"It will leak in three weeks anyway."

"Then it should be ours. Not theirs."

Henry, who had been watching this exchange, said: "Vivian. Are you comfortable with your name on the document that contains that number."

She looked at him.

"Henry," she said. "If we are going to do this, my name has to be on the document that contains that number. If my name is not on that number, then I am not really running this transition. I am just standing next to you while you run it. That is not the deal I am taking."

"Then your name is on it."

"Yes."

"Thank you, Vivian."

"You are welcome."

---

They broke for lunch at twelve-forty. Anika ordered sandwiches from the cafe in the lobby. Henry took his out to the small balcony off the eleventh-floor executive lounge and made two phone calls — one to Tomás Aguilar and one to Frank Lattimore, both of whom needed to be briefed before the document went out. Marcus took his back to his office to run the disclosure schedule one more time with the legal team. Daniela stayed in Conference Room B.

So did Vivian.

They sat at the table for a few minutes in silence with their sandwiches and the half-empty coffee cups.

Vivian said: "How long have you known this was coming."

"In its current form, four months. In a vaguer form, longer."

"How much longer."

"Maybe two years."

"You did not say anything."

"It was not my call to make. It was Henry's. He had to see it himself."

"Did he see it because you walked him to it."

"I walked him part of the way. The last part he walked himself."

Vivian nodded.

"Daniela."

"Yes."

"Are you going to be all right doing what we are about to ask you to do."

"What do you mean."

"You are about to lay off eighty thousand people, redirect eighteen billion dollars of capital, repurpose eleven warehouses, restructure four business units, and rewrite the operating model of the largest commerce company in human history. While the stock is dropping. While the press is on fire. While the analysts are shouting at Marcus. Are you going to be all right doing that."

Daniela took a bite of her sandwich. She chewed it slowly.

"I am going to be all right doing it," she said. "I am not going to be all right at the end of it. I am going to come out of this the other side and I am going to be tired in a way I have never been tired. I am going to need a year. I might need two. I am going to take them. Henry knows that."

"Has he agreed to that."

"He has. We had the conversation in September."

"Good."

"What about you."

Vivian set her sandwich down.

"I am going to do this," she said, "because if I do not do this, someone else does it, and they do it worse, and the six hundred and forty thousand people who work for me get a worse outcome than the one I can give them. I am going to do this because Henry asked me to, and because Cassandra asked me to, and because my own conscience asked me to, after I read Marcus's model three times and was unable to find a way to disagree with it. I am going to do this because the alternative is that I quit, and if I quit, I do not get to write the third page of the announcement, and I do not get to design the separation benefits, and I do not get to choose who runs each region of the transition, and I do not get to call the regional managers personally to tell them what is happening. The cost of leaving is too high. The cost of staying is also high. The staying cost is lower."

She picked up her sandwich.

"Also," she said, "I love this company. I have given it nineteen years. I am not going to walk out the door because the company is becoming something different. I am going to stay and help it become something different well."

Daniela nodded.

"Vivian."

"Yes."

"For whatever it is worth from me. I have been watching you for nine years. You are the right executive for this."

Vivian did not say anything for a moment.

Then she said, quietly: "Thank you, Daniela. That is worth a lot."

---

They worked through the afternoon.

Pages four and five were the plan and the timeline. Marcus drove most of those. The timeline had specific milestones — first warehouse conversion in February, first major external Carry customer announced in Q1 of Year 2, ESOP trust chartered and first equity tranche transferred by Q3 of Year 2 (eight percent), second tranche by Q3 of Year 3 (cumulative twenty-three percent), full operational separation of Argo Retail Co. complete by Q4 of Year 3, full ESOP transfer to one hundred percent employee ownership by the spring of Year 7. Vivian read each milestone and asked, on three of them, whether the dates were the dates the company would actually meet or the dates the company would aspire to meet. On two of them, Marcus moved the dates back by three months. On one, he moved it forward by six weeks because the team had quietly been ahead of the public timeline for two quarters.

Page six was the closing paragraph.

Henry wrote it himself, on his laptop, in the small hand that Daniela had seen him use for important pieces of writing for fourteen years. He wrote it during a forty-minute span around five o'clock during which he did not speak to anyone in the room. The rest of them kept working on the press materials. Anika edited the document on the shared screen. Cassandra dialed back in at five-forty and read through the full six pages on her end.

At six-fifteen Henry pushed his laptop back and read his paragraph out loud.

*"I have spent thirty-one years building this company. I am as proud of what we have built as I have ever been of anything in my professional life. I am also clear-eyed about the fact that the customer we built the company to serve is no longer the only customer in our store. A second customer has been here for fifteen years, and we have not been serving her. We are going to begin serving her. The shape of the company is going to change to make that possible. It is going to be hard. It is going to be hard on many of you personally. I am asking you, today, to give me, and Vivian, and Daniela, and Marcus, and Tomás, and the rest of the executive team, your trust and your work for the next three years and your patience for the next seven. We do not take it lightly. We will earn it every day.*

*In the spring of 1997 I wrote a letter to our first public shareholders. The letter said it was Day One for the Internet and, if we executed well, for Argo. The customer that letter named is still being served. What we are announcing today is that a second customer walked into the store and we are turning the platform around to face her. It is still Day One. It is also, for the people we have not been serving, the first day."*

He looked up.

Vivian was looking at him.

Cassandra, on the screen, was nodding slowly.

"Henry," Cassandra said.

"Yes."

"That is your paragraph."

"Yes."

"Don't let anyone touch it."

"I will not."

---

Thursday was the external work. Anika and the communications team built out the press release, the analyst briefing deck, the FAQ for the trade press, the four pre-recorded video segments with Henry that would be released at staggered times across the morning of the announcement, and the talking points for the regional spokespeople who would be answering questions in seventy-three countries simultaneously. Marcus and the IR team rehearsed the analyst call three times. Priya Bhattacharya and the legal team finalized the 8-K filing and walked the disclosure language past three different outside counsels. Vivian spent the entire day on the phone — first with her three direct reports, who learned the news for the first time at eight a.m. Thursday morning under embargo until Friday, then with the twelve regional retail VPs, who learned at eleven a.m., then with the forty-two warehouse general managers in the U.S., who learned at two p.m. Each call was direct. Each call ended with Vivian saying, in her own voice, the same sentence: *I am not leaving. I am running this transition. You are going to hear from me again within a week.*

By Thursday evening the document had been read by twenty-three people inside the company. None of them had leaked it. Daniela had been holding her breath about that since Monday afternoon. The fact that it had not leaked was, she thought, the first piece of evidence that the executive team was capable of doing what they were about to attempt.

At nine-forty Thursday night Henry sent the final document to the company-wide distribution list with a delivery time of eight a.m. Friday Pacific. The send was scheduled. The send was confirmed. The system would deliver it to one point six million inboxes in seventy-three countries in a one-minute window starting at eight o'clock the following morning.

Daniela left the building at ten-fifteen.

She did not go home immediately. She drove around Lake Union once. She drove past the warehouse in Renton where she had stood on the floor in late October and recognized, in front of forty-three companies' inventory, what the future of Argo was going to be. The Renton warehouse was dark. The next shift came in at five a.m. The next shift, the following morning, would arrive to a different company.

She drove home.

She did not sleep well.

---

The internal communication went out at eight a.m. Pacific on Friday morning.

The first reaction Daniela saw was an internal Slack channel — the executive-coordination channel she watched on her phone — lighting up with three reactions in the first ninety seconds. By eight-oh-four, the all-employee Slack workspaces in every region were the kind of pace that the platform's notification system started rate-limiting individual users. By eight-twelve, the document had been screenshotted and posted to three external sites, two of them tech-news, one of them general-business. By eight-eighteen, CNBC had a chyron up that read *ARGO ANNOUNCES MAJOR STRATEGIC SHIFT — DETAILS DEVELOPING.* By eight thirty-one, the Wall Street Journal had a story up online attributed to two reporters who had been on the Argo beat for nine and eleven years respectively.

The market opened at six-thirty Pacific.

The stock opened down nine percent in the first three minutes of trading.

By eight forty-five it was down fourteen.

Henry's phone, which had been on his desk on the eleventh floor since he had arrived at seven, rang at nine-oh-two. The caller ID was Karen Vu, the firm's lead institutional-investor-relations director, calling from her car on I-5 where she was already driving to the office. Henry answered.

"Karen."

"Henry."

"How many."

"Three so far. Two of the top-twenty are demanding calls within the hour. One is asking for an in-person meeting next week. The activist desk at Cromwell Croft just posted a research note. Their note says they are evaluating a position. The note is the polite version."

"Schedule the calls. I will take them in order. Push the rest to Marcus."

"Henry."

"Yes."

"This is going to be a long day."

"I know."

He hung up.

He looked across his office at Daniela, who was standing in the doorway in the dark suit she had worn to every announcement of this scale across nine years.

"Daniela."

"Yes."

"It is starting."

"It is starting."

"Are we ready."

"As ready as we are going to be."

"Then let's go."

He picked up the phone.

---

*Argo Inc., in the fourth week of November of Year 1, became the first company at industrial scale to commit publicly to the customer the AI era was about to require it to serve. The communication had been drafted in two days, by five people, in a small conference room on the eleventh floor of the Argo headquarters. It had taken thirty years to write. It would take three more to be lived. It would take four more after that for the 640,000 owners of Argo Retail Co. to hold the title to the business they had built.*

*End of Part II.*
