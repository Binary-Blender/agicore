# Chapter 3: The CFO's Spreadsheet

Marcus Goldberg was alone on the forty-second floor of the Argo headquarters at eleven-forty on a Wednesday night in early November, with the building cleaned out and the cleaning crews gone and the lights off in every office on the floor but his own, and a single brass desk lamp on the corner of his desk illuminating two monitors and a yellow legal pad and a cold cup of coffee.

The spreadsheet was on the left monitor.

The spreadsheet had three hundred and forty-two tabs.

He had been building it for seven days.

Marcus was fifty-six. He had been a partner at Goldman Sachs for eleven years before he had joined Argo nine years ago at fifty-six minus nine equals forty-seven. He had run the equity capital markets desk at Goldman from 2010 to 2017. He had built financial models in his career that had moved stocks by thirty percent on the day of publication. He had told three CEOs over the course of his career that the strategy they were committed to was not going to work. Two of them had thanked him for it. One of them had stopped speaking to him for four years and then had apologized.

He had never built a model that told him what this one was telling him.

He had been building models since he was twenty-three years old.

He had been building this one for a week.

He had run it forwards. He had run it backwards. He had run it conservative on every variable, moderate on every variable, aggressive on every variable. He had run it with the three possible recession scenarios his macro team had laid out at the September strategy offsite. He had run it with the four possible AI-adoption scenarios his strategy team had laid out at the August one. He had run it with the seven possible regulatory scenarios Priya Bhattacharya had drafted for him in October, ranging from *no further antitrust action through Year 5* to *forced divestiture of the cloud business by Year 4*.

The model was robust to all of it.

The model was robust to all of it in a way that was not making Marcus comfortable.

It was making him certain.

---

He stood up from the desk. He walked to the window. He stood there with his arms folded and looked out at downtown Seattle. The lights were on in the Russell building across the street. The lights were on in the Columbia Center. The lights were on in the Smith Tower, which was the oldest building visible from his office and was lit, as it had been lit every night for one hundred and twelve years, by amber sodium-vapor lamps in its peaked roof.

He had been at the office since seven that morning. His wife Ruth had called at nine. He had told her he would be home by midnight. He looked at the clock on the wall. He was not going to be home by midnight.

He walked back to the desk. He sat down.

The two model variants were open in adjacent tabs.

He had been comparing them for an hour.

The first variant was labeled, in the cell A1 of the variant's top tab, *CONVENTIONAL PIVOT.*

The second variant was labeled, in the cell A1 of the second variant's top tab, *AGGRESSIVE PIVOT.*

He had named the variants himself. He had spent ten minutes choosing the names. He had considered *INCREMENTAL* and *STRATEGIC* and *PHASED* and *DECISIVE.* He had settled on *CONVENTIONAL* and *AGGRESSIVE* because those were the words an analyst report would use, and Marcus had spent a career writing for analyst audiences and reading analyst reports, and he knew that the way you framed the variants was the way the variants would be discussed.

He had also chosen the words because they were honest.

The conventional pivot was conventional. The aggressive pivot was aggressive.

The conventional pivot would kill the company in seven years.

The aggressive pivot would save it.

---

He turned to the legal pad on the right side of the desk.

He had been keeping the legal pad open for seven days. He had been writing on it in pencil, in the small precise handwriting he had used for his entire career, in numbered lines. The numbered lines were the assumptions he had stress-tested. The numbered lines were what kept him honest.

He read the lines he had written that day.

*47. Retail growth assumption: model uses 2.4% YoY through Y2, -1.8% Y3, -4.4% Y4, -5.8% Y5, -6.1% Y6, -5.4% Y7. Tested against Walmart 2014-2018 retail-trough analog (corrected for Argo cost structure). Tested against Macy's 2017-2021. Tested against Sears 2012-2016. Argo's curve is closer to Walmart's than to the others, but the closer comparison is structurally weaker because Walmart's recovery was driven by re-anchoring on a value proposition Argo does not have access to in the same form.*

*48. Cloud growth assumption: 34% YoY through Y2, 28% Y3-Y4, 22% Y5, 18% Y6, 15% Y7. Tested against AWS internal trajectory. Tested against Microsoft Azure 2018-2023. Slightly conservative against both. Strategy team thinks I am too conservative. I am keeping the conservative number.*

*49. Carry growth assumption: this is the big one. The model has Carry at 78% YoY through Y2 (against the current 91% off the small base), 64% Y3, 48% Y4, 36% Y5, 28% Y6, 22% Y7. By Y7 Carry is approximately 31% of company revenue. Strategy team thinks I am too aggressive. Tomás's team thinks I am too conservative. I am taking the midpoint of the two and stress-testing both endpoints.*

*50. Capital reallocation assumption: aggressive variant requires redirection of approximately $18B of capital over thirty-six months from retail capex to platform capex. Conventional variant moves $4B. The $18B number stress-tested against the historical maximum reallocation Argo has executed in any twelve-month period ($6B in 2020). The number is achievable. It is not comfortable. Daniela's operational team will need eighteen months to execute the warehouse conversions that are the largest single line item.*

He read the lines.

He set the pencil down.

He picked up the cold coffee. He drank it cold. He set the cup down.

He looked at the two model variants on the monitor.

---

The conventional variant had been the model he had thought he was going to recommend two weeks ago. The conventional variant was the model a CFO with twenty-five years of Wall Street experience and a fiduciary responsibility to a public-company shareholder base would, in the absence of clear evidence to the contrary, recommend by default.

The conventional variant said: invest incrementally in Carry. Keep Argo Retail as the strategic priority. Communicate to the market that Carry is a growth opportunity within the retail-first identity of the company. Run AWS as the cash cow. Defend the retail moat with cost discipline and incremental product investment. Buy back stock to support the share price during the moat-erosion period. Be patient.

The conventional variant produced, on the model:

*Year 5 revenue: $804B. Up 11.6% on a five-year cumulative basis. Annualized growth: 2.2%.*
*Year 5 operating margin: 6.1%. Down from 8.4% at start of Year 1.*
*Year 5 free cash flow: $44B. Down from $61B at start of Year 1.*
*Year 5 equity valuation, at the trailing forward multiple Argo would be expected to trade at given the growth and margin profile: approximately $1.04 trillion. Down 39.8% from the start-of-Year-1 valuation of $1.73 trillion.*

The conventional variant was not catastrophic. The conventional variant did not end the company. The conventional variant produced a stagnant, larger-than-it-needed-to-be, lower-margin, lower-growth Argo that would, over time, be the kind of company Marcus had spent his career at Goldman taking apart for activist investors.

The conventional variant was the slow death.

---

He turned to the aggressive variant.

The aggressive variant said: invest in Carry at the level a strategic priority justifies. Communicate publicly that Argo is reorganizing as a pure platform company. Exit retail completely. Transfer the retail business — six hundred and forty thousand employees, the largest division in the company — to an employee stock ownership trust across five to seven years, with the parent company holding zero equity in any retailer by the end of Year 7. Argo Retail Co. would operate, post-spin, as a customer of Argo Carry on identical terms to every other tenant. The transition would be financed by a combination of standard ESOP leverage against the retail business's pre-tax earnings, a debt facility the parent would retire across the transition window, and a one-time founder contribution from Henry personally that Marcus had modeled at four point two billion dollars. Cut retail capex. Increase platform capex. Run the company at a deliberate revenue plateau through Year 3 in exchange for a healthier company at Year 7 — a company that would, for the first time in fifteen years, no longer be the structural defendant in every antitrust file open against the platform layer.

The aggressive variant produced, on the model:

*Year 5 revenue: $748B. Down 0.3% on a five-year cumulative basis. Annualized growth: -0.1%.*
*Year 5 operating margin: 14.8%. Up from 8.4% at start of Year 1.*
*Year 5 free cash flow: $98B. Up from $61B.*
*Year 5 equity valuation, at the trailing forward multiple Argo would be expected to trade at given the platform-margin profile: approximately $1.72 trillion. Roughly equal to start-of-Year-1 valuation. By Year 7: $2.41 trillion. Up 39%.*

The aggressive variant did not have a higher Year 5 revenue. The aggressive variant had a lower Year 5 revenue.

The aggressive variant had a much higher Year 5 margin. The aggressive variant had a much higher Year 5 cash flow. The aggressive variant had a Year 5 equity valuation that did not decline.

The aggressive variant produced an Argo that was, by Year 7, a better company than the one Marcus had walked into nine years ago.

---

He read the two variants side by side for a long time.

He had been a CFO for a long time. He had run sensitivity analyses on the comparison for the last forty-eight hours. He had pushed every assumption he could push. He had asked himself, in the cold accounting that the legal pad had been holding him to for a week, whether the aggressive variant was a fantasy.

It was not a fantasy.

The aggressive variant was, on the model, the better outcome by every metric except short-term revenue growth.

The short-term revenue growth was the metric Wall Street had paid Argo for, for thirty-one years.

The short-term revenue growth was the metric Marcus had spent his career at Goldman teaching companies how to deliver to Wall Street.

The short-term revenue growth was the metric Argo's institutional shareholder base had bought the stock to obtain.

If Argo abandoned short-term revenue growth in favor of a five-year reconstitution into a platform business, the stock was going to move.

Marcus had been around stock movements for thirty years. He knew how the stock was going to move.

The stock was going to move down between fifteen and thirty percent in the first three trading days. The stock was going to find a bottom somewhere between twenty and forty percent below pre-announcement levels within ten trading days. The stock was going to stay at that bottom for between four and nine months. The stock was going to start moving back up between Year 1 Q3 and Year 2 Q1, depending on how the operational milestones landed.

Marcus knew the institutional shareholder base by heart. He knew that approximately sixty percent of the shares were held by funds whose mandate was platform-growth-or-equivalent and would, in his read, hold through the pivot once the strategic case was made clearly enough. He knew that approximately twenty-five percent of the shares were held by funds whose mandate was retail-growth and would sell within the first quarter after the announcement. He knew that approximately ten percent of the shares were held by index funds, which would do whatever the index did, which was nothing. He knew that the remaining five percent was held by retail investors and individual holdings, which would do whatever they did and which were not the variable that mattered for the price movement.

He knew which two activist hedge funds were going to take positions inside of ten trading days. He had been at Goldman with the founder of one of them. He knew the playbook the activist was going to run. He knew the proxy fight that activist was going to try to start would, if Argo did its work properly, not get traction.

He knew the names of the four analysts who would publish *Underperform* notes inside of forty-eight hours.

He knew the names of the three analysts who would publish *Buy* notes inside of seventy-two hours, after they had read the strategic memo carefully.

He knew which CNBC anchor would call for Henry's resignation by Day 4.

He knew which Wall Street Journal columnist would write the cautious-but-favorable piece by Day 7.

He knew all of it.

He had been in this business his whole life.

He knew exactly what the next ninety days were going to look like, if Henry chose the aggressive variant on Monday.

---

He stood up again. He walked to the window. He looked at the Smith Tower's amber roof for a long minute.

He thought about the three CEOs in his career to whom he had delivered bad news.

The first had been in 2006. A regional bank CEO. Marcus had told him, in a model with thirty-one tabs, that the bank's residential-mortgage book was going to take a forty-percent write-down inside of eighteen months if the underwriting standards did not change. The CEO had thanked him. The CEO had changed the underwriting standards. The bank had survived 2008. Marcus had been right.

The second had been in 2011. A specialty-retail CEO. Marcus had told him, in a model with fifty-one tabs, that the company's e-commerce position relative to Amazon was going to make the bricks-and-mortar store base unprofitable inside of three years. The CEO had thanked him. The CEO had not changed the strategy. The company had filed for Chapter 11 in 2014. Marcus had been right.

The third had been in 2017, at Goldman, during a private engagement Marcus could no longer remember the specifics of in detail, but he remembered the CEO. The CEO had been a friend. The CEO had not spoken to Marcus for four years after Marcus had delivered the model. Marcus had been right. The CEO had eventually called Marcus and apologized and said: *Marcus. You were right. I was wrong. I have been waking up to it slowly. I am sorry it took me four years.* Marcus had said: *I am sorry the model said what it said.* They had been on good terms ever since.

He had been right three times.

He was right now.

He was about to deliver a model to a founder he respected — a founder he had taken the Argo job specifically to work with, a founder who he believed was the best living example of what a long-term operator could do at scale — that told that founder that the company the founder had spent thirty-one years building needed to die in its current form so that a better version could live.

It was not the first time he had had to do it.

It was the first time the recipient was someone he believed in this much.

He thought: *Henry will not stop speaking to me. Henry is not that kind of person. Henry will read the model. Henry will run his own check on it. Henry will look at the assumptions. Henry will challenge me on three or four of them. He will be right to challenge me on those three or four. We will work through them together. And then Henry will, on Monday, in the conference room on the eleventh floor, in front of Daniela and Cassandra and the senior team, present this model as the basis for the most consequential pivot of his career.*

He thought: *that is what is going to happen.*

He thought: *I have known Henry for nine years. He has changed his mind in public when the evidence shifts, more than once. He will change his mind here.*

He thought: *the question is not whether Henry will change his mind. The question is whether the board will change theirs.*

He thought: *Walter Brandt is not going to change his.*

He thought about Walter for a moment. He had been on the board with Walter for nine years. He liked Walter. Walter had been an institutional-investing executive for forty-one years before he had joined the Argo board. Walter knew what a platform pivot looked like. Walter would understand the model on a first read. Walter would also know, in a way Marcus knew Walter knew, that the model said the company Walter had been part of for twenty-three years needed to be dismantled.

Walter would not vote for it.

That was all right.

Marcus did not need Walter to vote for it.

Marcus needed eight of the eleven.

He thought he had eight.

---

It was twelve-forty.

He closed the conventional variant.

He left the aggressive variant open on the screen.

He opened a new document.

He had spent seven days on the model. He had spent zero time on the summary. He sat at the desk and wrote the summary in twenty-two minutes.

The summary was four pages. The first page was the comparison of the two variants in a single table. The second page was the assumptions, the assumptions tested, and the assumptions held. The third page was the cash flow trajectory of the aggressive variant in detail. The fourth page was the equity-market impact analysis — the move in the stock, the holders Marcus expected to sell, the holders Marcus expected to hold, the analyst response, the activist response, the recovery curve.

The summary was deliberately not a recommendation.

Marcus did not write recommendations. Marcus wrote analyses. The recipient of the analysis made the recommendation.

He attached the full model. He attached the legal-pad assumptions list, which he had scanned earlier in the evening from the actual yellow pad on his desk. He attached a one-page glossary of terms in case anyone in the room on Monday was not fluent in the language.

He opened his email client.

He typed the addresses.

He typed Henry first. Then Daniela. Then Cassandra Yu. Then Tomás Aguilar. Then Vivian Reyes. Then Frank Lattimore. Then Priya Bhattacharya.

Seven recipients.

He typed the subject line.

*The Numbers.*

He typed the body.

*I have run this six different ways. I have one conclusion. The conclusion is in the attached.*

He read it once.

He did not edit it.

He attached the four-page summary, the model, the assumptions list, and the glossary.

He looked at the screen.

It was twelve fifty-eight.

He clicked send.

The screen showed *Sending...* for two seconds.

The screen showed *Sent.*

He sat in the chair for a long minute.

He thought: *that is the most important email I have sent in nine years at this company.*

He thought: *six of the seven people will read it within an hour of waking up.*

He thought: *Henry will read it inside of forty minutes of opening his phone.*

He thought: *Vivian will read it and her stomach will go cold.*

He thought: *Cassandra will read it and smile.*

He thought: *Tomás will read it and clear his Monday calendar.*

He thought: *Daniela will read it and call me.*

He stood up. He turned off the desk lamp. He closed the laptop. He took his coat off the back of the door. He walked out of the office. He walked the long corridor past the dark glass-walled offices of the other senior executives, none of whom were there, all of whom would be at Monday's meeting. He took the elevator down to the underground garage. He drove home on I-5 north with the radio off and the wipers on intermittent and his hands at ten and two.

He pulled into his driveway in Madison Park at one-thirty-five.

Ruth was asleep.

He did not wake her.

He set his keys on the kitchen counter. He drank a glass of water at the sink. He went upstairs. He undressed in the dark. He got into bed.

He slept inside of three minutes.

---

Henry Castellan woke at five-fifty on Thursday morning.

He had slept badly. He had slept badly every night for the seventeen days since Daniela had walked into his office on a Friday afternoon and told him what the math was telling her. He had also, in the seventeen days, been preparing. The day with Cassandra on Monday had been the spine of the preparation. The conversations with Daniela on Tuesday and again on Wednesday afternoon had been the structure that hung on the spine. The meetings with Tomás and with Frank Lattimore on Wednesday had been the operational layer.

Marcus's model was the part that had not yet arrived.

Henry was awake at four-ten. Marina was already gone — she had a five-thirty flight to a project site in Vancouver and Liu had logged the gate clearance for the second car at four. He walked downstairs in the dim hallway lights and through the breezeway to the gym wing. Travis was already there with the first set racked. Henry warmed up. He pulled three-eighty for triples. He moved to the bench. He read his phone between sets.

He had two hundred and forty-three emails since he had gone to bed.

He sorted by sender.

There was one from Marcus Goldberg, sent at 12:58 AM.

The subject line was *The Numbers.*

Henry sat on the bench with the bar racked above him and read the email and opened the attachment and read the first page.

He read it twice.

He read the second page.

He read the third.

He read the fourth.

He closed the attachment. He set the phone down on the bench beside him.

He stood up.

Travis was at the squat rack putting plates on for the next movement.

"Travis."

"Yes."

"I'm done."

"Set."

"I'm done. I have to go in."

Travis nodded. He did not ask why. He took the plates off the bar.

Henry showered in the gym wing in four minutes. He came back through to the main house in the suit he had laid out the night before. He drank a glass of water at the sink. He picked the phone back up. He texted Cassandra: *Marcus came in. Monday.* Cassandra texted back inside twenty seconds: *I have it. See you Monday.*

The Suburban was at the front door at four fifty-eight. Liu was in the passenger seat. The follow car was four lengths down the drive with its headlights on. Henry got in. He read the rest of Marcus's attachment in the back seat on the way to the ferry.

---

The CFO had run the model six different ways and had not been able to make it say anything but the same thing. The founder had read the email at four-thirty AM on the morning after it had been sent, between sets on the bench in the home gym, and had been in the Suburban heading to the office inside of forty minutes. The COO had spent the night reading the attachment in bed with her husband asleep beside her and had texted Marcus at four AM with the sentence *We are going to do this.* The board chair had read the email at seven, on the terrace of her apartment in Belltown, with her coffee and her newspaper, and had not been surprised by what the model said because she had been waiting for the model to say it for six months. The general counsel had read the email at seven-thirty and had cleared her Friday calendar. The cloud EVP had read the email at six-forty and had called his head of platform engineering before seven. The retail EVP had read the email at six-fifteen in the kitchen of her house in Medina, with her two daughters eating cereal at the table beside her, and had set the phone down on the counter and stood at the counter for a full two minutes without moving.

Seven recipients.

Seven readings.

Three days until the meeting.

The board meeting was the following Monday.

The rest of it started then.
