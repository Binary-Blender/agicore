# Chapter 10: The Retail VP

Vivian Reyes stood up at the podium of the Wells Fargo Arena in Des Moines, Iowa, on the second Tuesday of March, and looked at four thousand two hundred employees who had been bused in from every Argo Retail facility within a three-hundred-mile radius, and read the first paragraph of the statement she had written by hand, the night before, in her hotel room.

"My name is Vivian Reyes. I run Argo Retail. I am here to tell you what is happening to our division. I am going to tell you in plain language. I am going to take your questions. I am going to stay in this room with you until every person who wants to ask a question has asked it. The schedule for this town hall is open-ended. We will be here as long as we need to be."

The room was quiet.

"Argo Retail is eliminating eighty-four thousand positions over the next twelve months. The reductions are part of the company's strategic shift to a platform model. The reductions will affect every region, every function, and every level of the organization. The reductions will be communicated to individuals through a process that begins on April first and runs through the end of the calendar year. The roles affected are in the categories the platform model no longer requires at the same scale. The roles not affected are in the categories the new model continues to require, sometimes at higher levels."

She paused.

"I want to say something before I take questions."

She set the paper down.

"I am not here to tell you this is good news. It is not good news. Some of you in this room are going to lose your jobs. Some of you in this room are going to keep your jobs. Some of you in this room are going to be told in May that you are losing your job and then told in August that there is a different job for you somewhere else in the company if you want it. I am not going to pretend I can tell you, today, which category you are in. I do not have the individual lists with me. I will not have the individual lists for several weeks. I came here to tell you what was happening before you read it in the press, not to tell you what was happening to you specifically. The specifics will come. They will come on a schedule. The schedule is in the packet you received as you came in. I am asking you to read the packet."

She picked the paper back up.

"Now I will take questions."

A hand went up in the third row.

She pointed.

The man stood. He was wearing the navy polo shirt of an Argo Retail warehouse supervisor. He said, "Vivian. How do you sleep."

The room held its breath.

She did not flinch.

She said, "Some nights I sleep. Some nights I don't. The nights I don't, I am up at four AM thinking about the people in this room and in thirty-three other rooms like this one over the next three weeks. That is the honest answer. I do not have a better one. I do not deserve one."

The man sat back down.

The next question came from a woman in the eighth row.

The town hall ran four hours and twenty minutes.

---

Vivian did thirty-four town halls in three weeks.

She did them across thirty-four cities. She flew commercial. She did not bring an entourage. She brought a deputy from her communications team and a paralegal from Priya Bhattacharya's office and her chief of staff. The four of them traveled together. They did not stay in different hotels. They did not eat in different restaurants. They sat together at the same gates and on the same planes and in the same hotel restaurants, and Vivian read the question-and-answer briefings on the planes and the cars and the early mornings before the events.

She did them in Des Moines, Indianapolis, Columbus, Memphis, Nashville, Charlotte, Atlanta, Jacksonville, Orlando, Tampa, Birmingham, Dallas, Houston, San Antonio, Phoenix, Las Vegas, Salt Lake City, Denver, Kansas City, St. Louis, Chicago, Milwaukee, Minneapolis, Cleveland, Pittsburgh, Detroit, Philadelphia, Newark, Boston, Hartford, Albany, Buffalo, Sacramento, and Portland.

She did not delegate any of them.

The third week was the hardest. By the time she got to Buffalo, on a Thursday in late March, she had not been home for nineteen days and she had lost six pounds and she had developed a cough that her chief of staff had been trying, unsuccessfully, to get her to see a doctor about.

She did the Buffalo town hall.

She flew to Albany.

She did the Albany town hall.

She flew home to Seattle on the Saturday of the third week.

She slept for fourteen hours.

She got up on Sunday morning and made coffee and sat at the kitchen counter and her husband Daniel sat down next to her with his own coffee and said, "Vivian."

"Yes."

"You are going to see a doctor on Monday."

"Daniel."

"Vivian. You are going to see a doctor on Monday."

She nodded.

She saw the doctor on Monday. The cough was bronchitis. She got an antibiotic. She took a week of half-days. She came back to a full schedule on April third.

---

The communications about specific positions began on April first.

The process was, by Daniela Park's design, methodical and humane in the ways such processes can be. Each affected employee received a one-on-one meeting with their direct manager. Each meeting was scheduled. Each meeting was thirty minutes long. Each meeting was followed by a written summary that documented the conversation, the severance terms, the benefits-continuation terms, and the transition support available. Each affected employee was assigned an internal HR partner for the next ninety days. Each affected employee had the right to apply to any open position elsewhere in the company. Each affected employee had access to a six-month outplacement service.

The process was not perfect. The process generated, in the first three weeks, eleven hundred internal grievances. Vivian read the summary of every grievance. She personally reviewed forty-seven of them where the case suggested the process had gone wrong. She personally reversed eleven decisions where the case showed the manager had failed to apply the criteria correctly. She personally apologized, in writing, to each of the eleven employees whose decisions were reversed.

Cassandra Yu called her on a Wednesday in mid-April.

"Vivian."

"Cassie."

"You are reading every grievance."

"I am reading every grievance."

"That is not scalable."

"I know it isn't. I am going to do it through May anyway."

"Why."

"Because the eighty-four thousand are the consequence of a decision I helped make. The decision was the right decision. I am going to make sure the execution is the right execution. The execution is in the grievances. I am going to read them."

Cassandra did not say anything for a beat.

Then she said, "All right."

She paused.

"Vivian."

"Yes."

"You are doing this the way the company is going to remember it being done. Twenty years from now, the people who are still here are going to remember that you read every grievance. The people who are not still here are going to remember it too."

"Cassie."

"Yes."

"I am not doing it to be remembered."

"I know. That is the part that is going to be remembered."

She hung up.

Vivian sat at her desk for a minute.

Then she opened the next file.

---

The ESOP transition structure was announced on a Thursday in June.

The press release ran eight pages. The first four pages laid out the corporate structure and the transition mechanics. Argo, Inc. would remain the parent company. Argo Retail Co. would be established as a separate Delaware corporation effective January first of the following year, capitalized by contribution from Argo, Inc. of the operating assets of the retail business. Concurrent with the capitalization, a chartered Employee Stock Ownership Plan trust would be established for the benefit of all Argo Retail Co. employees, governed by an independent trustee firm and a fiduciary committee of seven members (three appointed by Argo, Inc. for the first three years, transitioning by Year Five to four worker-elected seats with two independent fiduciaries and one Argo, Inc. seat that would expire at the completion of the transition). The ESOP trust would acquire Argo, Inc.'s equity in Argo Retail Co. in tranches across the transition window. The first tranche — eight percent — would transfer at closing on January first, funded by the company's own pre-tax earnings under standard leveraged-ESOP mechanics plus an initial $4.2 billion contribution from Henry Castellan personally. Subsequent tranches would transfer on a publicly disclosed glidepath through Year Seven, at the end of which Argo, Inc. would hold zero equity in Argo Retail Co. and the ESOP trust would hold one hundred percent. Argo Retail Co. would operate as a tenant on the Argo Carry platform on terms identical to those offered to every other tenant.

The fifth page of the press release named the leadership team. Vivian Reyes, Interim Chief Executive Officer of Argo Retail Co. The interim title was the deliberate choice. The mandate was to manage the company through the transition, build the worker-governance infrastructure, and hand the operation to a worker-elected board of directors no later than the closing of the final ESOP tranche in Year Seven. Three of her existing direct reports would join her in the new entity. The other six would either move to Argo Carry or to other parts of the parent company.

The sixth and seventh pages were quotes from Henry Castellan and from the executive director of the ESOP Association, who had been a consulting party to the structure for four months. The eighth page was a quote from Vivian.

Vivian had written her own quote.

It said: *The Argo Retail business serves over two hundred million customers in twenty-six countries. Six hundred and forty thousand people built it. Six hundred and forty thousand people are going to own it. My job between now and Year Seven is to make sure the business that gets handed to them is a business worth owning. I will lead the transition. I am looking forward to the work.*

The conversation in which Vivian had accepted the CEO role had taken place six weeks earlier, in Henry's office, on a Thursday in late April.

It had lasted forty minutes.

---

Henry had set the conversation up the previous Friday. He had called her from his car on his way home from the office. He had said: *Vivian. Next Thursday. Three o'clock. My office. I want to talk about the new structure.* She had said, *I'll be there.* She had hung up.

She had spent the weekend preparing.

She had spent it not by reading documents but by sitting on her back deck on Bainbridge Island with a notebook and writing down, in her own handwriting, what she wanted out of the next five years of her career. She had spent two hours on Saturday morning writing the first version. She had spent another hour on Sunday morning writing the second. The second version had been three pages long. The third version, which she had written on Monday night, had been one page.

The one page had three bullets.

The first bullet said: *I want to run a real business with real customers and real operating accountability.*

The second bullet said: *I want the business to be honest about what it is. I want it to compete on its merits. I do not want a structure that pretends the business is something it is not.*

The third bullet said: *I want the people who come with me to be people who want this work, not people who are coming because they cannot get other work.*

She had folded the page. She had put it in the front of her notebook. She had brought it to the meeting.

---

Henry's office at three.

Henry had cleared his calendar. He had told his assistant Eliza to hold the door and not put anyone through. He had a pot of coffee on the side table. He poured two cups. He brought one to Vivian. He sat down in the chair across from her.

"Vivian."

"Henry."

"I am going to lay out what I am thinking. Then I want to hear what you are thinking. Then we are going to figure out together what the right structure is."

"All right."

He laid it out.

He spoke for fifteen minutes. He explained the ESOP transition structure — the trust mechanics, the seven-year glidepath, the worker-elected board succession, the $4.2 billion contribution he was making personally, the legal and fiduciary architecture the outside ESOP advisory had been building for four months. He explained the operating relationship with the parent company during the transition. He explained the platform-tenant relationship with Argo Carry, which would be on identical terms to every other tenant from the closing date. He explained the interim governance structure — three Argo, Inc. seats on the fiduciary committee through Year Three, transitioning to four worker-elected seats and two independent fiduciaries by Year Five, with the final Argo, Inc. seat expiring at the end of Year Seven. He explained the compensation structure for Vivian personally during the transition, which was structured to align her with the long-term health of the business that would be owned, by the end, by people who were not her. He explained the timeline. He explained what would not change — Vivian's reporting line to him for the next twelve months as the new entity stood up, her access to the parent company's resources during the transition, her seat at the parent company's executive team meetings through the end of Year 2.

He stopped.

He said, "I want you to be the interim CEO of Argo Retail Co. I want you to build the transition. I want you to run it. I am asking you because no one else in this company can do what this role requires. The role requires running a real operating business through a seven-year ownership transfer while simultaneously building the worker-governance infrastructure that the business will be handed off to. The role requires negotiating with a fiduciary committee that does not work for you. The role requires teaching six hundred and forty thousand people how to own the company they work at. The role requires handing it to them and walking away. The new business needs someone who knows the retail customer better than anyone in this company. The new business needs someone who can do the hard parts of running a focused operation while doing the harder parts of giving it away. The new business needs you."

He paused.

"I also want to say something else. The role is smaller than the role you have now in some ways. Your authority inside the parent company will shrink. You will not run the largest division anymore because the largest division will not exist anymore. You will run an interim CEO role with a defined endpoint at Year Seven. After that you will have built something that has never existed at this scale before and you will hand it to the people who built it with you. The role is a smaller role in some real ways. It is also a more honest role than the role you have now. It is the role the business actually has. I want you to think about whether the smaller and more honest role is the role you want to do."

She did not say anything for a beat.

Then she said, "Henry."

"Yes."

"I have already thought about it."

"And."

"This is the right structure. I will run the transition. I would rather hand a real business to the people who built it than run a fake one for an owner who would be sued for its existence every year for the next decade."

He looked at her.

"Vivian."

"Yes."

"Thank you."

She nodded.

"There are conditions."

"Tell me."

She opened her notebook. She read the three bullets.

He listened.

When she was done, he said: "Yes to all three. The first one is the easiest yes I have ever given. The second one is the answer to the question the whole company has been asking for a year. The third one is the right answer. I will help you choose the people who come with you. The choice is yours."

"All right."

"Vivian."

"Yes."

"I want you to know one more thing."

"Yes."

"When Argo Retail Co. is two years into the transition and the first two ESOP tranches have closed and the worker-governance scaffolding is in place, I am going to ask the board to give you a seat on the parent company's board. Not yet. Not at the start. When the new structure has been demonstrated. When the new entity is healthy. When the company can see who you are in the new role. Two years out. I want you on the board of Argo, Inc., as the interim CEO of Argo Retail Co., until the day the ESOP is one hundred percent vested and Argo Retail's board is its own. The seat is yours through that day. After that day you and I sit down and we figure out what you want to do next. That is the offer."

She looked at him.

"Henry."

"Yes."

"I would accept that."

"Good."

He stood up.

She stood up.

They shook hands.

She walked out of the office.

She walked down the hall to her own office.

She closed the door.

She sat at her desk for ten minutes without moving.

Then she opened her notebook and started writing down the names of the people she wanted to bring with her.

---

The personal cost came in waves through July and August.

Daniel said, on a Sunday morning in mid-July, *Viv. I want to take a week off in August. Just the two of us. Somewhere where we don't have to talk about the company.* She said, *Yes.* They went to Sun Valley in the second week of August. They hiked. They did not talk about Argo. They drank wine. They came home on Sunday night. She was at work on Monday at six.

Her older daughter, who was twenty-three and in graduate school at Berkeley, called her on a Saturday in late July and said, *Mom. Are you OK.* Vivian said, *I am OK. I am tired.* Her daughter said, *I read the piece in the Times. The one about the layoffs.* Vivian said, *I read it too.* Her daughter said, *Was it fair.* Vivian said, *It was fair.* Her daughter said, *I love you, Mom.* Vivian said, *I love you too.* They got off the phone.

The bronchitis came back in early August. The doctor put her on a second course of antibiotics. She took a week of mornings off. She came back at full speed by the third week.

Two of her direct reports left the company in July.

The first was Maria DeStefano, who had been the SVP of Argo Retail Stores — the small physical-retail footprint Argo had built across forty-six U.S. cities. Maria had been with the company for fifteen years. The new structure did not have a place for her function at her level. Vivian offered her a smaller role inside Argo Retail Inc. Maria thought about it for two weeks. Then she said, *Vivian. I have done what I came to do here. I want to go run something of my own.* She took a CEO role at a regional retail chain in the Midwest. The departure was clean. Vivian threw her a farewell dinner. They cried twice during the toasts.

The second was Anand Iyer.

Anand had been the VP of Merchandising for eight years. He had been at Argo for nineteen. He was fifty-three. He was, of Vivian's eleven direct reports, the one she had worked with longest, fought with most, and trusted most. He had been the person she had called from the airport on her way to Des Moines in March. He had been the person she had called from her hotel room in Buffalo in March. He had been the person who had read the third draft of every town-hall script.

He had told her in mid-July that he was leaving.

He had not been forced out. There was a role for him in the new subsidiary. The role was, in his judgment, smaller than he wanted to take. He had been offered a chief commercial officer role at a luxury-goods company in New York. He had thought about it for three weeks. He had told Vivian on a Tuesday afternoon in her office.

She had said, *Anand. I understand.* She had said, *Thank you for telling me before anyone else.* She had said, *We will throw you a dinner.* She had said, *I want it to be the two of us first. Just one dinner. Just us. Before anyone else.*

He had said, *Yes.*

The dinner was on the last Friday of August.

---

The restaurant was a steakhouse two blocks from the Argo headquarters. It had been there for thirty years. Vivian and Anand had eaten there, together or with others, more times than either of them could count.

They sat at a corner table on the second floor. The waiter knew them. He brought them a bottle of wine without asking. He brought them the menu they did not need.

They ordered.

They drank the first glass of wine.

Anand said, "Vivian."

"Yes."

"Tell me a story."

"What kind of story."

"An old one."

She laughed.

She told him the story of the second-quarter board meeting of Year minus eleven, when she and Anand and a third person, who had since left the company, had argued for ninety minutes against the existing CEO of Argo Retail (the one before her) about a private-label expansion strategy they had thought was wrong. They had won the argument. The expansion had been scaled back. The previous CEO had taken six months to forgive her. He had eventually forgiven her. He had retired four years later. She had taken his job.

Anand laughed.

He told her the story of the supplier visit to Bangladesh in Year minus seven, when they had spent four days walking factories with two other executives and had eaten dinner at a small place near the hotel that had served the best fish curry he had ever had in his life, and how he had tried for nine years to find the recipe and had never found it. He had cooked seventeen versions of it. None had been right.

She laughed.

They told stories for two hours.

They ordered a second bottle.

They told more stories.

Near the end of the meal, Anand set down his fork.

He looked at her across the table.

He said, "Vivian."

"Yes."

"You handled this. I want you to know I noticed."

She did not say anything for a beat.

She had not heard those words from anyone inside the company for the eight months since the announcement. She had heard them from Daniel. She had heard them from her daughter. She had heard them from her own mother. She had not heard them from anyone inside the building. Not from Henry, who had told her many other things but not that one. Not from Cassandra, who had told her she was doing the work the company would remember being done. Not from Daniela, who had been doing her own work and had not had time to say it. Not from Tomás, who had shaken her hand in the cafeteria in February and let the handshake be the saying of it.

She had been waiting for one person inside the building to say those exact words.

She looked at Anand across the table.

She said, "Thank you. I noticed I was handling it."

He laughed.

She laughed.

She also, in the same moment, felt her eyes fill. She picked up her water glass. She drank from it. She set it back down. She did not look at him for a moment.

Across the table, Anand picked up his own water glass. He drank from it. He set it back down. He did not look at her.

They sat for a moment.

They did not acknowledge what they had each just done.

The waiter brought the check.

Vivian paid.

They walked out onto the sidewalk together. It was a warm Seattle night in late August. The light was the long horizontal light Seattle had on August evenings when the sun was setting behind the Olympics.

Anand said, "Vivian."

"Yes."

"I will see you."

"I know."

"Take care of the new entity."

"I will."

They embraced.

They walked in opposite directions down the sidewalk.

Vivian got into a car a block away. She rode home. She walked into her house. Daniel was reading on the couch. He looked up.

He said, "How was dinner."

She said, "He told me I handled it."

Daniel set the book down.

He said, "Come here."

She sat next to him.

She put her head on his shoulder.

She said, "I think I'm going to sleep for a while."

He said, "Sleep."

She slept.

The work would continue Monday. The Argo Retail Co. stand-up would begin in earnest in September. The new entity would launch on January first along with the first ESOP tranche transfer. The eighty-four thousand reductions would be complete by November. The platform would carry on growing. The parent company would keep transforming. The board would meet again in October. The press would write more pieces. Some pieces would be fair. Some would not.

Vivian Reyes, on the last Friday of August in the second year of the pivot, slept on her husband's shoulder for two hours on the couch in the living room of the house they had bought sixteen years earlier, and she did not think about Argo for any of those two hours, and when she woke up Daniel was still there and the lamp was still on and the book was still open on his lap, and she said, *Daniel,* and he said, *Vivian,* and she stood up and walked upstairs with him and went to bed.

The next morning she got up at six.

The work continued.
