# Chapter 9: The Warehouse in Goodyear

Eric Madsen worked the four-A shift at the Argo fulfillment center in Goodyear, Arizona. Four AM to two PM. He had been working that shift for six years. He had started on the swing shift, moved to mornings after his second year, and stayed on mornings because the schedule worked for the family. Maya worked the day shift at the Argo customer-service site three miles down the road. The kids were at the daycare on Bullard Avenue between seven and four. He picked them up. She started dinner. The schedule had been the schedule for four years.

The schedule changed on a Tuesday in early February.

He had clocked in at 3:54 AM. He had walked the four hundred yards from the parking lot to the warehouse floor in the dark. The Goodyear winters were dry and the mornings were cold and he had been wearing the same fleece jacket for three years. He swiped in at the badge reader at 3:58. He logged into the picking-station handheld at 4:01.

At 4:04 the announcement came over the floor PA.

*All hands. Mandatory meeting at six AM. Main floor. Center pad. No exceptions.*

He looked up at the speaker.

He looked at the picker next to him. Her name was Carla. They had worked the same aisle for fourteen months.

"Carla."

"Yeah."

"You hear that."

"I heard it."

"What's the meeting."

"I don't know."

He kept picking.

He thought about the meeting for the next two hours.

---

The meeting started at six.

Fourteen hundred workers on the center pad of the warehouse floor. The pad was the open area near the inbound dock where they did the quarterly all-hands and the safety briefings and, twice in Eric's six years, the meetings that came down from corporate with bad news.

The general manager was a man named Hector Reyna. Hector had been running the Goodyear facility for four years. He was forty-six. He had three kids. He had worked his way up from the floor at a different Argo facility in Texas over twelve years.

He stood on the loading-dock step. He held a piece of paper. He did not look at it. He had memorized what was on it.

"Good morning."

He waited a beat.

"I'm going to tell you what I know. I'm going to tell you what I don't know. I'm going to take questions for as long as you have them. We are not going to start picking again until we are done with this conversation. The schedule is suspended."

The room was quiet.

"This facility is being converted from Argo Retail fulfillment to Argo Carry platform fulfillment. The conversion was announced this morning. The work begins immediately. The conversion will take approximately ninety days. During that period this facility will receive less inventory from Argo Retail and more inventory from third-party retailers. By the end of the conversion this facility will be handling the inventory of approximately forty-three different companies. None of those companies will be Argo Retail."

He paused.

"The conversion affects every job in this building. Some jobs are going away. Some jobs are changing. Some jobs are getting bigger. The new operating model needs fewer total workers in certain roles and more total workers in certain other roles. The math, when it all sorts out, is this. Of the fourteen hundred of us in this building, three hundred and twenty of us will be laid off. Two hundred and eighty will be retained at the same wage. Eight hundred will be retained with a wage increase, because the platform-fulfillment work is more complex and the pay band is higher. Twelve will be promoted into newly created mid-level positions."

He paused again.

"I'm going to read those numbers one more time."

He read them again.

The room stayed quiet.

"You will get a letter today. The letter will tell you which of those four categories you are in. The letters will go out by close of shift. If you are in the three hundred and twenty, the letter will tell you your last day, the severance terms, the benefits-continuation terms, and the outplacement services Argo is providing. If you are in the eight hundred, the letter will tell you your new wage and your new role. If you are in the two hundred and eighty, the letter will tell you your role is unchanged. If you are in the twelve, the letter will tell you what your new title is and what your new responsibilities are."

He looked at the floor.

"I want to say one thing. I have been at this company for twelve years. I have worked alongside many of you for the last four. I did not choose who got which letter. The decisions were made at the regional level based on the new operating model and the skill mapping. I read every name on every list before the lists were finalized. I did not pull any names. I did not change any decisions. I am not going to pretend that I would have, even if I could have. The decisions are what they are. I am sorry for the three hundred and twenty of you. I will help where I can. The questions are open."

A man near the back raised his hand.

Hector pointed.

The man said, "Hector. When do we get our letters."

"By close of shift today."

A woman in the middle raised her hand.

"What kind of outplacement."

"Six months of services through a third-party firm. Resume writing, interview prep, job-search assistance. There is a separate Argo internal transfer program. If you want to relocate to another facility, we will help you. Three other facilities in the region have openings. They are not in Goodyear. They are in Phoenix, Tucson, and El Paso."

Eric raised his hand.

Hector pointed.

Eric said, "Hector. Severance terms."

"Two weeks per year of service. Capped at sixteen weeks. Plus three months of continued health insurance."

Eric nodded.

He did not ask anything else.

The questions went on for forty minutes.

When the meeting ended Hector said, *Picking resumes at seven. Take twenty minutes. Get a coffee. Talk to your friends. Then back to it.* The room broke up. Eric walked to the break room with Carla. They did not talk. He bought a coffee from the vending machine. He drank it standing up. He thought about Maya.

---

He got the letter at one-fifteen.

It was in his locker. White envelope. Argo logo. His name printed in the address window.

He opened it.

He was in the eight hundred.

His new wage was twenty-six dollars and forty cents an hour. His old wage had been twenty-two dollars and ten cents. The letter said the wage increase would take effect on the first pay period of the new operating model, which was approximately ten weeks out. His new role was Platform Operations Specialist, Tier 2. The letter included a one-page description of the role. It included responsibilities he had not had before. It included some software training he was going to do over the next three weeks.

He read the letter twice.

He folded it. He put it in his back pocket.

He went back to the picking station.

He picked for the rest of his shift.

He did not call Maya.

He drove home at two-twenty.

He pulled into the driveway at two-forty.

Maya's car was already there.

That was the first thing he noticed.

Her shift did not end until four-thirty. She did not get home before five.

Her car was in the driveway at two-forty.

He sat in the truck for a moment with the engine off.

He got out.

He went inside.

---

Maya was at the kitchen table.

She had not changed out of her work clothes. She had a glass of water in front of her. She had a piece of paper next to the water. He could see the Argo logo on the paper from the door.

She looked up.

She had been crying.

She was not crying now.

"Maya."

"Eric."

"What happened."

"I got laid off."

He set his keys on the counter.

He sat down across from her.

He did not say anything for a moment.

Then he said, "I got the wage increase."

She looked at him.

"How much."

"Four-thirty an hour. Twenty-six forty."

She did the math in her head.

"That's about nine thousand a year."

"Yes."

She did not say anything.

He did not say anything.

The kitchen clock ticked. The refrigerator hummed. The dog, who was a six-year-old mutt named Pepper they had rescued from the shelter in Phoenix three years earlier, walked into the kitchen and put his head on Maya's leg. She put her hand on his head.

She said, "I have to pick up the kids."

"I'll go."

"I'll go. I need to drive."

"Maya."

"Eric. I need to drive. Stay here. Start the rice. I'll be back in twenty."

She got up. She picked up her keys. She walked out.

He sat at the table for a long minute.

Then he stood up. He got the rice out of the cabinet. He measured it. He started the rice cooker. He chopped an onion. He chopped a bell pepper. He pulled chicken thighs out of the freezer to thaw under cold water. He moved through the kitchen the way he had moved through the picking aisles, which was to say, with his hands knowing where things were and his mind somewhere else.

His mind was on the household budget.

He had been doing that math, in his head, since he had walked out to the truck.

The math was not impossible. The math was hard. They had been a two-income household. They had bought the house in Year minus two on the assumption of two incomes. They had a mortgage of two thousand four hundred dollars a month. They had a car payment of three hundred twenty. They had daycare at fifteen hundred. They had a credit card with a balance of about four thousand from the move and the new bedroom set. They had a savings account with about nine thousand in it. They had a 401(k) with about thirty-six thousand in it, which they were not going to touch.

His wage increase was about nine thousand a year. Maya's wage at the Argo customer-service site had been about forty-six thousand a year. The math, before counting severance, was a net loss of about thirty-seven thousand a year. The math, after counting Maya's severance of about thirteen thousand and three months of insurance continuation, was a one-time cushion that bought them about four months at the current burn rate.

Maya was going to have to find work fast.

The math told him that.

He set the chicken in the pan.

He heard the garage door open.

---

The kids came in.

Mateo was four. Sofia was two. Mateo had a paper crown on his head from daycare and a smear of something blue on his cheek. Sofia was in Maya's arms, asleep.

"Daddy."

"Hi buddy."

"I made a crown."

"I see that."

"Daddy what's the blue."

"That's on your cheek. We'll wash it after dinner."

"OK."

Mateo ran into the living room. Maya carried Sofia down the hall to put her down on the couch with a blanket. She came back into the kitchen. She washed her hands.

She said, "Is the rice on."

"Rice is on. Chicken is going."

"OK."

She set the table.

They ate at six.

They did not talk about the letters in front of the kids.

The kids ate. Mateo told them a story about a turtle at daycare. Sofia woke up halfway through dinner and ate three bites of rice and fell back asleep in her chair. They cleaned up. They put the kids to bed at seven-thirty. Mateo asked Eric for two stories instead of one. Eric read both. He came back downstairs at quarter to nine.

Maya was on the couch.

The TV was off.

She had a glass of wine.

He sat down next to her.

She said, "I want to think for a few days before we make any decisions."

"OK."

"I don't want to take a worse job tomorrow because I'm scared. I want to look for two weeks and see what's out there."

"OK."

"We have the severance."

"We have the severance."

"We have the savings."

"We have the savings."

She breathed out.

"Eric."

"Yes."

"I'm proud of you for the promotion."

He looked at her.

"Maya."

"I am. I am proud of you. I want to say it now because I might not be able to say it later. I am proud of you. The new job is a good thing. It's a good thing for our family. I am going to be glad about it as soon as I am done being scared about my own situation."

"Maya."

"Don't say anything. Just sit here with me."

He sat with her.

They sat on the couch for an hour.

Then they went to bed.

---

The training started the next Monday.

It ran three weeks. Eric did it in shifts that overlapped his old picking schedule. Half the day was training. Half the day was picking under the old operating model. The picking was winding down. The new inventory was starting to arrive. The first third-party retailer's products came in on a Wednesday in the second week. It was a small home-goods company from Oregon. Eric did not know the name. He saw the boxes come off the truck. He logged them into the new inventory system. He moved them to the platform-aisle slots Tomás Aguilar's team had designed and Hector's team had built out.

The new aisles were different.

The old aisles had been organized by Argo's product taxonomy — books here, kitchenware there, electronics in the back. The new aisles were organized by tenant — Tenant 14 (an apparel brand from Brooklyn) here, Tenant 22 (a kitchenware company from Atlanta) there, Tenant 41 (the home-goods company from Oregon) in the back. Each tenant had its own aisle. Each aisle had its own shelf taxonomy, determined by the tenant. Each aisle's inventory was tracked separately in the new system. Each aisle was, in operational terms, a small business inside the warehouse.

Eric found, after the first two weeks, that he liked the new system.

He liked it because the work was more varied. Different tenant aisles had different workflows. Some required white-glove handling. Some required slip-sheet repackaging. Some required custom return-label workflows that the old Argo retail process had not used. The handheld scanner showed him, for each pick, the tenant-specific procedure. He had to read it. He had to think. He had to make decisions. He had not, in the old picking job, had to make decisions for years.

He told Carla, in the break room on a Friday in the third week of training, *I think I like this better.* Carla had been in the same training cohort. She said, *Yeah. Me too. I'm not telling Maya that.*

He had not told Maya that either.

He was telling Maya, at home, the things that were hardest about the new work. The cognitive load. The unfamiliarity. The volume of new procedures he had to memorize. He was telling her those things because those were the things she could meet him on.

He was not telling her, at home, that the work was, after two weeks, more interesting than the work had been before. That he was getting more authority. That he was talking to the operations team more. That he was, for the first time since he had started at Argo, looking forward to the shifts.

He was aware, every day, that the reason he was getting more interesting work was that his wife was no longer getting any work.

He did not know how to talk to Maya about that.

He did not try.

---

Maya looked for two weeks. She applied to thirty-one positions. She got six interviews. She got two offers.

She took the one at the Walmart distribution center in Buckeye.

It paid eighteen dollars and twenty cents an hour. Her old Argo wage had been twenty-two dollars. The Walmart job was the night shift, eight PM to four AM. The commute was thirty-five minutes each way. The schedule meant she would be sleeping when the kids were awake and awake when the kids were asleep. They would see each other on weekends and for two hours in the late afternoon.

She started on a Monday in mid-March.

She came home at four-fifteen the first morning. Eric was up. He had made coffee. He had been getting Mateo's school bag ready.

She came in the door.

She said, "It's fine."

"Yeah."

"It's a warehouse. The work is fine. The people are fine. The supervisor is fine."

"OK."

"I'm going to sleep until two. Then I'll get up and have a few hours with the kids before I go in."

"OK."

She went to bed.

Eric made breakfast. He took Mateo to school. He took Sofia to daycare. He drove to Goodyear. He clocked in at three-fifty-eight. He worked the four-A shift. He drove home at two-twenty. He woke Maya up at two-thirty. They sat in the kitchen with the kids at three. They had two hours together. She left for Buckeye at six. He put the kids to bed at seven-thirty. He went to bed at nine.

That was the new schedule.

The new schedule was the schedule for a while.

---

The last Saturday in March.

Eric got up at six. Maya was still asleep — she had gotten home at four-thirty and had been in bed for ninety minutes. He let her sleep. He made coffee. He read the paper. The Sound was a phrase his cousin in Seattle used. In Goodyear in March the morning was bright and clear and the desert sun was already up over the eastern horizon by quarter to seven.

The kids slept until eight.

Maya woke up at nine.

She came into the kitchen in her bathrobe. She poured coffee.

She sat down at the table.

She had a notebook with her.

She said, "I want to do the budget."

"OK."

"I want to do it on paper. I want to put it on the refrigerator. I want both of us to look at it every day until I'm comfortable that we know where we stand."

"OK."

He got the bills out of the drawer. He brought them to the table. They worked through it together.

The mortgage was twenty-four hundred a month. The car was three twenty. The daycare was now nine hundred because Sofia had moved to the part-time tier when Maya had switched to nights. Insurance, utilities, gas, food, phones, internet — fourteen hundred a month. Credit card minimum, ninety. They had cut the streaming services down to one. They had stopped eating out except for one Sunday-evening pizza. They had stopped buying anything that was not on a list.

Maya's Walmart paycheck, after taxes, was about twenty-six hundred a month. Eric's new Argo paycheck, after taxes, would be about thirty-four hundred a month once the new wage kicked in. The total household income, net, was going to be about six thousand a month.

The total household expenses, by Maya's count, came to five thousand one hundred a month, with a buffer of about three hundred a month for things they had not budgeted for.

Maya wrote it all out on a single sheet of paper. She wrote the numbers in pencil. She wrote *Madsen Household Budget — Q2* at the top.

She wrote at the bottom: *We will hold to this through June. We will revisit on July 1.*

She signed at the bottom. *Maya Madsen.*

She slid the paper across to Eric.

Eric read it.

He signed under her name. *Eric Madsen.*

He slid it back.

She looked at it for a minute.

She got up. She walked to the refrigerator. She took down the kindergarten flyer that had been there for two weeks. She put the budget paper up under a magnet shaped like a small green chili pepper that the kids had gotten her for Mother's Day two years earlier.

She stood there for a moment with her hand on the refrigerator.

She said, "OK."

She turned around.

She said, "I'm going to wake the kids up."

She walked down the hall.

Eric sat at the table.

He finished his coffee.

He looked at the paper on the refrigerator.

The paper said what it said. The budget held. The household held. The kids were going to wake up and be the kids. The work was the work. The new shift was the new shift. The next quarter was the next quarter.

He put the coffee cup in the sink.

He went down the hall to help Maya with the kids.

---

*Eleven warehouses converted in the first wave. Three thousand five hundred workers laid off. Eight thousand four hundred workers retained at higher wages. Eighty-seven thousand workers reassigned. The pivot's first wave was, statistically, a net benefit to the workforce of the converted facilities. The pivot's first wave was also a net loss for three thousand five hundred specific households whose names did not appear in the analyst reports. The Madsen household was one of them. They survived. Many did. Some did not. The book does not pretend the math justifies the loss. The book also does not pretend the loss invalidates the math. Both are true.*
