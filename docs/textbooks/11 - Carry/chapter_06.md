# Chapter 6: The Crash

Owen Friedlander was at his desk on the tenth floor at five forty AM Pacific Monday — the building's heat just coming up — three monitors on. Institutional shareholder roster on the left. Bloomberg on the right. The morning IR brief in the middle.

The trade desk in New York opened the Carrick book at the bell.

The stock printed down six and a half percent in the first eleven seconds.

The board vote had been the previous Wednesday. The stock had recovered eight percent Wednesday afternoon and another four on Thursday. It had given up nine on Friday after Drew Sallinger had appeared on a Bloomberg television panel at four PM Eastern.

The interviewer had asked Sallinger about his short position.

Sallinger, on tape: *The math in the model does not work. The personal contribution from Westerlund is a fig leaf. The ESOP is a fig leaf. The platform timeline is a fig leaf. The board ratified a strategy nine to one because Westerlund made the vote a referendum on his own job. The board ratified the job. The strategy is not ratified by anybody who has to make money on it.*

The interviewer: *Mr. Sallinger. You are short Carrick at the size of eight billion dollars. What is your exit price.*

Sallinger: *I do not have an exit price. I have a target. The target is Westerlund stepping down inside twelve months. If he steps down at any point in the next twelve months I cover. If he does not, I roll the position and double it.*

*Double it.*

*Double it.*

The clip had run nine times across financial television over the weekend.

Owen had watched it forty-two times.

He had watched it the forty-second time at four AM Monday in his kitchen in Madison Park, in a bathrobe, Ruth asleep upstairs, coffee maker running in the corner, the eleven million Sallinger had referred to as a *fig leaf* in his eyes, and the eleven million dollars of personal capital Owen had committed to Carrick equity at twenty-eight he had not yet been able to walk down from his career as the sixteen-year-old version of himself who had read his first Lewis book at a Cleveland public library and decided he was going to be a Wall Street partner before he was forty.

He had been a Wall Street partner at thirty-three.

He had left Wall Street at forty-nine to work for Cole.

He had read the *fig leaf* line forty-two times because he had been on Wall Street long enough to know the line was a script and not a tantrum. A script meant the next eight days were going to be the eight days Sallinger had been waiting for since the morning the memo had landed.

Owen had been ready for eight days since the memo had landed.

He had been ready for ninety days before it.

---

The stock printed down ten percent in the first ninety seconds.

By six AM Pacific it was down fourteen.

By the half-hour, sixteen.

The trade desk in New York routed twenty-three institutional-investor calls to Owen's desk between six AM and seven. He took twenty of them himself. He routed three of the smaller ones to his deputy, Liam Park.

Each of the twenty institutional calls ran between six and eleven minutes. Owen used the same opening sentence on each.

*Thank you for the call. I'm going to walk you through the financial model. I'm going to take every question you have. I'm going to ask you, at the end of the call, what your time horizon is. If your time horizon is less than four quarters, I'm going to tell you to sell. If your time horizon is greater than four quarters, I'm going to tell you to hold. If your time horizon is greater than twelve quarters, I'm going to tell you to add.*

Of the twenty, eleven told Owen by the end of the call that their time horizon was greater than twelve quarters.

Of those eleven, seven added across the following five trading days.

The other nine sold.

Owen tracked the position changes on a spreadsheet on the third monitor.

By Friday close the institutional rotation was twenty-one percent of the shareholder base.

The rotation was the rotation Owen had modeled in August.

It had landed seven days inside the window the model had predicted.

The model was the model.

---

The Sallinger call came at eleven eleven AM Pacific Tuesday morning.

Owen had been expecting it.

He had told Cole at the Sunday-night dinner at the Bainbridge house that Sallinger was going to call him, personally, on the second trading day after the Bloomberg interview, at a time Sallinger was going to choose to be theatrical, and that Owen was going to take the call without Cole on the line and without a script and without notes — because Sallinger was going to be reading Owen's tone, and Sallinger would hear a script in three sentences.

The call came in on the direct line.

Owen picked up on the second ring.

"Owen Friedlander."

"Owen. Drew Sallinger."

"Drew."

"You watched the Bloomberg piece."

"I watched it forty-two times."

Sallinger laughed.

It was a real laugh. Owen had read Sallinger laughing in deposition transcripts from three of the activist campaigns. He had taught himself the difference between the laugh Sallinger used when he was working and the laugh Sallinger used when he had been told something he had not heard before. The laugh on the phone Tuesday was the second kind.

"Forty-two times."

"I've been counting since Friday afternoon. I'm at forty-two as of this morning. By the time we get off this phone I'll be at forty-three."

"You were at Goldman."

"Sixteen years. ECM desk through 2017."

"Under Jane Pirelli."

"Under Jane Pirelli."

"Jane Pirelli would have told you not to take this call."

"Jane Pirelli is going to tell me Saturday morning over a phone call that she was wrong about me taking this call. Jane has been wrong about exactly four things in twenty-four years. This is going to be the fifth."

Sallinger laughed again. First kind this time.

"Owen. I'm going to ask you one question. I'm going to ask it because the answer is the question my whole position turns on. The answer is going to be in your tone. I've been doing this work for twenty-one years. I'm going to know the answer before you finish your second sentence. The question is this."

A pause.

"Walk me through the founder-contribution mechanics on the ESOP."

Owen sat back in his chair.

He had been waiting for the question ninety days.

He had been waiting because he had known, when the model was being built in August, that the question was the question any serious activist on the Carrick book would ask first — the four point two billion was the line that decided whether the pivot was a strategic move or a vanity project, and the founder-contribution mechanics were the mechanics that determined whether the math worked.

He answered.

Fourteen minutes.

He walked Sallinger through every page of the ESOP contribution schedule. Four point two billion as a single irrevocable transfer at the closing of the trust on January first. Funded from Cole's personal holdings — not from the company. Cash, not equity. Wired from Cole's personal account at JPMorgan Private Bank to the trust's account at State Street the morning of the memo. Cleared at 9:42 AM Pacific Monday. The four point two billion was no longer in Cole's name on Tuesday morning. The four point two billion was already in the trust.

Sallinger was quiet for a beat.

"Owen."

"Yes."

"Are you telling me Cole already wired the money."

"Cole wired the money at nine forty-two AM Pacific the morning the memo went out. He wired it before the stock opened on the East Coast for the second day of trading. The trust holds the cash. The IRS Form 5500 will reflect the contribution in the Q1 filing. The contribution is in the public 8-K we filed Wednesday afternoon. The wire confirmation is on Page Sixty-Eight of the 8-K appendix. I'm happy to send you the PDF."

Sallinger went quiet for nine seconds.

"Send me the PDF."

"Sending it now."

Owen emailed the PDF from the second monitor without breaking the call.

"Owen."

"Yes."

"I had not read past Page Sixty in the 8-K. I'm going to read Page Sixty-Eight after we hang up. I'm going to call you back inside two hours."

"I'll be at this desk."

"I'm asking you one more question. I'm asking it knowing the answer is going to determine whether the call I make to you in two hours is the call that closes the conversation or the call that opens it. The question is this. Did Cole wire the four point two before the board vote on Wednesday."

"Cole wired the four point two before the memo went out on Monday."

"Before the board vote."

"Cole wired the money at 9:42 AM Pacific Monday. The board vote was Wednesday at 9 AM Pacific. The money was in the trust for forty-seven hours and eighteen minutes before the board voted."

"Cole moved the money before he had board authorization to send the memo."

"Cole moved the money because the money moving was the precondition for the memo being honest. The board could have voted no Wednesday. The money would have stayed in the trust. The pivot would have failed. Cole would have been out as CEO. The trust would still have held the four point two billion. The trust is a separate legal entity with its own trustee and its own beneficiaries. The trustee is Don Bell at the ESOP Association. The beneficiaries are the six hundred and forty thousand Carrick Retail employees. The cash was not Cole's to take back at any point after 9:42 AM Pacific Monday. Cole bet his entire net worth on the strategy before the board voted. The board voted yes. The strategy is now the strategy. The money is now the workers' money. There is no fig leaf, Drew. There is a man who put four point two billion dollars of his own money into a trust he cannot get back, sixty-two hours before he found out whether he was still going to be the CEO of the company that was going to operate alongside the trust."

Sallinger said nothing.

Owen waited.

"Page Sixty-Eight."

"Page Sixty-Eight. The wire confirmation. The signed irrevocable contribution agreement. The trustee's countersignature. Don Bell's notarized statement. All four are on Page Sixty-Eight."

"I'm going to call you back."

He hung up.

Owen set the phone down.

He looked at the stock ticker on the right monitor.

Down fifteen percent on the day.

---

Sallinger called back at one twenty-two PM Pacific.

The call lasted ninety-eight seconds.

"Owen. Westerlund is in New York Friday night."

"Yes."

"The 47th Street meeting."

"Yes."

"I'll be there."

"I know you will."

"Tell Cole I read Page Sixty-Eight."

"I will."

"And Owen."

"Yes."

"Jane Pirelli is going to call you Saturday morning. She's going to tell you she was wrong about you taking the call. She's going to be wrong about being wrong. You should never have taken this call. The reason it was the right call to take is that I asked the question I asked, and the question was the question Cole already had the answer to, and you delivered the answer the way only a man who has run a model six different ways for ninety days could deliver it. Tell Cole I'm going to be in the room Friday night because the question I asked you was the wrong question. The right question is one I'm going to ask Cole in person. I'm going to ask it across the table. I'm going to listen to his answer. I'm going to decide whether to close the position in the room or after the weekend. If I close it in the room, the position is closed inside ten trading days, and I lose between four hundred million and six hundred million depending on the cover speed. If I close it after the weekend, the position is closed inside ten trading days at the same loss range. The reason this matters is that I do not want Cole to think the New York meeting is about whether I close the position. The position is closing. The New York meeting is about whether I'm going to be the activist who helped him land the pivot or the activist who is going to spend the next ten years explaining why he did not."

"I will tell Cole."

"Friday night."

"Friday night."

"Goodbye, Owen."

He hung up.

Owen sat in the chair a long minute.

He picked up the phone.

He dialed the inside line to Cole's office.

Eliza answered.

"Eliza. I need three minutes with Cole."

"He has a window at one thirty-five."

"On my way up."

He took the elevator to the eleventh floor. He walked into Cole's office at one thirty-three PM.

Cole was at the credenza with the Echo bud vase in his hand. He set it down. He turned around.

"Cole. Sallinger is in the room Friday night. The position is closing inside ten days regardless of what you say to him. The question he's going to ask in the room is a different question from the one he asked me on the phone. You are not going to know the question until he asks it. The right answer to the question is going to be the right answer in your head, not on a brief."

Cole looked at him a beat.

"Owen."

"Yes."

"What was the question Sallinger asked you on the phone."

"He asked me to walk him through the founder-contribution mechanics on the ESOP. The real question was whether you had moved the four point two before the board vote."

"And."

"I told him you moved the money at nine forty-two AM Monday. Page Sixty-Eight of the 8-K. He read Page Sixty-Eight. The position is closing inside ten days because Page Sixty-Eight is the document that closed the position."

"Owen."

"Yes."

"Page Sixty-Eight was your call. I wouldn't have put the wire confirmation in an 8-K appendix. I would have buried it in the contribution agreement schedule and let the institutional desk find it on their own. You put it on Page Sixty-Eight because you knew Sallinger was going to read it and you knew Sallinger needed to read it before the New York meeting. That was your call. I'm telling you in this office, between you and me, that Sallinger closing the position is your work. I'm not going to tell that to anybody else. I'm telling it to you."

"Cole."

"Yes."

"Thank you."

"You're welcome."

Owen turned and walked back to the door.

At the door he stopped.

He turned.

"Cole."

"Yes."

"Friday night. Eat first. Sallinger is not going to let you eat in the room. The question he's going to ask you is the kind of question that's asked across an empty table. Eat at the airport. Drink water in the room. Do not order anything."

"I'll eat at the airport."

"Good."

He walked out.

---

The stock closed Tuesday down eight percent on the day. Recovered from the morning's fifteen at the close on a single block trade Sallinger Capital had placed in the last fourteen minutes of the session that the trade desk had identified as a partial cover of the short.

By the close on Friday the stock was down twenty-six percent from the pre-memo level.

By the following Friday close it would be down twenty-one.

By Day Ninety it would be down sixteen.

Owen left the office at nine forty-three PM Pacific Tuesday.

He drove home to Madison Park.

Ruth was at the kitchen table reading.

She looked up.

"Owen."

"Ruth."

"How was the call with Sallinger."

"The model held."

"Sit down. There's wine."

He sat down.

He drank the wine.

The model held.
