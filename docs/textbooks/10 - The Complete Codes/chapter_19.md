# Chapter 19: The Values

*Where your values come from — and why the answer is "all three."*

---

Where do your values come from?

This is not a casual question. Your values determine your priorities, your decisions, your relationships, your life. They are the invisible architecture shaping everything you build. Get the architecture wrong and the building collapses — not immediately, but inevitably.

So where did you get yours?

Most people have never asked. They operate on inherited values — absorbed from family, culture, religion, peer groups, media — without ever examining whether those values are genuinely theirs. They live according to a blueprint they did not draw, in a house they did not design, and wonder why it doesn't quite fit.

Three philosophical traditions offer three radically different answers to the question of values. And the examined life requires all three.

---

## THE STORM POSITION: VALUES ARE CREATED

Nietzsche was uncompromising on this point: **the powerful create values; the weak receive them.**

He looked at history and saw a pattern. Every moral system — every code of right and wrong, every hierarchy of what matters — was created by someone. Not discovered floating in the cosmos. Created. By human beings. In particular circumstances. For particular purposes.

A faith note: Nietzsche would say the Ten Commandments were created by a community that needed social cohesion. The Catholic tradition says they were *received* — revealed by God and recognized as true because they correspond to human nature and the moral law written on every heart. The difference matters: if moral values are objective and rooted in God's nature, then what Nietzsche calls "creating values" is closer to what the Church calls *discerning your vocation* — discovering what you specifically are called to do with the gifts you were given. The values themselves aren't invented; your relationship to them is.

The Ten Commandments were created by a community that needed social cohesion — that is Nietzsche's reading. Chivalric codes were created by a warrior class that needed legitimacy. The values of consumer capitalism were created by an economic system that needs consumers. None of these floated down from heaven. All of them were human constructions, shaped by human interests.

This is not a cynical observation — it is a liberating one.

If values are created, then you can create yours. You are not bound to inherit the values of your parents, your culture, your era. You can examine them, test them against your own experience, and — here is the crucial move — *re-choose* them consciously or *replace* them deliberately.

**The Complete Code: Values are created, not found in nature. Every moral system was constructed by human beings for human purposes. This means you have the power — and the responsibility — to create your own.**

The Storm tradition takes this further with Nietzsche's concept of **self-overcoming**. The person who passively receives their values is not truly living — they are being lived *by* their values. The person who creates their values is authoring their own existence.

The Ubermensch — Nietzsche's image of the fully realized human — is not someone with a particular set of values. It is someone who *chooses* their values through the conscious exercise of will. Who examines what they believe, discards what does not serve life, and affirms what does — with full awareness that the affirmation is a choice, not a discovery.

The danger here is obvious. If values are created rather than discovered, what prevents them from being arbitrary? What prevents the powerful from creating values that serve only themselves?

Nietzsche had an answer: the eternal recurrence. Would you will this value forever? Would you live by this principle for all eternity? If yes, it has passed the most rigorous test available. If no, it is not truly your value — it is a convenience.

---

## THE GARDEN POSITION: VALUES ARE CULTIVATED

Mencius and Confucius offer a very different account.

Values are not created from nothing. They are *cultivated from seeds already within you*.

Mencius's Four Sprouts — the innate capacities for compassion, shame, courtesy, and wisdom — are not blank material waiting for you to impose meaning on them. They are tendencies. Directions. The beginnings of something that, properly nurtured, grows into virtue.

The Sprout of Compassion grows into Ren — benevolence, humaneness, genuine care for others. The Sprout of Shame grows into Yi — righteousness, the sense of what is fitting and just. The Sprout of Courtesy grows into Li — propriety, the ability to navigate social situations with grace and respect. The Sprout of Wisdom grows into Zhi — discernment, the capacity to distinguish true from false, good from bad.

These are not *chosen* in the way Nietzsche meant. They are *given* — part of your nature as a human being. But they are not automatic either. Like seeds, they require soil, water, sunlight, and tending. Left uncultivated, they wither. Properly cultivated, they flourish.

**The Complete Code: Some values are already within you, waiting to be cultivated. Mencius's Four Sprouts — compassion, shame, courtesy, wisdom — are innate equipment, not external impositions. The task is not to create them but to tend them.**

The Garden position adds a crucial dimension that the Storm position lacks: **relational values**.

You are not a solitary creator standing alone against the void, forging values from raw will. You are a relational being — constituted by your connections. Your values emerge not just from your individual will but from your relationships: what you owe to your parents, your children, your community, your ancestors, your descendants.

Confucius understood that the individual does not exist in isolation. You are always someone's child, someone's friend, someone's colleague, someone's neighbor. These relationships carry obligations that are not arbitrary — they arise from the nature of the relationship itself.

The parent owes the child nurture and guidance. Not because some external authority commands it, but because that is what the parent-child relationship *is*. The friend owes the friend honesty and loyalty. Not because society says so, but because friendship without these qualities is not friendship.

**The Complete Code: You are a relational being. Some values arise not from individual will but from the nature of your connections. What you owe to others is shaped by who they are to you. This is not constraint — it is the architecture of a meaningful life.**

The danger of the Garden position: it can become conservative in the worst sense — maintaining inherited values simply because they are inherited, resisting change because change threatens tradition, confusing the way things have always been done with the way things should be done.

---

## THE CELESTIAL POSITION: VALUES ARE DISCOVERED

Now the third voice enters, and it says something neither of the other two will say:

**Some values are not created or cultivated. They are discovered. They exist independently of human beings altogether.**

This is Plato's position, and it is the boldest of the three.

Beauty is not something we invent. It is something we perceive. When you encounter genuine beauty — a mathematical proof, a sunset, a piece of music that stops your breath — you are not projecting value onto a neutral object. You are *recognizing* something that was already there. Beauty is a feature of reality, not a feature of your preferences.

The same is true of Truth and Goodness. Mathematical truths are discovered, not invented. The Pythagorean theorem was true before Pythagoras. Pi existed before anyone calculated it. These are not human creations — they are features of reality that human minds are capable of perceiving.

And if Beauty and Truth are real — if they exist independently of human perception — then Goodness may be too. There may be genuine moral truths that exist whether or not any human being recognizes them. Justice may be as real as the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter.

**The Complete Code: Some values may be real in the strongest sense — not created by human will, not merely cultivated from human nature, but features of reality itself. Beauty, Truth, and Goodness may be transcendent properties of being. The Celestial tradition says: seek them.**

The Celestial position demands humility. You are not the creator of all values. You are not even the final authority on what matters. You are a perceiver — someone capable of seeing, partially and imperfectly, the eternal Forms that underlie particular instances of beauty, truth, and goodness.

This is the *periagoge* — the turning. The moment when you stop projecting your preferences onto the world and start asking what the world is showing you. The moment when you move from "what do I want to value?" to "what is genuinely valuable?"

The danger of the Celestial position is equally obvious: it can become dogmatic. If values are discovered rather than created, then whoever claims to have discovered them has a terrifying amount of authority. History is littered with people who claimed access to transcendent truth and used that claim to justify atrocity.

---

## THE SYNTHESIS: ALL THREE ARE NECESSARY

Here is what the examined life requires: **you must inherit, discover, AND create your values.**

Not one. Not two. All three.

Each process addresses a different dimension of your existence. Each fills a gap the others leave open. And each keeps the others honest.

**You INHERIT relational values from tradition (Garden).** The obligations that arise from your connections — to family, community, the generations before and after you — are not arbitrary impositions. They are the architecture of social life, refined over millennia, encoding practical wisdom about how human beings can live together well.

You did not choose to be born into relationships. But you were. And those relationships carry values that are not yours to create from nothing. Loyalty, reciprocity, respect for elders, care for children — these are inherited because they work. They have been tested by time.

**You DISCOVER transcendent values through contemplation (Celestial).** When you encounter genuine beauty, you are not projecting. When you perceive genuine truth, you are not inventing. When you recognize genuine goodness, you are not creating. You are seeing something real — something that exists whether or not you notice it.

These discovered values serve as a corrective. When your created values conflict with transcendent ones, you have a problem. If the values you have forged through sheer will lead you toward what is ugly, false, and harmful, something has gone wrong in the forging.

**You CREATE personal values through self-overcoming (Storm).** Within the framework of inherited obligations and discovered truths, there is enormous space for individual creation. What kind of life will you build? What will you emphasize? What challenges will you seek? What meaning will you make?

This is the domain of creative freedom — but it is not unlimited freedom. It is freedom *within* a framework of relational responsibility and transcendent orientation.

**The Complete Code: The complete person inherits relational values from tradition (Garden), discovers transcendent values through contemplation (Celestial), and creates personal values through self-overcoming (Storm). All three processes are necessary. Each keeps the others honest.**

A faith note — the Catholic synthesis: Moral values are objective and discovered (Celestial position). Your specific calling within those values is cultivated through relationship and community (Garden position). And the courage to live those values in a hostile world requires the Storm's self-overcoming. All three processes are necessary. None operates alone. The Church has always held this integration — natural law, community, and personal conversion are the three legs of Catholic moral life.

---

## HOW THEY KEEP EACH OTHER HONEST

Each value-source corrects the pathologies of the others.

**The Storm corrects the Garden.** Without the Storm's insistence on individual creation, inherited values become oppressive tradition. "We've always done it this way" becomes a prison. The Storm says: examine. Re-choose. If the inherited value still serves life, affirm it consciously. If it doesn't, create something better.

**The Garden corrects the Storm.** Without the Garden's relational grounding, individually created values become solipsistic. The person who creates values in isolation, without regard for how they affect others, is not a rebel — they are a narcissist. The Garden says: your values affect other people. Account for that.

**The Celestial corrects both.** Without the Celestial's transcendent orientation, both inherited and created values lack a standard against which to measure themselves. How do you know if your tradition is a good tradition? How do you know if the values you've created serve life or diminish it? You need something above both tradition and personal will — some independent standard of beauty, truth, and goodness — to evaluate against.

**The Storm corrects the Celestial.** Without the Storm's insistence that values require active affirmation, the Celestial's discovered values become passive — contemplated but never lived. The Storm says: it is not enough to see the Good. You must will it. You must embody it. You must create a life that expresses it.

**The Complete Code: Inherited values without examination become oppressive tradition. Created values without relational grounding become narcissism. Discovered values without active embodiment become passive contemplation. The examined life integrates all three.**

---

## THE RESSENTIMENT TRAP

Nietzsche identified a particular failure mode around values that applies across all three sources: **ressentiment**.

Ressentiment is the resentment of the powerless who, unable to achieve or create, redefine what they lack as contemptible.

The person who cannot create calls creation pretentious. The person who cannot achieve calls achievement corrupt. The person who cannot love deeply calls deep love naive.

This is not critique. Critique is honest evaluation. Ressentiment is the inversion of values driven by envy — calling the grapes sour because you cannot reach them.

Ressentiment can infect all three value-sources.

**In inherited values:** "My tradition didn't value ambition, therefore ambition is bad." Instead of honestly evaluating whether the tradition is right about ambition, you defend the tradition because it flatters your limitations.

**In discovered values:** "I perceive that transcendent Beauty favors what I happen to find easy." Instead of honestly following the Forms wherever they lead, you interpret them to validate your existing preferences.

**In created values:** "I've created a value system that conveniently excuses everything I was going to do anyway." Instead of using value-creation to challenge yourself toward growth, you use it to justify stagnation.

**The Complete Code: Ressentiment — the resentment that redefines what you cannot achieve as contemptible — is a poison that infects all value systems. The antidote is honesty: do your values challenge you to grow, or do they merely flatter who you already are?**

The test is simple. If your values never make you uncomfortable, never challenge you, never require sacrifice — they are probably not values. They are rationalizations.

Genuine values cost something. They pull you in directions you would not naturally go. They demand more of you than you want to give.

## BUILDING YOUR VALUE FRAMEWORK

Here is a practical process for integrating all three value-sources.

**Step One: Audit your inherited values (Garden).** What did you absorb from family, culture, religion, education? List them. Be thorough. These are the values you operate on by default — the ones that drive your behavior when you are not paying attention.

**Step Two: Test inherited values against experience (Storm).** For each inherited value, ask: Does this serve life? Does it make me and those around me more alive, more capable, more connected? Would I choose this value if I were choosing fresh? Re-affirm what passes the test. Release what doesn't.

**Step Three: Seek transcendent values through contemplation (Celestial).** Spend time with beauty — genuine beauty, not manufactured stimulation. Spend time with truth — mathematics, philosophy, the deep structure of things. Spend time with goodness — acts of genuine care, justice in action, excellence in craft. Ask what these experiences reveal about what is genuinely valuable, independent of your preferences.

**Step Four: Create personal values through self-overcoming (Storm).** Within the framework of relational obligation and transcendent orientation, ask: What kind of life do I want to build? What challenges will I seek? What will I emphasize, celebrate, sacrifice for? Create your own hierarchy of what matters — knowing it will evolve as you evolve.

**Step Five: Apply the eternal recurrence.** To each value — inherited, discovered, or created — apply Nietzsche's test: Would you live by this value forever? Would you will it for all eternity? The values that survive this test are the values worth building a life around.

**The Complete Code: Build your value framework deliberately. Audit what you inherited. Test it against experience. Seek what is transcendently real. Create what is uniquely yours. Apply the eternal recurrence to all of it. The resulting framework is yours — truly, consciously, examined.**

**The Celestial Contribution:** Some values may be real in the strongest sense — features of reality itself. Beauty, Truth, and Goodness may exist independently of human perception. Seek them through contemplation.

**The Synthesis:** The complete person inherits, discovers, and creates values. Each process addresses a different dimension of existence. Each keeps the others honest.
