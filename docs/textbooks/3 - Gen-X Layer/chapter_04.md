# Chapter 4: Cultural Porosity

You are sixteen, or seventeen, or somewhere in there — old enough that the math has started to bite when you try to read about it seriously, young enough that you still think you are supposed to be able to understand anything if you try hard enough. The thing you are trying to understand, this week, is public-key cryptography. You have encountered the idea in passing — a sentence in a science magazine, a paragraph in a book about computers, a reference in something you read about the NSA — and the idea has lodged in your head with the specific texture of a thing that should not be possible. The conventional cryptography you have been able to grasp: you and the other person agree on a key in advance, you scramble the message with the key, the other person unscrambles it with the same key, and as long as nobody else has the key, the message is private. Fine. Mechanical. Symmetrical. Makes sense. The problem is that you have read that there is a kind of cryptography where the two parties do not have to agree on anything in advance — where one of them publishes a key, in the newspaper if they want to, and anyone in the world can use that key to encrypt a message that only the publisher can decrypt. You have read this. You have read it more than once. You do not understand how it can be true. The mechanism feels like sleight of hand. The mechanism feels, in fact, like a lie — like one of those things that science writers occasionally claim about a result they have not personally verified. You have asked the math teacher, who has not heard of it. You have asked the computer teacher, who has heard of it but cannot explain it. You have looked it up in the encyclopedia at the school library, which has not been updated since 1981 and does not mention it.

You are at the bookstore on a Saturday — the kind of bookstore that has a long magazine rack along one wall, three rows deep, with the computer magazines on the bottom because the computer magazines are the heaviest. You pick up the new BYTE because you always pick up the new BYTE, and you carry it to the cafe in the back, and you sit down with a coffee that you are pretending to like, and you start flipping through. The magazine is six hundred pages thick, the way BYTE was that decade, and the table of contents runs for two full pages, and somewhere in there — in the columns section, between a piece on the new Motorola chip and a review of a database package — there is an article by Penn Jillette. The Penn Jillette. The magician. Half of Penn & Teller, who you have seen on television and whose books you have read because his books are the kind of thing the kid like you reads. Penn Jillette has written an article for BYTE, and the article is about public-key cryptography, and you sit there in the cafe with the coffee you do not like and you read it, and within a page he has explained the thing that no one has been able to explain to you. The trick is in the math, which Penn does not pretend you can follow in detail, but which he describes in terms of a metaphor — a padlock, or a box with a slot, or some specific physical analog whose particulars you will half-forget but whose structure will stay with you for the rest of your life. The key insight, the thing you had been missing, snaps into place. The two keys are not symmetrical. They are mathematical inverses, generated together, easy to compute in one direction and computationally infeasible in the other. The publisher publishes the easy direction. The encryptor uses the easy direction. The decryptor uses the secret hard direction. The whole edifice is built on a one-way function — multiplication of large primes is easy, factoring their product is hard — and the asymmetry of difficulty is what makes the whole thing possible. You read the paragraph twice. You read it a third time. The understanding is real, and it has been delivered to you by a magician in a computer magazine, in a Saturday afternoon at the bookstore, with a cup of coffee you are still pretending to like.

It is the year 2018, or maybe 2019, or possibly something in between. You are an adult now, with a job and a marriage and the small set of disposable indulgences that come with both, and one of the indulgences is that you are in Las Vegas for a few days, and one of the things you have decided to do while in Las Vegas is to see Penn & Teller live, at the Rio. You have wanted to see the show for years. You buy the ticket. You go to the theater. The show is everything you had hoped for and more — the precision of the bits, the warmth Penn brings to the stage despite his persona as the loud aggressive half of the duo, Teller's quiet uncanny gravity, the bullet catch, the cups and balls done transparent and somehow more astonishing for it. After the show, the announcement comes that Penn and Teller will both be in the lobby to meet the audience. They do this every night. They have done it for years. The line forms. You join it. You shuffle forward with the other people who have decided to wait, and you have a moment to think about what you are going to say, and you realize that you have not prepared anything, and the line is moving faster than you expected, and now you are next.

You are in front of him. Penn is taller than you expected, which everyone says, and he is doing the thing he does for every fan, which is the warm direct attention of a man who knows that this brief encounter is significant for the person in front of him and who has decided to be present for it. You open your mouth and what comes out is the thing you did not plan but that was waiting in the back of your head the whole time: "I loved that article you wrote in Byte magazine on public key encryption." There is a beat. Penn looks at you. His face does something — a small adjustment, a re-focusing, the kind of expression a person makes when they have been ready for one kind of conversation and have been handed another. And then he says it. He says: "WHOAH, that's a DEEP CUT." The words come out in his stage voice, the big public Penn voice, but with the specific timbre of genuine recognition underneath. He is not performing the moment for the line behind you. He is genuinely surprised, and the surprise has been welcome. He looks at you for a second longer than the line allows. You nod. He nods back. The next person in line is already stepping up. You move on. You are so pleased that you do not know what to do with yourself for the rest of the night, and you remember it now, years later, with a clarity that is unusual even for the formative memories.

The story is small. It deserves the space the chapter is giving it because it is also, simultaneously, large. It contains, in a single compressed narrative, every major theme this book is trying to develop. Cultural porosity: a stage magician published a technical article in a computer magazine, and nobody at the time thought this was unusual, and the article was good. Frontier journalism: BYTE was the venue for that article, and BYTE was the venue because BYTE was not "tech media" in the contemporary sense — it was shared exploration infrastructure, a thick monthly journal where the practitioners of an emerging field could read each other and be read. The status logic of the era: Penn was the right person to publish that article not because he had cryptography credentials — he did not — but because he could explain the thing, and clear explanation gained you status in the substrate the magazine served, and the editors of the magazine knew it. The persistence of the bond: thirty years later, a man who had read the article once, in a bookstore, on a Saturday, could name it to its author, and the author would recognize the reference and honor it with the only response the moment required, which was three syllables and a tone of voice that meant *I see you, and you see me, and we both know what we are talking about.* The DEEP CUT was the entire chapter compressed into a phrase. It worked because both of them had been there.

The structural reason a magician was the right person to explain cryptography deserves its own examination, because it is not the obvious thing that the casual reader assumes. The casual reader assumes that Penn Jillette was a particularly intelligent celebrity who happened to take an amateur interest in technical subjects, and that his article was a good piece of amateur science writing by an unusually capable enthusiast. This is wrong, or at least it is a thin version of the truth. The deeper reason Penn was the right person to write that article is that stage magic and cryptography share their structural mechanics. Both are disciplines built on asymmetric knowledge. The magician knows where the coin went; the audience does not. The keyholder knows how to decrypt the message; the eavesdropper does not. Both are built on hidden information that is hidden in plain sight — the coin is, in most cases, somewhere the audience could in principle see if they knew where to look; the encrypted message is, in most cases, sitting in plain text on a wire that anyone with a sniffer can capture. Both are built on controlled revelation: the magician decides when to reveal the coin, the keyholder decides when (and whether) to share the decryption. Both are built on the management of mental models in the observer: the magician designs the trick so that the audience constructs the wrong model of what is happening; the cryptographic protocol is designed so that the attacker constructs an intractable model of what would have to be done to break it. Penn Jillette had spent his professional life inside the mental architecture that public-key cryptography exemplifies. He did not have to learn that architecture to write the article. He had to translate his existing architecture into the vocabulary of mathematics, which is a much smaller act than the act of original conceptual work. The article was good not because Penn was a polymath; it was good because the two fields he was bridging shared their bones.

This kind of cross-domain explanation is, structurally, what cultural porosity makes possible. The era — call it roughly 1975 to 1995, with the personal computer revolution as its center and BYTE magazine as one of its journals of record — was a moment in technical history when the boundaries between disciplines were unusually soft. Magicians wrote about cryptography. Philosophers wrote about cellular automata (Douglas Hofstadter's "Metamagical Themas" column in *Scientific American*, which ran from 1981 to 1983, was named in part as an anagram of his predecessor Martin Gardner's "Mathematical Games" column and as an explicit signal that the playful interdisciplinary tradition was being passed forward). Science-fiction writers wrote about computation (Vernor Vinge's *True Names*, 1981, prefigured cyberspace before there was a vocabulary for it). Mathematicians wrote about music (Douglas Hofstadter again, in *Gödel, Escher, Bach*, 1979). Musicians wrote about software (Brian Eno's writing on generative systems began appearing in the early 1980s). Cognitive scientists wrote about computers (Marvin Minsky's *The Society of Mind*, 1986). Computer scientists wrote about philosophy (the foundational AI literature is full of such crossings). The porosity ran in both directions. Anyone who could think clearly across an abstract structure was welcome at the table, because the table itself was new and the placement of chairs had not yet been formalized.

The structural reason this porosity was possible — and the reason it has become so much harder to reproduce in the contemporary technical environment — is the absence of what the author has come to call, in shorthand, the IT layer. Before computing became institutionalized, credentialized, professionalized, and bureaucratized, there was no IT layer to mediate access to the technical conversation. There was no industry certification body whose accreditation served as the gatekeeper to publication. There was no enterprise-software vendor whose product roadmap defined the legitimate set of topics for technical writing. There was no Slack workspace whose membership criteria filtered for relevant background. There was no LinkedIn whose endorsement system implicitly ranked contributors. The field of computing in the era of the Penn article was so structurally young that it had not yet developed the immune system that mature professional fields develop to keep amateurs out. The lack of an immune system was not a flaw. It was the structural feature that made the era productive in the specific way it was productive. Amateurs were not amateurs in the dismissive sense; they were participants in a field that had not yet decided who got to be a professional. Penn Jillette was not a professional cryptographer. He was a professional magician. He was also, by the operative logic of the era, a legitimate contributor to a computing magazine, because the magazine's standard of admission was *did you write something good* and Penn had written something good. The standard sounds quaint. The standard was, in fact, extraordinarily powerful, because it allowed the field to draw on every clear thinker in the surrounding culture rather than only on the clear thinkers who happened to already have a computing credential.

The contemporary technical environment is structurally different. It has hardened. Most major technical fields now have professional bodies, accreditation pathways, conference programs gated by peer review, journals with impact factors, and corporate publication infrastructures whose gatekeeping protocols filter for credentialed contributors. The hardening is not malicious; it is what mature fields do, and it has costs and benefits that historians of professionalization have been arguing about for a century. The cost relevant to this book is that cross-domain explanation has become harder. A magician today, writing an article on public-key cryptography for a major contemporary publication, would face a review process designed to question the magician's credentials before evaluating the magician's explanation. The article might still get published, in the corner of the publication reserved for celebrity guest contributions, with a hedging note that the author is "not a cryptographer." The article would not be the Penn Jillette BYTE piece. It would be marketing wrapped around content. The structural posture of the field would not allow the writer the dignity that the BYTE editors had been able to extend without effort.

It is worth situating the Penn article in its specific technical-historical moment, because the technical-historical moment matters to the story's meaning. Public-key cryptography had been introduced to the academic literature in 1976, by Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman in their paper "New Directions in Cryptography," which appeared in the IEEE Transactions on Information Theory and which announced — to a small academic audience that did not initially grasp its significance — that the millennia-old problem of secure communication between strangers had been solved in principle. The RSA algorithm followed in 1977, in a paper by Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir, and Leonard Adleman that provided a concrete construction satisfying the Diffie-Hellman framework. Steven Levy, in *Crypto* (2001), documents the long, contested, often clandestine emergence of these ideas into public awareness — a process that ran through the late 1970s and 1980s and that was complicated by active opposition from the National Security Agency, which understood the implications of strong cryptography in private hands well before the general public did. By the time Penn Jillette's BYTE article appeared, public-key cryptography had been in the academic literature for roughly a decade but was only just beginning to reach a general readership. The cypherpunk movement that would popularize the technology in the 1990s — PGP in 1991, the early Crypto Wars, the rise of public-key infrastructure for the commercial web — was still ahead. Penn's article was, in this sense, frontier journalism in the strict technical sense: a contemporary explanation of a recently invented technology to a readership that had not yet encountered authoritative explanations of it elsewhere. The article occupied the explanation-vacuum that the academic literature had not yet filled at the popular level, and it filled the vacuum by translating the mathematics into the language of a magician — which, as the next paragraphs argue, was structurally exactly the right move.

There is one further dimension of the Penn story that the chapter must do justice to, because it is the dimension that the contemporary AI moment most directly inherits. The bond the era created between participants persisted, intact, across thirty years. The bond was not based on shared employment, shared organizational membership, shared geographical location, or shared social network. It was based on shared participation in a field at a particular moment of its emergence. The participants did not have to know each other to recognize each other. They had to have been there. The recognition could be triggered by the smallest signal — three syllables in a meet-and-greet line — because the signal indexed an entire structure of shared experience that both parties could reconstruct from the index alone. This is what the DEEP CUT recognition was. It was Penn Jillette and a stranger acknowledging that they had both been participants in an early digital civilization, that the civilization had produced certain shared artifacts, that those artifacts were now decades in the past, that they were both still carrying the memory of those artifacts in working order, and that this fact about both of them was good. The whole exchange took perhaps six seconds. It contained more recognition than most professional conversations contain in an hour.

There is an additional structural point about why the recognition worked, and it has to do with the specific cognitive labor that good explanations require. To explain a difficult idea well — well enough that a sixteen-year-old in a bookstore on a Saturday can understand it for the first time after weeks of failed attempts — the writer must hold the idea in mind clearly enough to distinguish its essential mechanics from its incidental decorations, must identify a metaphor whose structural geometry matches the structural geometry of the idea closely enough to carry the reader across the gap, and must write the metaphor down in language that has been worn smooth by enough revision that the reader does not stumble over the prose on the way to the insight. This is a substantial labor, and the people who can perform it on technical subjects are a small minority of the population, even among technical experts. Penn Jillette was capable of performing it because his stage-magic career had given him decades of practice at the analogous task — designing routines whose structural geometry would lead an audience to construct a particular mental model and then collapse the model at the moment of revelation. The transfer was nontrivial but proximate. The era allowed such transfers because the publication infrastructure was open to whoever could do the work, regardless of which professional silo the person had been credentialed into. The recognition decades later, in the meet-and-greet line, was the recognition of two people who had both understood, in their respective ways, that the labor of clear explanation is itself a craft worth honoring, and that the people who can perform it constitute a kind of distributed guild whose membership is detectable across decades by signals as small as a magazine reference.

The contemporary AI moment is, in its strange way, producing similar bonds, on a shorter timescale, between participants who recognize each other in the comments under technical posts, in the small audiences of the better Substacks, in the conference talks that go viral inside a tiny audience and remain invisible outside it. The bond is the same bond. It is the recognition between participants in an early phase of a field, when the field is still small enough that participation itself is a marker. Whether the contemporary bonds will persist for thirty years remains to be seen; the field is still too young to know. But the bonds are forming now, and they are forming around the same structural conditions that produced the bonds the Penn story rests on — the porosity, the openness, the absence of an immune system, the rewarding of clear explanation over credential, the felt sense that the people doing the work are the people defining what the work is.

The Penn story is, finally, the chapter's emotional anchor and the book's emotional anchor, because it converts the abstract structural argument of the previous three chapters into a single thing the reader can hold. The frontier was real. The porosity was real. The cohort was real. The bonds were real. They persisted. They are still capable of being honored, three syllables at a time, by people who recognize each other across decades on the strength of a magazine article one of them wrote and the other one read. The book's larger argument — that the cognitive substrate produced by this era is now propagating through artificial intelligence systems into the lives of every contemporary technical practitioner — is best held in mind alongside the smaller, warmer fact that the people who lived through the era recognize each other when they meet. They always will. The substrate is not just in the model weights. It is in the people who taught the model what English at depth sounds like, and it is in the people who type into the model now, and it is in the moments when those two populations notice each other and nod.

The next part of the book moves from the cultural frontier to the machines themselves — the instruments through which the substrate was assembled. The first of those instruments was a specific kind of room, late at night, with a specific quality of light and a specific quality of silence, and the cognitive habits that room produced are the habits the substrate carries forward most directly. The room is the next chapter.
