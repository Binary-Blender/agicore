# Chapter 7: The Modem

The dial tone first. Steady, monotone, the unbroken note of a phone line that has not yet been asked to do anything. Then the tones of the digits — touch-tone, if you were lucky, the seven or ten or eleven distinct musical pairs the modem played into the mouthpiece as fast as the remote switch could accept them; pulse dial, if you weren't, the older mechanical clicking that took half a second per digit and made you wait through 9s and 0s that felt longer than the other digits even though they were the same. The phone line clicked once when the remote end picked up. There was a half-second pause. Then the answering modem began its tone.

It was a single sustained note, somewhere around 2,100 hertz, the carrier that the answering modem held to announce that it was ready to negotiate. Your own modem heard the tone and responded — a different pitch, a slightly higher chirp, the originating side of the handshake. The two modems exchanged this opening greeting for a second or so, and then the negotiation proper began. The sound was famous: a brief warble, a descending sweep, a burst of what sounded like static but wasn't, a rapid back-and-forth scraping of frequencies as the two endpoints measured the channel between them and agreed on which of their many possible signaling rates the line that night could carry. There was a pause. Then a longer pause that lasted exactly long enough to make you wonder if the negotiation had failed. Then the carrier locked in — a continuous, slightly noisy hiss at a steady level — and the modem dropped into data mode, and the little speaker on the front panel went silent, and the CD light came on.

You had a connection. The other machine, somewhere — across the city, across the country, occasionally across an ocean — was now exchanging characters with yours, at whatever speed the handshake had settled on. If your ears had been trained, you knew the speed already from the negotiation. The 1,200 baud connection sounded different from the 2,400. The 9,600 sounded different from the 14,400. The 28,800 V.34 handshake had its own distinct opening — a longer sequence of probing tones, a more elaborate negotiation, the modem visibly working harder because the channel was being measured more carefully. The 33,600 added another step. The 56K V.90 connection, which arrived in 1998 and ended the era, sounded different again — louder, more confident, almost arrogant in its assumption that the line would behave.

The handshake took anywhere from twenty seconds to a full minute. You sat through it. There was nothing else to do. You watched the LEDs, listened to the speaker, and waited for the carrier to lock. When the lock came, the experience was relief. You had paid for the call to begin and you would pay for it to continue, and the cost was being incurred whether or not the connection succeeded; a failed handshake at long-distance rates was a real and noticeable loss. Success was a small celebration.

The modem was the second instrument of the late-night room. It is worth describing precisely, because the sound it made has become the only thing most people remember about it, and the sound was the smallest part of what it taught.

The technical anatomy is worth setting down for the record. Consumer modems entered the home in volume in the early 1980s, after the Hayes Smartmodem 300 (1981) established the AT command set that would define modem control for the next twenty years. The 300 was capable of transmitting three hundred bits per second over an ordinary phone line — about thirty characters per second, slower than a competent typist. Progress was steady through the decade: 1,200 baud became common by 1985, 2,400 by 1988, 9,600 by 1991. The 14,400 V.32bis standard arrived in 1991 and became the dominant home speed through the early 1990s. The 28,800 V.34 standard followed in 1994. The 33,600 enhancement came in 1996. The 56K V.90 standard, which exploited an asymmetry in the digital telephone backbone to push the downstream rate past what the analog channel could nominally carry, arrived in 1998 and represented the practical limit of dial-up technology. The whole consumer-modem era lasted, in its dominant form, about seventeen years.

Each speed step was a real event. When you upgraded from 2,400 to 9,600, you upgraded your relationship to the network. Files that had taken an hour took fifteen minutes. Pages that had loaded in a halting trickle now loaded in a perceptible rush. The marginal value of each baud-rate doubling was enormous, because the activities you could do at the new speed were qualitatively different from the activities you could do at the old. The 1,200 baud connection was for text. The 9,600 was for text plus the occasional small file. The 14,400 was for downloading a real program over the course of an evening. The 28,800 was for downloading an album-length collection of MP3s over the course of a night, once MP3s arrived. The 56K was for browsing a web that had begun to include images deliberately.

The speed mattered, but the speed was not the lesson. The lesson was the latency, and the latency was the lesson because the latency was inescapable.

A file transfer at 1,200 baud, of a file you would now consider trivially small — say, a hundred kilobytes — took roughly fifteen minutes if nothing went wrong, and twenty-five if something did. A larger file — five hundred kilobytes, a real program of the period — took an hour. A multi-megabyte file, of the kind that started to circulate in the era of the 14,400 modem, took most of a night. You started the transfer before you went to bed. You woke up in the morning to discover whether the transfer had completed or whether the line had dropped at three in the morning, leaving you with a partial file and a phone bill and the obligation to start over.

The latency was not a bug. The latency was the condition of the medium. There was no way to make a 1,200 baud modem faster than 1,200 baud short of upgrading to a different modem, which cost two hundred dollars and required your parents' permission. There was no way to make a long-distance file transfer cheaper than the per-minute rate the phone company charged. The cost and the time were the price of admission, and the price was non-negotiable.

What the cost and time produced, in the people who paid them habitually, was patience of a particular and unusual kind. It was not the patience of a person who is willing to wait for something pleasant. It was the patience of a person who has internalized that the operation in progress is being performed by a machine doing the best it can over a channel that cannot be hurried, and that the appropriate response to the duration of the operation is to do something else while it runs, to check on it periodically, to accept that it might fail, and to begin again if it does. This is patience of a structural kind. It is patience with the medium, not with the outcome. It is the patience required by any operation whose duration is set by physical limits and not by the operator's wishes.

This patience is the great unrecognized inheritance of the modem era. It is the cognitive trait that distinguishes practitioners who can work productively with slow systems from practitioners who cannot, and the slow systems in 2025 are not modems; they are language models running long-context inference, agent loops executing multi-step plans, batch jobs processing large datasets, simulations advancing one timestep at a time toward a result that will appear when it appears. The modern user who clicks a button and then waits three seconds before clicking it again, convinced that the first click did not register, is the user who never learned modem patience. The modern user who issues a long-context query, walks away from the screen, and returns ten minutes later expecting a thoughtful response — that user is operating, often without realizing it, on the cognitive template the modem installed.

The modern AI agent, executing a complex task across multiple tool calls and several minutes of inference time, asks of its operator a relationship to duration that the modem trained for. The agent is doing the best it can over a channel that cannot be hurried. The appropriate response is to do something else while it runs, to check on it periodically, to accept that it might fail, and to begin again if it does. The cohort that paid for fifteen-minute file transfers in 1989 paid, also, for a cognitive habit that has now become an asset on a different medium.

There was a second training the modem provided, less often noticed, that worked in parallel with the patience training. This was commitment training. The modem connection cost money. The amount varied — a local BBS call cost the same as any local call, which is to say nothing on an unmetered line and a few cents on a metered one; a long-distance BBS or commercial service like CompuServe or GEnie cost real per-minute fees that added up across an evening; an international call to a BBS in another country was an event that you planned and budgeted for. The cost meant that you did not idle. You did not connect and then wander away to do something else; the meter was running, and every minute you were on the line and not using it was a minute you were paying for. The connection was not free, and the non-freeness of it imposed a discipline of intentionality. You logged on with a purpose. You executed the purpose. You logged off.

This is unusual now. The modern network is effectively free, or at least its cost has been abstracted into a flat monthly fee that decouples usage from price. The user can leave a connection open all day, refresh a page every two minutes, alt-tab between four streaming services in a single afternoon, and pay nothing more than they would have paid for using none of these things. The decoupling has consequences. Without the meter, the discipline of intentionality erodes. The connection becomes ambient rather than purposive, present in the background rather than engaged with deliberately.

The cohort that learned to connect with intent carries the intent into AI work. A long-context model query that costs measurable money in API credits is, for this cohort, a familiar kind of object — an expensive call you make with purpose and observe with care, not an ambient activity you fire off carelessly. The behavior of treating each substantial inference as an investment rather than a default is the behavior the modem trained, transposed onto the new medium. The cohort that paid by the minute knows, in its bones, that the call costs something and that the call should accomplish something proportionate.

Bruce Sterling's *The Hacker Crackdown* (1992) — which is, among its other virtues, a participant-ethnography of the early networked culture — captures the texture of the era in passing. The phreakers and the hackers and the BBS sysops and the early commercial-service users were all dealing with the same medium, with its same costs and its same latencies, and the medium produced a particular kind of person: a person for whom every connection was an event, every transfer a commitment, every conversation across the wire a deliberate act conducted at expense. Sterling's subjects were not patient by temperament. They were patient because the medium demanded it. The medium has changed; the patience persists in the people the medium produced.

There is one more thing the modem taught, and it is worth naming because it has no modern equivalent and because the absence of an equivalent is part of what makes the cohort's relationship to networked machines distinct. The modem was audible. The negotiation happened in front of you, in your room, as sound. You could hear the connection forming. You could hear it fail. The audibility meant that the machine's behavior was not abstract — it was a sensory event in the same space as your own body. When a connection succeeded, you heard it succeed. When it failed, you heard it fail. You knew, instantly and somatically, the state of the link.

Modern networked communication is silent. The TCP handshake completes in milliseconds; you do not hear it; you do not even know it happened, except that the page loaded or did not. The silence of the modern network is efficient. It is also a kind of loss. The audible handshake produced, in the people who grew up hearing it, a felt sense of the network's presence — an awareness that the connection was a real thing made of physical processes happening at measurable speeds, not a magic property of having a device. This awareness translates, in the AI era, into something useful: a felt sense that the model's response is a real thing made of physical processes happening at measurable speeds, not a magic property of having a chat window. The cohort that heard modems negotiate does not, in general, believe that AI models are magic. They believe that AI models are very fast, very expensive, very capable processes running on machines whose hum they cannot hear but whose presence they can feel.

The chirp is gone. The hiss is gone. The trained ear that could distinguish 14,400 from 28,800 by the opening tones is mostly retired now, or working with grandchildren who have no idea what their grandparent is talking about when they describe the sound. The modem itself is gone, replaced by network infrastructure that does the same work without the audible part. What remains is the patience the modem taught, the commitment the modem trained, the somatic awareness of network as physical process that the audible handshake installed in the people who heard it ten thousand times across a decade of evenings.

There is also a social dimension of the modem that deserves to be named, because the modem was not only an instrument of solitary network access but the gateway to the first networked social environments the cohort encountered. The BBS — the bulletin board system that the next part of the book takes up in detail — was reached through the modem, and the experience of reaching it was inseparable from the modem's specific affordances. The user dialed a number, waited for the handshake, logged in, conducted whatever business they had with the system, logged out, and hung up. The session was bounded by the dialing and the disconnection. There was no always-on connection to a forum, no persistent presence in a chat, no ambient awareness of distant interlocutors. You went to the BBS, you did what you went there to do, and you came home.

This bounded-session character of early networked sociality is worth examining, because it produced a kind of online engagement that has no modern equivalent. The BBS visitor was visiting. They were not living on the system. They were not passively present in the background of their own life. They had come, with intent, for a specific duration, to do a specific thing, and then they would leave. The mode of engagement was visiting-as-an-event, not living-as-a-default. The cohort learned this mode at the modem and brought a residual version of it into subsequent online environments — into the early web, into the early chat services, into the contemporary social platforms — where the mode now reads as restraint, or as healthy boundaries, or as wisdom that the platforms themselves do not encourage. The mode is none of those things; it is simply the inherited default of a generation that learned online behavior in a medium that required intentional connection and active disconnection.

There is one final dimension of the modem's training that deserves to be set down, which has to do with the experience of failure. Modem connections failed often. The line was noisy. The remote machine's modem was a different brand and the negotiation did not converge cleanly. The phone switch dropped the call halfway through a transfer. A burst of line noise corrupted a packet and the protocol — XMODEM, ZMODEM, Kermit, whichever was in use — either recovered or didn't, depending on its sophistication and luck. The user lived inside a regime in which every long operation might fail and the failure might come at any point during the operation and the user was responsible for the recovery.

This produced a particular relationship to failure that the modern computing environment, with its enormously more reliable network infrastructure, has erased. The modem user assumed failure was probable, planned for it, and developed reflexes for handling it. The download that failed at ninety percent was not an outrage; it was a foreseeable outcome that had to be accommodated. The standard tool was the ZMODEM resume — the protocol's ability to pick up an interrupted transfer at the point of failure rather than restarting from the beginning. The user who had not used ZMODEM resume often enough to have it in their muscle memory was a user who had not yet been initiated into the medium's reality.

The cognitive trait this produced is something like operational pessimism — the assumption, baked deep into the user's expectations, that any nontrivial operation might fail and that the appropriate response to failure is not anger but recovery. Modern AI agents exhibit failure modes that are different in character from modem failures — they hallucinate, they truncate, they misunderstand instructions, they produce plausible nonsense — but the appropriate response is structurally the same. The user who has internalized operational pessimism handles AI failure with the same equanimity they once handled a dropped file transfer: they note the failure, identify what was salvageable, and resume from the most useful checkpoint. The user who has not internalized operational pessimism handles AI failure as a betrayal — the system was supposed to work, it didn't, and the failure feels personal. The first relationship is the productive one. The modem trained it in the cohort that grew up paying for it.

It is worth recording one further texture, because it locates the modem experience in a particular emotional register that the technical description alone does not capture. The modem belonged, in most households, to a single phone line that the entire family shared. Connecting to a BBS or to a commercial service meant tying up the line. Outgoing calls became impossible during your session. Incoming calls would be greeted by your modem's answering tones if you had configured auto-answer, or would simply go unanswered. The arrangement made the modem a household-political object — its use was negotiated, sometimes rationed, occasionally fought about. The teenager who wanted to be online and the parent who wanted to make a phone call had a real conflict whose terms were technological and whose resolution was social. The cohort learned, in this context, that network access was a scarce resource that affected other people, and that using it implied a small obligation to be economical and considerate about the using.

This household-political dimension of network access produced a sensibility about online activity that has eroded as connectivity has become ambient and continuous. The cohort that grew up negotiating for line time tends to retain a residual sense that online activity is consequential, that connecting is not free, and that the connection is a thing one does rather than a state one lives in. This sensibility, transposed onto AI work, produces practitioners who tend to ask whether a particular AI query is worth making rather than firing off queries reflexively. The economic discipline shows up in the work as a kind of considered restraint, which the practitioners themselves often do not recognize as inherited because the inheritance happened so early and so quietly that it has become invisible to them.

These traits are now assets. They were not designed to be assets. They were the side effects of paying for slow things, and the paying produced people whose relationship to slow things had matured beyond the modern default.
