# Chapter 28: Forgotten Futures

The bookstore is one of the used ones on the second floor of an old building, the kind whose stairs creak in a way that the owner has long since stopped apologizing for. Sometime in the early 2010s. You are looking for nothing in particular. The AI section is small and shelved between Philosophy and Computer Hobbies, which is itself shelved next to a category labeled simply Manuals — a graveyard of WordPerfect 5.1 guides and OS/2 Warp companions, books that were authoritative for eighteen months and obsolete for thirty years. You pull a paperback off the AI shelf because the spine has faded to the color of skim milk and you cannot read the title without tilting it into the light. *Machines Who Think*. Pamela McCorduck. 1979. The cover has an illustration of a chess piece in front of a punch-card-styled pattern. You open it to the middle, where she is describing the Stanford Heuristic Programming Project and the work on DENDRAL, and you find yourself reading three pages standing up.

What strikes you is not the prose, which is excellent, or the cast of characters, which is brilliant. What strikes you is that McCorduck, writing in 1979, is describing — with precision, with technical literacy, with what reads from the present as eerie confidence — the systems the AI industry would spend the next forty-five years trying and failing and finally beginning to build. Rule-based reasoning over structured knowledge. Explainable inference chains. Domain-specific expertise encoded in declarative form. The vocabulary is older than yours; the architectures are not. You buy the book for three dollars and a state quarter, take it home, and finish it in a weekend. You feel, the whole time, that you are reading a transmission from a future that arrived later than scheduled and through a different door than expected.

The book stays on the shelf. You add to it, over the next few years, a worn copy of Norbert Wiener's *Cybernetics* (1948), a discarded library copy of Feigenbaum and Feldman's *Computers and Thought* (1963), a paperback of Marvin Minsky's *The Society of Mind* (1986), the original Hayes-Roth, Waterman, and Lenat *Building Expert Systems* (1983). The shelf becomes a small archive of a discipline that was confident in 1979, embattled by 1989, and largely abandoned as a named field by 1999. You return to it whenever you read a press release about a new agentic framework or a knowledge-graph platform or a retrieval-augmented reasoner. The press releases describe themselves as new. The shelf, calmly, says they are not.

The argument this chapter makes is that the AI substrate carries forgotten futures. The cohort that came of age reading McCorduck and Hofstadter and Minsky did not learn AI in the deep-learning sense; that mathematics had not yet matured for the consumer reader. What that cohort learned was an architectural vocabulary — symbolic representation, rule-based inference, expert systems, knowledge engineering, cybernetic feedback, adaptive control — that subsequent decades treated as a dead branch of the field. The ideas were not wrong. They were early. The compute was not adequate to the architectures. The data was not available to populate the knowledge bases at scale. The integration infrastructure that would have let an expert system reach into the operational substrate of a real business did not exist. The architectures starved on inadequate substrate. The cohort that read the books carried the ideas forward as latent knowledge, available to be revived when the substrate caught up. The substrate has caught up. The ideas are returning.

The expert-systems era of the 1970s and 1980s is the cleanest case study, because the rise and the collapse are both well documented. MYCIN — the medical-diagnostic system developed at Stanford by Edward Shortliffe under Bruce Buchanan's supervision, between roughly 1972 and 1980 — was a rule-based reasoner that diagnosed bacterial infections and recommended antibiotic therapy at the level of an attending infectious-disease specialist, in clinical evaluations conducted by the Stanford Medical School itself. The system was never deployed in production. The reasons were not technical. They were institutional and infrastructural: the system ran on a DEC PDP-10 the size of a refrigerator, took inputs through a keyboard at a time when no clinician had a keyboard at the bedside, and operated in a regulatory environment that had no category for software-as-clinical-decision-support. The architecture was sound. The substrate refused it. MYCIN remained a research demonstration whose published evaluations are still cited as the gold standard for what rule-based clinical reasoning could achieve. The system that achieved them was never used to treat a patient.

DENDRAL, the chemical-structure-elucidation system that preceded MYCIN in Feigenbaum's lab, had similar properties and a similar fate. It reasoned over mass-spectrometry data to propose molecular structures consistent with observed fragmentation patterns; it codified the heuristic knowledge of expert organic chemists into rule sets that could explore the structural space more thoroughly than any human chemist had time to do. It worked. It was used. It was extended into Meta-DENDRAL, which could induce its own rules from training examples — the earliest form of what would later be called machine learning, in 1976, twenty years before the term reentered popular usage. The system was extraordinary. The mass-spectrometry community absorbed pieces of its methodology and forgot the architecture that produced them. The lineage was severed not because the science failed but because the next generation of chemists trained on different tools and the institutional memory of what DENDRAL had been atrophied.

XCON, Digital Equipment Corporation's configuration system for VAX hardware orders, was the commercial success that the expert-systems community pointed to as evidence the architecture worked at industrial scale. It went into production at DEC in 1980, configured tens of thousands of VAX systems over the following decade, saved DEC tens of millions of dollars annually, and was credited with making the company's complex hardware product line economically viable. The system was implemented in OPS5, a rule-based production-system language. It contained, at its peak, over seventeen thousand rules. It was the largest deployed expert system in the world. When DEC's hardware business contracted and the company was eventually acquired by Compaq in 1998, XCON was retired alongside the product line it had configured. Its operational lessons — that knowledge-base maintenance is harder than initial knowledge acquisition, that rule interactions become combinatorially difficult past a certain scale, that the human experts who feed the system must be incentivized to keep doing so — were preserved in academic papers and forgotten by the industry that followed.

The collapse of the expert-systems boom in the late 1980s and early 1990s, often referred to as the second AI winter, is conventionally attributed to overpromising by vendors and the brittleness of the systems when they were taken outside the narrow domains in which they had been built. Both diagnoses are correct. Neither is complete. The deeper diagnosis is that the substrate was inadequate. The compute was inadequate: a serious expert system needed dedicated hardware (the Lisp machines from Symbolics and Lisp Machine Inc. that the era produced) and the dedicated hardware was outcompeted on price-performance by general-purpose workstations from Sun and DEC. The data was inadequate: the rules had to be elicited from human experts in slow, expensive interview sessions, because there was no operational data store from which the rules could be induced. The integration was inadequate: the expert system sat in a corner of the enterprise, talking to no other system, and the operational workflows that should have invoked it had to be re-engineered manually to route through it. Each inadequacy was solvable in principle. Together they made the architectures uneconomical, and the funding moved elsewhere.

The cybernetics legacy is the parallel case, on a longer timescale. Norbert Wiener's *Cybernetics* (1948) and *The Human Use of Human Beings* (1950) named a discipline that proposed to study control and communication in animals and machines under a single formalism. The vocabulary Wiener developed — feedback, homeostasis, signal-to-noise, information as a quantitative measure — became foundational to electrical engineering, control theory, and information theory, each of which crystallized into its own discipline and forgot the parent that had named them. W. Ross Ashby's *Design for a Brain* (1952) and *An Introduction to Cybernetics* (1956) developed the formal apparatus further: the law of requisite variety, the homeostat, the analysis of adaptation in deterministic systems. Heinz von Foerster's second-order cybernetics, at the Biological Computer Laboratory in Illinois through the 1960s and into the 1970s, extended the framework to systems that observe themselves observing — the recursive layer that became, decades later, one of the central architectural questions in autonomous systems. The Macy conferences from 1946 to 1953 hosted the foundational discussions, with Wiener, Ashby, von Foerster, Margaret Mead, Gregory Bateson, John von Neumann, Claude Shannon, and Warren McCulloch in attendance — a roster that reads, from the present, as the founding generation of half a dozen modern fields.

Cybernetics as a named discipline faded by the 1970s. Its vocabulary was absorbed into the fields it had spawned. Stafford Beer's work on managerial cybernetics, including the famous Cybersyn project in Allende's Chile (1971-1973), demonstrated the framework at industrial scale and was abandoned when the political environment that hosted it collapsed. The academic departments that had taught cybernetics renamed themselves systems engineering or operations research or computer science. The undergraduate curriculum that would have transmitted the vocabulary to the next generation lost its host. By the time the cohort that read McCorduck and Hofstadter at fifteen reached graduate school, cybernetics was a footnote in the history of computing — a discipline mentioned for its founders' brilliance and presumed not to have produced anything of lasting practical value.

The presumption was wrong. The vocabulary cybernetics produced is exactly the vocabulary contemporary AI work requires. Feedback loops are the foundation of every adaptive system. Homeostasis is the formal name for what production engineers spend their careers maintaining under shifting load. Requisite variety — Ashby's law that a controller must have at least as much state-space variety as the system it controls — is the architectural reason a fixed rule set will fail against an evolving environment, and the architectural justification for systems that can extend their own rule sets under operational pressure. Second-order observation is what every audit trail attempts to make available: a system that records what it did and why, available for inspection by an observer outside the loop. The vocabulary was developed too early for the substrate to demand it at industrial scale. The substrate now demands it. The vocabulary is being recovered, in vocabulary that has forgotten its origin, by practitioners who would benefit from knowing where their concepts came from.

The pattern across the expert-systems and cybernetics cases is the same. Architectures that were sound at the level of theory failed in their first era because the substrate could not support them. The substrate has now caught up. Compute is effectively unlimited at the relevant scales. Data is abundant and continuously generated by the operational systems the architectures will govern. Integration is solved: any modern application can speak to any other through standard interfaces. The conditions that made the original architectures uneconomical are gone. What remains is the architectural question — what should an industrial cognitive system look like, now that it can be built — and the cohort with the latent knowledge to answer it is the cohort that read the original books at the right age and never quite let the ideas go.

This is the appropriate point in the book to name the architecture that has, in the past several years, begun to crystallize around the question. The architecture is called Agicore. It is the subject of this book's companion volume — *Agicore: Theory, Architecture, and Practice* — which the author has written in parallel with this one, and which the reader can consult for the full technical treatment. The compressed account, sufficient for the present argument, is this. Agicore is a domain-specific language for declaring cognitive production systems. The language admits a small set of primitive declarations — RULE, FACT, STATE, PATTERN, SCORE, MODULE, ACTION, WORKFLOW, REASONER, and several others — that compile to deterministic Rust and TypeScript code. The compiled system runs without any AI in its decision path: rules match facts, actions fire, workflows advance through stages, all of it as deterministic as any expert system from the 1980s and orders of magnitude more capable, because the substrate is now adequate. AI participates at the edit boundary — proposing changes to rules, drafting new patterns, refining thresholds — but never in the runtime. When the running expert system encounters a condition it cannot handle, it pulls an andon cord; the cord halts the workflow cleanly, captures the failure with full provenance, and routes the failure case to a reasoner that proposes a fix. The proposed fix passes through mechanical gates — tier verification, sandbox replay, regression testing, optional shadow evaluation, optional human approval — before it touches the running system. Every transition is recorded in a SHA-256 hash chain. The architecture is a 1980s expert system reincarnated on adequate substrate, with the lessons of the 1980s collapse incorporated.

The continuity is exact, not metaphorical. The RULE declaration in Agicore is the rule of MYCIN, restated in syntax the contemporary compiler can verify. The MODULE declaration is the knowledge module of OPS5, modernized to admit type contracts on its inputs and outputs. The STATE and PATTERN declarations encode the conditions and triggers that expert systems of the previous era had to express in less ergonomic syntax. The SCORE declaration generalizes the certainty factors that MYCIN famously pioneered. The MUTATION_POLICY declaration — the governance layer that controls how AI is allowed to propose changes to the rules — is what the expert-systems community of the 1980s would have built if they had had to govern AI-proposed mutations to their rule bases, which they did not, because the AI that could propose such mutations did not exist yet. The architecture absorbs the prior era's lessons and extends them with the governance machinery the present era requires. The compiler emits ten thousand lines of Rust and TypeScript from a few hundred lines of declarations, which is exactly the productivity multiplier the original expert-systems community sought from their declarative substrates and never quite achieved.

The lessons of the 1980s collapse are visible throughout the Agicore architecture, encoded structurally so that they do not have to be remembered as procedure. The brittleness problem — that an expert system fails ungracefully outside its design envelope — is addressed by the andon cord, which makes the failure mode explicit and routes it into an improvement loop rather than allowing the system to silently default or to propagate corrupt state downstream. The knowledge-base maintenance problem — that rule sets degrade as the underlying world evolves and the original experts retire — is addressed by the operational-pressure evolution model, in which the running application's own failures drive the proposals that update the rule base, continuously and without requiring a human expert to be interviewed. The combinatorial-interaction problem — that rule sets past a certain size develop unpredictable interactions — is addressed by the sandbox-and-regression-suite gate, which mechanically tests every proposed addition against historical traffic before deployment. The integration problem — that the expert system sat in a corner — is addressed by the compiler's emission of full application stacks, with the expert system embedded in the operational substrate by construction. Each of the original failure modes has a structural answer in the new architecture. The structural answers were not invented from nothing. They were extracted from forty years of operational experience with the categories the original architectures attempted and failed to industrialize.

The cohort that read McCorduck in 1979 and built MYCIN-shaped systems in their imaginations through the 1980s and 1990s — and who watched the field abandon those systems for reasons that were never quite about the architectures themselves — is the cohort that can now read the Agicore declarations and feel, with the recognition that this book is in part the archaeology of, that the architecture is continuous with what they already knew. The vocabulary has updated. The compiler is real where the compilers of the prior era were sketches. The mechanical gates that govern AI's participation are formalized where the governance discussions of the prior era were aspirational. The shape, however, is the same shape. The forgotten futures are returning. They are returning in the architecture the cohort that remembered them is building.

There is a parallel forgotten future worth naming, because it belongs to the same lineage and because the present chapter would be incomplete without it. The blackboard architecture, developed at Carnegie Mellon by Lesser, Erman, and Reddy as part of the Hearsay-II speech-understanding project from roughly 1973 through 1980, proposed a model of cooperative problem solving in which independent knowledge sources contributed hypotheses to a shared workspace, and the workspace's contents drove the next round of contributions. The architecture was elegant and was, like MYCIN and DENDRAL, abandoned not because it was unsound but because the substrate could not support its requirements at the scale the speech problem required. Hearsay-II ran at perhaps one one-hundredth of real time on the hardware available to it; the gap between architecture and substrate was too large to close with the engineering of the period. The blackboard's structural insight — that cooperative reasoning across independently maintained knowledge bases is a productive model when the substrate can support it — has since reappeared, unnamed, in dozens of contemporary multi-agent architectures. The vocabulary has changed. The architecture is recognizable.

The Cyc project, begun by Doug Lenat at MCC in 1984 with the explicit goal of encoding the commonsense knowledge required for human-level reasoning into a single large knowledge base, deserves its own paragraph because it is the case the field most loves to cite as evidence that symbolic AI does not work at scale. The diagnosis is partly correct and partly wrong. Cyc accumulated millions of assertions over its decades of development, and the assertions are real knowledge about a real ontology. What Cyc could not solve was the inference scaling: the deduction over a knowledge base that large produced more candidate inferences than the engine could evaluate within useful time bounds. The substrate was inadequate. The contemporary observation, which the architects who lived through the Cyc era should be permitted to make, is that retrieval-augmented language models are doing approximately what Cyc was attempting to do, with the inference work outsourced to a statistical engine that can complete it in milliseconds and the knowledge representation outsourced to natural-language text that can be ingested at corpus scale. The architecture is not identical. The aspiration is. The cohort that read Lenat's papers in the 1980s recognized the aspiration immediately when the contemporary systems began achieving fragments of it.

The frame problem, articulated by McCarthy and Hayes in their 1969 paper of the same name, named the formal difficulty that has haunted symbolic AI since: when an action changes the world, how does the reasoner know what did not change, without enumerating every fact that the action could not possibly have affected? The frame problem was diagnosed as fatal to naive symbolic approaches in the 1970s and 1980s, and it was. The contemporary response, embedded in the architectures the present chapter is describing, is that the frame problem dissolves when the reasoner does not have to enumerate the world but instead has access to a substrate that the world updates continuously and that the reasoner can query when relevant. The deterministic Agicore runtime does not maintain a complete world model; it maintains the state the declarations specify, and the andon cord fires when the state is found inadequate. The substrate has made the frame problem manageable by making the world inspectable rather than requiring it to be enumerated. The forgotten future returns through a different door than the original architects expected, and finds itself surprised at the welcome.

The closing observation, which the next chapter will develop into the trilogy's central thesis, is that the architectural inheritance carries a moral inheritance with it. The expert-systems community of the 1980s knew, before they had any AI capable of proposing changes to their rule bases, that an architecture in which an automated reasoner could modify the rules of an industrial production system without governance was structurally dangerous. They wrote about it. They proposed governance frameworks. They could not implement the frameworks because they did not have the reasoners that would have required governing. The reasoners have now arrived. The architectures they require for safe operation were forecast forty years ago by the cohort that is now old enough to be writing the architectures into code. The forecast and the implementation are arriving in the same minds. The substrate is adequate. The discipline is articulated. The next chapter shows what the cohort that watched WarGames at the right age finally learned to do with all of it.
