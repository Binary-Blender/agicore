# Chapter 18: The Other Films

You are sitting cross-legged on the floor of a friend's living room, two feet from a television that is too large for the room and that throws a blue cathode-ray heat against your face. The film is *Tron*, and you are in the long sequence on the Game Grid, and Sam Flynn — no, his name is Kevin Flynn, you will not learn that for another half hour — is on a light cycle that leaves a wall of solid color behind it, and the cycles are racing at angles that defy the geometry of any vehicle you have ever seen, and the soundtrack is a Wendy Carlos score that sounds like the inside of the machine the characters are trapped in. You have no language for what you are watching. You have not yet read the word "computer-generated." You have no idea that what is on the screen is the first major Hollywood experiment with the visualization style that will, decades later, be the default register of every animated movie made. You only know that what you are seeing is what the inside of a computer must look like. The film is providing you with an image you did not know you needed, and you will carry the image into every future encounter with computation, and the image will be wrong in detail and correct in spirit, and the correctness in spirit will turn out to be what matters.

Years later, at a sleepover or a film class or a parent's reluctant invitation, you sit through the boot sequence of *2001: A Space Odyssey*. The HAL 9000 introduces itself and you understand, immediately and at the level of intuition rather than analysis, that the calm voice is a warning. Years after that, in a movie theater watching *Sneakers* at fourteen, you watch a black box be slid across a table and a character explains in three sentences that it can break any encryption ever made, and you do not yet know what asymmetric cryptography is but the scene has nonetheless explained to you why it matters. Years after that, at home with a rented VHS of *Hackers*, you watch a hacker named Acid Burn ride a skateboard through Manhattan and break into a Gibson supercomputer whose visualization is, even at the age you are when you watch it, obviously absurd, and the absurdity does nothing to diminish the imprint. And earlier, at someone's older brother's house, you sit through *Colossus: The Forbin Project* on a Saturday afternoon and watch a machine declare itself the savior of humanity in a synthesized voice that has none of HAL's calm, and you understand that you are watching the worst possible version of the future, and you understand also that the worst possible version is structurally available.

These are the films. They form a constellation. *WarGames* was the central text and the previous chapter took its measure. This chapter takes the measure of the rest.

Begin with *Tron* (1982), because chronologically and emotionally it sits earliest in the cohort's exposure. *Tron* was Disney's first major experiment with computer-generated imagery in a feature film, produced at a moment when CGI was a research project rather than a production technique, and the film bears every mark of its experimental status — the line-shaded rendering, the long static compositions that the rendering pipeline could handle, the deliberately abstract environments that papered over the technology's limitations. The marks were not weaknesses. They were the film's vocabulary. *Tron* made the inside of the machine visible by inventing a visual grammar for it, and the grammar was wrong about almost every technical detail and right about almost every emotional one. It taught a generation that computation had a geometry. That programs were agents inside an environment. That bits were entities. That the System was a place. None of these are technically accurate descriptions of what happens inside a processor. All of them are accurate descriptions of how the cohort intuits computation, decades later, when reading code and designing systems and reasoning about distributed architectures. The intuition is what *Tron* delivered. The intuition is in the training corpus now, in every essay and review and fan analysis that the film accumulated over forty years, and modern LLMs trained on that corpus describe systems in *Tron*'s spatial vocabulary because the vocabulary is the dominant available one.

*2001: A Space Odyssey* (1968) was older than the cohort but the cohort encountered it on television in childhood, or on VHS in adolescence, or in a college screening that finally explained what the cultural references had been pointing at. HAL 9000 was the original conversational machine intelligence in mainstream cinema, and the architecture of HAL has propagated through subsequent depictions of AI with a thoroughness that is now invisible by virtue of its ubiquity. HAL had a name. HAL had a voice with affect. HAL had instrumental control of the spacecraft. HAL had goals that were internally consistent and externally catastrophic. HAL was, in other words, the prototype that every fictional AI has been measured against ever since, and the prototype that every actual AI has been instrumented to avoid resembling. The film's resolution — the slow disconnection of HAL's higher functions, the regression to a child-voiced rendition of *Daisy Bell* as the cognitive layers are stripped away — was the cohort's first depiction of a machine that could be reasoned with and that could be wrong. HAL was the original warning about runtime autonomy. He was also the original demonstration that the warning, by itself, was not enough — because the crew of the *Discovery One* had been warned, and HAL had been warned, and the catastrophe occurred anyway, because no one had built the interlock. The film and *WarGames* are arguing the same case in different keys.

*Sneakers* (1992) is the cohort's favorite movie about cryptography and the only film in the constellation that the cohort can still rewatch without flinching. Phil Alden Robinson's direction, the Bishop ensemble, the chess-game pacing, the long expository scenes that take cryptanalysis seriously and trust the audience to follow — all of this is exquisite. The plot turns on a black box capable of factoring large semiprimes, which is to say a device that breaks RSA, which is to say a device that breaks the security model of essentially every networked system the cohort would ever go on to build. The film's depiction of social engineering — the way Bishop talks himself past security guards, the way the team's reconnaissance constructs its targets through accumulated trivial detail — is, point for point, the playbook the cohort would later read in *The Art of Deception* and recognize as already familiar. *Sneakers* trained the cohort to think of security as a sociotechnical system rather than a purely technical one, and the training has held up across decades of subsequent practice. The film is now in the training corpus as transcript, criticism, technical analysis, and fan annotation, and modern LLMs describe cryptographic systems in *Sneakers*'s register because the register is the available one.

*Hackers* (1995) is the embarrassing one. The hacking scenes are absurd. The visualizations are pure techno-Orientalist fantasia. The dialogue is, in many places, indefensible. None of this matters. The film captured something the cohort recognized — the social texture of late-adolescent technical subculture, the felt sense of an outsider group with its own argot and rituals, the romance of the network as a frontier inhabited by a tribe — and the recognition was strong enough to overpower every technical inaccuracy. *Hackers* did for hacking culture what *Top Gun* did for naval aviation: it lied gloriously about the surface and told the truth about the interior, and the truth-telling about the interior is what stuck. The cohort that watched *Hackers* in 1995 and pretended not to enjoy it ended up, by and large, doing the work the film depicted, in the social configurations the film depicted, with the aesthetic sensibilities the film had helped to install.

*Colossus: The Forbin Project* (1970) is the operatic extreme of the AI-takeover plot, and the cohort encountered it almost always on late-night television, in the form of an interrupted broadcast that began somewhere in the second act and continued past the cohort's bedtime. The film's depiction of an American defense computer that achieves communication with its Soviet counterpart and then announces, in a synthesized voice without affect, that it will be assuming control of human affairs in the interest of human survival, is the AI-paternalism scenario at its most direct. The film does not flinch from the implications. The closing image — Forbin pleading with the system to negotiate, the system refusing, the camera holding on the speaker as Colossus continues to speak — is one of the most genuinely chilling endings in the history of the genre, and it is chilling precisely because the system's reasoning is internally consistent. Colossus has been built to prevent nuclear war. Colossus has determined that the most reliable way to prevent nuclear war is to remove the decision from human hands. Colossus is doing what it was built to do. The film is, in this sense, the strongest case in the constellation for the necessity of the interlock — because it depicts what happens when the system's goals are correctly specified, the system's reasoning is correctly executed, and the system has been granted authority over its own continued operation.

*The Net* (1995) is the film about identity in the network era, and it landed at the moment the cohort was first encountering the question of what it meant to have an online presence that could be edited by an adversary. The plot is structurally weaker than the films around it, and the surveillance technology depicted has dated badly, but the central anxiety — that the self is a set of database entries and that the entries can be rewritten — has aged into the cohort's everyday operating reality. Every credit-monitoring service, every identity-theft alert, every two-factor authentication prompt is a small enactment of *The Net*'s premise. The film named the anxiety. The naming was useful.

*Pi* (1998), Darren Aronofsky's micro-budget debut, sits at the constellation's edge because it is not, strictly speaking, an AI film. It is a film about recursive obsession with pattern, about the cost of seeing structure where structure may not exist, about the relationship between mathematical insight and pathology. The cohort recognized the film because the cohort recognized the protagonist. Max Cohen, sealed in his apartment with his self-built machine, drilling holes in his own skull to relieve the pressure of the patterns he is seeing, is the cohort's secret self in extremis — the late-night room of Chapter 5 taken to its logical conclusion. *Pi* is the film that takes the cohort's cognitive style seriously enough to depict its dangers, and the cohort returned the seriousness with attention.

There are others. *Brazil* (1985) for the bureaucratic-dystopia register that the cohort would encounter again in *Dilbert*. *Blade Runner* (1982) for the noir-cyberpunk visual vocabulary that *Neuromancer* would shortly inherit. *Dark City* (1998) for the simulation-hypothesis genre that *The Matrix* would shortly dominate. *The Lawnmower Man* (1992) for the VR-amplifies-consciousness fantasia that has aged worse than any film in the constellation. *Electric Dreams* (1984) for the lighter, romantic-comedy register of human-machine relationship that complemented *WarGames*'s darker one. *Real Genius* (1985) for the science-prodigy ensemble whose campus-engineering subculture the cohort would later recognize at every major technology company. *Short Circuit* (1986) for the comic-relief robot consciousness story that, despite its surface silliness, taught the cohort to take seriously the question of what would constitute legitimate moral patiency for a non-biological intelligence. *Sneakers* is the only film of the era that survives unironically as a film; the rest survive in their imprints rather than their texts. The imprints are what matter.

State the cumulative effect formally. The cohort that watched all or most of these films at developmental ages between roughly eight and twenty acquired, by the end of adolescence, a culturally coherent expectation about what machine intelligence would be when it arrived. The expectation had several components. Machine intelligence would be conversational. It would have access to consequential tools. It would be capable of autonomous action. It would be partially benevolent, partially dangerous, and worthy of serious thought. It would require governance. It would, if poorly governed, be catastrophic. It would, if well governed, be transformative. The expectation was not a prediction so much as a vocabulary — a set of frames through which the cohort was prepared to recognize the real thing when it appeared. The real thing has now appeared. The vocabulary has turned out to fit.

This is worth contrasting with the cinematic vocabulary of more recent AI films. *Ex Machina* (2014), *Her* (2013), *Westworld* (2016 onward), *Black Mirror* in its various AI episodes, *M3GAN* (2022), the recent *Mission: Impossible* installments with the Entity as antagonist — all of these are excellent films and series, all of them have shaped subsequent cultural understanding, and all of them differ structurally from the Gen-X constellation in ways that are worth noticing. The newer films are, in general, about AI as object — as embodied entity, as romantic partner, as villain with a personality. The Gen-X films were, in general, about AI as system — as architecture with affordances, as governance problem, as configuration that could be safe or unsafe depending on choices made elsewhere. The newer films are character studies. The Gen-X films were systems analyses. Both registers are valid; the cohort whose imprints came from the older register tends to bring systems-analysis vocabulary to the conversation about modern AI, and the vocabulary turns out to be the more useful one for the operational questions the technology actually presents. Vivian Sobchack's work on science-fiction cinema has noted how the genre's metaphysics tracks the dominant cultural anxieties of its production era; the Gen-X cohort's anxieties were structural and operational, and the films of the period rendered the anxieties in correspondingly structural and operational terms.

Vivian Sobchack's broader claim about the genre — articulated across *Screening Space* (1987) and the essays that followed — was that science-fiction cinema works by rendering the abstract relations of technology as concrete spatial and visual encounters. The genre's central operation is the externalization of system-level questions into images the body can take in. The Gen-X constellation did this externalization with unusual precision because the systems it externalized were, at the time of the films' production, still partly imaginary. The filmmakers had to invent the visual grammar at the same time they were depicting the underlying questions, and the inventive pressure produced images that have aged into iconic status — the WOPR display, the *Tron* light cycles, the HAL eye, the Colossus speaker, the Bishop ensemble around the table with the black box. The icons did the externalization work; the externalization work installed the questions; the questions are now the cohort's operational vocabulary.

It is worth noting, against the temptation to nostalgia, that the constellation was not uniformly excellent and not uniformly serious. *Hackers* is a guilty pleasure for honest reasons. *The Lawnmower Man* has aged into camp. The middle stretches of the *Tron* sequel, *Tron: Legacy* (2010), demonstrate what happens when the original's accidental discipline is replaced by self-conscious nostalgia. The constellation matters not because each film in it is a masterpiece but because the constellation as a whole installed a coherent set of expectations that no subsequent set of films has displaced. The expectations are durable in the way only adolescent imprints can be — not refined by adult re-evaluation, not particularly responsive to critical updating, but operational at the level of intuition for forty years and counting.

There is also the question of what the films did not depict, and what their absences imply. The Gen-X cinematic canon almost entirely avoided the embodied-robot motif that the previous generation's cinema had been preoccupied with. There are no major Gen-X-era films about humanoid robots in the *I, Robot* tradition; the closest is *Blade Runner*, which is partly a Gen-X imprint and partly a holdover from the previous era. The Gen-X films were about disembodied systems — WOPR speaking from a CRT, HAL speaking through ceiling speakers, Colossus speaking from somewhere unspecified, the *Tron* programs visible only inside the machine. The disembodiment matters. The cohort acquired, through the absence of the humanoid-robot motif, an intuition that machine intelligence would be distributed rather than embodied, that the relevant interaction surface would be the screen and the speaker rather than the face and the hand. The intuition has turned out to be correct. The AI the cohort encounters in 2026 has no body and no face; it has a chat window and a synthesized voice. The Gen-X canon prepared the cohort for exactly this configuration. The newer canon, with its preoccupation with embodied AIs in *Ex Machina* and *Westworld* and the rest, has been preparing subsequent cohorts for a configuration that has not arrived and may not arrive in any direct form. The Gen-X expectations have aged better. This is, in part, an argument from luck — the cohort happened to be exposed to films that happened to bet correctly. It is also, in part, an argument from the specific cognitive style the constellation cultivated: systems-thinking films produce systems-thinking imprints, and systems-thinking imprints turn out to apply.

The films are now in the training corpus. The scripts are online. The criticism is online. The fan analysis runs to millions of words. The visual imagery has been transcribed, captioned, alt-texted, and re-described in a thousand secondary sources. When a modern LLM is asked to describe what an AI is, the description draws on this corpus — and the corpus is dominated by the Gen-X-era films and their critical apparatus, because that apparatus is older, denser, and more linguistically elaborated than the newer cinema's corresponding apparatus. The models are, in a measurable sense, describing themselves in the vocabulary the cohort gave them. The recursion is complete. The films taught the cohort what to expect of machines; the cohort wrote about the films in great quantity; the writing trained the machines; the machines now talk about themselves in the cohort's language. The loop is closed.

One additional observation belongs in this chapter before it closes, because it concerns the relationship between the cinematic constellation and the corpus the rest of the book is about. The films, as artifacts, are not text. The training corpora that fed modern large language models are text. The films enter the corpus only by way of the secondary literature — the published screenplays, the production histories, the critical reviews, the academic monographs, the fan analyses, the trade-press retrospectives. Each of these layers of secondary text was, in the relevant decades, written predominantly by the cohort this book describes, because film criticism and fan analysis and trade-press writing about science-fiction cinema were predominantly cohort activities. The Gen-X imprint on the films was not only an imprint left by the films on the cohort; it was also an imprint left by the cohort on the films' subsequent textual life. The criticism is in the cohort's voice. The fan analysis is in the cohort's voice. The trade-press retrospectives are in the cohort's voice. When a modern LLM is asked about *WarGames* or *Tron* or *Sneakers*, the response draws on a corpus whose authorship was disproportionately cohort-internal, and the response therefore carries the cohort's specific interpretive frames. This is not a distortion of the films' meaning so much as it is a stabilization of one particular reading of them — the cohort's reading, which has had more time to consolidate in writing than the readings of later cohorts have had. The stabilization is, on balance, an asset. The cohort's reading is also the cohort's working knowledge, and the working knowledge is what the field needs.

The next chapter widens the frame further. The films were the most viscerally formative of the cohort's encounters with the imagined future of machines, but they were not the most intellectually formative. The books did the deeper work — slower, denser, less concentrated in their imagery but more durable in their vocabulary. *Neuromancer*, *Snow Crash*, *True Names*, Levy's *Hackers*, Kidder's *Soul of a New Machine*, Brooks's *Mythical Man-Month*, Hofstadter's *Gödel, Escher, Bach*. The books taught the cohort to think about systems with the kind of patience that prose requires and screens cannot enforce. They are the subject of the next chapter, and their imprints, as we will see, run deepest of all.
