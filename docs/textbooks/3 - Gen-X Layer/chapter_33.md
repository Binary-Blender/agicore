# Chapter 33: Geography Shapes Cognition

You are reading a man page for an old Unix utility — `sed`, say, or `awk` — and the prose has a particular flavor you cannot quite place. It is dense, terse, written by someone who assumed the reader had already done the reading; it gestures at theory but refuses to teach it; it ends, abruptly, mid-thought, the way a paper ends when the author has decided the working scientist will pick it up from there. You have been reading man pages for thirty years and this one is doing something the others do not. You scroll up. The byline is Brian Kernighan. The location, given in the colophon, is Murray Hill, New Jersey. You feel the prose click into place. You are reading a Bell Labs document, written by a Bell Labs person, and the cognitive style is not a personal style — it is a regional accent, picked up over years of writing for a specific peer audience inside a specific institution in a specific small town that produced the densest concentration of postwar R&D in human history. You close the page and pull up another one — `make`, this time, by Stuart Feldman, also Bell Labs, also Murray Hill — and the accent is the same. You pull up a third — a Lisp Machine manual from MIT Tech Square, mid-1980s, by someone you have never heard of — and the accent is different. You can hear it. Where Bell Labs writes for the colleague who is already in the room, MIT writes for the colleague who could be made to be in the room by being given enough rope. Then you pull up a fourth — a Cray Research training manual from Chippewa Falls or Mendota Heights, somewhere in the Twin Cities orbit, mid-1970s — and the accent is different again, plainer, more practical, less theoretical, more concerned with making the thing run on a Tuesday morning and less concerned with making the thing legible to the philosopher of computation. You are not reading three texts by three authors. You are reading three regions, each speaking through its authors, each carrying a cognitive style that the local ecosystem produced, each propagating that style into the training corpus along the same channel.

This chapter is the introduction to the second axis of substrate inheritance, which Parts I through VI have, until now, treated as a single national pool. They were right to treat it that way for the work they were doing: the cohort thesis is a temporal claim, and the temporal claim is coherent without disaggregating geographically. The substrate did inherit from a particular cohort whose adolescence coincided with the personal computing emergence. The cohort did produce a disproportionate share of the technical web. The model does carry the cognitive style of that cohort in its outputs. None of those claims requires regional disaggregation to be true. But each of those claims becomes more legible — and the book's archaeological project becomes more complete — when the regional dimension is added. The cohort did not develop a single uniform cognitive style. It developed a constellation of regional styles, produced by specific local ecosystems with specific institutional anchors, specific climatic conditions, specific industrial heritages, specific university traditions, specific defense-spillover pipelines, specific dominant employers, and specific local mythologies about what computing was for. The regional accents flowed into the corpus through the regional cohorts that authored disproportionate shares of regionally specific technical English. They are still readable in the model's output to a reader who knows what to listen for. The previous chapters traced the cohort. The next six chapters trace the constellation.

The mechanism for how a regional ecosystem shapes a cognitive style is not subtle and is worth stating in operational terms. A child or teenager who develops technical interests is not formed in a vacuum; they are formed by the specific local mix of institutions, employers, hobbyist communities, mentors, magazines, university programs, libraries, summer programs, and serendipitous adult relationships that the local ecosystem makes available. A teenager in Minneapolis in 1985 had access to a different mix than a teenager in Albuquerque in 1985, even if both were equally bright, equally curious, and equally motivated. The Minneapolis teenager could intern at Control Data or Honeywell, could attend a user-group meeting at the University of Minnesota, could find a magazine subscription waiting at the public library because the librarians knew which magazines the technical kids needed, could hear someone's parent talk about what they had done at Cray Research that week. The Albuquerque teenager could not do any of those things; the Albuquerque teenager could, however, walk past the MITS office on Linn Avenue and know that personal computers were a real local industry, could attend a meeting at a Sandia-adjacent hobbyist club, could read New Mexico technical newsletters with a different orientation than the Twin Cities newsletters carried. Both teenagers grew up to write technical prose. The prose they wrote carried, embedded in its rhythms and assumptions, the formation of the ecosystem that had produced them. When their prose flowed into the training corpus along the same channel as everyone else's, the corpus inherited not one cognitive style but a layered set of regional accents that had been blended at the corpus level but were still detectable, in aggregate, in the outputs of models trained on the mixture.

There is a more general claim about regional culture and cognitive style that this chapter wants to anchor before the specific regional chapters develop it. The economic geographers and urban historians have known for a long time that technology industries do not distribute themselves evenly across territory. They cluster, and the clusters persist across decades — sometimes across centuries. AnnaLee Saxenian's *Regional Advantage* (1994) traced the divergence between Silicon Valley and Boston's Route 128 in the 1980s, arguing that the two regions, which had been roughly equivalent in technological capability in 1975, diverged sharply by 1990 because of differences in their regional cultures of inter-firm relationships, labor mobility, and information-sharing norms. The argument was about industrial geography, but the implication for cognitive style is direct: if regional cultures differ enough to produce divergent industrial outcomes, they differ enough to produce divergent cognitive habits in the people they form. Margaret O'Mara's *The Code* (2019) extended this analysis with a wider geographic and historical scope, tracing the relationships between defense spending, regional universities, and the postwar technology corridors that emerged in distinct American cities. Her account makes clear that the standard "Silicon Valley as origin point" narrative is itself a regional artifact — a story told by one region about itself, in which the contributions of other regions are absorbed and re-narrated as preludes to the main event in California. The standard narrative is not false. It is partial. The regional chapters that follow are an attempt at the un-partialing.

There is also a climatic mechanism that deserves naming, because it shows up explicitly in two of the regional chapters (Minnesota and the Pacific Northwest) and implicitly in several others. Long indoor seasons — produced either by extreme cold (Minnesota, parts of Wisconsin, parts of Massachusetts) or by extreme rain and cloud cover (Seattle, Portland, the Pacific Northwest more broadly) — push the technically curious child toward indoor hobbies that reward extended focused attention. The basement-computing-in-January experience is not metaphorical; it is operational. A child in Minneapolis in February 1984, having walked home from school in -12°F windchill, has reasons to spend the next six hours in front of a Commodore 64 that a child in San Diego, California, on the same February day, does not have. The Minneapolis child does not necessarily emerge with deeper technical skills as a result — the San Diego child has access to the same machine — but the Minneapolis child's relationship to the machine is shaped by the season's structure of attention in a way that the San Diego child's is not. The Pacific Northwest is the second large region with this climatic pattern, and its postwar tech history is, plausibly, partly a consequence of it. The chapters on Minnesota (Ch 34) and the Pacific Northwest (Ch 35) treat the climatic mechanism as one of the load-bearing variables in producing the regional cognitive style. It is not the only variable, and the chapters do not claim that it is. But it is one of the variables, and it has been undertheorized in the popular discussion of regional tech culture, which has tended to focus on universities and venture capital while treating weather as an aesthetic detail.

The institutional mechanism is more obvious and more thoroughly studied in the academic literature, and it deserves a brief enumeration here so that the regional chapters can refer to it without restating it each time. Each major regional tech cluster has, at its center, one or more anchor institutions that shaped the regional cognitive style by training the practitioners, employing the engineers, hosting the research, publishing the journals, and serving as the local reference point for what "good technical work" looked like. In the Twin Cities, the anchors were Cray Research, Control Data Corporation, Honeywell Information Systems, Univac/Unisys, Medtronic, and the University of Minnesota's computer science department. In the Pacific Northwest, the anchors became Microsoft, Boeing, the University of Washington's CS department, Nintendo of America, and (later) Amazon. In Boston-Cambridge, the anchors were MIT (the AI Lab, the Lab for Computer Science, Tech Square, the Lisp tradition), DEC, Lotus, Symbolics, Wang Labs, the Route 128 corridor's defense contractors. In central New Jersey, the anchor was Bell Labs — alone in a category, because Bell Labs at its peak (roughly 1947 through 1984) was unlike any other industrial research institution in human history, and the region's cognitive style was shaped accordingly. In Pittsburgh, the anchor was Carnegie Mellon's School of Computer Science, Newell and Simon's symbolic-AI tradition, and the Robotics Institute that Raj Reddy founded in 1979. In Champaign-Urbana, the anchor was the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, host of PLATO from the 1960s onward and host of the NCSA where Mosaic was built. In Salt Lake City, the anchor was the University of Utah's computer graphics program, run by Ivan Sutherland and David Evans from 1968 onward, producing a graduate cohort that included Ed Catmull, Jim Clark, John Warnock, and Alan Kay. In Albuquerque, the anchors were the MITS company, Sandia National Laboratories, the University of New Mexico, and the surrounding defense-research substrate connected through Los Alamos. In Silicon Valley, the anchors were Stanford, Stanford Research Institute, Xerox PARC, HP, Fairchild Semiconductor, Atari, Apple, Intel, and the venture capital infrastructure that bound these institutions together economically. Each anchor produced a regional accent. Each regional accent flowed into the corpus through the engineers, researchers, technical writers, and hobbyists the anchor trained. The corpus blended the accents. The model returns the blend. The accents are still detectable in the blend, the way a perfumer can detect the individual notes in a complex scent.

There is a particular point worth making about the asymmetry of regional visibility in the popular narrative, because the asymmetry shapes how readers will receive the chapters that follow. Silicon Valley's contribution to postwar computing is large and real. Its contribution to the popular narrative of postwar computing is larger than its actual contribution, because Silicon Valley produced — alongside its technical work — a particularly effective mythology engine that wrote the dominant history of the field while the field was still developing. The mythology engine was not a conspiracy; it was an emergent property of a region whose financial model required IPOs, whose IPOs required investor interest, whose investor interest required compelling founder narratives, and whose compelling founder narratives required a media ecosystem (the technology press, the business press, the eventual rise of technology-specific publications and personalities) that grew up alongside the industry it was covering. The result was that, by roughly 1995, the dominant story of postwar computing was a Silicon Valley story in which the other regional contributions were either invisible or had been absorbed into Silicon Valley's account of itself. Microsoft's first four years in Albuquerque became a footnote; the Boca Raton IBM PC team became a footnote; the Utah computer graphics program became a footnote unless one happened to read deeply into Pixar's prehistory; the PLATO system became a footnote unless one happened to read about Ray Ozzie's career arc; Bell Labs became a footnote despite producing the transistor and Unix and C and information theory; and the regional accents of the technical writing that flowed out of these other centers were absorbed into a generic "computer industry" voice that erased their distinctness. The regional chapters that follow are not an attempt to deflate Silicon Valley. They are an attempt to restore the constellation of which Silicon Valley is one prominent node — a node that did real work and deserves real credit, but a node, not the totality.

The regional thesis has an additional implication for the cohort thesis that deserves naming. The previous chapters treated the cohort that authored the technical web as a relatively unified group with shared cognitive habits. That treatment was simplifying; the cohort was, in reality, regionally distributed, and the regional distribution affected the cognitive habits the cohort produced. A Stack Overflow answer written in 2010 by a Twin Cities engineer who came up through Honeywell's training program reads differently than a Stack Overflow answer written in 2010 by an MIT Lisp graduate who came up through Symbolics. Both engineers are members of the same chronological cohort. Both grew up in the same broad national technical environment. Both wrote for the same platform. But their cognitive styles diverge in ways that are detectable in their prose, and the divergence is not random — it tracks regional formation. When a modern language model is trained on a corpus containing both engineers' answers, the model learns from both — but the model also learns, in aggregate, that "Stack Overflow answers" is not a uniform style. There is a range. The range maps, in part, to regional cognitive styles that have not previously been named at the corpus-archaeology level. The book's central claim about the substrate, then, gains an additional dimension: the substrate is not just temporal (a cohort formed in a particular historical window); it is also spatial (a constellation of regional ecosystems that each contributed a distinct accent to the blend). The model carries both dimensions, and both dimensions are detectable in its outputs.

There is a methodological honesty owed to the reader at this point, before the specific regional chapters develop their cases. The regional thesis is harder to support empirically than the cohort thesis was, because the granular composition of training corpora by author region is not a quantity that the major model providers publish. We can know, with reasonable confidence, that Common Crawl is dominated by Northern Hemisphere English. We can know, with somewhat less confidence, that Stack Overflow's contributor base is geographically skewed toward North America and Europe. We cannot, however, easily produce statistical estimates of how much of the technical content in the major training corpora was authored by people who grew up in Minnesota versus people who grew up in Washington versus people who grew up in Boston. The regional claim is, accordingly, a claim built more from the qualitative evidence — the detectable accents in technical writing, the institutional histories of the regional anchors, the geography of the technical-employment market over the relevant decades — than from the corpus-level quantitative analysis that the cohort claim could marshal. The regional chapters proceed with this honesty in mind: they make their cases through specific institutional histories, specific cultural artifacts, specific industrial heritage, and the qualitative pattern-matching that is the only available evidentiary register for the claim at the corpus level. The reader is invited to test the claims against their own experience of the regional cultures, and the reader's pushback — when it comes — will be part of the ongoing development of the regional archaeology. This is, in that sense, a more provisional chapter than the cohort chapters were. The provisionality is part of the work.

The regional chapters proceed in a particular order, and the order is worth previewing here so that the reader can hold the structure in mind. **Chapter 34: The Minnesota Layer.** The Twin Cities and the surrounding Upper Midwest substrate. The author's own home region, treated with the personal-history grounding that the book's voice register has used throughout, and the load-bearing cold-weather indoor-cognition argument that pairs with the Pacific Northwest chapter that follows it. The supercomputing heritage (Cray, CDC), the medical-device cluster (Medtronic, 3M), the retail-systems cluster (Target, Best Buy), the Mystery Science Theater 3000 reading of basement cognition's self-portrait, the Mall of America as semantic accelerator, the Minneapolis music scene as the DIY basement-studio twin of the basement-coding scene, the "smart weird kid" energy that is recognizable as a Midwestern accent in the corpus. **Chapter 35: The Cold Weather Twin.** The Pacific Northwest as Minnesota's climatic and cognitive twin. Microsoft's Albuquerque-to-Bellevue migration and what the move did to the regional substrate. Nintendo of America's Redmond presence and the Japan-to-Pacific-NW cultural pipeline. Boeing's century-old engineering anchor and the parent-and-Apple-II pattern that produced a generation of Seattle technical kids. Amazon's emergence as the second-wave Seattle tech anchor and the cloud-infrastructure-as-substrate consequence. The grunge music scene as the cognitive parallel to the Minneapolis sound. **Chapter 36: The Coastal Foundation.** Bell Labs and the East Coast technical tradition. The Murray Hill record (transistor, Unix, C, information theory, the laser) and the AT&T breakup as the moment the substrate was unbundled. MIT's AI Lab as the source Levy documented in 1984. The Lisp tradition and the symbolic-AI school. DEC and the engineering middle class. Route 128 as the original "Silicon Valley before that name took hold." **Chapter 37: The Forgotten Origin Points.** Albuquerque, Champaign-Urbana, Salt Lake City, Pittsburgh. The four origin points whose contributions Silicon Valley later absorbed. Microsoft in Albuquerque from 1975 through 1979. PLATO and Mosaic at the University of Illinois. The University of Utah computer graphics program. The Newell-Simon symbolic-AI tradition at CMU and the Robotics Institute that grew out of it. **Chapter 38: The Mythology Engine.** Silicon Valley itself, treated with both the recognition it deserves and the precision the popular narrative has often lacked. What the region actually built, what it absorbed from elsewhere, how its mythology engine came to dominate the narrative, and the constellation of smaller regional centers — Austin, NYC/NJ finance, LA, Atlanta, Florida, Boulder — that round out the geographic constellation.

The reader will notice an asymmetry in the regional treatment that the chapters acknowledge but do not apologize for. Minnesota gets a chapter to itself; Boston and Bell Labs share a chapter; four origin points share another chapter; Silicon Valley and six smaller centers share a chapter. The asymmetry is partly a function of the author's depth of personal knowledge (the Minnesota chapter is grounded in the author's own home region and so can do more granular work) and partly a function of the substrate-contribution depth that each region merits (Bell Labs alone produced enough substrate to support its own book, but the book has scope constraints). The asymmetry is also pedagogically deliberate: the Minnesota chapter is structured to function as the worked example of how to do regional archaeology, so that the reader who finishes the chapter can extend the framework to their own home region whether or not their region appears in the chapters that follow. The framework is generalizable. The book's regional coverage is not exhaustive, and the chapters explicitly invite the reader who grew up in a region the book does not cover (Madison, Boulder, Phoenix, Detroit, Toronto, Tel Aviv, Bangalore, Shenzhen, Berlin) to extend the framework outward. The substrate, properly understood, is a constellation in motion, not a fixed catalog.

There is a closing observation worth making at the start of Part VII, because it bears on how the reader should approach the regional chapters. The cohort thesis was, fundamentally, a claim about the past — about a specific window of historical time when a specific generational cohort produced the substrate that modern models inherited. The regional thesis is also a claim about the past, but it has a different relationship to the present and the future. Regional ecosystems are still operating. The Twin Cities are still producing technical kids. The Pacific Northwest is still producing technical kids. Boston, Pittsburgh, Champaign-Urbana, Salt Lake City, Albuquerque, Austin, Atlanta, Boulder — all of these regions continue to host the universities, employers, and hobbyist communities that shape new cohorts. The regional accents the model carries today are accents from the substrate's last formation period — roughly 1985 through 2015 — but the regions that produced those accents are still producing new ones. The next major training-corpus update will inherit from a generation of writers who are, at this writing, in their twenties and thirties, and who carry their own regional formations from cities that are partly the same cities as the previous cohort's and partly different cities. The substrate is a moving target. The regional archaeology, accordingly, is an archaeology of a tradition that is still being added to. The chapters that follow describe a constellation as it exists in the model's weights today. The constellation will look different in five years, and different again in ten, and the differences will be readable to anyone willing to do the work. The framework is the durable thing. The specific accents are the perishable evidence. The book offers both, and trusts the reader to use the framework to keep listening to the perishable evidence as it shifts.

The next chapter is the Minnesota chapter, and it does the worked example. It is the only chapter in Part VII that opens with the author's own first-person memoir vignette rather than with a generic regional-recognition scene, because Minnesota is the author's home region and the personal grounding is the honest way to write the chapter. The other regional chapters work from secondary sources, archival evidence, and the qualitative pattern-matching that the regional thesis admits as its evidentiary register. None of the regional chapters claim more than the evidence supports. All of them claim that the regional layer is real, that it is detectable in the corpus the model trained on, and that recognizing it is part of recognizing the substrate honestly. The constellation is the map. The map is partial. The work continues.
