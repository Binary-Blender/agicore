# Chapter 10: BBS Culture

You dial in for the first time on a Thursday night, sometime after the family has gone to bed, because the phone line is finally yours. The number is written on a torn corner of notebook paper that a friend passed you at school, or that the guy two seats over at the user group meeting jotted down without quite making eye contact. Three hundred baud or twelve hundred, depending on the year. The modem chirps through its handshake, the carrier locks, and the screen fills with an ANSI welcome banner the sysop typed himself — green and cyan block characters spelling out THE NIGHT OWL or DRAGON'S LAIR or some pun on the operator's CB handle from a decade earlier. There is a copyright notice with the sysop's real first name, a town, a state. He is letting you into his house.

The login prompt asks for your handle, then a password, then for you to verify that you are who you say you are by answering a question only locals would know. The questionnaire is mandatory and slightly weird: what high school do you go to, what is your favorite Infocom game, would you describe yourself as a phreaker or a hobbyist. The sysop will read these answers in the morning before he validates your account, and if he does not like what he sees he will simply delete it, and you will dial back and get the message ACCOUNT TERMINATED — CONTACT SYSOP, which means do not dial here again. But he validates you, because most sysops validate most people, because the point is not exclusion but the building of a thing.

The main menu loads. File areas, message bases, door games, a section called BULLETINS where the sysop posts opinions about his cat or about the local pizza place or about a piece of pirated software that has been making the rounds with a virus baked into the loader. The terminal is a window into another person's bedroom — his curated files, his message base, his door game high score table where the regulars trade trash talk under handles you will come to recognize. He is in his thirties or maybe forties. He has a wife and a job and a second phone line installed just for the board, which she tolerates. He is letting strangers into his computer because the building of the community is the point and there is no other way to build it. You will log off after fifty-seven minutes because the user-time limit is sixty and you do not want to get bumped mid-message. You will come back tomorrow.

The bulletin board system, as a technological artifact, is almost insultingly simple. A telephone line. A modem. A consumer-grade PC running host software — Wildcat! from Mustang Software, PCBoard from Clark Development, Renegade, Telegard, Spitfire, or one of dozens of others. A hard drive large enough to hold the file area, which in the early years meant ten megabytes and in the later years meant a few hundred. The software answered incoming calls, presented menus, served files, accepted message posts, ran door games. There was nothing architecturally novel about any of it. The novelty was that an individual could afford to own and operate a piece of communications infrastructure that, in any prior era of human history, would have required a corporate license and a building.

A subset of boards specialized aggressively — pirate boards trading commercial software cracked of their copy protection, phreaking boards trading information about telephone-system exploits and the operating particulars of long-distance carriers, anarchy boards trading the contents of the Anarchist Cookbook and its endless derivative files, hacking boards trading exploits and stolen credentials, BBS systems run by and for specific religious or political subcultures, adult boards operating in the legal gray zones of pre-internet pornography distribution. These specialty boards constituted a meaningful fraction of the total population, and the law-enforcement encounters with them — most famously the Steve Jackson Games raid in 1990, in which the Secret Service seized the company's BBS and held it for months, an event that catalyzed the founding of the Electronic Frontier Foundation — were the defining political encounters of the BBS era. Bruce Sterling's *The Hacker Crackdown* (1992) is the standard reference for these events. The political memory the cohort carries from this period is specific: that law-enforcement institutions did not understand the technology they were policing, that they would seize entire computer systems on the basis of vague suspicions, that the relationship between operators and the state had been adversarial from the beginning, and that the civil-liberties infrastructure that now sits between users and the surveillance state was built in direct response to the abuses of this period. The EFF, the Computers, Freedom and Privacy conference series, the entire legal-and-civil-liberties tradition around digital rights — all of it has its origin moment in the BBS-era encounters between sysops and police.

The sysop was the central cultural role. He — and the sysops were overwhelmingly he, though the rare she ran some of the most distinctive boards in the country — combined the functions of technical operator, social moderator, content curator, and small-town mayor. He paid for the phone line out of pocket. He read the message bases every morning and intervened when arguments turned ugly. He decided what files would be uploaded into the public area and which ones would sit in a private holding directory until he could verify they were not malware or pirated commercial software, depending on his moral position on the matter. He had a moral position on the matter. Every sysop had one. The board's character flowed from his judgment, which was applied in his living room, in the middle of the night, between feedings of an infant or between segments of a late-night talk show on a television in the next room. The cultural work was domestic. The infrastructure was domestic. The proto-internet ran out of basements.

The door games deserve a paragraph of their own because they are how the BBS taught a generation what asynchronous multiplayer would feel like. Trade Wars 2002, a space-trading game in which you logged in once a day to spend your turns expanding your empire while other players spent theirs trying to ambush you. Legend of the Red Dragon, a fantasy text RPG by Seth Robinson that ran in the background as a kind of communal soap opera, where the in-character flirtations between two regulars would become real-world flirtations and sometimes real-world relationships. BRE — Barren Realms Elite, a strategy game that lasted weeks per round. The door game architecture was elegant: a single-player game running on the sysop's machine, played by many users one at a time, with a persistent shared world. Each player got a fixed number of turns per day. The world ground on whether you logged in or not. The pattern that streaming gamers and MMO players now consider native to multiplayer was prototyped in 1989 on a 386 in someone's spare bedroom.

The genre conventions the door games established are still operating, three decades later, inside the mobile-game economy that has now extracted them into something resembling a global industry. The daily login bonus, the energy-replenishes-over-time mechanic, the persistent leaderboard with daily and weekly resets, the in-character chat channel adjacent to the gameplay, the special events that ran for limited windows of real time — all of these were invented or refined in the door-game era and then ported, with attribution rarely preserved, into the structures of *Clash of Clans* and *Candy Crush* and the entire free-to-play mobile catalog. The original designers did not patent the patterns. They could not have; the patterns were emergent properties of the asynchronous-turn-based-multiplayer constraint that the BBS architecture imposed. The constraint produced the pattern. The pattern survived the constraint and propagated into every subsequent environment where similar constraints applied. What the door games' designers earned for the invention was the gratitude of their player communities and, occasionally, modest royalty checks from the sysops who licensed their work for ten or twenty dollars per board.

The message base was the discourse layer of the BBS and the one that most directly anticipated the public web forum and the threaded social-media reply structure. Each board had multiple message areas, sometimes called subboards or conferences, organized by topic — general chat, technical discussion, classifieds, role-playing, off-topic, the controversial topic the sysop had decided to allow against his better judgment. Messages were threaded: each reply was attached to its parent, the tree of conversation expanded over days and weeks, and a thread that had grown to a hundred messages was a community artifact in itself, with regulars and lurkers and the inevitable newcomer whose first post the regulars would either welcome or rip apart depending on how it had been written. The regulars learned, over their first few months on the board, the social conventions of the local message base — what topics were welcome, which sysops tolerated which kinds of provocation, when a thread had reached its natural end, when the appropriate response to a difficult post was to ignore it rather than to engage. The conventions varied from board to board within the same metropolitan area; a regular who frequented three boards learned three slightly different cultures and developed the social fluency to operate inside each. The cohort that came up through this discipline absorbed a specific kind of social competence that subsequent platform-era users have not had to develop, because the platforms have largely homogenized their cultural norms across their entire user base.

The file area was the gift economy made concrete. Shareware games, freeware utilities, text files, ANSI art collections, ASCII music files, GIFs of variable legality, archive collections of every kind. The convention — enforced by sysops with ratios, but more importantly internalized by users as ethical code — was that you uploaded as much as you downloaded. The point was not commerce. The point was the maintenance of the collection. A board with a thin file area was a dead board. A board with a rich file area was alive, because the regulars were feeding it, because they understood that the collection only existed because they were collectively willing to feed it. The economic structure was older than capitalism: a gift economy, in the anthropological sense, in which status flowed from contribution and the goods themselves were not fungible with money because the relationship was the point. Yochai Benkler would later formalize this pattern as commons-based peer production. The BBS regulars had been operating it for fifteen years before the academic language arrived.

FidoNet was the proto-internet, and the qualifier is precise. From 1984 onward, Tom Jennings's protocol allowed BBSs to call each other in the middle of the night and exchange message bundles, routing them across the continent through a hierarchy of regional hubs. A message you posted in the FidoNet echo called HAM_PROG would be in Australia within forty-eight hours, having hopped through perhaps six independent operators' machines along the way, each of whom had agreed to carry the traffic out of pocket because that was how the network worked. The store-and-forward architecture predated commercial email; the addressing scheme — your zone, your net, your node, your point — predated the URL. By the early 1990s FidoNet had over thirty thousand nodes worldwide. It was the largest amateur-built data network in human history at that point, and it ran on the same hardware the door games ran on, in the same basements, under the same sysops, who absorbed the cost of the late-night long-distance calls as part of what it meant to be in the hobby. The internet did not replace FidoNet by being technically superior. It replaced it by being free at the point of use to the end participant. The FidoNet sysops, when the internet arrived, simply added an internet gateway to their boards and kept running. Some of them are still running.

The conventions the BBS established have outlived the BBS itself by three decades and counting. The ASCII signature file appended to every message — your handle, your favorite quote, a small piece of personal ASCII art — became the email signature. The threaded message base became the web forum, the subreddit, the Discord channel. The moderator who reads the room and intervenes when conversations turn ugly is doing the sysop's job, in the sysop's tradition, under a different brand. The custom of asking newcomers to introduce themselves, the custom of distinguishing between users by handle rather than legal name, the custom of having a section of the board reserved for off-topic chat about pets and pizza and politics — all of these were running in 1988, and all of them are now built into every social platform that exists, often without anyone in the platform's design team being aware of where the conventions came from. The platforms inherited them through their users, who inherited them through the chain of online communities going back to dial-up. Howard Rheingold, in *The Virtual Community* (1993), gave the academic articulation of what the BBS sysops had been doing all along: that the small, locally moderated, gift-economy-based online community was a viable social form, capable of producing real friendship, real conflict resolution, real ongoing collective effort. The WELL, which Rheingold documented, was a BBS by any reasonable definition; it ran on a VAX rather than a PC, and its users skewed older and more credentialed, but the social architecture was identical. What worked in Sausalito for the WELL was working in Toledo and Akron and Tulsa for boards whose names no one outside the local area code remembers.

The economics of running a BBS bear noting because they shaped the culture in specific ways. A serious board required a dedicated phone line (twenty to forty dollars a month, depending on the carrier and the region), a dedicated computer (a few hundred to a few thousand dollars, depreciated over years), a modem (a hundred to several hundred dollars, replaced every couple of years as speeds improved), and the host software (sometimes commercial, sometimes shareware, sometimes free). Multi-line boards — boards that could accept several simultaneous callers — required a phone line per concurrent user and a multi-port serial card and host software capable of multi-line operation, scaling the costs accordingly. The largest hobbyist boards, with dozens of phone lines, were paying their operators' households several hundred dollars a month in direct infrastructure costs alone. The operators absorbed these costs because the operation was their hobby, their identity, their public-facing self. Some boards eventually charged annual membership fees of fifteen or thirty dollars to defray costs; many never did, on principle. The principle was that the board belonged to its community and that the community's contribution in the form of files, messages, and presence was the appropriate compensation to the sysop. The principle was operational. It worked.

Jason Scott's documentary *BBS: The Documentary* (2005), an eight-part oral history assembled from interviews with hundreds of sysops, captured the moment in the early 2000s when the surviving BBS operators understood that their era had ended and that the record needed to be made. The conventions of internet usage that Scott documents — the etiquette, the file-area discipline, the political negotiations between operators over what content was acceptable, the genuine personal grief experienced by regulars when a beloved board went dark — are not retro curiosities. They are the cultural prototypes from which the modern internet was assembled, often by people who did not know they were copying from a prototype. The sysop is gone as a job title. The work is still being done.

The cognitive imprint the BBS era left on its participants is specific and durable. It taught that online community was a hand-built thing, maintained by named individuals out of pocket, sustained by the active reciprocity of its members. It taught that the moderation of human discourse was real work, requiring real judgment, applied by real people who could be wrong but who were accountable to their community. It taught that the file collection was a commons that the commons had to maintain. It taught that the proto-network was a thing humans made together in the middle of the night, one phone call at a time. The cohort that learned these things at fifteen or seventeen carried the assumptions into their professional work, into their open-source projects, into their internet-startup founding teams, into the README files of the libraries that became foundational, into the corpus of pre-2010 internet text that now teaches AI how online humans behave toward each other. The AI has been trained on a literature that takes the sysop's assumptions for granted. The user who finds the AI's social register congenial is often a user whose own social register was formed in the same tradition.

The technical conventions developed inside BBS culture were as specific as the social ones, and they propagated outward in the same way. The ZMODEM file-transfer protocol, written by Chuck Forsberg in 1986 as an improvement on XMODEM and YMODEM, became the universal default for BBS file transfer because it handled line-noise recovery, supported batch transfers, and allowed sessions interrupted by phone disconnects to be resumed from the point of interruption. The protocol embodied a specific design assumption — that telephone lines were noisy, that connections would drop, that the system should be designed around the failure mode rather than around the ideal case. Subsequent generations of network protocols inherited the same assumption, sometimes consciously and sometimes by parallel evolution. The TCP retransmission window, the HTTP range request, the BitTorrent piece selection, the resilience patterns built into every modern distributed system — all of them are operating on the same intuition that Forsberg encoded into ZMODEM, because the engineers building them grew up with ZMODEM as the reference example of a protocol that did not assume the network was clean.

The QWK message-packet format, developed by Mark Herring in 1987, allowed regulars to download all of the new messages on a board in a single compressed bundle, read and reply to them offline using a dedicated reader program, and upload their replies in another single bundle on the next connection. The architectural pattern — synchronize the local mirror with the remote canonical store, edit offline, push the deltas back — is the architectural pattern that twenty years later would underlie Git, Dropbox, and every cloud-sync system worth its name. The pattern was being operated by teenagers running OLR clients on 386 machines before the academic networking community had a name for it. The pattern persisted in the cohort's intuitions about how distributed state ought to be managed, and the pattern reappears in their later design work, often without explicit recognition of the lineage.

The persistence of the sysop role in attenuated forms across the modern internet is worth one further note. The Discord server moderator, the subreddit's volunteer mod team, the GitHub organization owner who decides which contributors can push to the main branch, the maintainer of a Mastodon instance, the operator of a Matrix homeserver — all of these are sysops, doing the sysop's work, often without knowing the title. They are paying the hosting costs out of pocket or out of patronage donations. They are reading the room and intervening when conversations turn ugly. They are deciding what content is acceptable. They are absorbing the social labor that makes the platform livable. The mass-platform era of the 2010s tried to abstract this labor away into algorithms and policy teams, and the results — the various content-moderation crises of the major social platforms — demonstrated empirically that the work cannot in fact be abstracted away. It can only be moved. The platforms that did the moving best are now the platforms that look most like federated networks of small communities each with its own sysop. The convergence is back toward the BBS model, at scale, on new infrastructure, with the same underlying social truth.
