# Chapter 22: Woz, Not Jobs

The moment of articulation arrived for you somewhere in your forties, during a glossy product keynote that you watched at home with the volume low. The product on stage was perfectly designed. The presentation was perfectly choreographed. The narrative arc was perfectly structured. The presenter moved through the slides with the cadence of someone who had rehearsed the cadence for weeks, and the slides revealed each successive product feature at precisely the moment that the cadence required, and the audience laughed and applauded at precisely the moments the script anticipated. The whole experience was an artifact, in the way a film is an artifact, and you respected the craft of it the way you respected the craft of a film, and you also noticed a felt sense in your chest that you had not noticed at the Macintosh introduction in 1984 when you were a child watching footage of Jobs unveiling that beige box and the audience reacting to the synthesized voice. The 1984 felt sense had been something like wonder. The 2010s felt sense was something like being marketed to. You sat in your living room and noticed the difference and thought, for the first time, the phrase that the rest of the chapter exists to develop. *Woz, not Jobs.* Three syllables. The phrase did the work that a hundred pages of ethical specification would have failed to do. It compressed a position you had been holding for years without naming, and the act of naming it changed how you operated in every subsequent technical decision you ever made. The phrase was steering vector, confession, and choice all at once. It was, you would only realize later, the ethical center of gravity of the entire book you would eventually write.

The chapter has to begin by ruling out the wrong readings, because the wrong readings are available and would damage the argument if allowed to stand. The wrong reading is anti-Jobs. Steve Jobs was a brilliant figure whose contributions to consumer computing are not in serious dispute. The original Macintosh was a revolutionary machine. The iPhone reshaped a global industry. Pixar, NeXT, the Apple-after-Sculley return, the entire arc of Jobs's late career — the work was extraordinary, the products were good, the influence on the field was real. The wrong reading is also the hero-villain reading. Wozniak and Jobs were collaborators at the founding of Apple; their work together produced the Apple I and the Apple II; the personal relationship was complicated and was, by both men's accounts, mutually generative for the period in which they were collaborating. The argument of this chapter is not that one of them was good and the other was bad. The argument is that the two of them represented two different philosophies of how to make technology, both legitimate in their respective domains, but with dramatically different downstream cultural effects when one or the other becomes dominant in a given era. The current era — the AI era — has tilted heavily toward the Jobs archetype, and the cohort that grew up watching both figures before the mythology around either had hardened can see the asymmetry. Naming the asymmetry is the work the chapter does.

The Jobs philosophy can be enumerated honestly. Jobs optimized for presentation: the keynote was a refined art form in his hands, and the keynote was treated as the actual interface between the company and the world. Jobs optimized for narrative: the products were arranged in stories, the company was arranged in stories, the personal mythology was arranged in stories. Jobs optimized for product integration: the hardware and the software and the services and the supply chain and the retail experience were treated as a single integrated artifact under unified design authority. Jobs optimized for mystique: the company's internal operations were closed, the products were unveiled rather than launched, the next thing was always shrouded in confidentiality until the moment of reveal. Jobs optimized for control: over the supply chain, over the developer ecosystem, over the experience of using the products, over the conversation around the products. Jobs optimized for perfectionism: the products were polished until they were polished, and then polished some more, and the perfectionism was treated as a virtue rather than as a cost. Jobs optimized for centralized authorship: the products bore the imprint of a single creative authority that the company, by its nature, organized itself around. All of these are legitimate optimizations. All of them have produced extraordinary work in the hands of the people who excelled at them. Apple under Jobs is the most successful instance of this philosophy in the history of consumer technology, and the success was real, and the products were genuinely good, and none of what follows is meant to dispute any of that.

The Woz philosophy can be enumerated with equal honesty. Wozniak optimized for playful invention: the work was done because the work was interesting, and the products were vehicles for exploring ideas that the engineer wanted to explore. Wozniak optimized for elegant engineering: the Apple I motherboard achieved its functionality with extraordinary economy of components, and the elegance of the design was treated as an end in itself rather than as a means to a market goal. Wozniak optimized for openness: he gave away the Apple I schematics at the Homebrew Computer Club; he documented his designs in detail in early issues of *Byte* and other magazines; the Apple II shipped with extensive technical documentation that explicitly invited the user to extend and modify the machine. Wozniak optimized for the joy of creation: he has said, in *iWoz* and in numerous interviews across the decades, that the early Apple work was the most fun he ever had, and that fun was the actual motivation, and that the financial and corporate consequences were a side effect of the fun rather than the goal of the work. Wozniak optimized for intellectual curiosity: he kept reading, kept inventing, kept teaching, kept showing up at conferences as a participant rather than as a celebrity, and he did this well past the point at which he could have retired into mythology. Wozniak optimized for the beauty of systems: he found computers beautiful, he found the act of designing them beautiful, and he treated the beauty as worth preserving even when it would have been more lucrative to industrialize the beauty into something less beautiful. All of these, too, are legitimate optimizations. They produced different work than the Jobs optimizations produced. The work Wozniak's optimizations produced was, in some ways, the foundation of the work that the Jobs optimizations later refined and sold.

The Woz archetype is, additionally, the anti-mythic technologist. This is the part that takes the rest of the chapter to develop, because the anti-mythic posture is harder to recognize than the mythic posture and is more important than the mythic posture for the argument the book is making. Wozniak never seemed particularly interested in becoming a myth. He left Apple in 1985, when the company was making him uncomfortable, and he did not spend the subsequent forty years jockeying for narrative position. He taught fifth grade for years. He showed up at user-group meetings as a participant. He answered email from random fans. He gave technical talks at small conferences. He published an honest, sometimes embarrassing, sometimes self-deprecating memoir that did not attempt to construct a heroic origin story. He has, by his own account, been substantially less effective at the things mythic technologists are supposed to be effective at — the empire-building, the celebrity-cultivation, the manipulation of public perception — than he could have been if he had wanted to be. He has not wanted to be. The wanting-not-to-be is the part of the archetype that matters. It signals that the work was the actual point, that the technical achievement was sufficient unto itself, that the secondary goods that flowed from the work — fame, wealth, narrative position — were never the actual goal even when they arrived in substantial quantities. The cohort that grew up watching Wozniak across the decades has watched a master class in declining to become a myth, and the master class has shaped, in the cohort, a permanent skepticism toward the mythic posture in any technologist who adopts it.

The connection to the transparent cups philosophy of Chapter 13 is direct and load-bearing. Woz-style engineering survives inspection. The Apple I schematic was openly distributed; the Apple II documentation was complete and inviting; the work itself was good enough to be impressive even with the inner workings visible. The trick worked with clear cups. The Jobs philosophy, by contrast, depended structurally on a degree of mystique — on the next thing being shrouded, on the inner workings being protected, on the audience being managed rather than informed. This is not a moral judgment on Jobs; the Jobs philosophy was effective at what it was effective at, and the mystique was a deliberate and skilled choice. But the philosophies are structurally different, and they produce different downstream cultures. A culture saturated with the Jobs philosophy produces hidden roadmaps, embargoed previews, sealed organizations, and a public discourse organized around the next unveiling. A culture saturated with the Woz philosophy produces published documentation, open source code, accessible authors, and a public discourse organized around the next interesting thing someone learned how to do. Both cultures can be productive. The argument of this chapter is that the AI era has tilted, decisively, toward the first culture, and that the cohort which grew up inside the Woz philosophy is structurally well-positioned to notice the tilt and to push back against it.

The diagnosis of the AI era is the chapter's hardest sentence to write, because the diagnosis is contested and the chapter has to honor the contestation without ducking it. The contemporary AI industry incentivizes founder theater. It incentivizes branding. It incentivizes guru aesthetics, hype cycles, hidden prompts, performative futurism, the cultivation of intellectual mystique around what is, technically, a class of stochastic-generation systems that the field has been openly publishing papers about for a decade. The mystique sells. The mystique funds rounds. The mystique organizes the discourse. And the mystique, considered as a cultural pattern, is recognizably Jobs energy — without, often, the Jobs craftsmanship that made the Jobs energy work. The keynotes are not as good as the original Apple keynotes. The product polish is not as deep as the original Macintosh polish. The narrative integration is rough where Jobs's was seamless. The mystique is being deployed by practitioners who have not done the underlying work that mystique was, in its Jobs form, deployed to package. The result, in many corners, is a culture that performs the rituals of brilliance without delivering the brilliance the rituals were originally meant to package. *Civilizations need both archetypes. But the AI era currently suffers from a severe shortage of Woz energy.* That sentence is delivered verbatim because it is the chapter's load-bearing claim, and stating it cleanly is the only honest way to make the argument the chapter exists to make.

The corollary observation is that the most transformative AI work being done right now, at the technical level, is being done by people whose public presence is closer to Woz than to Jobs. They are quiet. They are technically dense. They post their work openly. They write tutorials and documentation. They show up in repository comment threads with patches and explanations. They give technical talks to small audiences and answer questions afterward. They do not have publicists. They do not have keynote teams. They do not have brand consultants. They have, instead, work — accumulated over years of careful, joyful, curious building — and the work survives inspection in the way that Woz's Apple II schematics survived inspection. These practitioners are the cohort the trilogy has been describing. They are the inheritors of the substrate the previous chapters traced. They are, in the AI era specifically, dramatically undervalued by the surrounding industry's reward structure and dramatically valued by the underlying technical reality of who is actually producing useful artifacts. The disjunction between the surface culture's rewards and the underlying technical reality is the disjunction that the Woz archetype names. The disjunction is real. The disjunction matters. The cohort that grew up watching Wozniak knows what to do about it.

I followed both of these people throughout their lives, and eventually concluded that Woz had the better philosophy. The conclusion did not arrive in a single moment. It arrived through time, through observation, through maturity, through watching consequences unfold over decades. I watched Jobs's products and admired them. I watched Jobs's keynotes and learned from them. I watched the company Jobs built and respected it. And I watched, also, the way Jobs's philosophy propagated through the culture — the way the keynote-as-product-launch model proliferated, the way the founder-as-prophet model proliferated, the way the closed-roadmap model proliferated, the way the mystique-as-marketing model proliferated — and I watched the cumulative effect on the broader engineering culture of the field, and I noticed that the cumulative effect was, by the late 2010s, not entirely good. I watched Wozniak through the same period — the teaching, the showing up at conferences, the email replies to random questions, the willingness to be technical rather than mystical — and I noticed that Wozniak's philosophy was being inherited, quietly, by the cohort of working engineers who were building the things that actually worked. The inheritance was not loud. The inheritance was, in fact, structurally allergic to loudness. But the inheritance was real, and the cohort that had absorbed it was, by every measure I could apply, producing more interesting work per person and more durable work over time than the cohort that had absorbed the alternative inheritance. The conclusion landed slowly. The conclusion was that the cohort had been right to be quietly suspicious of the mystique. The cohort had been carrying the right archetype forward all along.

The connection to the architectures the trilogy's final part will examine is the closing thread of this chapter. Those architectures — the ones the next several chapters will name explicitly — are deeply Woz-style. They are built because the architecture itself was beautiful and worth exploring. They are not built because market research demanded them, because investors requested them, or because branding consultants optimized for them. They are documented openly. The source is visible. The decision logs are public. The work survives inspection. The trick is being done with clear cups. The cohort that built the architectures built them under the Woz philosophy without always realizing that the philosophy had a name, and the philosophy's name, when finally articulated, is the three syllables this chapter has been trying to earn the right to deliver as its closing line. The future may not belong to those who perform intelligence best. It may belong to those who still genuinely love building things. *The AI era does not need more people pretending to be Steve Jobs. It needs more people willing to build like Steve Wozniak.* The cohort that has been quietly carrying the Woz philosophy forward for forty years is the cohort the rest of Part V will describe. The empirical inheritance chapters that follow are, in this sense, an accounting of what Woz energy looks like when it shows up in modern AI work. The accounting begins with the next chapter, which examines how the Gen-X layer surfaces in the actual textual behavior of contemporary language models.
