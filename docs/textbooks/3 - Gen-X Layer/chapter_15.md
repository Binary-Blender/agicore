# Chapter 15: Shareware and the Trust Economy

You are thirteen years old and you are sitting at the kitchen table with the checkbook your mother has left in the drawer, trying to write a check for fifteen dollars to a software author in Glendale, Arizona, whose name you have read at the bottom of a text file on a floppy disk. The text file is called REGISTER.TXT and it sits at the root of the disk along with the program executable and a README and a manual saved as plain text. You opened the manual first, the way the author asked you to. You read the description, you tried the program, you used it for two weeks, you found it useful — it is PKZIP, or it is Procomm Plus, or it is one of the Apogee shareware episodes, or it is a piece of communications software, or it is a small text editor with macros, depending on the year. The author has written, in the text file, in plain words: if you find this program useful, please send fifteen dollars to the following address. There is no enforcement mechanism. The program will work perfectly well if you never pay. There is no expiration timer, no nagging dialog at startup (or if there is, it is brief and polite), no degraded mode for the unregistered. The author is asking. The author trusts that some percentage of readers will respond.

You write the check carefully because your handwriting is still a teenager's handwriting and you want it to look like a person who could be a real customer. You fold the check inside a piece of typing paper so that it does not show through the envelope. You address the envelope with the author's name and address as printed in the text file. You stamp it with a twenty-five-cent stamp. You walk it to the mailbox at the end of the driveway, lift the red flag, and stand there for a moment with the lid open before you let it go, because dropping fifteen dollars of your own money into the United States Postal Service feels weightier than any commercial transaction you have made in your life. You let it go. Three weeks later a manila envelope arrives with your name on it. Inside is a printed manual with a real cover, a registration certificate with your name on it in dot-matrix print, sometimes a sticker for your monitor, sometimes a personal note from the author thanking you. You sit on your bed and look at the certificate. You have just learned, in operational form, that the gift economy can survive contact with money — that authors and users can be in direct relationship, that trust by default can produce real exchange, that the system works.

Shareware as a distribution model was articulated explicitly in 1982 by Andrew Fluegelman, the editor of PC World, who released his communications program PC-Talk under a license he called Freeware (a term he later trademarked and then released into the public domain) — the program was free to copy and distribute, with a suggested donation of twenty-five dollars to the author if the user found it valuable. The same year, Jim Knopf released PC-File, a database program, under similar terms. The two authors, working independently, had converged on the same model. By 1984 the third major early shareware author, Bob Wallace, released PC-Write — a word processor — under terms that made the model formal: distribute freely, register if you find it useful, registered users receive printed documentation and a copy on disk. The three programs together earned their authors substantial money, surprised the trade press, and demonstrated that the model worked. The Association of Shareware Professionals was formed in 1987 to codify standards and to provide a kind of self-regulatory framework for what was, in practice, an honor system at industrial scale.

The model worked, in the sense that it produced viable businesses, because the structural assumptions it depended on were operationally true. Authors could reach users at near-zero marginal cost via the BBS file areas, the user-group disk libraries, the shareware catalogs that mail-order houses began publishing — Public Software Library, the PC-SIG catalog, the various disk-based and CD-ROM-based shareware compilations that appeared throughout the late 1980s and 1990s. Users could evaluate software at zero marginal cost by trying it. The transaction friction was therefore reduced to the moment of the user's evaluation — to the question of whether the software was useful enough to make the registration worth the fifteen or twenty-five dollars the author was asking. The friction was internal to the user. The answer was the user's own honest accounting. And enough users answered yes, frequently enough, to fund the model.

The exemplars are now legendary. Id Software's release of the *Doom* shareware episode in December 1993 — the first episode of the game free, the second and third available only by mail-order purchase — funded the company through its next several years of development and created the playbook that the entire PC game industry would imitate for the rest of the decade. Apogee Software, founded by Scott Miller in 1987, refined the shareware-game model into what Miller called the "Apogee model": release the first episode as full shareware, sell the remaining episodes; this funded *Commander Keen*, *Wolfenstein 3D* (the predecessor to *Doom*), *Duke Nukem*, *Raptor*, and many others. PKWare, founded by Phil Katz, distributed PKZIP and PKARC as shareware through the 1980s and 1990s, became one of the most widely-used software utilities in the world, and Katz became wealthy on the model. Datastorm Technologies sold Procomm Plus on a shareware-and-then-commercial-upgrade path. The Mock Turtle Software studio, Quarterdeck (DESQview), Borland in its early years, and dozens of others operated in or adjacent to the shareware market. The independent author market was, by the early 1990s, a substantial commercial sector.

The trust-economy assumption that underwrote the model was historically unusual and is now historically distant. The assumption was that authors and users were in honest direct relationship — that the author would deliver real software, that the user would assess it honestly, that payment would follow value rather than precede it. The structural conditions that made this assumption viable were specific. Distribution costs were low. Enforcement costs were high (you could not actually compel a user to pay; the technology of digital rights management was primitive and easily defeated). Cultural norms supported honest accounting (the user groups, the BBS communities, the early-internet communities all reinforced the gift-economy logic). The audience was small enough that authors and users could plausibly think of themselves as members of the same community. The conditions held. The model worked. The cohort that grew up inside the model absorbed the trust assumption as default — as the way commerce between authors and users was supposed to work, given the chance.

The transition to the subscription-and-app-store economy of the 2010s was not a transition in user preference. It was a structural change driven by the consolidation of distribution into platform stores (Apple's App Store in 2008, Google Play in 2008, Steam earlier and at growing scale, the various mobile carrier app stores), by the consolidation of compute into cloud services (Amazon Web Services from 2006, the parallel rise of Software-as-a-Service business models), and by the venture-capital-driven imperative to convert every product into a continuous-revenue stream. The subscription model assumes the opposite of the shareware model's underlying premise. It assumes that users will not pay unless they are forced to, that value extraction must be continuous rather than occurring at the moment of value delivery, that the relationship between producer and consumer is adversarial in the limit. The platform takes thirty percent. The publisher retains the customer relationship as their core asset and resists any author-direct contact that might let the author build a relationship independent of the platform. The user, who in the shareware era could write a check to the author of the software they used, in the subscription era pays a monthly fee to a platform that holds the author at arm's length.

The friction that this transition has produced in the cohort raised on shareware is not nostalgia and is not simply economic. It is structural. The shareware-raised user encounters subscription software as an ethical violation rather than as a business model, because they have firsthand experience of an alternative model that worked, that produced viable software and viable businesses, and that respected the direct author-user relationship as the constitutive social unit of the exchange. The discomfort is the discomfort of someone who has been moved from a high-trust environment into a low-trust environment without consenting to the change, and who is being told that the low-trust environment is the natural state of affairs. The cohort that did not grow up in the shareware era has no such reference point and registers the subscription model as the way software is and always has been. The asymmetry is, again, a feature of the broader cultural archaeology this book is undertaking — different cohorts carry different default assumptions about what economic arrangements are normal, and the assumptions trace to specific historical environments rather than to abstract preferences.

The platform-store transition deserves a more careful treatment because the structural change was not symmetric across its participants. The platform took thirty percent of every transaction. The author lost the direct relationship with the user. The user lost the option of paying the author directly. All three parties — author, user, platform — experienced different shifts in their position. The author gained distribution scale they could not have achieved on their own. The user gained discovery and one-click purchase and a curated catalog that promised safety from malware and fraud. The platform gained an extractive position in every transaction within its ecosystem and the leverage to dictate technical and business terms to authors who depended on it for distribution. The aggregate effect on the cohort raised on shareware was specific: they could see that the platform had taken the relationship apart and inserted itself between the parties that the shareware model had kept in direct contact. The cohort raised on shareware understood the platform's thirty percent not as a market price for a useful service but as a tax on a relationship the platform had not constructed and did not need to mediate. The understanding was not always articulated. The discomfort it produced was usually articulated in terms of frustration with specific features of the platform — the difficulty of contacting a developer, the inability to pay a developer directly, the platform's intermediation of customer support. The deeper structural critique remained beneath the surface, sometimes for years, until the cohort began to find each other again in conversations about indie distribution and direct patronage and federated alternatives.

The cultural footprint of the shareware era includes one element that deserves explicit naming, because it is now almost invisible: the shareware text file as a literary form. The READ.ME, REGISTER.TXT, README.1ST, INSTALL.TXT, MANUAL.TXT — the cluster of plain-text files that shipped alongside a shareware program — was a genre of writing with conventions as specific as any other minor literary form. The voice was first-person, addressed directly to the user, often by a single named author. The structure followed a predictable arc: the author introduced themselves, described the program, explained how to install and use it, articulated the registration terms, acknowledged contributors and inspirations, listed known issues, and sometimes closed with a personal note about why the program was written. The register was warm without being unprofessional, direct without being curt, specific without being verbose. The form taught a generation of programmers how to write technical documentation for non-technical readers, and the form survives in the modern README.md file at the root of every open-source repository, where the conventions are recognizable across the decades. When the modern language model is asked to draft a README for a new project, what comes back is structured according to this genre, because the genre is dense in the training data, because the cohort that wrote the shareware text files carried the conventions forward into every subsequent generation of software documentation.

The shareware ethos has persisted in specific corners of the contemporary software ecosystem, against substantial structural pressure. The free-and-open-source-software movement carries the gift-economy logic forward in a form that does not require payment at all, and that has produced the bulk of the internet's foundational infrastructure — the Linux kernel, the BSD operating systems, the open-source databases and web servers and language runtimes on which the entire commercial cloud ecosystem rests. The patronage models that have emerged on Patreon and Substack and Ko-fi for individual writers and creators reproduce the shareware direct-author-user relationship in a different medium — pay the author directly, receive their work, the relationship is the unit of the exchange. The pay-what-you-want models that have appeared in indie game distribution (the Humble Bundle being the canonical example) and in some independent music distribution (the Bandcamp model) explicitly invoke the shareware assumption that users will pay fairly given the option. The shareware logic, in each of these cases, is being deliberately preserved against the dominant tendency of the platform economy to extract maximum rent at every transaction.

Yochai Benkler, in *The Wealth of Networks* (2006), gave the most sustained theoretical account of why commons-based peer production — the broader category that includes both the open-source movement and the shareware trust economy — produces value that the conventional market does not. His central argument was that the dominant economic theory of the late twentieth century, which assumed that the only viable models for organizing productive activity were the firm and the market, had failed to account for a third organizational form that the networked information economy had made viable at scale. The third form was commons-based production: distributed individuals, motivated by intrinsic factors and by the social rewards of contribution, producing artifacts that were freely available to all, coordinated through lightweight social and technical infrastructure rather than through wage employment or market exchange. Benkler's analysis was the academic articulation of what the shareware authors and the user-group librarians and the BBS sysops had been operating for two decades. The shareware model was the commercial cousin of the commons-based form, halfway between the gift economy of the open-source community and the price economy of the conventional software industry. The cohort that lived through the shareware era did so at the inflection moment when the commons logic was at its most economically functional — when authors really could make a living on direct user relationships, when the platform tax was zero, when the trust economy actually paid its participants.

The crippleware and nagware variants of shareware deserve a brief note because they represent the model under stress. Some shareware authors, dissatisfied with the registration rates they were seeing, began to ship versions of their programs with deliberately limited functionality — features disabled until registration, time-limited trials that expired after thirty days, mandatory startup nag screens that reminded the unregistered user of their unregistered status with increasing frequency. The community response to these variants was mixed. Some users accepted them as a reasonable accommodation to economic reality. Others regarded them as a violation of the underlying shareware ethic, which had been that the program should work fully on the honor system and that the registration request should rely on the user's good faith rather than on coerced behavior. The debate inside the Association of Shareware Professionals over what counted as legitimate shareware practice ran for years and was never fully resolved. The history matters because the debate prefigured the much broader debate that would later erupt around digital-rights management, around in-app-purchase coercion in mobile games, around the gradual transition from purchased software to perpetually-rented software. The shareware community was the first venue in which the trust-economy assumption met its commercial-pressure limits, and the unresolved arguments from that period are arguments the broader software industry is still having three decades later, often without knowing the origin of the dispute.

The cognitive imprint the shareware era left on its participants is consequential. The cohort that wrote checks to software authors at thirteen learned that direct economic relationship with creators was possible. They learned that producers and consumers could deal honestly with each other at scale without an intermediating platform. They learned that the friction they later experienced inside platform-mediated commerce was not the natural state of affairs but a recent imposition. When they encountered, much later, the platform economy in its mature form, they had a structural alternative in mind — a memory of a different way the same exchange had once worked, in their own lifetimes, with their own hands. The memory is not nostalgia. It is reference data. It is the operational evidence that the alternative is feasible. It informs how the cohort thinks about every economic question in the contemporary technology landscape, often without their being able to articulate where the structural intuition came from.

The propagation into the training corpus is subtler than in the previous chapters but no less real. The shareware-era documentation, the README files that accompanied shareware programs, the registration text files, the author bios printed on the inside covers of the manuals, the editorial discussions in the trade press about shareware as a model — all of this is in the training data, often in the same archives that carry the BBS file areas and the user-group newsletters and the Usenet discussions. The model learns, from this corpus, that direct author-user relationships are a legitimate organizational form. The model can reproduce the register of the shareware READ.ME file when asked. More importantly, the model has been trained on a literature that takes the shareware ethic as part of the background context of software culture, and that ethic shapes the model's defaults when it is asked to think about software economics. The shareware logic has been refreshed in the corpus by every subsequent generation of writers who themselves grew up inside it and who carried its assumptions into the technical writing of subsequent eras.

The argument is now nearly complete for Part III. The hive mind that produced the training corpus was constituted by the BBS sysops, the Usenet regulars, the user-group librarians, the magazine writers, and the shareware authors — five overlapping social formations, with substantial member overlap, operating in adjacent rooms across a thirty-year period, building together the working corpus of technical English and the cultural assumptions encoded into it. The corpus is now the substrate that teaches modern AI how to write, how to argue, how to think about software, how to relate to its users. The hive mind did not know it was building the substrate. It was operating on its own immediate logic — the next BBS post, the next Usenet reply, the next user-group demo, the next magazine column, the next shareware release. The substrate accumulated underneath as a side effect. The substrate is what we are excavating.

One specific anecdote about the id Software shareware release will serve as the chapter's closing artifact. When *Doom* shipped its shareware first episode on December 10, 1993, the file was uploaded to the FTP server at the University of Wisconsin–Parkside, which had agreed to host it. The server collapsed under the load within minutes. The administrators removed all other files temporarily to make room. The download flood continued for days. The mirror sites that came online to handle overflow were themselves volunteer-operated, often at universities whose administrators had been persuaded by sympathetic graduate students to allocate bandwidth for the cause. The distribution architecture for one of the most economically successful software releases of its era was, on launch day, a federation of volunteer-administered university FTP servers, coordinated by no central authority, sustained by goodwill and by the shared cultural assumption that mirroring a popular file was a legitimate use of academic infrastructure. The author of *Doom*'s first episode was id Software. The publisher was id Software. The distributor was the cohort itself. The transaction layer — the moment of payment, when a user mailed in their order for the second and third episodes — was the United States Postal Service. The model worked because every layer of the infrastructure was operating on assumptions consistent with the shareware ethic. When the model stopped working a decade later, what had changed was not the goodwill of the cohort. What had changed was that the infrastructure layers had been consolidated into entities for whom the shareware ethic was not a legitimate input.

Part IV turns from the social formations that produced the substrate to the cinematic and literary texts that taught the same cohort how to think about what it was building. The films and books were the priming layer that gave the substrate its imaginative shape — that taught a generation what it would feel like to talk to a machine that talked back, decades before the talking back was possible.
