# Chapter 37: The Forgotten Origin Points

You are standing in the doorway of a small office on Linn Avenue in Albuquerque, in March of 1975, and the door is propped open with a phone book because the heat from the soldering bench in the back room has pushed the indoor temperature past what the swamp cooler can handle. The strip mall outside is the kind of strip mall that the desert produces — single-story, flat-roofed, the parking lot cracked from the sun, a barbershop on one end and a laundromat on the other, and in the middle a glass storefront with white vinyl letters that say MITS. Inside the office, on a workbench against the back wall, sits an Altair 8800 — a metal box about the size of a stereo receiver, painted blue, with toggle switches and red LEDs across the front panel. There is a teletype next to it, a paper tape reader, and a tangle of ribbon cable that disappears behind the bench into something you cannot see. A man in his mid-thirties — Ed Roberts, the founder, an Air Force veteran who started this company in 1969 selling model-rocket telemetry kits to hobbyists — is at the workbench with a long-haired twenty-two-year-old who flew down from Boston last week. The twenty-two-year-old is Paul Allen. He saw the Altair on the cover of *Popular Electronics* at a Harvard Square newsstand in January, ran across the street to his friend Bill Gates's dorm room at Currier House, and the two of them placed a phone call to MITS the next month claiming they had a working BASIC interpreter for the machine. They did not yet have one. They wrote it on a PDP-10 simulator at Harvard over the next eight weeks, Allen flying down to Albuquerque this morning with the paper tape in his coat pocket, the two of them having decided he would load the tape into a machine he had never physically touched and hope the simulator he had cross-developed on was accurate enough that the interpreter would actually run. It runs. It runs on the first attempt. Roberts shakes Allen's hand. Bill Gates is still in Cambridge, finishing his sophomore-year exams, and will fly down later. The company that the two of them are about to found does not yet have a name. They are tossing around variants — Allen-Gates, Gates-Allen, something with "micro" in it, something with "soft" in it. The decision will be made in a few weeks. The decision will be that they will call it Micro-Soft, with a hyphen, soon to drop the hyphen, and the company will be founded here, in Albuquerque, in April of 1975, and will remain here for four formative years before it moves to Bellevue in January of 1979. You are standing in the room where the personal-computer era began, and the room is not in California.

The room is the load-bearing fact of this chapter, because the popular narrative of personal computing — the narrative that begins in a Cupertino garage with two long-haired Steves and a wire-wrap board — is not wrong about Cupertino, but it is wrong about the beginning. The beginning was in Albuquerque, in a strip mall, in a state that the coastal narrative of computing has rarely treated as anything but a flyover, and the people who built the beginning were a Georgia-born Air Force engineer named Ed Roberts and two college-age kids from Seattle who got there because of a magazine cover, not because of a venture capitalist. The four origin points this chapter treats — Albuquerque, Champaign-Urbana, Salt Lake City, Pittsburgh — share a structural similarity that bears stating before the specific cases develop. Each of the four built something foundational. Each of the four built it before Silicon Valley's dominant narrative had formed. Each of the four watched its foundational contribution be absorbed, decades later, into a generic "computer industry" story whose center of gravity was retrospectively placed in California. The absorption was not malicious. It was emergent. Silicon Valley produced — alongside its real technical work — a particularly effective mythology engine, and the mythology engine wrote the dominant history while the field was still developing. The four origin points this chapter treats were too geographically dispersed, too institutionally singular, and too far from the mythology engine's writers to produce competing narratives that the popular memory would carry forward. So the contributions persisted in the artifacts — Microsoft, Mosaic, Pixar, PostScript, the symbolic-AI tradition that chain-of-thought prompting has lately recovered — while the regional sources became footnotes. The job of this chapter is to put the regional sources back into the body of the text.

The Albuquerque story begins, as noted, with MITS — Micro Instrumentation and Telemetry Systems, founded by Ed Roberts in 1969 in partnership with three Air Force colleagues who would later leave the venture to Roberts alone. The company's first product was a model-rocket telemetry kit, sold to the hobbyist community through the back pages of *Popular Electronics* and *Model Rocketry* magazines. The company drifted, through the early 1970s, into electronic calculators — which was, briefly, a lucrative market and then a catastrophically unprofitable one after Texas Instruments entered and crushed the margins. By 1974, MITS was in deep financial trouble. Roberts, in a last-ditch attempt to keep the company alive, bet on the Intel 8080 — a brand-new microprocessor that nobody had yet built a consumer product around — and designed a kit computer that hobbyists could assemble themselves. The kit cost $397, ran the Intel 8080, had front-panel toggle switches for input and LEDs for output, and did essentially nothing useful out of the box. Roberts called it the Altair 8800. He persuaded Les Solomon, the technical editor of *Popular Electronics*, to put the Altair on the magazine's January 1975 cover. He expected to sell two hundred units in the first year. He sold two hundred in the first afternoon. By the end of January, the orders had backed up far enough that MITS could not ship the kits as fast as the checks were arriving, and Roberts found himself running, suddenly, the first commercially successful personal computer company in the world — from a strip mall in Albuquerque, with a staff he was scrambling to hire from the local engineering community, and with no software for the machine he was shipping. This is the situation Allen and Gates walked into in March of 1975 when they offered him a BASIC interpreter. The deal was struck. Microsoft was founded in Albuquerque a few weeks later. Its first office was inside the MITS building. Its early employees — Steve Wood, Marc McDonald (the very first hire, employee number three after Gates and Allen), Bob Wallace, Bob Greenberg, Jim Lane, and others — were hired locally, in Albuquerque, through the engineering networks that Sandia and the University of New Mexico and the wider defense-research substrate had produced. Microsoft remained in Albuquerque from April 1975 through January 1979 — almost four years at the company's most formative stage, the period during which it transitioned from a two-man BASIC shop into a company shipping language products to multiple hardware vendors, and the period during which the company's early engineering culture was established by people whose cognitive formation had happened in a desert-engineering environment one hundred miles south of Los Alamos.

The wider Albuquerque substrate matters here, and it has been thinly treated in the popular Microsoft histories. Sandia National Laboratories, founded in 1949 as the engineering counterpart to Los Alamos's physics, employed thousands of engineers in Albuquerque through the postwar decades — people who designed the non-nuclear components of weapons systems, the telemetry and instrumentation that made high-energy physics measurable, and the embedded electronics that the national-security state required at a volume civilian industries could not yet support. Los Alamos itself, a hundred miles north up the Rio Grande, employed a population of physicists, computational engineers, and weapons designers whose children went to school in northern New Mexico and whose engineering culture spilled across the state. The University of New Mexico's engineering school served as the regional training ground for those children and for the wider Sandia-adjacent kids. The MITS office on Linn Avenue did not appear in a vacuum. It appeared in a city whose engineering culture was already a generation deep, and the early Microsoft hires absorbed that culture by working alongside its products. The move to Bellevue in January 1979 was driven by Gates's personal ties to Seattle — his family was there, his parents' business and civic networks were there, his sister was at the University of Washington — not by any deficiency of the Albuquerque environment. Roberts himself sold MITS to Pertec in 1977, eventually leaving the computer industry to attend medical school and become a small-town family physician in Georgia. The popular narrative that Microsoft "started in Seattle" is a regional-mythology compression that erases four formative years and the entire desert-engineering ecosystem that hosted them. Paul Allen's memoir *Idea Man* (2011) treats the Albuquerque period with the depth it deserves; the dominant narrative does not. The Albuquerque footnote is one of the load-bearing examples of how the regional sources of personal computing got absorbed.

The Champaign-Urbana story is, if anything, more startling, because it concerns an artifact that operated for two decades before the popular internet existed and that prefigured every major social-computing pattern of the 1990s and 2000s. The artifact is PLATO — Programmed Logic for Automatic Teaching Operations — an educational-computing system designed by Donald Bitzer and his collaborators at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign starting in 1960 and operational, in increasingly elaborate forms, through the 1980s. PLATO was, on its surface, an educational platform: it ran computer-assisted lessons for students at the university and at remote sites that connected over leased lines, and it pioneered a great deal of the pedagogical-software tradition that the educational-technology industry has been mining ever since. But what PLATO became, by the mid-1970s, was the first online community at scale — and the phrase "first online community at scale" carries the weight of a claim that the popular history of social computing has rarely registered. PLATO had Talkomatic, a multi-user real-time chat program, in 1973. It had Notes, a threaded online forum system, in 1973. It had multi-user games — Empire (a real-time space-combat game with persistent universe state) and Avatar (a multi-user dungeon ancestral to every MMO that followed) — by the late 1970s. It had shared-screen collaboration. It had a graphical terminal, the plasma-display panel that Bitzer's group invented specifically for PLATO and that IBM later licensed for its own products, capable of rendering bitmapped graphics and touch input at a moment when most computer systems were still character-only. It had, at its peak, thousands of simultaneous users logged in from dozens of remote sites across North America, sending each other messages, playing games together, leaving notes in topic-organized forums, and forming the relational patterns that the WELL, USENET, and the broader social-computing tradition would later inherit. Decades before the internet. Decades before USENET. Decades before AOL. The pattern of "log in, see who else is online, send messages, play games together, leave notes for the next user" was operational in Illinois in 1973, and the people who lived inside that pattern carried its lessons forward into every adjacent system they later built.

Brian Dear's *The Friendly Orange Glow* (2017) is the definitive history of PLATO, and it is the load-bearing source for any serious treatment of the system. Dear, who himself worked on PLATO as a student in the late 1970s, spent two decades interviewing the surviving practitioners and reconstructing the system's social and technical history from the surviving archives and the surviving people. His book is the reason the PLATO story can be told at all in the present tense — most of the original artifacts have been lost, and the institutional memory survives largely in the people Dear interviewed before they died. The PLATO veterans whose subsequent careers carried the system's lessons outward are the names that any student of social-computing history should know. Ray Ozzie — PLATO user as an Illinois undergraduate, later the architect of Lotus Notes (which was, in design intent, an attempt to reproduce PLATO Notes in the corporate-PC era), later the Chief Software Architect of Microsoft from 2006 through 2010, succeeding Bill Gates in that role. Tim Halvorsen, Ozzie's collaborator on early Notes and on the wider Iris/Lotus tradition. The founders of CompuServe absorbed PLATO conventions through the engineers they hired. The founders of the early online-services industry — every dial-up bulletin board, every commercial online service of the 1980s, every educational network that emerged before the internet's commercialization — were either PLATO veterans themselves or were learning from people who had been. The system's influence was diffuse and pervasive in the way that influences are when they propagate through people rather than through institutions. PLATO did not produce a single dominant successor that carried its name. It produced a generation of practitioners who carried its patterns into everything they touched.

The same University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign campus later hosted the National Center for Supercomputing Applications — NCSA — founded in 1986 as one of the original five NSF-funded supercomputing centers. NCSA's job was to make supercomputing accessible to academic researchers across the country, and the job required, among other things, building software that ordinary researchers could use to visualize and share their results. At NCSA, in 1992 and 1993, an undergraduate programmer named Marc Andreessen — working as a paid student employee at $6.85 an hour — collaborated with the staff programmer Eric Bina on a project to build a graphical web browser for the still-experimental World Wide Web that Tim Berners-Lee had developed at CERN in Switzerland in 1989-1991. The Web itself was Berners-Lee's invention. The browser that made it usable to a mass audience was the NCSA Mosaic that Andreessen and Bina built in Illinois. Mosaic was released in early 1993 — first for Unix, then for Windows and Macintosh later that year — and within months had transformed the Web from a niche academic system into something that ordinary people could install on their home computers and use to browse hypertext content with embedded images. The web was born in Switzerland. It was made usable in Illinois. The distinction matters because the subsequent narrative compression — "the web came out of Silicon Valley" — has obscured both of the actual origin points, and the Illinois origin point has been the more thoroughly obscured because Andreessen, after graduating in 1993, moved to Silicon Valley to co-found Netscape with Jim Clark, and the company that commercialized Mosaic became, in the popular memory, the place Mosaic itself was made. Steven Levy's reporting on the Mosaic period — published across his long career at *Newsweek*, *Wired*, and elsewhere — treats the NCSA origin with appropriate weight, but the popular narrative has rarely caught up.

The Salt Lake City story is, on its surface, the most narrowly technical of the four — it concerns a single university computer-graphics program — and yet its consequences ramify into nearly every visual-computing system in modern use, including the rendering pipelines that contemporary AI image and video models depend on. The program was founded at the University of Utah in 1968 by David Evans, a Utah native and Mormon engineer, and Ivan Sutherland, whose Sketchpad system at MIT in 1963 had effectively invented the field of interactive computer graphics. Evans and Sutherland set up the Utah program as a small graduate-research operation, funded heavily by ARPA, with a single explicit mission: to produce the cohort of researchers who would build the field of computer graphics. Over the following decade, the program produced what may be the densest concentration of consequential graduate students any single computing program has ever produced. Ed Catmull received his PhD in 1974, writing the first computer-graphics dissertation on what would become texture mapping and z-buffering, and went on to co-found Pixar — the company that would later transform animated film and whose technical lineage runs straight back to Salt Lake City. Jim Clark received his PhD the same year, 1974, and went on to found Silicon Graphics in 1982 (the company that built the workstations every Hollywood visual-effects house ran during the 1990s) and then to co-found Netscape in 1994 with the same Marc Andreessen who had built Mosaic at NCSA — a transcontinental marriage of two of this chapter's origin points whose product was the company that, briefly, defined the commercial web. John Warnock received his PhD from Utah in 1969, joined Xerox PARC, and later co-founded Adobe Systems in 1982 with Charles Geschke, where he invented PostScript and the broader desktop-publishing revolution that PostScript made possible. Alan Kay, while not technically a Utah PhD, worked extensively with Sutherland's group as a postdoctoral researcher and later named the Utah period as one of the formative environments of his thinking — and Kay's subsequent work at Xerox PARC on Smalltalk and the Dynabook concept gave conceptual shape to the personal computer as we know it. Henri Gouraud, also a Utah graduate, originated Gouraud shading in 1971, an algorithm that every real-time 3D system has used in some form ever since. Martin Newell, working at Utah in 1975, modeled the now-famous Utah teapot as a reference geometry, and the Utah teapot remains, fifty years later, the universal benchmark object for testing rendering algorithms.

The Salt Lake City ecosystem extended beyond the university's graphics program. WordPerfect was founded in Orem in 1979 by Alan Ashton, a BYU computer-science professor, and Bruce Bastian, his graduate student, and became, through the 1980s, the dominant word processor on the IBM PC platform — its market share peaking near 50 percent in the late 1980s before Microsoft Word's Windows version eventually overtook it. Novell was founded in Provo in 1979 and rebuilt from 1983 onward by Ray Noorda, and through the 1980s and early 1990s the company's NetWare product was the dominant local-area-networking software on the planet — every office that ran a file server through that period was, in overwhelming likelihood, running Novell. The Mormon engineering tradition that produced this regional cluster is worth naming explicitly without overclaiming for it: the LDS Church's strong emphasis on education, its missionary tradition that produced a generation of young adults fluent in foreign languages and accustomed to discipline, and the BYU and University of Utah pipeline that fed graduates into the regional employers produced an engineering culture with specific cultural markers — meticulous documentation, family-business sensibility, conservative engineering choices, long-tenured employees, and a particular work ethic that the contemporaries in California sometimes found culturally alien but consistently respected for its output. The most consequential through-line, however, runs from Sutherland's group to modern AI. Every neural network that produces visual output — every DALL-E image, every Stable Diffusion render, every Midjourney composition, every Runway video frame — sits on top of a rendering substrate whose intellectual ancestry traces directly back to the algorithms, data structures, and conceptual vocabulary that the Utah cohort developed in the 1970s. The contemporary AI image is, computationally, a triangle-rasterization descendant of work done in Salt Lake City fifty years before the model that produced it was trained. The lineage is unbroken. The popular narrative of AI image generation rarely mentions Utah. The lineage is there nonetheless, and any reader who works through the rendering pipelines that the modern generative-vision systems depend on will encounter the Utah names directly.

The Pittsburgh story is the oldest of the four and concerns the foundational tradition of artificial-intelligence research itself. The story begins at the Carnegie Institute of Technology — soon to merge with the Mellon Institute and become Carnegie Mellon University — where Allen Newell and Herbert Simon began collaborating in the mid-1950s on what would become the foundational program of symbolic AI. Newell and Simon, alongside Cliff Shaw at the RAND Corporation, built the Logic Theorist in 1955-1956 — a program that could prove theorems from Whitehead and Russell's *Principia Mathematica*, and that proved, in fact, thirty-eight of the first fifty-two theorems in chapter 2, including one whose proof was more elegant than Whitehead and Russell's own. Newell and Simon presented this work at the Dartmouth Conference in the summer of 1956 — the conference at which John McCarthy coined the term "artificial intelligence" — and from that moment forward, Carnegie Tech (later CMU) was one of the field's three principal institutional homes alongside MIT and Stanford. Newell and Simon followed the Logic Theorist with the General Problem Solver in 1957, which formalized the means-ends analysis approach to problem-solving that subsequent symbolic AI would build on for thirty years. Simon won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1978 for his work on bounded rationality, which was deeply intertwined with the AI research; Newell and Simon shared the Turing Award in 1975 — the first time the Turing Award went to AI researchers, recognition of a field they had effectively founded. Newell's *Unified Theories of Cognition* (1990), published shortly before his death, remains one of the foundational texts of cognitive science and articulates the SOAR architecture that Newell and his students had developed through the 1980s. Pamela McCorduck's *Machines Who Think* (1979) is the definitive popular history of the founding period and treats the Carnegie Tech contribution with appropriate weight; Margaret Boden's *Mind as Machine* (2006) provides the contemporary scholarly synthesis of the symbolic-AI tradition that Newell and Simon launched.

Newell and Simon's student Raj Reddy, who joined CMU in 1969 after a PhD with John McCarthy at Stanford, founded the CMU Robotics Institute in 1979 — one of the largest and most consequential robotics programs in the world, and an institution whose graduate students have gone on to lead robotics research at nearly every major university and industrial laboratory. The wider CMU symbolic-AI tradition is the school of thought that, by the conventional narrative, "lost" the empirical contest to the connectionist revolution of the 2010s — the deep-learning wave, the transformer architecture, the generative models that have lately defined the public face of AI. The symbolic school argued that intelligence was symbolic manipulation, rule-following, search through structured state-spaces; the connectionist school argued that intelligence was pattern-matching across distributed representations learned from data. The empirical contest, judged on benchmarks, was won decisively by the connectionists. The historiography that has flowed out of that win has treated the symbolic tradition with a kind of polite condescension — as a road not taken, a tradition whose time has passed, a school that lost. The historiography is being revised, slowly, as it becomes clear that the symbolic tradition is in fact being recovered inside the connectionist paradigm. Chain-of-thought prompting, the technique by which a language model is asked to reason step-by-step through a problem before producing its answer, is a direct descendant of the means-ends decomposition that the General Problem Solver embodied — the same decomposition strategy, implemented now through prompting rather than through hand-coded rules. Tool-use frameworks, in which a model decomposes a problem into a sequence of symbolic operations and delegates each operation to a specialized tool, are a direct descendant of the symbolic-AI vision that treated intelligence as orchestration over discrete operators. Neuro-symbolic systems, the active research frontier of the late 2020s, are an explicit attempt to combine the connectionist pattern-matching strength with the symbolic compositional reasoning that the Newell-Simon tradition built the formal vocabulary for. The Pittsburgh tradition did not lose. It was, for a time, eclipsed. It is being recovered now, and the recovery is happening inside the connectionist systems that the popular narrative had treated as its replacements.

The closing thesis of this chapter is the absorption pattern that all four origin points share. Each of these four built something foundational, and Silicon Valley — earning its prominence through real work over the subsequent decades — later absorbed each contribution into a narrative whose center of gravity was retrospectively placed in California. Microsoft's Albuquerque years became a footnote that the popular Microsoft history rarely treats. PLATO became a footnote unless the reader happened to follow Ray Ozzie's career arc into Lotus Notes and on into Microsoft, where the PLATO patterns continued to propagate inside the corporate-computing tradition. The Utah graphics program became a footnote unless the reader happened to read deeply into Pixar's prehistory, into Silicon Graphics' founding, into Adobe's origin story — none of which begin in Cupertino, all of which begin in Salt Lake City. The Newell-Simon tradition became a footnote in the popular narrative of AI's history, which now tends to begin with Yann LeCun and his colleagues training neural networks to recognize handwritten digits in the late 1980s, treating the previous three decades of symbolic-AI research as a kind of false start. The argument of this chapter is not that Silicon Valley stole these contributions — it did not, and saying so would be ahistorical and ungenerous. Silicon Valley earned its prominence through real work, real engineers, real companies, and a real cultural and institutional infrastructure that produced enormous value. The argument is that the dominant narrative of computing's origin compressed the actual geography into a single point, and that recognizing the constellation of origin points — Albuquerque, Champaign-Urbana, Salt Lake City, Pittsburgh, and the other regional nodes the surrounding chapters treat — is part of recognizing the substrate honestly. Each origin point's regional accent flowed into the corpus through the engineers it produced. The accents are still readable in the model's outputs to a reader who knows what to listen for. The desert-engineering matter-of-factness of the early Microsoft documentation. The community-builder warmth of the PLATO-tradition writers who carried Talkomatic and Notes into their later platforms. The rendering-pipeline precision of the Utah-trained graphics engineers whose textbooks taught a generation. The formal-systems rigor of the Carnegie Tech symbolic-AI writers whose chain-of-thought arguments are being recovered now inside the very models that were once thought to have made the tradition obsolete. The accents persist because the writing persists, and the writing persists because the people who wrote it had something to say. The constellation is large. The mythology engine compressed it. The compression can be undone, one origin point at a time, by the reader willing to walk back to the strip mall on Linn Avenue, to the plasma-display terminal in the basement of the University of Illinois, to the graphics lab in the Wasatch foothills, to the cognitive-science seminar room in Pittsburgh, and to listen for the accents that the model is still, sentence by sentence, giving back.
