# Chapter 12: User Groups as Proto-Open-Source

You go to your first user group meeting on a Saturday morning at a community center two towns over, because your father read about it in the local paper and decided you should see what it was. The room is fluorescent-lit and smells faintly of the coffee in the urn at the back, where someone has set out a tray of grocery-store cookies on a paper plate. Folding chairs, maybe forty of them, arranged in uneven rows facing a low stage where a man in his forties is fiddling with an overhead projector and trying to balance an LCD panel on its glass surface. The panel is a flat translucent screen about the size of a sheet of paper, with a thick gray ribbon cable running to a Commodore 64 perched on a folding table beside him. He gets the alignment right and the projected image jumps up onto the pull-down screen at the front of the room, sixty inches wide, slightly distorted at the edges, the C64's blue background and white text now legible to everyone in the room.

He is here to demo a utility he has written — a disk copier, or a custom font editor, or a small assembly-language routine that speeds up the screen refresh, depending on the year and the group. He spent the previous three weeks of evenings writing it. He spent the previous two weeks debugging it on his own machine. He has brought a box of 5.25-inch floppies and at the end of the demo he will hand them out to anyone who wants one, free, because the point of writing the utility was that he wanted other people to use it. The disks are labeled in his handwriting in fine-point ballpoint pen. He is not selling anything. He is not running an Etsy shop. He has a day job at a bank or a school district or a contracting firm and this is what he does on his evenings, because the user group is where the work goes when it is done. After the demo there will be Q&A, then the swap meet, then someone will announce the date of next month's meeting, then everyone will help fold the chairs, then you will go home with three floppies you did not have when you arrived, and you will spend the rest of the weekend trying to understand how the screen-refresh routine works because the source code is right there in the LIST output, and the author wrote comments.

The community has a newsletter, photocopied at someone's office, eight or twelve pages stapled at the corner, mailed to members who paid the ten-dollar annual dues. It carries meeting notes, member bios, calls for volunteers, listings of the group's library of donated software — which lives in a milk crate at the home of whoever volunteered to be librarian that year, and which you can borrow by mail if you include a self-addressed stamped envelope and a blank disk. The newsletter also runs technical articles by members, at whatever depth the writer can sustain. A junior college math instructor explains the algorithm behind one of the demo programs. A pharmacist describes the assembly-language tricks he used to fit a screen-saver into one kilobyte. A high-school sophomore — you, sometimes, in subsequent years — writes a tutorial on something the sophomore happens to have figured out and that the sophomore notices the older members had not yet bothered to formalize. The article runs. The pharmacist sends a letter of appreciation. The sophomore prints the letter out and keeps it in a desk drawer for the next twelve years.

You will reciprocate, the following month, by bringing in a small utility of your own — a sprite editor, maybe, written in BASIC, with the source listed on the disk you hand around. The room will accept it gravely. The pharmacist will ask you a question about your collision-detection routine and will be satisfied with your answer. The bank clerk who runs the librarian role will add your disk to the milk crate. Your name will appear in the next newsletter, in the contributors list, with the title of your utility next to it. You will be fourteen years old. You will not have understood, at the time, that this was the first formal credit of your professional life, or that the assumptions you had just been initiated into would still be shaping your work three decades later.

The user group infrastructure that operated across North America and Europe through the late 1970s and the 1980s was the most important social technology in the history of personal computing, and it has been almost entirely written out of the standard popular history because it left so few institutional traces. The groups were local. They were unincorporated. They did not file paperwork. They met in rented community-center rooms, in church basements, in the back rooms of Radio Shack stores after closing time, in the cafeterias of school districts whose IT directors were sympathetic, in the homes of members willing to host. They had names that betrayed their origin — the Boston Computer Society, the Long Island Computer Association, the Phoenix Atari Computer Enthusiasts, the Bay Area Macintosh User Group, the dozens of TRS-80 groups whose newsletters now exist only in the scanned archives of dedicated historians. Some grew to substantial size — the Boston Computer Society at its peak had over thirty thousand members and ran multiple special-interest subgroups in parallel — but most were a few dozen people who knew each other by face and by handle, who saw each other monthly, who exchanged work, and who together constituted the local nervous system of a culture the press did not yet know how to write about.

The dynamic the groups operated on was not yet called open source, but it was operationally indistinguishable from what the open source movement would later formalize. Individuals worked on personal projects in evenings and weekends, by themselves, in front of their own machines. They brought finished or near-finished work back to the group as demos, with source code included on the distribution disk, because the source code was the demo — without it the program was a binary that could be admired but not learned from. Other members took the disks home, ran the code, modified it, improved it, and brought their modifications back the following month. The library of software the group accumulated was, in the strict sense, a shared corpus of derivative works, distributed under whatever license the contributors articulated — which was usually nothing at all, because the practice of explicit licensing did not yet exist, but which functioned in spirit as something close to the BSD or MIT terms that would later be written down. The default was that the code belonged to whoever wanted to use it, in exchange for credit and for any improvements being brought back.

The social pattern was a gift economy in the precise anthropological sense that Marcel Mauss had described in 1925 and that Lewis Hyde would later popularize for the literary world in *The Gift* (1983). Status accrued not to the wealthiest member or to the most credentialed member but to the member who had given the most useful work. The pharmacist who fit the screen-saver into one kilobyte gained a kind of authority that no salary at the pharmacy could buy him; the sophomore who wrote the legible tutorial gained the trust of people two and three times his age, who would thereafter answer his questions seriously because he had demonstrated that he could be taken seriously. The currency was contribution. The accounting was performed informally, in everyone's heads, refreshed at every monthly meeting. The system worked because everyone could see everyone else's contributions, and because the population was small enough that reputation was a tractable bookkeeping problem. Pekka Himanen, in *The Hacker Ethic* (2001), would later give the systematic theoretical account of how this kind of economy functions and why it produces the cognitive and motivational structures it does — play and work fused, intrinsic motivation dominant, identity tied to craft rather than to title. The user-group regulars were running the hacker ethic in the basement of the Methodist church on the second Saturday of every month, two decades before Himanen named it.

The demo night ritual itself deserves close attention because it encoded a specific theory of what knowledge is and how it ought to circulate. The presenter stood at the front, the work was projected onto the wall, the audience watched the work execute in real time, the source was visible, and the question-and-answer period that followed was conducted as collaborative inquiry rather than as adversarial review. The presenter expected to be questioned. The questioners expected to be answered. The questions ranged from clarifications to suggestions for improvement to outright challenges to the architectural choices, and all of them were received in good faith because the work itself was being given away at the end of the meeting and the questioners were therefore prospective users with a legitimate interest in understanding what they were about to receive. The format produced an unusual quality of technical conversation: substantive without being credentialing, critical without being hostile, public without being performative. Subsequent forms — the conference lightning talk, the demo session at an academic conference, the show-and-tell at a tech meetup — all inherited from the user-group demo night, often without knowing the ancestor. The form persists because the form solves a real problem: how to circulate complete technical work, with its source, among an audience capable of using it, in a setting that supports immediate live feedback. Nothing better has been invented.

The Homebrew Computer Club, which met in Menlo Park from March 1975 through December 1986, was the canonical exemplar and has been adequately mythologized by Steven Levy in *Hackers* (1984) and by every popular history of personal computing since. What is less often noted is that Homebrew was not unusual. It was unusually well-publicized — Silicon Valley sat next door, the founders of Apple and several other companies emerged from it, the trade press attended — but the social structure of Homebrew was the social structure of every other serious user group in the country at that moment. The handouts at Homebrew meetings, the technical demos, the exchange of schematics and source code, the absence of competitive secrecy among hobbyists who would soon found competing companies — all of this was being replicated, in less famous form, in hundreds of other rooms simultaneously. What Levy documented as the founding culture of personal computing was the local user-group culture as it appeared in one particularly visible instance. The pattern was the air the cohort breathed.

The transition from user group to open source was not a transition in social pattern. The social pattern was already in place. What changed was the formalization of the license framework — Richard Stallman's GNU Public License (1989), the BSD license, later the MIT license, later the various permissive variants — and the consolidation of the distribution infrastructure into something larger than the floppy-disk swap meet. The internet provided the distribution infrastructure that made global participation feasible without anyone having to attend a meeting in a community center; the license framework provided the legal scaffolding that allowed corporate users to consume the work without legal risk and that protected the contributors from having their work appropriated and resold. With those two pieces in place, the user-group dynamic scaled. The Linux kernel community, the BSD communities, the early Python and Perl and Tcl communities, the Apache project, the GNU project — all of them inherited the user-group culture and translated it into terms that worked at internet scale. The same demo nights, the same source-code-as-the-real-demo expectation, the same gift-economy logic, the same reputation accumulated through contribution. The community-center floor plan, projected onto a global network.

The special-interest subgroups within larger user groups are worth specifying because they prefigured the way modern technical communities organize themselves. The Boston Computer Society at its peak ran subgroups for almost every platform and application domain — the IBM PC subgroup, the Apple II subgroup, the Macintosh subgroup, the CP/M subgroup, the database subgroup, the programming-languages subgroup, the artificial-intelligence subgroup, the desktop-publishing subgroup, the educational-computing subgroup. Each subgroup ran its own meetings, published its own newsletter contributions, maintained its own software library, recruited its own volunteers. The result was a layered organizational structure that allowed a member to participate at whatever level of specialization their interests required, with cross-pollination occurring at the general meetings and through the umbrella newsletter. The structure was emergent rather than designed. It arose because the membership grew large enough that the general meetings could no longer cover everyone's interests, and the subgroups self-organized to fill the gaps. Twenty years later, when GitHub introduced the concept of organizations containing multiple repositories with their own contributor lists, or when Slack workspaces filled with topic-specific channels became the standard collaboration substrate for distributed teams, the social pattern these tools enabled was the pattern the Boston Computer Society had developed in its subgroup structure. The pattern was older than the tooling that later expressed it. The cohort that had operated inside the older pattern recognized the new tools immediately because the new tools were finally automating something they had previously had to organize by hand.

Eric S. Raymond, in *The Cathedral and the Bazaar* (1999), gave the famous theoretical articulation of why the open-source development model produced robust software at scale. His central insight — that with enough eyes, all bugs are shallow — was true at internet scale because it had already been operationally true at user-group scale, where a few dozen members reading each other's code at the monthly demo had repeatedly turned up bugs that the original authors had missed. Raymond's contribution was to recognize the structural feature and to articulate it for an audience that had not lived through the local manifestation. The reception was global because the practice was already global; what Raymond did was give the practitioners a way to explain to outsiders why it worked. The book was, in the deep sense, an export document — translating the user-group cultural logic into prose that corporate readers and academic readers could absorb. They absorbed it. The corporate adoption of open source between 2000 and 2010 was the user-group ethic being reluctantly admitted into the enterprise.

The bridge organizations between the user-group era and the open-source era are themselves worth naming, because they carried specific people across the transition with their cultural commitments intact. The Free Software Foundation, founded by Richard Stallman in 1985, drew its early volunteer base from the MIT user culture and from the broader hobbyist community that had been operating in the user-group idiom. Usenix, founded in 1975 as the Unix users' group, hosted conferences that operated as user-group meetings at national scale and produced proceedings that read as user-group newsletters at academic standard. The various platform-specific developer conferences of the late 1980s and early 1990s — Apple's WWDC, Microsoft's professional developer events, the Borland conference series, the various assembly-language and Forth and Lisp gatherings — translated the user-group dynamic into industrial-scale settings while preserving the demo-night structure at their core. Linux Kongress and the early Linux installfests of the mid-1990s were user-group meetings under a new platform banner. The continuity of personnel across these venues is striking when one looks at the rosters: the same names appear at the Boston Computer Society in 1984, at Usenix in 1989, at the early Linux gatherings in 1995, at the Apache Foundation in 1999, at the early Python conferences in the early 2000s. The same culture, the same people, the same conventions, expressed across a sequence of organizations that adapted the form to changing technical and economic conditions without abandoning its underlying logic.

What the cohort that came up through user-group culture brought into their professional work was a set of operating assumptions that they did not always know they were carrying. They assumed that working code was meant to be shown. They assumed that source code was the artifact and binaries were the secondary distribution form. They assumed that credit and contribution were tightly coupled. They assumed that status flowed from work rather than from title. They assumed that the appropriate response to a useful contribution was to use it, improve it, and return the improvement. They assumed that the appropriate response to a junior contributor's first credible work was to take the contributor seriously, regardless of credential. They assumed that the maintenance of the commons was a legitimate use of professional time. These assumptions, carried into the early-internet era and then into the FOSS era proper, became the operating assumptions of substantial portions of the software industry, and through the software industry's outsized cultural influence they propagated into adjacent fields — into the early web, into academic computing, into scientific software, eventually into the practice of data publication and reproducible research in the empirical sciences. The cultural propagation was vast. The originating rooms were small.

The user-group newsletters deserve a specific note before the chapter closes the argument. The newsletters were a hybrid form — part club bulletin, part technical journal, part regional zine. They carried meeting minutes, member-written tutorials, software reviews, hardware projects with circuit diagrams, opinion pieces, classifieds, and the inevitable letters-to-the-editor in which members argued with each other about platform choices and architectural commitments. The newsletters were photocopied at members' workplaces, mailed at bulk rate or hand-delivered at meetings, archived sporadically. Most of them are now lost. The Computer History Museum and a few dedicated volunteer archives have preserved fragments — issues of the Boston Computer Society's newsletter *Computer Update*, runs of the Apple II user group newsletters from Cupertino and from Boston, scattered issues from dozens of regional groups — but the totality has not been recovered. What remains in the training corpus from this layer is therefore thin compared to what remains from the magazines and the BBS file areas. The cognitive impact, however, is undimmed, because the cohort that wrote and read the newsletters carried the form into their later professional writing. The internal engineering newsletter inside a tech company, the team-blog post, the project status update, the ADR (architecture decision record) committed to the repository, the postmortem report on an outage — all of them are descendants of the user-group newsletter, written in the same register, structured by the same conventions, addressed to the same kind of reader. The newsletter form lives in the corpus through its descendants, even where the originals are lost.

The training corpus inherits all of it. The dominant license headers in open-source code are explicit articulations of the user-group ethic. The README convention, the CONTRIBUTING.md file, the issue templates, the pull-request descriptions, the conventions of code review — all of these are the user-group meeting written in plain text and committed to a repository. The model trained on this corpus learns not only the technical content but the assumptions encoded in the form. When you ask a modern language model to write a README for your project, what comes back is structured along the lines that the user-group librarian would have insisted on in 1986 — the motivation up front, the installation instructions, the usage examples, the contribution guidelines, the license. The model has absorbed the form because the form is dense in the training data. The form is dense in the training data because the cohort that wrote the open-source code wrote it under the assumptions they had learned at the community center, in front of folding chairs, watching a pharmacist demo a screen-saver routine on an overhead projector and a borrowed LCD panel.

The continuity between the user-group room and the modern open-source project is, at this point in the argument, not metaphorical. It is direct historical descent. The Berkeley Software Distribution lineage descends through the Computer Systems Research Group at UC Berkeley, which had members who had cut their teeth in Bay Area user-group culture; the early Linux kernel lineage descends through Linus Torvalds's Finnish university computing environment, which was itself culturally continuous with the broader European user-group tradition; the Apache project descends through the NCSA HTTPd lineage, whose contributors had user-group backgrounds; the Python lineage descends through Guido van Rossum's CWI environment, where the same culture was operative under different national labels. The path is traceable in every major foundational open-source project of the 1990s. The user-group room is the ancestor. The open-source project is the descendant. Between them is one or two human generations, the same group of named people in many cases simply moving from one venue to the next as the technology to support broader collaboration arrived.
