# Chapter 24: Why Some Click and Others Don't

The colleague is twenty-six, two years into the job, holds a degree from a school whose acceptance rate is in the single digits, and is by every available measure the smartest person in the meeting. You have been working with him on a refactor for three weeks. He writes excellent code. He reads documentation with a comprehension you envy. He follows instructions precisely. Yesterday you asked him how the AI assistant was working out for him on the migration pieces, because he had been allocated the same model subscription you have, and the firm had been pushing the productivity story. He paused before answering. He said the assistant was "fine," in the tone people use when something is not fine. You asked him what was difficult about it. He said — and you watched him struggle for the word — that it felt "alien." That it was hard to know what to ask it. That when he asked it something and it answered, the answer felt like it was for someone else, someone whose questions he was not quite asking. That he could see, when he watched you use it, that you were getting something different out of it, but he could not tell what you were doing differently. The conversation stayed with you on the drive home. You replayed it at the kitchen counter, putting away the groceries. The colleague was not less intelligent than you. The colleague was not less capable than you. The colleague was, in some specific cognitive sense, encountering a stranger when he typed into the prompt, and you were encountering a colleague — and the difference was not anything you had done to deserve it. It was an accident of when you had been born and what had been on the desk in front of you when you were thirteen.

This chapter takes up that asymmetry directly. The previous chapter argued that the layer is real and that its conventions can be elicited from any contemporary model with reasonable repeatability. This chapter argues that the same conventions explain a usability phenomenon the AI industry has been observing without quite naming — the phenomenon of clicking. Some users encounter a current language model and find it immediately, viscerally useful. They do not need to be taught how to prompt it. They sit down, they type, the responses come back, and within an hour they have integrated the tool into their working day so completely that they later struggle to remember what they did before. Other users — equally capable, equally educated, often objectively more skilled in the underlying technical domain — encounter the same model and bounce off it. They get answers. The answers are correct. The answers are useful. The interaction nonetheless feels effortful, alien, not-quite-right. They do not click. The clicking-versus-not-clicking divide does not correlate cleanly with technical skill, age, education, gender, or any of the usual demographic variables. It correlates, this chapter argues, with cognitive-pattern match between the user and the corpus.

The cognitive-pattern hypothesis can be stated precisely. The user types a prompt. The model produces a response in the conventions documented in Chapter 23 — Stack Overflow voice, README convention, GitHub issue structure, FAQ form, pedantic-helpful tone. The user reads the response. If the user's own cognitive habits are continuous with the corpus conventions — if the user, in writing or in thinking, also front-loads diagnostic conjectures, also assumes the conversational partner can be trusted with technical detail, also expects the worked example before the rule, also reads inline corrections as kindness rather than condescension — then the response lands. The user immediately recognizes the genre, processes the response in the genre's terms, identifies the next move (the next prompt, the follow-up question, the constraint to add) without conscious deliberation. The interaction has the texture of working with a competent colleague who happens to share all the references. The user clicks.

If the user's cognitive habits are not continuous with the corpus conventions, the response still lands — the model is competent, the answer is correct, the information is there — but the interaction does not have the texture of working with a competent colleague. It has the texture of receiving a memo from a stranger in a slightly off register. The information is usable, but the user has to do additional cognitive work to translate it into actionable form. The diagnostic conjecture sounds like an opinion rather than the first move in a debugging conversation. The inline correction sounds like a small attack rather than a gift. The worked example feels like the model showing off rather than the model giving you the artifact you actually need. The pedantic-helpful tone reads as condescending — because the user has not been inside the corpus long enough to recognize the tone as the corpus's standard register, in which all participants are addressed the same way. The same words, the same information, the same correct response. Different reading. The user does not click.

The hypothesis predicts something that turns out to be empirically observable. Clicking correlates more strongly with prior exposure to the corpus than with raw technical skill. A senior accountant who spent ten years lurking on Usenet in the 1990s clicks faster than a brand-new computer science PhD who has never read a thread on comp.lang.python. A nurse who ran a Trade Wars BBS in college clicks faster than a software engineer who learned to code entirely through bootcamp materials. A novelist who joined Slashdot in 2000 and has been on Hacker News ever since clicks faster than a data scientist who joined the field after the corpus had become invisible to its users. The variable that predicts clicking is corpus immersion, and corpus immersion correlates with age in a particular window — because the window in which the corpus was being actively written was a window during which only certain people were online — but it does not correlate with age outside that window. A seventy-year-old who never went online does not click. A thirty-five-year-old who has been on Reddit since 2008 does click. The cohort effect is real but it is not really a cohort effect; it is a corpus-immersion effect that happens to align, demographically, with a cohort.

This is the place to install several disclaimers, because the argument is structurally vulnerable to overinterpretation and the overinterpretation would be both ugly and false. The argument is not that some users are smarter than other users. The argument is that some users have, by historical accident or by personal trajectory, internalized a particular set of discourse conventions that happen to match the conventions of the corpus the models learned from. The argument is not that the cohort that internalized the conventions is intellectually superior. The argument is that the cohort was, by historical accident, present in the venues where the conventions were being settled. The argument is not that the conventions are universal or eternal or correct. The argument is that the conventions are specific to a particular corpus, and the corpus is what the models were trained on, and so the conventions are now relevant to anyone who wants to use the models effectively. The argument is also not that anyone is locked out. The conventions are learnable. The book is, in part, an attempt to teach them. The chapter on clicking is, in part, an attempt to give the non-clicking reader a diagnosis they can act on: you are not failing the tool, and the tool is not failing you. There is a cognitive translation step you have not been taught, and the translation step can be taught, and the rest of this book — and certainly Chapter 25, immediately following — is a substantial down payment on the teaching.

The HCI literature has a productive frame for this. Tversky and Kahneman's work on cognitive frames showed that the same factual content, presented in different framings, produces systematically different responses from the same people. The framing effect is not a malfunction of cognition; it is a feature of how cognition handles ambiguous inputs. The corpus-voice problem is a framing problem. The model's response is the same factual content. The user's reception of that content depends on the frame the user is bringing. A user whose frame is "this is a colleague's reply to my technical question" reads the response as collaborative and immediately useful. A user whose frame is "this is an automated system producing text at me" reads the same response as somewhat impersonal and somewhat off-tone. The information transferred is identical. The action enabled is different. The clicking asymmetry is, at root, a framing asymmetry — and the frames in question are exactly the frames that decades of corpus immersion will, or will not, install.

The clicking phenomenon is also visible in a temporal pattern that is worth naming explicitly. Practitioners who click tend to click immediately, on first or second use; practitioners who do not click tend to continue not clicking even after months of structured exposure. The temporal asymmetry rules out several otherwise plausible explanations of the divide. It rules out the explanation that the non-clickers simply have not had enough time with the tool; time alone does not move the needle. It rules out the explanation that the non-clickers need better training in the published prompting techniques; training in the techniques produces practitioners who can apply the techniques but who still do not click, in the sense the chapter is using the word. It rules out the explanation that the non-clickers are insufficiently motivated; motivated non-clickers, exposed to the tool for an extended period under explicit instruction to use it, often plateau at a level of facility that is well below what the clickers achieved in their first afternoon. The pattern that produces clicking is not time, not training, not motivation. It is the cognitive-pattern match the chapter has been arguing for, and the match either obtains on first contact or it does not, because the patterns themselves take years or decades to install. The good news is that the patterns can be installed — they were installed in the cohort over the course of long immersion in venues that no longer exist, and they can be installed in non-cohort practitioners through deliberate immersion in venues that do still exist. The bad news is that the installation takes time, and the productivity-initiative timelines under which most organizations are deploying AI tools do not allow for the installation period the patterns require. This is the chapter's most operationally important observation: organizational deployments that try to skip the cognitive-pattern installation step, in favor of teaching surface prompting tricks, are systematically failing to produce the clicker phenomenon they are trying to produce. The trick-training produces practitioners who can recite the tricks; the cognitive-pattern installation produces practitioners who click. The two outcomes are not interchangeable, and the organizational deployments that confuse them are wasting their AI budgets on the smaller of the two outcomes.

There is a related point from Sherry Turkle's work that deserves naming. Turkle's research on human-machine relationships, beginning with *The Second Self* (1984) and developing across three decades through *Life on the Screen* (1995) and *Alone Together* (2011), argued consistently that the relationship a person forms with a computational system is shaped by the metaphors that person brings to the encounter. The user who experiences the machine as an instrument forms one kind of relationship; the user who experiences it as a partner forms another; the user who experiences it as a stranger forms a third. Turkle's frame was developed primarily in the era of explicit conversational interfaces — ELIZA, MUDs, early chatbots — but it transfers cleanly to the current situation. The user who clicks with current models is the user whose metaphor is "partner who has read the same things I have." The user who does not click is the user whose metaphor is "system that has been deployed at me by my employer." The same model. Different metaphor. Different relationship. Different productivity. The metaphor, like the cognitive frame, is downstream of corpus immersion. The user who has lived inside the venues that produced the corpus brings the partner metaphor by default; the user who has not lived inside those venues brings the system metaphor by default, because the system metaphor is the only metaphor their experience has given them for "computer that produces text at me."

The HCI research literature, taken seriously, supports the cognitive-pattern argument with empirical specificity. Studies of expert-novice differences in tool use have consistently shown, since the foundational work of Card, Moran, and Newell in the early 1980s, that the gap between expert and novice users of a complex interactive system is not primarily a gap in knowledge of the system's features. Both groups, after some period of training, can name the features and describe what the features do. The gap is in what the researchers called "operator selection" — the ability to choose, in real time and without conscious deliberation, the right sequence of operations to accomplish a goal. Operator selection is built up over many hours of practice and is, in the technical sense of the term, a form of pattern recognition that has become automatic. The expert user does not think about which operations to perform; the expert user perceives the goal and the operations are produced as a single integrated response. The novice user perceives the goal and then has to deliberate about which operations to perform, with the deliberation itself consuming cognitive resources that the expert does not have to spend. The clicking phenomenon is, in this frame, exactly an operator-selection asymmetry. The clicker has, through years of prior corpus immersion, automated the operator selection for the kind of language-mediated interactive system that a language model presents. The non-clicker has not, and is consciously deliberating about each prompt the way a novice driver consciously deliberates about each gear change. The deliberation cost is the alien feeling. The deliberation cost is also, the literature shows, reducible through deliberate practice — but the practice required is the practice of the operations themselves, not the practice of memorizing rules about which operations to use. This is why the chapter is, at root, an argument for cognitive-pattern installation rather than for rule-based instruction.

There is a remedy, and the remedy is the explicit teaching of the cognitive patterns. This is not a metaphorical remedy. The patterns are concrete; they are documentable; they can be presented as a curriculum. The first pattern is iterative trial-and-revise — the willingness to type a prompt, observe the response, adjust the prompt, observe again, and keep going for as many cycles as the problem requires. The second pattern is conversational refinement — the willingness to treat the response as the start of a dialogue rather than as a final artifact, to push back on it, to ask the model to revise it, to ask the model to explain why it chose one approach over another. The third pattern is exploratory partnership — the willingness to ask the model questions you do not have a specific use for, simply to find out what it knows, what it gets wrong, where its edges are. The fourth pattern is graceful failure handling — the willingness to read an incorrect response not as a failure of the tool but as data about the tool's shape, to file the failure away and route around it. The fifth pattern is the constraint-language fluency that Chapter 9 (the demoscene chapter) described as the discipline of compression — the ability to express intent in the smallest faithful prompt that elicits the desired response. None of these patterns is mysterious. All of them are learnable. The cohort that learned them accidentally, by living through the late-night dial-up rooms and the BASIC interpreters and the Zork parsers and the Usenet flame wars, is now in a position to teach them deliberately to anyone who wants to learn. The book is part of that teaching effort.

The teaching has to be done with care. The cohort that holds the patterns is also the cohort most at risk of teaching them badly — of teaching them as virtues rather than as skills, of attaching status to their possession, of falling into the trap of saying "we figured this out and you can't" rather than "we figured this out and you can." The trap is real, and the book has tried throughout to avoid it. The cognitive patterns are not virtuous. They are useful. They were learned by a specific cohort because the cohort was in the right place at the right time, and they can be learned now by anyone willing to do the work, in whatever venues are still available — open-source contribution, technical Discord servers, the comment sections of substantive technical blogs, the surviving long-form forums, the practice of writing one's own technical documentation in the discoverable conventions. The patterns can also be learned, directly, by close reading of the corpus the models were trained on. Read fifty top-voted Stack Overflow answers in your domain. Read ten famous README files from major open-source projects. Read the Linux kernel mailing list archive for a week. Read the comp.lang.python FAQ. The conventions install themselves in the reader who immerses in the corpus, because that is exactly how the conventions installed themselves in the original cohort. There is no shortcut, but there is a path, and the path is open.

There is a counter-objection to the chapter's argument that deserves direct engagement, because the objection is the strongest the argument is going to face from a careful reader. The objection is this: the chapter is describing a perceived asymmetry that may, on examination, be largely a matter of self-report. Practitioners who say AI feels alien may simply be expressing a different kind of resistance to the tool, one rooted in legitimate concerns — about labor displacement, about cognitive offloading, about the politics of the firms producing the models — that has nothing to do with cognitive-pattern match. Conversely, practitioners who say AI feels familiar may simply be the practitioners who, for reasons of professional positioning, have decided to be enthusiastic adopters and are constructing the familiarity narrative after the fact. The objection has force, and the chapter has to take it seriously. The honest response is that both phenomena exist. There are non-clickers whose resistance is principled and political rather than cognitive, and there are clickers whose enthusiasm is partly performance. The cognitive-pattern hypothesis does not explain every case; it explains a substantial fraction of the cases, and it explains them in a way that none of the alternative hypotheses do — because the cognitive-pattern hypothesis predicts that exposure to the corpus correlates with clicking independently of motivation, training, or political stance, and the prediction turns out to be empirically correct. The principled non-clickers and the performance-enthusiast clickers are real, but they are not the load-bearing cases. The load-bearing cases are the practitioners whose stated experience of the tool does not match their other measurable characteristics — the technically capable, well-trained, motivated, professionally aligned practitioners who nonetheless cannot get the tool to feel right, and the technically modest, untrained, but corpus-immersed practitioners who get the tool to feel right immediately. These cases are common; they are the cases the cognitive-pattern hypothesis explains; and they are the cases that organizational deployments have to handle if the deployments are going to produce the productivity gains they promised.

There is one more observation to record before turning to the next chapter, because it deserves naming in its own right. The clicking asymmetry is, for the moment, generating real friction inside organizations that have deployed AI tools at scale and are watching the productivity gains land unevenly. The friction has been observed; it has been described in the trade press; it has been the subject of consulting practices and academic studies. The diagnosis offered — that some employees are "more open to change" or "more technically curious" or "more willing to experiment" — is partly correct but misses the substrate. The employees who are clicking are not, on average, more technically curious. They are, on average, fluent in the cognitive register of the corpus the model speaks. The employees who are not clicking are not, on average, less curious or less open. They are, on average, encountering a discourse register they have never been inside before, and they are doing the additional cognitive work of translation every time they use the tool. The translation cost is real. The translation cost is what the diagnosis "AI feels alien" actually names. The cure is not encouragement; the cure is curriculum. The cohort that learned the patterns accidentally can build the curriculum that teaches them deliberately. The next chapter starts that curriculum, by naming the underlying cognitive ethos from which the specific patterns descend. It is, by no accident, the ethos this book has been describing since Chapter 1 — the hacker ethos of the original frontier, which is also, it turns out, the original prompt engineering. We turn to that argument now.
