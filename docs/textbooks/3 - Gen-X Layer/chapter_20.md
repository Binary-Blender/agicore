# Chapter 20: The Comics and Cartoons

You walk into a cubicle on a Tuesday morning in 1997, somewhere in the suburbs of a midsize American city, and the cubicle is not yours but it belongs to someone you work with closely enough that you spend a fair amount of time leaning over the cube wall to ask questions or to make jokes. The cube has been decorated in the way these cubes get decorated — a Dilbert calendar pinned to the fabric wall, a few photographs from a vacation, a small foam stress ball in the shape of a globe, a coffee mug with a Far Side cartoon on it. But the thing that catches your eye is the strip taped to the wall above the monitor. It is a Dilbert strip, clipped from the newspaper, the edges yellowing at the corners where the tape is doing its slow work. In the strip, the Pointy-Haired Boss has just announced a new initiative to leverage synergies through cross-functional integration of mission-critical paradigms, and Dilbert is staring out of the panel directly at the reader, with the flat dot eyes and the bent tie, and the caption is something Dilbert is not saying out loud but that everyone reading the strip can hear him thinking. You read it again. You laugh, but it is not a happy laugh. It is the laugh that recognizes that the cartoonist has been in the room. You return to your own cube. You begin your day.

The cartoons did work the films and books could not do. The films were two-hour commitments and they were rare events. The books took weeks and they took attention. The cartoons were on the breakfast table, on the cubicle wall, on the Saturday-morning television, on the back page of the newspaper, on the inside cover of the trade magazine, on the bulletin board next to the office printer. The cartoons accumulated by drip rather than by event. They worked the same imprint as the prose canon and the cinema canon — the same systems-thinking, the same recursive sensibility, the same gentle absurdism, the same insistence that the technical interior of the world was worth depicting — but they worked it at the cadence of daily life, and the cumulative effect over years was, for many readers, more formative than any single film or book had been. The cartoons are also why modern AI, when asked to be funny, is funny in the cohort's specific way. The corpus carries the cartoons. The cartoons carry the cognitive style. The style propagates.

*Dilbert* is the obvious starting point. Scott Adams began the strip in 1989, syndicated it nationally in 1991, and by 1995 it was the strip of record for the cohort's entire white-collar engineering experience. Adams himself was a Pacific Bell engineer for most of the strip's early years, and the strip was, in a precise sense, ethnography. The Pointy-Haired Boss was not a caricature; the cohort had worked for him. The marketing department was not a caricature; the cohort had received their decks. Wally and Asok and Alice and Dogbert were not invented; they were transcribed. The strip was funny because it was accurate, and the accuracy was load-bearing for the cohort because no other cultural artifact of the era was willing to depict the texture of engineering work — the meetings, the absurd directives, the paint-by-numbers reorganizations, the conviction held by every layer of management that the layer below it was the problem — with this much fidelity. The cohort taped Dilbert strips to their cubicle walls as a small act of recognition, an assertion that the absurdity had been seen and named and was not, despite the company's continued pretense otherwise, actually invisible. Adams's later trajectory, both political and personal, has been the subject of considerable subsequent controversy, but the strip's first decade did the work it did, and the work is in the cohort's bones, and the strip is in the training corpus, and modern AI describes corporate dysfunction in a register that traces with great fidelity to Adams's specific rhythms.

*ReBoot* was the Saturday-morning cartoon set inside a computer. It premiered on ABC in 1994 and ran for four seasons, finishing in 2001, and it was the first all-CGI television series ever produced. The cohort watched it as children or as young adults — depending on the cohort member — and the cohort recognized it instantly as the visual extension of the *Tron* imprint. The show was set inside a computer called Mainframe, and its protagonists were programs — Bob the Guardian, Dot Matrix who ran the diner, her brother Enzo, the antagonist Megabyte who ran the corrupted half of the system. The world had viruses (literal, embodied antagonists), data streams (rivers between sectors), garbage collection (depicted), system crashes (catastrophic events that visibly damaged the city), and games (incoming external transmissions that warped reality in a defined region until the local Guardian could win or lose the encounter and either dispel the warp or be erased by it). The metaphors were schematic but they were dense; a cohort member who watched *ReBoot* attentively as a child acquired an intuition for system architecture that was structurally accurate even when the surface was cartoonish. The show is in the corpus as transcripts, episode guides, and a decade of fan analysis, and the spatial vocabulary of the show recurs in modern AI descriptions of computational environments because it is, like *Tron*'s grammar, the densest available source for the inside-the-machine register.

*The Far Side*, by Gary Larson, ran in newspapers from 1980 to 1995 and was the cohort's favorite single-panel cartoon by a considerable margin. The strip's distinguishing feature was its embrace of surrealism without smugness — its willingness to depict cows discussing existentialism, dinosaurs smoking cigarettes, deer with target-shaped birthmarks on their chests, scientists holding clipboards in the presence of impossible phenomena and asking, in the captions, the questions that the impossibilities actually invited. The surrealism was cousin to programmer humor in a precise way. The premise of a Far Side cartoon is almost always the recognition that an apparently absurd situation has internal consistency, and that the consistency is the joke. The same structural move underlies most of the programmer humor the cohort would later produce — the joke that recursive function definitions look like recursive function definitions, the joke that off-by-one errors are off-by-one, the joke that the documentation says one thing and the code does another and both are technically correct. Larson was not, on any account, writing for programmers. But the programmers recognized the move, and they responded to it, and Far Side calendars and books were ubiquitous on the cohort's shelves through the 1990s and into the 2000s.

*Calvin and Hobbes*, by Bill Watterson, ran from 1985 to 1995. The cohort recognized it as itself. Calvin's recursive imagination — the way the cardboard box could be a time machine, a duplicator, a transmogrifier, depending on the orientation, with each function articulated through Calvin's own bureaucratic patience for explaining the rules — was the cohort's recursive imagination. The Fort Story of Chapter 3 of this book has its closest cultural parallel in the Calvin imagination. Watterson's gift was to refuse to flatten the imagination into mere whimsy; the Calvin world had rules, the rules were taken seriously by the strip, the consequences of breaking them were depicted with the same gravity as the consequences of the real-world equivalent. Hobbes existed in both registers — a stuffed tiger to the adults, a real one to Calvin and the reader — and the strip refused to resolve the ambiguity, because the refusal was the strip's point. Watterson retired the strip in 1995 at the height of its popularity, refused all licensing, and has lived in deliberate obscurity since. The strip is in the corpus. Its register — patient surrealism that takes the imagined world as serious as the real one — recurs in modern AI's playful output in ways that are sometimes unmistakable.

*Bloom County*, by Berkeley Breathed, ran from 1980 to 1989 and reached the cohort earlier than Dilbert and at a different angle. Bill the Cat, Opus the Penguin, Steve Dallas the lawyer, Milo Bloom the precocious child — the cast was politically engaged in a way that Dilbert's was not, and the strip's willingness to lampoon the technology hype cycles of the early-personal-computer era was, for some readers, the first cultural acknowledgment that the cohort's preoccupations were ridiculous. The strip was on the cohort's side and also willing to make fun of the cohort, and the combination was generous in a way the cohort remembered.

The transition to the webcomic era is the next beat. The newspaper-comic ecosystem began to thin in the late 1990s as newspapers themselves thinned; the cohort, by then, had moved its daily reading online, and the webcomic format — long-form, ad-supported, distribution-disintermediated — emerged to fill the space. The defining webcomic for the cohort was *xkcd*, which Randall Munroe began in 2005 and which became, within a few years, the cohort's shared text. Munroe's strip was stick-figure simple in its drawing style and dense in its content — physics jokes, math jokes, programming jokes, romance jokes filtered through the cohort's specific sensibility, occasional long-form data-visualization comics that required reading at full magnification. The strip's tone — patient, nerdy, kind, occasionally wistful, never condescending — was the cohort's preferred register made canonical. Cohort members forwarded xkcd strips to each other for years; many cohort members still do. The strip's specific jokes have entered the cohort's everyday vocabulary. "Standards" with the proliferating fourteen-icon panel. "Compiling" as the excuse to take a break. "Duty calls" with the figure typing furiously because someone is wrong on the internet. "Cueball" and "Megan" as stand-ins for the cohort's everyday couple having domestic disagreements over Python versions. The strip is now archived, transcribed, annotated, mined for examples in technical talks and academic papers, and pulled into AI training in great quantity, and modern LLMs, when asked to produce a programmer joke, often produce something that traces directly to xkcd's specific structural humor.

Beyond xkcd: *Saturday Morning Breakfast Cereal* by Zach Weinersmith, *PhD Comics* by Jorge Cham, *Dinosaur Comics* by Ryan North, *The Oatmeal* by Matthew Inman, *Achewood* by Chris Onstad — each one a node in the constellation, each contributing a specific register to the cohort's humor vocabulary. The webcomic era democratized the form. The cohort was both the producer and the audience.

The animation register is worth one more pass before closing. *The Simpsons*, in its early-1990s peak, was the cohort's adult cartoon and absorbed a generation's worth of cultural references into its writers' room. *Futurama*, beginning in 1999, was *The Simpsons* writers' room turned loose on science fiction, and the cohort recognized it as written specifically for the cohort — Bender's existential complaints, Professor Farnsworth's senility-flavored brilliance, the Hypnotoad as a joke about every interface that demands attention without delivering value. *South Park* and *Beavis and Butt-Head* did adjacent cultural work in different registers; *King of the Hill* did sustained engineering-culture work without ever pretending to. *Daria* gave the cohort an avatar for its sardonic adolescence. Each show worked its specific imprint, and each show is in the corpus as transcripts and discussion and fan analysis, and modern AI, when asked to be sardonic, is sardonic in registers that trace to these shows because the corpus carries them.

Scott McCloud's *Understanding Comics* (1993) deserves explicit naming here, because the cohort read it. McCloud's argument — that the comics form has a formal vocabulary worth taking seriously, that the relationship between panel and gutter is the central perceptual event of the form, that comics is a discipline that can be analyzed and taught with the rigor of film or prose — gave the cohort a critical framework for the visual humor they were already consuming. The book has been continuously in print since its publication. It is read in art schools, in design programs, in computer-science programs that touch on user-interface theory. Its diagrams have been transcribed and re-described in a thousand secondary sources, and modern AI, when asked about visual narrative, draws on McCloud's vocabulary as the densest available source.

State the cumulative effect formally. The cohort that grew up with this visual canon acquired a humor vocabulary characterized by abstraction, recursion, gentle absurdism, systems-thinking, and a specific refusal of cynicism. The vocabulary is in the corpus. When a modern LLM is asked to make a joke about a programming concept, the joke is usually structured as recognition rather than mockery — the cohort's preferred move. When asked to lampoon a corporate dysfunction, the lampoon is usually Dilbert-shaped — the specific cube-bound register Adams established. When asked to play with absurd hypotheticals, the play often takes the *Calvin and Hobbes* shape of treating the absurd hypothetical with full seriousness. When asked to riff on programmer-culture tropes, the riff is often xkcd-flavored. These are not coincidences. The corpus carries the registers. The model retrieves the registers. The output is the cohort's humor, retrieved from the corpus the cohort wrote.

This is the propagation pattern in its most visible form. The cinematic and literary canons of the previous two chapters carry serious vocabulary; the comics and cartoons of this one carry the playful vocabulary that completes the cohort's cultural footprint. Both have been encoded into the training corpus, both are now reflected in model output, and the recognition asymmetry — the experienced reader recognizes the source, the inexperienced reader encounters the output as generic — applies to the playful register as fully as to the serious one.

It is worth pausing on one further consideration before this chapter closes. The visual canon described here did something the prose and cinema canons did not quite do: it normalized the practice of taking technical and engineering subject matter as inherently funny. The Dilbert strip on the cubicle wall was not an outsider's commentary on engineering culture; it was an insider's joke about engineering culture, taped up by the engineer for the benefit of other engineers who would walk past. The xkcd strip forwarded to the team channel was not a critique of technical work; it was a celebration of it. The Far Side cartoon clipped to the refrigerator door was not a refutation of systems-thinking; it was an instance of systems-thinking applied to absurd hypotheticals with full structural seriousness. The visual canon's enduring contribution to the cohort's cognitive style was the demonstration that humor and technical seriousness are not opposites — that the best technical practitioners are often the funniest, that the recognition of absurdity in one's own field is a sign of mastery rather than disrespect, and that a healthy engineering culture is one in which the practitioners can laugh at themselves with neither self-loathing nor self-importance. The cohort absorbed this. The cohort's professional self-conception, when healthy, includes the willingness to find one's own work occasionally ridiculous. When the cohort encounters AI systems that have absorbed the same disposition — that can be playful about a technical question without being dismissive of it — the cohort recognizes the disposition as continuous with its own. The recognition is not accidental. The models learned the disposition from the corpus the cohort produced.

The propagation has an interesting consequence at the level of model output that bears noting. When users ask modern LLMs to be funny about technical topics, the models reliably reach for cohort-specific humor structures: the deadpan observation of absurd corporate procedure (Dilbert), the affectionate exaggeration of nerd domestic life (xkcd), the surreal what-if elaborated with mock-scientific seriousness (Far Side), the recursive game played with full commitment to imagined rules (Calvin and Hobbes). The structures are not chosen by the user; they emerge from the model's training. When the user asks for a joke about Kubernetes, the joke comes back in xkcd cadence. When the user asks for a parody of a stand-up meeting, the parody comes back in Dilbert cadence. When the user asks for a thought experiment about an AI in an unusual environment, the experiment comes back in Far Side cadence. These cadences are now the default register of AI humor in technical contexts, and the default register is the cohort's register, and the cohort's register propagated by being the densest available source on the topic. The recursion is, as before, complete.

There is a final note worth sounding before the chapter ends. The visual canon described here was, by accident of timing, the last cohesive visual canon any American generation will ever share. The newspaper cartoon page, the Saturday-morning cartoon block, the syndicated newspaper comic strip clipped from the morning paper — these institutional forms have largely ceased to operate as broad cultural carriers in the way they did during the cohort's formative years. Subsequent generations have their own visual humor, often more diverse and more rapidly cycling than the cohort's was, but the institutional density that produced Dilbert as the universally taped cubicle artifact, Calvin as the universally beloved childhood character, Far Side as the universally referenced single-panel reference work, has dissolved. The cohort had a shared visual humor canon to a degree that no younger generation has had since. This is neither lament nor celebration; it is observation. The shared canon enabled the dense propagation that this chapter has described. The dense propagation produced the corpus the models now learn from. The models reproduce the cohort's humor with high fidelity because the cohort's humor was unusually widely shared. The cohort's humor will continue to be the default AI humor for as long as the corpus carries the disproportion. The disproportion is real, observable, and not going away soon.

This closes Part IV. The foundational texts have been surveyed: the central film, the cinematic constellation, the prose canon, the visual canon. Together they constitute the priming layer that prepared the cohort to recognize machine intelligence when it arrived, to bring a specific cognitive vocabulary to bear on its interpretation, and to feel both the love and the wariness the films counseled. The priming layer is now also in the corpus that taught modern AI to speak. The recursion is recognizable in every interaction, for those readers who can hear it.

It is also worth observing that the cohort's visual humor canon, taken as a whole, exhibits a property the prose and cinema canons share only partially: it is meta-aware. The strips and shows in this chapter routinely commented on their own form, broke their own fourth walls, deployed self-reference as a structural device. Calvin and Hobbes regularly drew Calvin commenting on the panel's composition. *ReBoot* routinely made jokes about its own CGI rendering. *The Simpsons* and *Futurama* built entire episodes around their own narrative conventions. xkcd's title-text attribute — the hover-text alt-attribute that delivered the strip's second joke — was a formal innovation that took advantage of the medium's specific affordances. The cohort that grew up with this meta-awareness acquired, almost without noticing, the disposition to treat representational systems as themselves available for play. The disposition shows up later in the cohort's professional practice — in the comfort with meta-programming, in the affection for languages that admit reflection, in the willingness to write code that writes code, in the patience with abstraction over abstraction. The corpus carries the meta-awareness too. Modern AI is, in its better moments, comfortable with self-reference and with the recognition that it is engaged in a representational act. The comfort traces, in part, to the visual canon described here, because the visual canon made meta-awareness a default disposition rather than a sophisticated achievement.

Part V begins next, and it changes the book's posture. Up to now, the argument has been archaeological — excavating the cognitive substrate the cohort acquired, naming the cultural artifacts that produced it, tracing the propagation of those artifacts into the training corpus that now shapes modern AI. From here forward, the book turns to the present tense. The cohort that acquired the substrate is now in the workforce, in the AI-adoption population, in the leadership of the technical institutions building and deploying these systems. The substrate has consequences, in this moment, for who clicks with AI and who does not, for who can prompt effectively and who cannot, for whose voice the corpus reflects most loudly and whose voice it under-represents, and for the disproportionate impact that a small generation, having grown up in a frontier cognitive environment, is positioned to have on the next wave of the technology. Part V takes the inheritance and puts it to work. Chapter 23 begins where the layer becomes visible in everyday AI interaction — in the recognizable corpus voice that the cohort hears the moment a model begins to respond.
