# Chapter 25: The Hacker Ethos in Prompt Engineering

You are trying to get the model to produce a particular kind of summary. You have a long document — a contract, or a research paper, or a technical specification — and you need a one-paragraph précis that preserves the document's load-bearing claims without flattening its qualifications. You write the obvious prompt: "Summarize the following document in one paragraph." The model produces a competent paragraph that loses every interesting thing about the document. You revise: "Summarize the following document in one paragraph, preserving the qualifications attached to the central claims." Better, but the qualifications come back in a generic register that has nothing to do with the document's own voice. You revise again: "Summarize the following document in one paragraph, preserving the qualifications attached to the central claims, in the voice of the document's author." Closer. Still not right — the model is approximating the voice, not reading it. You try the thing you should not have to try, the thing that should not work: "Summarize the following document in one paragraph. First, in two sentences, describe the author's characteristic prose tics — specific words they overuse, specific sentence-shapes they favor, specific qualifiers they reach for under pressure. Then write the summary in that voice." The model does it. The model identifies three tics correctly. The summary is, suddenly, a real précis of a real document. You sit back, because the move that worked was the move you would have used on a finicky BASIC interpreter in 1985 to get it to do something its manual did not document — you decomposed the task into a procedure the system could execute, you front-loaded a small piece of explicit reasoning the system would otherwise have skipped, and you let the system show its work before producing the artifact you wanted. The phrase "let's see if we can get it working" surfaces, unbidden. It is the phrase you have used your entire working life. It is older than the field that now uses it. You have just done prompt engineering by doing what the hacker ethos has always done, and the hacker ethos has been the prompt engineering all along.

This chapter develops the structural argument that what the AI industry has begun to call prompt engineering is, in cognitive content, identical to the original hacker ethos as described in the foundational texts — Steven Levy's *Hackers* (1984), Eric S. Raymond's "How to Become a Hacker" essay (1996, revised through the 2000s), Pekka Himanen's *The Hacker Ethic* (2001). The argument has three steps. First, the hacker ethos is not a set of personality traits but a set of cognitive moves — specific operations a practitioner performs on an unfamiliar system to make it yield to intent. Second, prompt engineering, when it works, is the same set of cognitive moves performed on a different substrate — a language model rather than a BASIC interpreter or a Unix shell. Third, this identification is not metaphorical or analogical; it is structural. The practitioner who has internalized the hacker ethos is, by that fact, already a prompt engineer; the practitioner who has not internalized the ethos is being asked to invent it from scratch under the time pressure of a quarterly productivity initiative, which is why the inventing goes badly for so many people. The chapter walks through five canonical hacker moves and shows their direct prompt-engineering analogues, and then closes with the pedagogical implication: prompt engineering as a discipline can be taught more rigorously by teaching the underlying ethos than by teaching the surface tricks.

The first canonical hacker move is "let's see if we can get it working." The phrase is older than the field. It surfaces in Levy's interviews with the original MIT Tech Model Railroad Club hackers in the late 1950s, who used it in almost exactly the form contemporary engineers still use it. The move is structural. The practitioner encounters a system, has a goal that requires the system to do something it has not previously been observed to do, and proposes — to themselves and to whoever else is in the room — that the question of whether the goal can be reached is empirically open. The proposal contains an implicit theory: that the system has more in it than its documentation describes, and that the way to find out what is in it is to try things. The cognitive shape of "let's see if we can get it working" is anti-deference toward the published specification, optimistic toward the unobserved capability, and tolerant of the iterative process of finding out. The same cognitive shape, translated to prompts, produces the practitioner who does not assume the model will refuse a request before trying it, who phrases the request in the most direct form first, who treats a refusal or a poor response as one data point rather than as a verdict, and who continues to iterate until the system reveals what it can actually do. The opposite cognitive shape — call it the deference-to-published-capability shape — produces the practitioner who reads the model's terms of service, decides in advance what the model can and cannot do, and never asks it to do anything outside that envelope. The deference practitioner gets the published capability and nothing more. The hacker-ethos practitioner gets the actual capability, which is often three or four standard deviations above the published one.

The second canonical hacker move is "try the obvious thing first." This is one of the most badly misunderstood elements of the ethos, often confused with cleverness, when in fact it is the opposite. The hacker tradition has always insisted that before trying anything elaborate, the practitioner should try the simplest, most direct, most documented thing — the thing the system was clearly designed to handle. The reason is empirical: most problems are simple, and the elaborate approach to a simple problem produces more failure modes than the simple approach does. The move appears in Raymond's "Hacker Howto" as the discipline of "applying the principle of least surprise to yourself," and in the early Unix lore as the convention of "RTFM" — read the manual — before asking for help. The prompt-engineering analogue is precise. Before constructing an elaborate prompt with system roles, few-shot examples, chain-of-thought scaffolding, and structured output formats, try asking the model what you actually want in plain English. The plain-English request will, in a striking percentage of cases, produce exactly the result the elaborate prompt was designed to produce, and it will do so with fewer failure modes. The elaborate prompt is the right move when the obvious thing fails. The elaborate prompt is the wrong move as the first attempt, because it introduces unnecessary structure that often constrains the model away from the answer it would have given anyway. The practitioner who has internalized "try the obvious thing first" arrives at the elaborate prompt only after the obvious prompt has failed. The practitioner who has not internalized the rule arrives at the elaborate prompt by default and then cannot understand why their elaborate prompt is producing worse output than a colleague's plain-English request.

The third canonical hacker move is "RTFM, but then experiment past the manual." This is the move that distinguishes the hacker ethos from both the deference culture and the cowboy culture. The deference culture reads the manual and stops. The cowboy culture skips the manual entirely. The hacker reads the manual carefully, internalizes what it documents, then experiments to find the things the manual does not document — the undocumented behaviors, the edge cases, the emergent capabilities that the system's authors did not anticipate or did not bother to write down. The move is structural because it acknowledges both that the manual is necessary information and that the manual is incomplete information. Levy describes the original MIT hackers spending hours reading PDP-1 reference cards and then spending many more hours probing the PDP-1 for behaviors the reference cards did not mention; the cards were necessary but not sufficient. The prompt-engineering analogue is the practitioner who has read the model's published documentation (the system card, the prompting guide, the model behavior notes), has internalized what the documentation describes, and then experiments to find the model's actual behavior — which is, predictably, more capable in some directions than the documentation admits, less capable in others, and unevenly distributed across domains in ways the documentation does not map. The model has an enormous undocumented surface. The practitioner who maps that surface through experimentation arrives at a working model of the system that the documentation alone could never provide. This is a hacker move. It has always been a hacker move. It is now the central move of effective prompt engineering, and the practitioners who do it well are the practitioners who learned to do it on smaller systems decades ago.

The fourth canonical hacker move is "if it fails, try the thing that shouldn't work." This is the move that looks like superstition from the outside and is, in fact, the most rigorous of the hacker moves from the inside. The premise is that the practitioner's mental model of the system is necessarily incomplete, that the system's actual behavior may diverge from the mental model in ways the practitioner cannot predict, and that the failures the practitioner is observing are evidence of one such divergence. Given the divergence, the right next move is to try something the mental model says will not work — because if the mental model is wrong in the direction the failures suggest, the thing the mental model rejects may be the thing that actually works. The move is documented throughout the hacker literature, from the early days of trying to make programs run on hardware that "couldn't" run them (the demoscene tradition described in Chapter 9 is one extended exercise in this move), through the BSD networking code's pragmatic acceptance of behaviors that should not have worked but did, through the modern open-source culture's willingness to merge a patch that fixes a bug nobody fully understands. The prompt-engineering analogue is the practitioner who, after an elaborate prompt has failed, tries a less elaborate prompt; who, after a more specific prompt has failed, tries a more general prompt; who, after a structured-output request has failed, tries an unstructured one; who tries asking the model the opposite of the thing the documentation says to ask; who tries the prompt that everyone on the team has agreed is too naive to work. A surprising fraction of the time, the move that should not work is the move that works. The practitioner who has internalized the hacker ethos tries it as a matter of course. The practitioner who has not internalized the ethos cannot bring themselves to try it, because trying it feels embarrassing — and the embarrassment cost is, in fact, the price of admission to the move's effectiveness.

The fifth canonical hacker move is "treat the error message as data." Error messages have, in the hacker tradition, always been the most underused diagnostic instrument available to the practitioner. The non-hacker reads an error message as a verdict — the system has refused the request, the request must be revised or abandoned. The hacker reads an error message as data — the system has, by failing in a particular way, revealed something about its internal state that an examination of the request alone would not have revealed. The hacker reads the error message carefully, parses it for the specific component that produced it, looks up the component, traces the failure mode back to the request, and uses what the failure has revealed to construct a better request. Raymond's essays are full of this move; the comp.lang.* newsgroups of the 1990s were structured around it; the contemporary Stack Overflow answer that has been demonstrated in Chapter 23 is its modern apotheosis. The prompt-engineering analogue is the practitioner who, when the model produces a wrong or partial or hedged answer, does not throw the answer away but reads it carefully for the specific way in which it is wrong — because the way in which it is wrong is data about how the model is interpreting the prompt, and the data about the interpretation is what the next prompt needs to address. The wrong answer is not noise; it is signal about the model's reading of the request, and the signal is what tells the practitioner how to revise. This move is the hacker move that translates most directly to prompt engineering, and it is the move whose absence is most visible in the practitioners who struggle. The practitioner who treats the model's wrong answers as failures of the tool gives up too early. The practitioner who treats them as data refines toward the right answer in fewer cycles than the underlying probability would predict.

These five moves are not a complete enumeration of the hacker ethos, but they are the load-bearing ones, and they are the ones that translate most directly to effective work with language models. There are others — the cultural commitments to information sharing, to meritocracy of demonstrated work, to mistrust of authority that has not earned its credentials, to delight in elegant solutions — that the original ethos texts treat as central. Those cultural commitments shape the social context within which the cognitive moves are performed. The cognitive moves themselves, however, are the operative content. They are the part of the ethos that does the work, and they are the part that transfers directly into prompt practice. The cohort that internalized them in the 1980s and 1990s, by doing them on BASIC interpreters and Unix shells and BBS systems and early-web debugging consoles, is now in a position to perform them on language models — without conscious deliberation, with the fluency of long practice, in a way that produces results other practitioners experience as faster or more capable than their own.

There is a sixth move that the original list above does not name but that the hacker tradition has treated as the bridge between the cognitive moves and the social practice of the work. Call it "show your work and share it." The move is the one that connects the individual practitioner's iterative process to the larger collaborative culture from which the ethos emerged. The hacker who has just figured out a non-obvious thing — by trying the obvious thing first, by experimenting past the manual, by treating an error message as data, by trying the thing that should not work — does not keep the result to themselves. The hacker writes it up. The hacker writes it up in detail, with the failed approaches included, with the worked example, with the explanation of why the obvious approach did not work and why the eventual approach did. The hacker posts the write-up to the venue where other practitioners working on similar problems will find it: a newsgroup, a mailing list, a personal blog, a Stack Overflow answer, a project's documentation directory, a GitHub gist. The move is structural, and it is the move that converts individual cognitive effort into collective intellectual capital. The corpus this book has been describing is, in fact, the accumulated artifact of this move performed millions of times over forty years. The prompt-engineering analogue is not quite the same shape — the prompt practitioner is not usually publishing their prompts the way the open-source hacker published their patches — but the cognitive content is identical. The practitioner who shows their prompt-engineering work to colleagues, who explains why a particular prompt worked when an earlier prompt did not, who maintains a personal corpus of successful and unsuccessful prompts and the reasoning behind them, is doing the same cognitive operation the hacker tradition has been doing since the MIT Tech Model Railroad Club typed up their first hand-distributed manuals in the late 1950s. The move is what makes the cognitive work cumulative. Without the move, the cognitive work is wasted; with the move, the work compounds across practitioners and across time, and the compounding is what produces the disproportionate productivity gains the clicker cohort is experiencing right now.

The pedagogical implication is the chapter's most important claim, and it is the claim that should structure how prompt engineering is taught from this point forward. The current state of the practice is dominated by surface-level instruction: prompting tricks, formula templates, framework-specific incantations, prompt libraries to copy and paste. The surface-level instruction is not wrong, but it teaches the techniques without teaching the ethos that generates them. The result is practitioners who can apply the documented tricks but cannot improvise when the tricks fail. The deeper instruction would teach the underlying moves — try, observe, refine, try again; obvious before elaborate; read the documentation and then experiment past it; if it fails try the thing that should not work; treat errors as data — and then let the surface tricks emerge as the specific instantiations of those moves in the specific context of language-model prompting. A practitioner taught the ethos can derive the tricks. A practitioner taught only the tricks cannot derive the ethos, and cannot improvise outside the tricks they have been given. The first kind of practitioner is robust to change in the underlying model. The second kind is brittle, and will be obsolete the moment the model behavior shifts enough to invalidate the tricks they memorized. The first kind is, structurally, a hacker. The second kind is what the field gets when it tries to teach prompt engineering without the prerequisite ethos.

The argument also has a clarifying contrast with the formal-methods tradition in computer science, and the contrast is worth drawing because it sharpens what the hacker ethos is and what it is not. The formal-methods tradition — represented historically by figures such as Dijkstra, Hoare, and Wirth, and continued in modern guise by the type-theory and proof-assistant communities — operates on a different cognitive premise from the hacker ethos. The formal-methods premise is that the right way to make a system do what one wants is to specify the desired behavior precisely, prove that the specification is consistent, and then implement the specification with mechanical fidelity. The hacker premise is that the right way to make a system do what one wants is to try things and observe what happens, with the trying and observing themselves constituting the cognitive process by which understanding develops. The two premises are not, in the strongest possible reading, contradictory; one can imagine a practitioner who uses hacker methods to develop a working solution and then uses formal methods to verify and harden it. In practice, however, the two cognitive styles tend to be held by different practitioners and applied to different problems, and the contrast between them illuminates what the hacker ethos contributes. The formal-methods style works best in mature territories where the specification is well-defined and the cost of failure is high. The hacker style works best in frontier territories where the specification is unclear and the cost of any single failure is low. Current language models are, as Chapter 26 will argue at length, frontier territory by every available diagnostic. The cognitive style that fits the territory is the hacker style. The formal-methods style, applied to language-model interaction, produces practitioners who specify their prompts very precisely and then are surprised when the precise specification produces worse output than a colleague's casual exploratory request. The mismatch is not because formal methods are wrong; it is because formal methods are the wrong cognitive style for the territory the practitioner is working in. The territory is operationally hacker territory. The cognition that fits is hacker cognition. The book has been making variations of this argument since Chapter 2, and the formal-methods contrast is the version of the argument that lands cleanly with readers from the academic computer-science tradition.

This is not an argument that everyone who wants to use AI effectively needs to be remade in the image of a 1985 home-computer hobbyist. It is an argument that the cognitive content of the hacker ethos is teachable, and that teaching it is more durable than teaching surface tricks. It is also an argument that the cohort that already holds the ethos is positioned to teach it well, because the cohort holds it as embodied knowledge from forty years of practice, not as remembered principles from a textbook. The teaching can happen through deliberate curricula, through pairing in production work, through the kind of mentorship that the original hacker culture institutionalized as part of its normal operating procedure. The teaching will work, because the ethos itself is not gated by cohort membership — only by the willingness to do the kind of exploratory work that the ethos describes. Anyone who is willing to type a prompt, observe the response, adjust the prompt, observe again, and keep going until the answer is right is already practicing the ethos. The book's job, in this chapter, is to give them the vocabulary to recognize what they are practicing, the historical anchors that establish the practice as a serious discipline, and the permission to take their own iterative work seriously as the load-bearing skill it has always been.

The frontier feeling that the next chapter takes up is, in part, the feeling of practicing the ethos again at scale. The cohort that learned the ethos in the original frontier of the 1980s spent twenty years inside institutional substrates that did not reward it — that rewarded, instead, deference to process, conformance to framework, optimization within mature toolchains. The frontier has reopened. The ethos is operative again. The next chapter takes up that reopening directly, with a story about the cognitive collision between the ethos and the institutional substrate that for two decades had buried it. We turn to that argument now.
