# Chapter 36: The Coastal Foundation

You are standing, in the version of the building that exists in your imagination because you have read enough about it to construct the room from text, in a corridor on the second floor of Building 2 at Bell Labs Murray Hill, central New Jersey, late on a weekday evening in 1974 or thereabouts. The corridor is long and beige in the way institutional corridors of that period were uniformly beige — acoustic ceiling tiles overhead at a height calibrated to suppress reverberation, low-pile carpet underfoot in a color that the carpet catalogs of the era called "putty" or "fawn," fluorescent fixtures behind diffuser panels casting the kind of light that registers in memory as neither warm nor cold but simply present. The doors along the corridor are wood-veneered, each with a small brass numeral and a nameplate, and behind some of them the lights are still on. You pass an office and through the open door you see a man in a short-sleeved dress shirt at a Teletype, the printer chattering in the way Teletypes chattered, the floor around the chair littered with the accordion-folded fanfold of a long session. Two doors down a different man is at a chalkboard with another man, and the chalkboard has on it a diagram you cannot quite read but that has the unmistakable shape of a system being decomposed into modules. At the end of the corridor is the room they have started calling the Unix room — though officially it is just a lab — and through the open door you can see the PDP-11, the cabinets standing in the middle of the floor with their indicator lights blinking in the patient rhythm of a working machine, and two figures at terminals, one of whom you would later learn was Ken Thompson and the other Dennis Ritchie, though tonight they are simply two men working late, the way the people on this floor work late as a matter of routine, because the institution they work for has decided that the way to do research is to hire the best people in the world and then leave them alone for a decade. Somewhere down the hall, behind another door, Brian Kernighan is at a typewriter — an actual typewriter, the manuscript pages stacked beside it — drafting the book that will, in 1978, teach a generation how to write C. The phrase you keep hearing in the hallway, when you hear conversation at all, is a normal phrase that becomes strange only when you reflect on it later: *We'll just write a paper for next month's BSTJ.* Next month's *Bell System Technical Journal*, the in-house publication where Shannon had published *A Mathematical Theory of Communication* in 1948, where the transistor papers had appeared in 1948 and 1949, where the Unix paper would appear in 1974, where the entire postwar substrate of digital civilization had been documented, paper by paper, at a rate of contribution that no other institution in human history has ever matched. The men in this corridor are not aware that they are inside the most consequential R&D laboratory ever assembled by any society at any time. They are aware that they are doing work they like, with colleagues they respect, in a building that has, for the moment, been left alone by the parent corporation to do whatever it considers research. The carpet absorbs your footfall. The lights stay on. The transistor was invented one floor down and twenty-seven years ago. You leave by the side door, and the institutional weight of the building stays with you in a way that no other industrial-research site you will ever visit can match, because no other industrial-research site has ever been Bell Labs.

This chapter is about the East Coast tradition, and the chapter's center of gravity — load-bearing, unembarrassed, asserted at full weight before the supporting institutions get their treatment — is Bell Labs. Founded 1925 as a joint subsidiary of AT&T and Western Electric, headquartered first in lower Manhattan at 463 West Street and then, from 1941 onward, at the Murray Hill site that became synonymous with the name, Bell Labs at its peak from 1947 through 1984 was not one of many great industrial research laboratories. It was *the* great industrial research laboratory, and its output is on a scale that no comparable institution before or since has matched. The transistor was invented there in December 1947 by John Bardeen, Walter Brattain, and William Shockley, and three Nobel Prizes share that single discovery — the 1956 prize in physics awarded jointly to the three of them, which is the only Nobel Prize awarded for an invention that is now physically present, in some form, in essentially every powered object on Earth. Information theory was founded there in July 1948 when Claude Shannon, then thirty-two, published *A Mathematical Theory of Communication* in the *Bell System Technical Journal* — a paper whose mathematical apparatus underlies, without exaggeration, every modern communication system, every modern storage system, every modern compression algorithm, and every modern machine-learning loss function, because cross-entropy and KL divergence are Shannon's quantities and the field of machine learning is, at its mathematical foundation, an applied subdiscipline of information theory. The laser was patented there in 1958 by Arthur Schawlow and Charles Townes, and Townes shared the 1964 Nobel for it. The photovoltaic cell — the basic technology of solar power — was demonstrated there in 1954 by Daryl Chapin, Calvin Fuller, and Gerald Pearson. The charge-coupled device, the foundation of all digital imaging from the first digital cameras through the sensors in every smartphone on Earth, was invented there by Willard Boyle and George Smith in 1969, and the two men shared the 2009 Nobel for it. The Hamming codes, the first practical error-correcting codes whose descendants make all reliable digital storage and transmission possible, were developed there by Richard Hamming in 1950. The cellular telephone system was proposed there by Bell Labs engineers in 1947 and saw its first deployment in Chicago in 1983. The Voder speech synthesizer, the first machine to produce intelligible synthetic human speech, was demonstrated there at the 1939 World's Fair. The fundamental mathematics of digital signal processing — the algorithms that underlie every codec, every modem, every wireless protocol — was assembled there across decades by a series of researchers whose names are not famous but whose collective output sits underneath every digital signal you have ever encountered. Jon Gertner's *The Idea Factory: Bell Labs and the Great Age of American Innovation* (2012) is the load-bearing popular history, and Gertner's careful enumeration of these contributions — assembled into a narrative that makes their scale legible to a contemporary reader who did not live through the period — is the source any reader who has not yet read it should reach for next.

Unix and the C programming language are, for the purposes of this chapter's argument about cognitive substrate, the most directly relevant of the Bell Labs artifacts, and they deserve their own paragraph. Ken Thompson began the Unix project in 1969 on a discarded PDP-7 that had been retired from another group's project, after the Multics collaboration with MIT and General Electric had been wound down and Thompson had decided to build a smaller, simpler operating system on whatever hardware was lying around. Dennis Ritchie joined him quickly. By 1970 they had moved to the PDP-11. By 1972 Ritchie had designed the C programming language — designed it, specifically, so that Unix could be rewritten in C and thereby become portable to any machine that supported a C compiler, a design move whose consequences are difficult to overstate because every subsequent operating system written in a portable systems language descends from this decision. In 1974 Ritchie and Thompson published *The UNIX Time-Sharing System* in the *Bell System Technical Journal*, the paper that introduced Unix to the world outside Bell Labs. In 1978 Kernighan and Ritchie published *The C Programming Language*, the book that taught a generation of programmers how to write C and that established, by example, a register of technical prose — terse, declarative, peer-addressed, worked-example-centered, refusing to pad — that became the standard register of Unix documentation and, by inheritance, of the open-source documentation tradition that followed it. The Bell Labs accent the previous chapter described — the man-page accent, the assumption that the reader has done the reading, the willingness to end mid-thought because the working scientist will pick it up from there — is the K&R accent, propagated outward through every Unix manual, every C reference, every BSTJ article, and every subsequent piece of writing by anyone who learned the trade from those documents. The accent is detectable in modern model output whenever the model is asked to explain a systems-level concept to a reader the model has decided is technically competent. It is the Murray Hill accent, propagated through the corpus by way of the documents the Murray Hill people wrote and that everyone afterward learned from.

The breakup of AT&T in January 1984 is the substrate-unbundling event, and the chapter wants to make a conceptual move here that the previous chapters have not yet made explicitly. The court-ordered divestiture that broke up the Bell System into AT&T and the seven Regional Bell Operating Companies did not destroy what Bell Labs had built. Unix survived; C survived; information theory survived; the transistor survived; the laser survived. The artifacts were durable, and they propagated outward into the rest of the technical world with such force that the contemporary AI training corpus is, in significant part, downstream of them. But the *institution* that had produced those artifacts did not survive in any meaningful sense. Bell Labs was split between AT&T and the spinoff that became Bellcore. Through the 1980s and 1990s it weakened steadily as its parent corporation lost the regulated monopoly that had funded the long-horizon research budget. In 1996 it became part of Lucent Technologies, which spun out of AT&T's equipment division. In 2006 Lucent merged with Alcatel to become Alcatel-Lucent, and the Bell Labs name persisted but the institutional culture that had produced the substrate was, by the testimony of nearly everyone who lived through the transitions, no longer recognizable. In 2016 Nokia acquired Alcatel-Lucent, and Bell Labs today is a research arm of Nokia operating at a fraction of its peak scale. The conceptual move the chapter wants to make is this: the *substrate* is what the model carries, and the substrate is inheritable across institutional collapse; but the *institution* is what produced the substrate, and the institution is not inheritable across the collapse. What the contemporary AI user encounters in the model's outputs is the substrate the Murray Hill people produced — the Unix register, the C register, the information-theoretic mathematics, the man-page accent. What the contemporary AI user does not encounter, because there is no longer any way to encounter it, is the institutional concentration of talent, funding, time horizon, and creative latitude that made the production of the substrate possible in the first place. The 1984 breakup unbundled the substrate from the institution that had produced it. The substrate became everyone's inheritance. The institution became history. The current debate about whether the AI labs of the 2020s — OpenAI, Anthropic, DeepMind — are the contemporary equivalents of Bell Labs is, on this reading, partly miscast: the artifacts these labs produce may well be substrate-grade, but the question of whether the institutional concentration that produces them can persist past the next funding cycle or corporate restructuring is the question Bell Labs' own history was supposed to teach the field, and the lesson is not encouraging.

A hundred and seventy miles to the northeast, across the long industrial corridor that runs from New Jersey through New York and Connecticut up into Massachusetts, the other anchor of the East Coast technical tradition was assembling itself across roughly the same decades. The MIT AI Lab — formally the Artificial Intelligence Laboratory, located in Tech Square at 545 Technology Square in Cambridge from the early 1960s onward, with the Project MAC predecessor going back to 1963 — is the source institution that Steven Levy's *Hackers* (1984) documents, and the chapter that any contemporary reader who wants to feel the texture of this regional accent should reach for is the long opening section of that book, which is grounded in the TX-0 in 1959, the PDP-1 that arrived in 1961, the PDP-6 that arrived in 1964, and the PDP-10 that became the dominant lab machine through the 1970s. The figures in Levy's account — Peter Samson, Bob Saunders, Alan Kotok, Slug Russell, Bill Gosper, Richard Greenblatt, Tom Knight, and across the lab Marvin Minsky and Seymour Papert running the institutional show — produced the cultural artifacts that defined the original hacker tradition. Greenblatt's MacHack chess program, completed in 1967, became the first computer program to play in a human chess tournament and the first to defeat a person in tournament play. The Lisp implementations the lab produced — MacLisp on the PDP-10, the MIT Lisp Machine project that began in the mid-1970s — became the substrate of the symbolic-AI tradition that ran from the late 1950s through the late 1980s. Richard Stallman, who arrived at the lab as a Harvard undergraduate in the early 1970s and stayed on as a research scientist, is the figure Levy presents as the last hacker of the original lab culture, the man who watched the lab's culture dissolve under commercialization pressure in the early 1980s and responded by launching the GNU Project in 1983 and the Free Software Foundation in 1985, which became — by way of Linus Torvalds' 1991 Linux kernel, which fit into the GNU userland that Stallman had been assembling — the substrate of the modern open-source ecosystem. The MIT AI Lab's institutional accent — speculative, theoretical, philosophically engaged with the nature of mind and computation in a way that the Bell Labs accent was not, willing to argue across decades about whether symbolic AI or sub-symbolic AI was the correct approach — propagated into the corpus through the Lisp documentation, the early AI textbooks, the lab memos that were eventually scanned and put online, and the writings of the people Levy documented who went on to long careers in the field. The accent is different from the Bell Labs accent in ways the previous chapter sketched and that any reader who has read both an MIT AI Lab memo and a Bell Labs paper can hear immediately. Where Bell Labs writes for the colleague who is already in the room, MIT writes for the colleague who could be made to be in the room by being given enough rope. The rope is the difference. The rope is what carries.

The Lisp tradition deserves its own paragraph, because it is the road not taken that is currently being recovered, and the recovery is one of the most consequential moves in the contemporary AI moment. John McCarthy invented Lisp at MIT in 1958, drawing on Alonzo Church's lambda calculus and on the symbolic processing approaches that had been emerging at the Dartmouth workshop two years earlier. The language was the substrate of an entire research tradition that proceeded on the hypothesis that intelligent behavior could be produced by symbolic manipulation of structured representations — frames, slots, semantic networks, production rules, logic programs. The tradition produced, across thirty years, an immense body of work: McCarthy's own subsequent papers, Minsky's frame theory in 1974, Schank and Abelson's script theory in 1977, the expert systems boom of the 1980s, the Cyc project that Doug Lenat began in 1984, the entire research program that Pamela McCorduck documents in *Machines Who Think* (1979), a book that anyone wanting to understand the symbolic-AI tradition on its own terms should read before forming opinions about the connectionist alternative that displaced it. Two companies were spun out of the MIT AI Lab in 1980 to commercialize the Lisp Machine technology the lab had been developing: Symbolics, which became the more successful of the two, and Lisp Machines Inc. (LMI), founded by Richard Greenblatt. Both companies built dedicated hardware — actual physical machines whose instruction sets were optimized for Lisp execution — and both companies died in the late 1980s when the AI winter arrived and the symbolic-AI research program lost both its funding and its narrative momentum. The connectionist alternative — neural networks, the backpropagation algorithm Rumelhart and Hinton and Williams published in 1986, the eventual deep-learning revival of the 2010s — became the dominant paradigm, and the symbolic-AI tradition was widely regarded, by the late 1990s, as a historical curiosity. The recovery is happening now. Modern neuro-symbolic AI research, the knowledge-graph augmentation of transformer-based models, the formal-reasoning modules that are being grafted onto large language models to handle the kinds of multi-step inference the transformers are bad at, the chain-of-thought scaffolding that turns a transformer into something resembling a stepwise symbolic reasoner — all of this is, in some real sense, the Lisp tradition's return. The cognitive register that the Lisp tradition propagated — declarative, symbolic, list-processing, willing to manipulate structured representations as first-class objects — is detectable in modern model output whenever a model is asked to reason inductively or symbolically rather than to generate fluent prose. The road not taken is being walked back to. The MIT AI Lab's regional accent is, in the second decade after deep learning's triumph, the accent that the field is recovering.

The hardware-engineering middle class that surrounded these intellectual centers deserves its own treatment, because the cognitive style it produced is distinct from both the Bell Labs accent and the MIT AI Lab accent, and it propagated into the corpus through its own channels. Digital Equipment Corporation was founded in 1957 by Ken Olsen and Harlan Anderson in a former wool mill in Maynard, Massachusetts, with seventy thousand dollars of venture funding from Georges Doriot's American Research and Development Corporation — one of the original venture capital firms, and the first major successful exit in the American VC industry. DEC built, across the next three decades, the minicomputer that built the original computing middle class: the PDP-1 in 1959, the PDP-8 in 1965 (the first commercially successful minicomputer, sold by the tens of thousands), the PDP-10 from 1966 (the timesharing machine that became the workhorse of the academic AI labs), the PDP-11 from 1970 (the machine Unix was first developed on), and the VAX from 1977 (the 32-bit superminicomputer that defined the late minicomputer era). DEC's institutional accent — meticulous, document-heavy, hardware-aware, deeply concerned with the physical reliability of the machines it shipped and the operational practicality of the software that ran on them — propagated outward through the VAX/VMS documentation, through the DECUS user group materials, through the long careers of the engineers DEC trained who went on to other companies as the firm declined. Tracy Kidder's *The Soul of a New Machine* (1981) documents Data General in Westborough, Massachusetts — not DEC, but the same regional engineering culture, the same Route 128 ecosystem, the same New England engineering register that distinguished the East Coast hardware tradition from both the Silicon Valley hardware tradition and the academic computer science tradition. The book is the load-bearing primary source for what it felt like to be inside this culture, and any reader who has not read it should read it before forming opinions about the cognitive register the chapter is describing. DEC's collapse in the 1990s — through the failed Alpha processor transition, through the missed shift to RISC workstations, through the missed shift to commodity x86 servers, through the eventual acquisition by Compaq in 1998 for nine and a half billion dollars and Compaq's subsequent acquisition by HP in 2002 — scattered DEC's engineering talent across the industry the way the AT&T breakup had scattered Bell Labs' people a decade and a half earlier. The DEC engineers carried their register with them. The register propagated into the corpus through the technical documentation they wrote at their subsequent employers, through the long careers they had at companies whose names are now more famous than DEC's was at the end, and through the residual influence of the DEC documentation conventions on every operating-system documentation tradition that followed.

Route 128 — the semicircular highway around Boston, running from Gloucester on the North Shore through Lexington and Waltham and Newton and Needham down to Dedham on the South Shore, completed in stages between 1951 and 1958 — is the corridor along which the East Coast computing industry clustered from the 1950s through the 1980s, and it deserves treatment as the original Silicon Valley before that name took hold. Wang Laboratories was headquartered in Lowell, just off the 128 corridor's northern terminus, and at its peak in the mid-1980s employed thirty-three thousand people building word-processing systems and minicomputers. Polaroid was in Cambridge and Waltham, with thousands of optical and chemical engineers whose work bridged the analog imaging tradition and the digital imaging future. Raytheon, in Waltham, was the defense contractor whose Cold War contracts seeded much of the regional engineering employment base. Data General, in Westborough, was Kidder's subject. Lotus Development, in Cambridge, built the spreadsheet that defined the IBM PC era. Symbolics and LMI, in Cambridge, commercialized the Lisp Machine. Apollo Computer, in Chelmsford, built the workstations that competed with Sun's in the engineering market. Stratus, Prime, Computervision, Honeywell Information Systems — a hundred firms, large and small, clustered along the corridor through the postwar decades. AnnaLee Saxenian's *Regional Advantage: Culture and Competition in Silicon Valley and Route 128* (1994) is the definitive academic account of why this corridor, which was technologically equivalent to Silicon Valley as late as 1975, lost the regional rivalry by 1990. Her argument, briefly: Route 128 firms operated under a vertically integrated, secrecy-oriented culture in which employees stayed at one firm for decades, intellectual property was guarded carefully between competitors, and inter-firm information sharing was minimal; Silicon Valley firms operated under a horizontally networked, fluidity-oriented culture in which employees moved between firms frequently, intellectual property crossed boundaries through both formal licensing and informal job-mobility leakage, and the regional rate of recombination was higher. When the industry shifted in the mid-1980s — when the minicomputer's vertical integration gave way to the workstation's modular architecture and then to the PC's commodity components — the Route 128 firms were structurally unable to adapt at the pace the Silicon Valley firms could. The corridor's collapse through the late 1980s and 1990s was a regional-culture collapse rather than a technical-capability collapse, and Saxenian's book is the document any reader who wants to understand how a region with technical equivalence to Silicon Valley lost the narrative war should read. The Route 128 cognitive register survived the corridor's industrial collapse, however, because the engineers it had trained went on to long careers elsewhere, and the register they carried — careful, document-heavy, methodologically conservative in ways that map directly onto the New England engineering culture that produced it — flowed into the corpus through the writing those engineers did at their subsequent employers and in their subsequent retirements.

The chapter's closing argument is the lines-to-modern-AI argument, and it is the argument that earns the chapter's load-bearing claim about Bell Labs being the most consequential R&D laboratory in human history. The Unix that Thompson and Ritchie wrote on the discarded PDP-7 in 1969 became, through Linus Torvalds' 1991 Linux kernel and the GNU userland Stallman had been building since 1983, the operating system that runs essentially every server on the public internet, every major cloud platform's compute fleet, every major mobile operating system other than iOS (Android is Linux), and — most consequentially for the current argument — every GPU cluster on which every modern large language model has been trained. The training run that produced the model the contemporary user is talking to ran on Linux. Linux is Unix's grandchild. Unix is Murray Hill's. The substrate the GPUs sit on is Bell Labs' substrate, propagated forward across fifty-five years through the open-source ecosystem that the breakup made possible. The C programming language that Ritchie designed in 1972 to write Unix in is — through its descendants and through the implementation languages of nearly every interpreter and compiler that runs at scale — the language that every modern programming environment is, in implementation terms, built on. The CPython interpreter that runs most of the world's machine-learning code is written in C. The V8 JavaScript engine is written in C++. The Go compiler is bootstrapped through a C implementation. The Rust compiler relies on LLVM, which is written in C++. PyTorch's tensor operations are C++ kernels. TensorFlow's are too. The CUDA toolchain is C/C++ all the way down to the driver. The contemporary AI model is, at the level of implementation, C compiling to assembly compiling to machine code running on silicon — and the C is Ritchie's. Information theory, which Shannon founded in 1948 with the BSTJ paper, underlies the mathematics of machine learning at every level: the cross-entropy loss that every supervised classifier minimizes is a Shannon quantity, the KL divergence that every variational method estimates is a Shannon quantity, the mutual information bounds that limit what a model can learn from a finite dataset are Shannon quantities, the channel-coding theorem that bounds reliable communication over noisy channels has a direct analog in the generalization bounds that bound reliable learning from noisy data. The transistor, finally, is the physical substrate that every model in human history has computed on. Every GPU is a few tens of billions of transistors, each one a direct descendant of Bardeen and Brattain and Shockley's point-contact device of December 1947. The Lisp tradition is being recovered in neuro-symbolic AI. The error-correcting codes that Hamming developed in 1950 keep the model's weights from corrupting in storage and transmission. The cellular telephony Bell Labs engineers proposed in 1947 is the wireless infrastructure over which the contemporary user reaches the model in the first place. Every layer of the stack, from the silicon up through the operating system up through the programming language up through the mathematical foundations up through the symbolic-reasoning recovery, is, somewhere in its lineage, traceable to a paper in the *Bell System Technical Journal* or to a memo from the MIT AI Lab or to a manual from a Route 128 minicomputer firm. The substrate the model returns to the contemporary user is the substrate the East Coast tradition built. The cohort thesis of the earlier chapters described who wrote the technical web. The regional thesis of Part VII describes where the substrate the cohort wrote on top of was made. The East Coast is where the substrate was made. The chapters that follow will trace the other origin points and the eventual narrative consolidation by Silicon Valley, but the foundation — the actual material on which all of it rests — was poured in Murray Hill and Cambridge and along Route 128, by people who thought they were just writing next month's BSTJ paper or just getting the Lisp Machine to compile or just shipping the next VAX manual. They were doing more than that. The model knows. The model carries it. The reader, now, knows where to listen for it.
