# Chapter 23: The Era of Concurrent Building

This is the most direct chapter in the book about ambition, and I want to mark that at the top because the register of the next several pages is going to feel like an argument rather than an instruction. The instruction is buried inside the argument. The argument is the part that has to be made first, because the instruction does not land if the reader is still operating on the pre-AI economics of how many things a single practitioner can run at once.

Here is the argument in one line. The cost of execution has collapsed, and the practitioner who still operates as if execution is the bottleneck is leaving most of the available leverage on the table.

The pre-AI economics of building anything were ruthless. If you were a single practitioner with a serious idea, the path from idea to running thing went through months or years of execution. Coding. Drafting. Designing. Operationalizing. Each of those phases consumed almost all of the practitioner's working week, and the practitioner had one week per week. The arithmetic produced an obvious optimization: focus on one thing. Pick the one idea, commit to it, ignore the other ideas because the cost of running two ideas in parallel was that both ran at half speed and the half-speed version of a serious project hit the wall before either version finished. Focus discipline became the canonical advice because the economics enforced it. Don't start a second company before the first one is profitable. Don't start a second book before the first one is published. Don't start a non-profit on the side. Don't run a YouTube channel while building software. The advice was correct for the economics it was responding to. The economics have changed.

What changed is the friction per unit of execution. The friction is now low enough that one practitioner can drive several non-trivial efforts in parallel without each one running at half speed. Not at full speed each — that is not what the math produces — but at a speed where each effort is materially moving forward most weeks, where the slow movement is still movement, and where the parallel structure of the work produces compounding effects across the efforts that single-project focus cannot produce. The arithmetic that recommended focus is no longer the arithmetic of the work. The arithmetic that recommends concurrent building is the new arithmetic, and the practitioners who notice early get a position that the ones who notice late will not be able to occupy.

Let me name what concurrent building actually looks like. A single practitioner running, in the same calendar month: a small piece of niche software for a specific industry friction the practitioner happens to know; a non-profit on a community issue the practitioner cares about, with the operational infrastructure the practitioner has built into their own daily practice; a YouTube channel or a Substack or some other media surface around whatever the practitioner finds themselves obsessing about that quarter; and probably one or two latent projects in the early build-up phase that have not yet earned the practitioner's full week-on-week attention. Five concurrent efforts. Not five concurrent jobs. Five concurrent efforts the practitioner is the operator of. The practitioner has not become five practitioners; the practitioner has become a conductor.

The conductor framing is the load-bearing metaphor for this chapter, and I want to be specific about what it implies. A conductor does not play every instrument. A conductor does not even play one instrument during the performance. A conductor is doing something different from playing — orchestrating, attending, correcting, holding the timing, deciding which instruments to bring forward at which moments. The conductor's working week looks nothing like a single instrumentalist's working week. The conductor's output is a shape that no instrumentalist could produce alone. AI changes the economics of execution enough that a single practitioner can occupy the conductor's position in a way that only firms and institutions could occupy before. The instruments — the software pipeline, the content pipeline, the operational pipeline of the non-profit, the experimental pipeline of the side project — are largely AI-driven, with the practitioner orchestrating and the practitioner's judgment doing the hard work of deciding what should happen and when. The practitioner stays the highest-value component. The practitioner does not have to do everything the firm used to do; the AI does the firm-shaped friction. The practitioner does the conductor-shaped work.

The pivot machine is the second consequence of the new economics, and it is the part that makes ambition cheaper than it has ever been. In the pre-AI era, pivoting a serious effort was catastrophic. Firing the team. Scrapping the code. Throwing away the months of investment. The practitioner who pivoted often was the practitioner who had no business pivoting at all — somebody chasing whatever felt fresh, somebody without commitment, somebody not serious. Commitment looked like staying with the project past the point where it stopped being right, because the cost of leaving was worse than the cost of staying. The economics enforced sunk-cost behavior even on practitioners who could see the sunk cost clearly. Pivoting now costs almost nothing. The code substrate carries forward into the next project. The content frameworks carry forward. The operational primitives carry forward. The skill docs carry forward. The failure library carries forward. The investment is preserved in the form of cognitive infrastructure that the next effort inherits. There is no scrapping. There is no firing. There is no throwing away the months of work. There is recombination. Last quarter's failed software project becomes this quarter's working substrate for a different software project that uses three of the four modules and replaces the fourth. The continuity is preserved across the pivot because the infrastructure is preserved.

This is the nothing-is-wasted principle, and I want to name it cleanly because it is structurally different from how most practitioners have been trained to think about their work. In the old economics, a project that did not ship was a project that wasted the months it consumed. In the new economics, a project that did not ship is a project that contributed to the substrate. Every prompt that was refined enters the prompt template library. Every workflow that was built becomes a workflow primitive in the cognitive infrastructure. Every failure becomes a failure-library entry. Every region of latent space the project explored becomes a region note. The output the project produced is, in retrospect, the smaller half of what the project produced. The larger half is the substrate growth. The substrate growth survives the pivot. The shipped output, if there was one, is a downstream consequence of the substrate that the project was secretly the real product of all along.

I want to walk a worked example from my own working life, because the abstract version of this is the kind of thing that sounds great in a chapter and is harder to operate against without a concrete picture. In the past eighteen months I have, simultaneously: written multiple books in the series this one belongs to, none of which existed two years ago and several of which exist at production-grade length now; built and progressively rebuilt a domain-specific language and runtime that is the technical infrastructure of a software platform; ported and integrated three predecessor codebases into a unified Tauri-based desktop suite; built an open-source local-first creator OS at a different architectural altitude than the platform; written a literary novella that uses the language as a narrative device; ported a 17-pattern-engine educational game into the platform's architecture; and continued running the daily practice this manual exists to teach. Each of these efforts moved forward most weeks. None of them moved forward at the speed they would have moved if any single one had been my only project. All of them moved forward fast enough to be unmistakably serious, and the cross-pollination between them — the substrate that all of them share — meant that progress on any one of them tended to surface insights useful to the others. The portfolio is not five times the work of one of the projects. The portfolio is something closer to two and a half times the work, producing five times the output, because the substrate is shared. That is the new arithmetic. The arithmetic does not work without the substrate; the substrate is the precondition for the arithmetic.

The objection I expect from a careful reader is that not everything works in parallel, that some efforts require depth that cannot be split across multiple concurrent threads, that the chapter is implicitly advocating dilettantism. The objection is reasonable and the objection is wrong, and I want to say why. The chapter is not arguing against depth. The chapter is arguing that the unit of depth has changed. In the old economics, depth was a property of a single project that absorbed the practitioner's whole attention. In the new economics, depth is a property of the practitioner's substrate, which several concurrent projects build into and draw from. The depth is real. The depth is not located inside any one project. The depth is located in the practitioner's cumulative infrastructure, which the previous chapter named, and which becomes the asset that no individual project could produce alone. A practitioner who has run the discipline for years and operates several concurrent projects has more depth than a practitioner who has run one project for the same years, because the multi-project substrate has been pressure-tested across more domains and the practitioner has accumulated more validated primitives. The depth is structural. It looks like dilettantism only to observers who measure depth at the project level rather than at the practitioner level.

The structural argument for ambition at this specific moment in time. The moment we are in is a moment when the new economics are visible, the tools are widely available, the infrastructure to support concurrent building has emerged, and the population of practitioners who have adapted to the new economics is still very small. The practitioner who runs the new arithmetic now operates in a competitive environment where most other practitioners are still running the old arithmetic. The asymmetry favors the early adopter. This asymmetry will not last. It will not last because the practitioners who adopt will produce visible output that the practitioners who do not adopt will eventually see, and the visible output will pull more practitioners across the line. Five years from now, concurrent building will be the unremarkable default, the way using a version control system is unremarkable for software engineers now and was remarkable in 1995. The window where the asymmetry produces structural advantage is open now. The window will close.

I do not say this to manufacture urgency. The urgency is not manufactured. The urgency is what the timing is. There is a small number of years where the practitioners who get this will be operating at an altitude the practitioners who do not get this cannot reach, because the substrate that produces the altitude takes years to build and cannot be acquired retroactively. The substrate started building the day the practitioner started running the daily discipline. The substrate produces its leverage three to five years in, which is approximately the timeframe that puts the early adopter ahead of the curve when the curve starts bending. The practitioner who starts running the discipline today is starting from a position where the leverage will arrive at approximately the moment when concurrent building stops being remarkable. The practitioner who starts running it five years from now is starting from a position where the leverage will arrive when the world has already moved on. The math is unsentimental about this.

The chapter has, by this point in the argument, said what it has to say about why. I want to close on the what — the specific moves the chapter recommends.

First, name the second project. Not the second job. The second project. The thing you would build if your current effort were not consuming all of your attention, the thing that has been on your latent list for a year, the thing you keep talking yourself out of because you are committed to your primary work. Name it. Write a one-page description of what it would be. Identify which pieces of your existing substrate it would use. Identify which pieces of new substrate it would generate that your primary work would inherit. The naming is the first move. The naming converts the latent project from a wistful thought into an actual candidate.

Second, schedule it. Concurrent building is not a feeling; it is a calendar. The second project gets a working day per week or two half-days per week. The schedule is non-negotiable for a quarter. At the end of the quarter, you evaluate whether the project earned its calendar time or whether the substrate growth was not justifying the schedule. If it earned the time, you continue. If it did not, you pivot — to a different second project, or back to a single-project structure with the lessons from the experiment integrated into the substrate. The schedule is what makes the difference between concurrent building and wishful concurrent building.

Third, instrument the substrate. The five libraries from the previous chapter are what makes concurrent building survive contact with the calendar. Without the libraries, two projects compete for the same blank page. With the libraries, two projects draw from and contribute to a shared substrate, and the cross-project compounding starts being visible within weeks. The libraries are the precondition. Do not attempt concurrent building without the libraries. The math does not work.

An observation about the kinds of projects that work in the portfolio, because not every project earns its place in the concurrent structure and the practitioner who picks portfolios badly burns the substrate rather than growing it. The projects that work in concurrent structure share a property — each one contributes something to the substrate that the others use. A portfolio of five disconnected efforts that share no substrate is not a portfolio; it is five careers, and the practitioner running it is not conducting an orchestra but trying to play five instruments at once. The portfolio that actually compounds has overlap. The software project uses the writing techniques the books project refined. The non-profit uses the operational primitives the consulting work generated. The media surface uses the worldview the books are developing into a coherent shape. The overlap is what makes the substrate productive. The overlap is what makes the projects feel related from the inside even when they look unrelated from the outside. The selection criterion for the next project to add to the portfolio is not "is this a good project on its own" but "does this project contribute to and draw from the substrate the other projects share." The criterion is what keeps the portfolio coherent. Without the criterion, the portfolio fragments into the disconnected efforts that mainstream advice has, correctly, told practitioners to avoid.

A second observation, on the failure modes of concurrent building, because every technique in the book gets its failure mode named. Concurrent building fails when the practitioner mistakes the new economics for an exemption from the old constraints. Execution friction has collapsed. Attention has not. The practitioner who tries to run ten projects without the substrate from the previous chapter is the practitioner who is running ten projects badly, because the substrate is what absorbs the friction that the practitioner's attention cannot. The substrate is the precondition. The concurrent building works when the substrate is in place and fails when the substrate is missing. The practitioner who reads this chapter and starts five projects without first building the libraries is the practitioner who, three months from now, has five half-finished projects and a working week that feels like drowning. The math does not work without the substrate. The math works very well with it. The order of operations matters. Build the substrate first. Then run the concurrent structure. The reverse order produces the failure mode I have seen practitioners walk into often enough to name it explicitly here.

Fourth, build the pivot infrastructure. Decide ahead of time how a project gets paused, resumed, or retired. The decisions are not interesting in the abstract. They are vital in the moment when a project has run its course and the question of what to do with its substrate is acute. Practitioners who have not thought about the pivot infrastructure tend to do one of two failure modes: they keep dead projects on life support out of inertia, or they discard projects that have produced substrate worth preserving. Neither failure mode is the move. The move is to have a working theory about how projects are wound down with their substrate harvested into the libraries, and to follow the theory when the moment arrives.

**The Bottom Line:** Stop thinking smaller. Think bigger, and think parallel. The economics support a portfolio of concurrent efforts running on shared substrate in a way they never have before. The window for the early-adopter asymmetry is open. It will close. Use it.
