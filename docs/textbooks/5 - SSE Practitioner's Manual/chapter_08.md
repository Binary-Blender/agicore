# Chapter 8: Multi-Model Orchestration

I do not use one model. I use several, and I switch between them many times a day, deliberately. The practitioner who runs one model for everything is paying for capabilities they do not need on the cheap end and missing capabilities they do need on the expensive end. The right answer is not "find the best model and use it for everything." The right answer is "pick the right model for the work in front of you, and develop the discipline to switch without losing context."

This is the operational complement to the architectural pattern Agicore exposes through ROUTER and ESCALATION_CHAIN — the same insight applied to the practitioner's working session rather than to a production pipeline. The pipeline architecture chooses a model per request based on declared characteristics. The practitioner chooses a model per task based on the same kind of reasoning, executed in real time, with their own taste as the routing function.

I keep three or four tabs open at any working moment. A frontier model on one. A frontier model from a different vendor on another. A cheaper, faster model on a third. Sometimes a fourth tab with a model specialized for a narrow task — long-context summarization, code, image reasoning. I move between them constantly. The cost of the switching is low. The cost of using the wrong model is high. Most practitioners I watch in real time are running one tab. That is a strictly dominated strategy and they do not realize it.

The first thing to say about model selection is that it is a daily decision, not a configuration choice you make once. The model I use for the first hour of a session is often not the model I use for the third hour. Synthesis work goes to one model. Code generation goes to another. Long-form prose drafting goes to a third. Adversarial review — where I ask a model to find what is wrong with output the previous model produced — explicitly goes to a different vendor than the one that produced the output. The reasoning is structural: the model that produced the output has correlated failure modes with itself. The model that critiques its own output will miss exactly what it was inclined to miss in the first place. A different model with a different training distribution and different reinforcement history will find the seams.

Let me name the strengths matrix I actually use, with the caveat that this matrix is current as of the moment I am writing and the specific model names will date faster than the structural rules. The structural rules are what to internalize.

For synthesis work — pulling together a long, dense context into a coherent take that names the structural shape of the problem — I want the model with the strongest reasoning architecture available, the longest context window I can get, and a training distribution that rewards careful causal explanation. This is the model I will pay top-tier prices for. Synthesis is where the leverage is, and using a cheap model on a synthesis task to save a few dollars is the classic false economy. Save the dollars somewhere else. Use the frontier model when the work is "tell me what is structurally going on in this mess."

For code generation, I want a model that has been specifically trained on code, ideally with strong tool-use capabilities and a recent training cutoff for the language or framework I am using. The frontier general-purpose model is sometimes the right choice here, but not always — a model that is a few percent worse at general reasoning but materially better at producing valid, idiomatic code in my specific stack will win in practice. Code is one of the few domains where a specialized model can outperform a generalist with broader capabilities, because the validation signal for code is so much sharper than the validation signal for prose. I run the code model in a separate tab from the synthesis model, and I hand work back and forth.

For long-form prose drafting, I want a model with a distinctive voice and strong prose taste. Not all frontier models have this. Some of them produce technically competent but tonally flat output, which is fine for synthesis but disastrous for anything I intend a human to read for pleasure. I keep one tab dedicated to the model whose prose I genuinely like, and I route every drafting task to it. The output still needs editing — it always does — but the starting point is materially better.

For adversarial review, I want a different model than the one that produced the work under review. This is non-negotiable. I do not use the same model to critique itself, because the failure modes are correlated. I open the output in a tab running a different vendor's frontier model and ask, in language calibrated to invite genuine adversarial reading: *"Here is a piece of architectural analysis another AI produced. Read it as a senior engineer who is suspicious. Where is the reasoning thin? Where does it overgeneralize? Where does it sound confident without being earned?"* The cross-vendor critique catches a class of errors single-vendor critique systematically misses.

For exploratory work — the kind of work where I do not yet know what I am looking for, where I am throwing rough framings at the model and seeing what comes back — I use the frontier model. Exploration is where the model's depth matters most, and where saving money on the cheaper model produces flat, generic exploration that does not surface anything interesting. The cheap model will hallucinate just as much as the expensive model but with less coherent latent structure underneath the hallucination, which means the harvested-hallucination move (Chapter 17) yields less. Exploration is a frontier-model task.

For long-context summarization — taking a fifty-page document and producing a coherent synthesis — I want a model with a genuinely long working window and a training distribution that has been heavily exercised on long-context retrieval. Not every frontier model is equal here. Some advertise long context windows but degrade noticeably once you push past the first ten or twenty thousand tokens; others sustain quality across the full advertised window. I keep one tab dedicated to whichever model is currently best at this task, and I send all long-context summarization there regardless of which model is best at other things. The wrong choice produces summaries that are technically correct on the early parts of the document and increasingly generic on the later parts, which is the worst possible failure mode because it is hard to detect by spot-check.

For production work — the kind of work where I have already figured out what I want and I am running variants at scale — I use the cheaper, faster model. This is exactly inverted from how many practitioners deploy their model budget. They use the frontier model to do production work and the cheap model to do exploration, on the implicit assumption that exploration is "less important" and production "needs the best." This is backwards. Production work is well-specified. Cheap models can do well-specified work. Exploration is poorly-specified by definition, and the model's depth is what makes poorly-specified work productive. Spend frontier-model dollars on exploration; spend cheap-model dollars on production.

The handoff discipline is the next thing to develop. Switching models has a cost: the new model does not have the context the previous model had. The practitioner who switches models without a handoff protocol re-explains the same context fifteen times a day and wastes their own attention on the re-explanation. The protocol I use is the structured handoff document — a short summary, two or three paragraphs, that captures the working frame of the current session in a form the next model can ingest as opening context. I write the handoff document at the moment I decide to switch. The handoff document is for the practitioner as much as for the model: it forces me to articulate what I am actually working on, which is itself often clarifying.

A worked example. I am working on an architecture problem. I have been in conversation with Frontier Model A for an hour. I have refined the problem statement, surfaced three candidate architectures, and identified the trade-offs between them. I now want adversarial review. I write a handoff document that captures: the problem statement, the three candidate architectures, the named trade-offs, and the specific question I want adversarial review on — *"Find the structural weakness in the trade-off analysis. What am I underweighting?"* I paste the handoff into a fresh session with Frontier Model B. The fresh model reads cold but with maximum critical posture, because I have not let it see my own reasoning chain. It produces a critique that catches things Frontier Model A would not have caught about its own work. I take the critique back to Frontier Model A, paste the relevant parts, and continue the architecture refinement with the critique now in context. The whole exchange took perhaps fifteen minutes. The architectural quality is dramatically better than if I had stayed in one tab.

Adversarial validation across models is a technique I deploy whenever the stakes are high enough to justify the extra step. The structural rule is: never let the model that produced the work be the only model that has read the work. If the output is going into production, into a public artifact, into a decision that affects subsequent work — get a second-vendor read on it. The second read catches the things the first model was structurally disposed to miss. This is not paranoia; it is the same logic as code review, applied to AI output instead of human output.

The adversarial validation works particularly well when the second model is given an explicit critical posture — not just *"review this"* but *"find the structural weakness; assume the previous reviewer missed something important."* The framing matters. A model given a neutral review task averages over the corpus of reviews, which skews toward gentle and balanced. A model given an explicitly critical posture routes to a different region of the corpus, where reviewers are willing to be sharp. The output is more useful. The model is not being mean; it is being asked to occupy a particular reviewer position, and the position produces the critique register. I keep a small template for the adversarial-review prompt in my working notes, deploy it consistently, and audit my own confirmation bias by reading the critique with the openness I would want from a senior peer reviewing my own work.

The failure mode of multi-model orchestration is the practitioner who switches without discipline. They open a new tab, drop a half-formed question into a fresh model, get a different but not better response, and conclude that the second model is worse. The handoff was missing. The context the first model had been working from never made it into the second model's window. The second model is not worse; it is uncalibrated. A model without context produces shallow output regardless of how powerful it is. The discipline is not "switch models" — the discipline is "switch models with full context handoff."

The other failure mode is the practitioner who refuses to switch out of attachment to a particular model. They have spent a year refining their working relationship with Vendor A's frontier model. They know how it talks, what it responds to, where its blind spots are. They are reluctant to switch because the new model is unfamiliar. This is the false economy of single-vendor allegiance. A new model from a different vendor produces output that surprises you specifically because it has different blind spots than the one you are used to. The surprise is the value. Lean into the unfamiliarity. Switch deliberately. Develop fluency in three or four models the way you developed fluency in your favorite editor.

A note on cost. The cheaper-model-for-production rule pays for itself within days for any practitioner running serious volume. I have pipelines where Path A runs a cheaper model at one-tenth the cost of running the frontier model on the same path. The output quality on the production task is within margin of the frontier model because the task is well-specified. The cost savings free up frontier-model budget for the exploration tasks where the depth materially changes the output. The cost-aware practitioner spends more on AI overall and produces more total value than the cost-naive practitioner who uses one expensive model for everything.

A second note on cost, slightly counterintuitive. The cheap model is not just cheaper per call; it is also faster, and the speed difference matters operationally. A frontier model that takes twelve seconds to respond imposes a tax on every iteration. A faster model that responds in three seconds lets the practitioner cycle four times in the same wall-clock time. For exploratory work, the cycle count is dominant — you do not know what you are looking for, and the only way to find it is to throw more variations at the wall. The faster model lets you throw more variations. The frontier model's marginal depth advantage is sometimes outweighed by the iteration-count disadvantage. Use the frontier model for the moves that require depth. Use the fast model for the moves that require breadth.

There is also a calibration question about which model to use for the very first move of a session, before the work has revealed its shape. My default is the frontier model, because the opening of a session is when the latent space is least constrained and the model's depth matters most. The first few exchanges shape the trajectory for the rest of the session. Investing frontier-model dollars in those exchanges is high leverage. The cheaper models come in once the trajectory is set and the work is becoming more mechanical.

A third operational rule I have developed: write the handoff document before you have switched. The practitioner who switches first and writes the handoff after is in the new tab cold, often with the original tab already mentally closed, and the handoff degrades. The discipline is to write the handoff as the closing artifact of the current session — three paragraphs at the bottom of the conversation, a clear statement of the working frame — and then to switch with the handoff already prepared. The cost is twenty seconds at the end of the previous exchange. The benefit is that the new model opens at the correct state instead of having to be brought up to speed in the first three messages of the new session.

The move I make tomorrow morning is simple. I open my working laptop. I have at least three tabs open, each running a different frontier model. I have one tab open with a cheaper, faster model for production work. I have, in a working note somewhere I can find it, the strengths matrix that names which model I send which kind of work to. I switch tabs deliberately throughout the day, and I write handoff documents when I switch. The single-tab practitioner is leaving capability on the table. The multi-tab practitioner with disciplined handoff is the one whose work compounds. The discipline is small. The leverage is large. The cost of building the discipline is one week of slightly slower work while the handoff routine becomes muscle memory. After that week, the slower work disappears, and the practitioner is permanently operating at a capability level the single-tab practitioner cannot reach without changing their practice.

A final structural point. The multi-model discipline connects to the practitioner's broader stance from Chapter 1 — the master does not hand the sword to the robot, and the master also does not commit to one sword. The serious practitioner of any tool-using discipline maintains fluency across multiple instances of the tool, because the variation between instances is itself information. The carpenter who only owns one saw cannot tell which saw is right for which cut. The practitioner who only uses one model cannot tell which model is right for which work. Develop the fluency. Maintain the multiple tabs. Switch deliberately. The work that comes out is the work of someone who has internalized that no single model is the right answer to the entire space of problems they are working on.

**The Bottom Line:** Pick the right model for the work. The practitioner who uses one model for everything is paying for capabilities they don't need and missing capabilities they do.
