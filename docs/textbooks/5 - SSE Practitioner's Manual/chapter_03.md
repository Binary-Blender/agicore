# Chapter 3: Attune — Real-Time Awareness

The Think phase ends when I hit the first prompt. The Attune phase begins immediately and runs continuously until the session closes. It is the part of the daily practice I find hardest to teach because it is not analytical. It is somatic. The taste-response that fires when the model returns its first line is faster than my conscious mind, and the whole discipline of the Attune phase is learning to act on that response before the conscious mind has time to talk me out of it.

The setup is this. The model returns an output. There is a half-second window — sometimes shorter — where my body registers something before I read anything carefully. A slight lean-in. A flicker of interest. A subtle wince. A flatness behind the eyes. That micro-reaction is not a feeling about the output. It is my lifetime of accumulated taste running pattern recognition at full speed and reporting the result in the only language it has, which is somatic. The conscious mind catches up about a second later and starts producing reasons. The reasons are usually wrong. The micro-reaction is usually right.

The whole Attune phase is built on the discipline of trusting the micro-reaction. I have four working mechanics for doing that. They are not analytical procedures. They are sensitivity trainings — moves I run deliberately enough times that the noticing becomes automatic and the response becomes immediate. The four are the First-Line Test, the Surprise Detector, the Boredom Alarm, and the What's-Missing Protocol. Each one targets a different signal. Each one closes the gap between the somatic response and the practitioner's next move.

The First-Line Test is the most-used. The model returns a response. Before I read the whole response, I read only the first line. Then I stop. The question is binary: does the first line have rhythm and specificity, or is it generic? If it sounds like it could open any document on this topic — *In today's rapidly evolving landscape...*, *There are several approaches to consider...*, *To address this question, we must first...* — the trajectory is already bleeding. The model is generating from the broad center of the distribution rather than from the specific region the prompt was supposed to route into. Reading the rest of the response is wasted attention; the response will be whatever the broad center has to say about the topic, polished into competence and devoid of distinction.

The corrective is to stop the read, return to the prompt, and ask what about the prompt routed into the broad center. Usually the answer is that the prompt under-specified the practitioner-context the model needed in order to leave the broad center. Sometimes the answer is that the context window has filled with surface-level material that has pulled the model's response distribution toward generic averages. Sometimes the answer is that I asked the wrong question. In every case, the corrective is to fix the routing before continuing, not to scroll past the generic first line and accept whatever follows.

The First-Line Test takes about three seconds and saves entire reading sessions. The practitioner who reads the whole response before evaluating quality is wasting attention on outputs the first line already told them were below the bar. The discipline is to act on the first-line signal immediately. If the first line is flat, redirect. If it has rhythm, continue.

The Surprise Detector is the inverse mechanic. When the model produces output that genuinely surprises me — not output that is impressive in a generic way, but output that breaks a specific expectation I had — I stop. I do not scroll past. I do not nod and continue. I stop. The surprise is the signal that something coherent has surfaced from a region of latent space I had not specifically targeted. It is the most valuable signal the model produces and the easiest to lose. The default reading rhythm carries you past surprises in the same forward motion that carries you past competent fillers. The discipline is to break the rhythm specifically when surprise fires.

What I do when surprise fires is mechanical. I copy the surprising fragment into a separate file. I write one sentence about what surprised me. I write a second sentence about whether the surprise is a useful discovery or a hallucination. If I cannot tell yet, the next prompt is a pressure test of the surprising fragment specifically — challenge it, look for the contradiction, check the structural claim. The Surprise Detector and the harvested-hallucination move I develop later in the book are the same move at different scales. The Surprise Detector is the low-level somatic version that runs on every output. The harvested-hallucination move is what the practitioner does when an entire generation contains something coherent enough to be worth pressure-testing as a discovery candidate.

The Boredom Alarm is the third mechanic and the one practitioners resist most. It is the practice of treating boredom as structural data rather than as affective complaint. The signal is plain: my eyes glaze over. My attention drifts. I find myself reading the same paragraph a second time without having taken in the first read. The standard response to this is to push through — to discipline the attention back into the text and finish reading. The correct response is to recognize that the boredom is reporting something true about the output, and to act on it.

What the boredom is reporting is that the output has gone flat. The model is generating from a depleted region of context. The trajectory has stalled. The next paragraph is going to be no different from the current one because the underlying context that would have made it different is not present in the session anymore. The boredom is the somatic indicator of structural emptiness. Pushing through the boredom does not produce better reading; it produces continued reading of a section the practitioner has already correctly diagnosed as not worth reading. The corrective is to stop reading and redirect. New prompt. New angle. New constraint. Inject specificity. The boredom does not return when the redirect works. When it does return, redirect again.

This is the mechanic experienced engineers most often resist because they have spent careers learning to push through tedium in service of finishing work. AI work inverts the calculus. The model can produce indefinite quantities of competent-and-boring content; the practitioner's attention cannot. Pushing through boredom is not a virtue here. It is the rejection of the most reliable signal you have that the output is failing.

The What's-Missing Protocol is the fourth and deepest of the four. The previous three mechanics react to what the output is. The What's-Missing Protocol asks about what the output is not. Before I evaluate the content that is present in the response, I scan for the content that should be there but is not. The missing edge case. The unmade conceptual connection. The failure mode that any competent practitioner would have named. The constraint that would have changed the answer. The downstream implication the response has carefully avoided.

What the protocol is leveraging is that the practitioner's tacit knowledge of the domain includes a sense of what a complete treatment looks like — and the model's response, however polished, is almost always missing some specific piece of that completeness. The polish hides the absence. The reader who is reading for what is present is reading the polish and ratifying the absence. The reader who is reading for what is absent is reading the polish past the polish and noticing the structural hole.

In practice this looks like this. I read the response. Before I let myself feel any particular reaction to what it said, I run a quick mental scan of the topic and ask what the response did not address. If I am reading a database architecture proposal, I scan for: where is the partition strategy, where is the backup story, where is the operational telemetry, where is the multi-region failure case, where is the cost analysis. If I am reading a process redesign, I scan for: where is the change-management resistance, where is the second-order behavior, where is the budget impact, where is the failure of the previous version. The scan takes about thirty seconds and the next prompt is determined by whatever the scan surfaced as missing. The response is treated as a draft with a known gap rather than as a finished artifact. The model is then prompted to fill the gap. The output that comes back is the actual output I was trying to produce.

A note about why these four are somatic rather than analytical, because it matters and it is the part most people get wrong when they try to teach this. The conscious analytical mind is too slow to do this work in real time. By the time the conscious mind has parsed the response and constructed a critique, the practitioner has read three more paragraphs and the original signal has been buried under continued reading. The somatic register fires in fractions of a second. The body knows the first line is generic before the conscious mind has finished reading it. The body knows the surprise is significant before the conscious mind has constructed an explanation. The body knows the trajectory has gone flat before the conscious mind has accepted that the trajectory has gone flat. The practitioner's job in the Attune phase is to install enough of a habit that the body's signal triggers the next move directly, without waiting for the conscious mind to construct a justification. The justification can come later in the Observe phase, when the day's outputs are being audited at calmer pace. The signal must be acted on in real time or it is lost.

This is also why the four mechanics are best learned individually rather than all at once. I learned the First-Line Test first, and ran it for about three weeks before adding the Surprise Detector. The Boredom Alarm came next. The What's-Missing Protocol was the last to install, and it took the longest because it requires more domain expertise than the other three — the protocol needs the practitioner to know what completeness looks like in their domain in order to detect what is missing. A new practitioner can run the First-Line Test on the first day. The What's-Missing Protocol matures over months.

The failure mode of the Attune phase is the opposite of the Think phase's failure mode. The Think phase fails by not happening at all — the practitioner sits down and starts prompting without ever asking the four morning questions. The Attune phase fails by happening too analytically. The practitioner reads the entire response, constructs an evaluation, types a response, and never notices that the body had already produced a different evaluation in the first second. The somatic signal is overwritten by the analytical narrative. The analytical narrative tends to ratify whatever output the model produced because the analytical mind is post-hoc and the model's output gives it a frame to work inside. The somatic signal would have told you the output failed before the frame had a chance to form.

A piece of practical advice for installing the four mechanics. For one working week, do not actually evaluate the outputs. Just notice the first reaction your body has to each output and write down one word for what it was — interest, flat, surprise, boredom. Do not change the next prompt based on the word; just record the word and continue. At the end of the week, look at the words. Patterns will emerge. The boredom words will cluster around specific kinds of prompts. The surprise words will cluster around specific kinds of contexts. Once the patterns are visible, the next week you start acting on the signals in real time. The week of mere noticing is what builds the speed.

A note on what each mechanic produces in terms of next-move information, because the four are not just diagnostic — each one produces a different kind of input to the next prompt. The First-Line Test failing tells me the routing is wrong; my next move is a prompt that reroutes the model into the expert territory it should have been generating from. The Surprise Detector firing tells me that something coherent has surfaced from a region I did not specifically target; my next move is a pressure-test of the surprising fragment to determine whether it is a discovery candidate or a fabrication. The Boredom Alarm firing tells me the trajectory has gone flat; my next move is a constraint that pulls the trajectory off-flat, usually by introducing a specific operational case the current generic discussion has been avoiding. The What's-Missing scan surfacing a gap tells me what the next prompt should specifically target; my next move is a constraint that pulls the model into the missing region. The mechanics are not just signals about output quality; each is a generator of the next constructive move. The practitioner who runs them this way is not just evaluating outputs but using the evaluations as fuel for the next steering decision.

A note on why the half-second window matters, and why I keep coming back to it. The model returns its output. There is a measurable, almost physical sensation that occurs before the practitioner has actually read the words. The eyes have absorbed the first few tokens. The body has registered a tone. The tone has activated taste-recognition pathways that have been built up over years of working with related material. The taste-recognition fires before the conscious mind has constructed any opinion. That half-second is the entire window during which the practitioner has access to the most reliable evaluation the practitioner can make. After the half-second passes, the conscious mind takes over, and the conscious mind is significantly less reliable. The conscious mind has theories about what good output should look like. The conscious mind is invested in the prompt that produced the output. The conscious mind would prefer to keep moving rather than to admit the output failed. The half-second window is before any of that interference loads. The whole Attune-phase discipline is the discipline of making decisions inside the half-second window rather than after it. Beginners do not believe this window exists until they start watching for it; once they start watching for it, they cannot stop noticing it.

A note on what these mechanics produce together, because the four are not just four separate procedures but a coordinated practice. The output of the Attune phase, when all four are running, is a continuous stream of taste-based feedback that catches quality leaks and exceptional breakthroughs in real time. The leaks get caught the moment the first line goes flat. The breakthroughs get caught the moment the surprise registers. The flat trajectories get caught the moment boredom fires. The structural gaps get caught the moment the What's-Missing scan completes. Without the mechanics, every one of these signals fires anyway — the practitioner's body produces them whether or not the practitioner is listening — and every one of them gets buried under continued reading. The mechanics are not new signals. The mechanics are the discipline of acting on the signals that were already firing.

A note on what failure looks like for each mechanic, because each fails in a different way and the failures are worth recognizing. The First-Line Test fails when the practitioner reads the first line, registers that it is generic, and reads the next twelve lines anyway because the rest of the response might be better. The rest of the response is not better. The first line was the diagnostic. The reading after the diagnostic is wasted attention. The Surprise Detector fails when the practitioner registers the surprise and continues reading without isolating it. The surprise is now buried in the rest of the response and the discovery candidate is lost. The Boredom Alarm fails when the practitioner pushes through the boredom on the theory that the next paragraph might be better. The next paragraph is not better. The boredom was the signal. The What's-Missing Protocol fails when the practitioner runs it after evaluating what is present, rather than before. By the time the conscious mind has formed an opinion about what is present, the scan for what is absent is already biased by the opinion. The scan has to come first. The four mechanics fail in characteristic ways and the practitioner who runs them at speed learns to recognize their own characteristic failures.

The move you make tomorrow is this. Pick one of the four mechanics. The First-Line Test if you have no other preference. Run it every time the model returns an output for one working session. Do not run the other three. Just install the one. The practice will compound.

**The Bottom Line:** Your taste-response fires faster than your analytical mind. Learn to trust it. Learn to act on it before the analytical mind talks you out of it.
