# Chapter 7: Context Window Hygiene

Progressive Constraint Steering works inside a context window. The context window is finite. Over the course of a long session, the window fills with accumulated conversational state — the original prompt, the model's outputs, the practitioner's constraints, the model's revisions, the practitioner's clarifications. The window is not infinite and its effective working capacity is significantly smaller than its nominal token budget. Long sessions that ignore this fact produce a specific failure mode whose symptoms I have learned to recognize and whose corrective moves are the subject of this chapter.

What happens to the model's traversal as the window fills is predictable. The first ten turns of a session produce outputs that are sharp, specific, and routed into the expert region of latent space that the prompt established. Around turn twenty or thirty — the exact threshold varies by model and by domain — the outputs begin to drift. The drift is gradual and is not announced. The model's responses become slightly more generic. They start repeating constraint patterns the practitioner has already addressed. They surface fewer surprises. The specific routing the early prompts established is being averaged out by the accumulated context, and the model's response distribution is pulling toward the broad center of its training distribution. By turn forty or fifty in a heavily-used session, the model is producing competent-but-flat output that resembles what an unprompted model would have produced on the same domain — the practitioner's careful routing has been diluted by the volume of subsequent material.

The signals that the context is bleeding are specific and learnable. The first signal is increased generic-ness in the model's first lines — the First-Line Test from chapter three starts failing more often, even though the prompts have not changed in quality. The second signal is lost specificity — the model stops naming the specific operational details the practitioner has been establishing and starts naming generic categories. The third signal is repeated suggestions — the model proposes a move it already proposed earlier in the session, having lost track of the fact that the move was already accepted or rejected. The fourth signal is surface-level responses — the model that ten turns ago was producing nuanced technical positions is now producing summaries that read like an introductory blog post on the topic. These four signals usually appear together and they appear gradually. The practitioner who is not watching for them adapts to the degradation without noticing it has happened. By the time they do notice, the session has been operating at degraded quality for an hour.

The Attune-phase mechanics are what catch this. The First-Line Test fires on the increased generic-ness. The Surprise Detector stops firing — the model is no longer producing surprises because the model has lost the routing that made surprises likely. The Boredom Alarm fires steadily because the outputs have gone flat. The What's-Missing Protocol surfaces more obvious gaps because the model has lost the specific completeness it was previously delivering. The signals are present. The practitioner who is running the Attune phase notices them. The question this chapter answers is what to do when the signals fire.

I have four operational moves for managing the context window. They are different in scope and they apply at different points in a session. The four are explicit restatement, summary handoff, targeted reset, and silent reset. Each has a use case and each has failure modes. Knowing which one to deploy is most of the skill.

The first move is explicit restatement. This is the lightest of the four. The practitioner inserts a prompt that re-anchors the working frame near the end of the long session. The prompt names the specific architectural commitments the session has established and reinstates them as binding constraints on the next outputs. The shape of the prompt is something like: *Before we continue, let me restate where we are. We are designing X. We have committed to architectural choices A, B, and C. We have established constraints D and E. The next phase of the work is F. Continue from this anchor.*

What this does mechanically is push the routing-relevant material back to the recency end of the context window. The model's attention is biased toward recent tokens. The architectural commitments that were established forty turns ago are present in the window but they are not where the model is reading most heavily. The explicit restatement re-presents them in the recent context and routes the next outputs back into the region the early session established. The move takes one prompt and one model response. It works when the drift is mild — the routing has not been lost, just diluted. The practitioner can re-anchor and continue.

The move fails when the drift is more severe than the practitioner has diagnosed. If the underlying problem is that the session has accumulated contradictory commitments — the practitioner committed to one approach in turn five and a different approach in turn twenty without explicitly retracting the first — the restatement will not fix it. The model is still seeing both commitments and the model is still hedging between them. The restatement is the right move only when the working frame is coherent and just needs to be re-foregrounded.

The second move is summary handoff. This is the move I make at the natural end of a session that is going to continue in a future session. The session has produced something valuable. The session is not over because the work is done; it is over because the practitioner needs to stop for the day or because the context window has filled enough that continuing in it would be increasingly painful. The summary handoff is the move that closes the current session with a structured summary that will open the next session at full state.

What the handoff is concretely is a prompt that says: *We are closing this session. Produce a complete handoff document. It should include the goal of the work, the architectural decisions made, the open questions, the next moves planned, and any specific constraints or framings that the next session will need to inherit. Write it as if you are briefing a colleague who is going to pick this up tomorrow with no prior context.* The model produces the handoff. The handoff is saved as a working document. The next session opens with that document as the first prompt. The new session starts at the state the previous session ended at, but with a clean context window. The drift accumulated in the previous session does not carry forward. The architectural commitments do.

The summary handoff is the move I use most often for multi-day work. The practical effect is that the work runs in clean context windows for as long as it takes to produce something, then is preserved across sessions through the handoff document. The handoff document is also useful for me independently of the next session — when I return to the work a week later, the handoff document is what I read to remind myself what state the work was in. The document is the cartography object, the working notebook entry, the artifact that lets the work survive its own context window.

A note on the quality of the handoff document, because the document is only as useful as the model's ability to produce a complete one. I have learned to prompt for the handoff specifically rather than asking for a generic summary. The generic summary version of this move produces a document that is technically accurate but operationally useless — it lists what was discussed without naming what was decided. The specific prompt — *write this as a handoff to a colleague picking this up tomorrow with no prior context* — produces a document that names the decisions, the open questions, and the next moves. The framing of the prompt matters. The summary prompt is the wrong move. The handoff prompt is the right one.

The third move is the targeted reset. This is the move for the situation where the session has accumulated useful material in one area but has gone off-track in another. The practitioner wants to clear the off-track context without losing the on-track context. The targeted reset is a deliberate clearing of one subtopic while preserving the larger frame.

The move looks like this. The practitioner opens a new session and seeds it with only the material that was on-track in the previous session. The off-track material is left behind. The new session has the larger frame intact and the off-track exploration cleared. The work continues from a position equivalent to the previous session's on-track state without the drift the off-track exploration introduced.

I use this most when an exploratory tangent has produced something I do not want to keep but the surrounding work was valuable. The full reset would discard too much. The continued session would carry the tangent's residue forward. The targeted reset gives me the discard without the loss. The discipline of the move is being honest about what was on-track and what was not. The temptation is to seed the new session with too much — to bring the tangent forward because some piece of it might be useful. The discipline is to bring only what was clearly on-track and let the rest go. The practitioner who half-resets gets half the benefit.

The fourth move is the silent reset. This is the move whose existence I want to name explicitly because most practitioners do not know they can make it. The silent reset is starting a fresh session without telling anyone — including any human collaborators who might be watching the output — that the previous session is being discarded. The output continues. The work continues. The internal context window is fresh. The downstream artifact does not announce the reset, because announcing the reset adds nothing for anyone reading the artifact and risks misframing what the artifact is.

The silent reset is the move I use when I am producing a long artifact for an external audience and I notice mid-production that the context has bled. The bled context is producing flat output. Continuing under the bled context would produce a visibly weaker artifact in the second half than in the first. The honest move would be to declare the problem, restart, and acknowledge the discontinuity. The honest move is the wrong move. The reader does not benefit from knowing that the practitioner had to reset their context window halfway through. The artifact benefits from the second half being as strong as the first. The silent reset produces the second half from a fresh context, the practitioner seams the two halves together, and the reader gets a uniformly strong artifact. The reset is invisible to the reader by design.

The technique has a specific failure mode and I want to name it. The silent reset can become an alibi for sloppy work — the practitioner who resets every time the model produces something they did not like is not running silent resets; they are running the reject-regenerate loop with a different name. The silent reset is appropriate when the underlying context has bled and the bled context is producing systemically weaker output. It is not appropriate as a substitute for steering. The distinction is the same one developed in chapter five. The practitioner who can tell the difference between context bleeding and trajectory steering knows when to reset and when to steer. The practitioner who cannot tell the difference runs both moves badly.

The choice between the four moves is determined by what the practitioner is trying to preserve and what they are trying to discard. If the work is going to continue in the same session and the drift is mild, run the explicit restatement. If the work is going to continue across sessions, run the summary handoff. If the work is going to continue in the same session but a specific tangent needs to be cleared, run the targeted reset. If the artifact is being produced for external consumption and the context has bled, run the silent reset. The four moves cover the four situations.

A note on the handoff document as a working primitive, because the handoff document is the most consequential of the four moves and the one whose downstream effects are largest. The handoff document is also the object that the cartography practice developed later in the book takes as its raw material. The handoff documents accumulated across months become the practitioner's working map of the regions of latent space they have explored, the architectural decisions they have made, and the open questions still outstanding. The cartography practice in chapter twenty develops what to do with the accumulated handoff documents. The first move toward that practice is starting to produce the documents in the first place. Even if no further cartography practice ever runs on top of them, the documents make individual sessions resumable across arbitrary gaps and prevent the loss of context that destroys most long-running AI projects.

A note on the relationship between window hygiene and Progressive Constraint Steering, because the two techniques interact and the interaction is worth naming explicitly. Progressive Constraint Steering produces long sessions by design. The whole technique is the accumulation of constraint chains across many turns. The longer the chain, the more sophisticated the artifact, the more value the chain has produced — and the more the context window has filled. The two techniques pull in opposite directions on the same resource. The discipline is to manage the tension deliberately. Run constraint chains as long as the chain is producing genuine evolution; when the Attune-phase mechanics start firing the signals of context bleed — repeated suggestions, lost specificity, surface-level responses — the chain has hit the window's working capacity and the next move is not another constraint but one of the four hygiene moves from this chapter. Practitioners who run Progressive Constraint Steering without managing the context window get chains that go flat in the middle of important work; practitioners who manage the context window too aggressively reset before the chains have produced their value. The balance is felt rather than measured. The somatic signals are the diagnostic; the four hygiene moves are the corrective.

A note on the threshold at which context begins to bleed, because the threshold varies and the variation is worth understanding. The exact turn number at which a session's outputs start degrading depends on three factors. The first is the model. Different models have different effective working windows; some maintain specificity at fifty turns, others start degrading at twenty. The second is the density of the conversation. A conversation full of long model outputs and detailed practitioner constraints fills the window faster than a conversation of short exchanges. The third is the diversity of the content. A conversation that stays on a single topic with consistent terminology survives longer than a conversation that swings across domains, because the consistent terminology produces less of the cross-domain interference that flattens response distributions. The practitioner learns their own working models' effective thresholds by running the Attune mechanics consistently. The thresholds are not constants; they are working parameters that calibrate with experience.

A note on the cost of context window management, because the cost is real and worth naming. Each of the four hygiene moves has a cost. The explicit restatement costs a turn of pure restating with no new artifact produced. The summary handoff costs a turn of summarization plus the cognitive overhead of saving and retrieving the handoff document. The targeted reset costs the construction of a new session seed and the manual selection of what to preserve. The silent reset costs the seam between the two halves of the artifact that the practitioner has to manually integrate. None of these costs are trivial. The reason to pay them is that the alternative — continuing under degraded context — costs more. The degraded session produces flat output for the rest of its duration, which is usually many more turns of degraded production. The hygiene cost is fixed and bounded; the cost of not running hygiene compounds with every turn of continued degraded production. The arithmetic favors hygiene almost always. The practitioner who delays the hygiene move because the move feels expensive is making the wrong tradeoff. Run the move when the signals fire. The cost is paid; the recovery happens; the next thirty turns produce strong output again.

The move you make tomorrow is this. The next time you run a session that lasts more than thirty turns or that you intend to continue in a future session, run the summary handoff before you close. Prompt the model with the specific framing: *Produce a handoff document for a colleague picking this up tomorrow with no prior context. Name the goal, the decisions made, the open questions, the next moves, and the constraints that need to be inherited.* Save the document. When you return to the work, open the document as your first prompt of the next session. The new session starts at the state the previous session ended. The drift does not carry forward. The technique installs in one use.

**The Bottom Line:** The context window is not free space. Manage it like the scarce resource it is.
