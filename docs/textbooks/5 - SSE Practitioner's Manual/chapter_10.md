# Chapter 10: The Power of Pet Peeves

The previous chapter named implicit practitioner signaling as the foundational routing technique. This chapter zooms in on the single most information-dense version of that technique — the pet peeve — and develops why it is structurally so effective that I treat it as a first-class compression operator in my working vocabulary. A pet peeve is not a complaint. It is a unit of compressed lived experience that activates a payload of tacit knowledge in the model with a token cost so low it would be malpractice not to use them deliberately.

Let me unpack the claim. A pet peeve, as deployed in a prompt, is a short sentence — usually one clause, sometimes two — that names a specific failure mode the speaker has personally encountered and developed a strong negative reaction to. *"My pet peeve is teams backsliding after six months."* *"My pet peeve is activity-log tables that nobody budgeted retention for."* *"My pet peeve is onboarding flows that treat day one and day thirty as the same problem."* Each is one sentence. Each is operationally specific. Each names a failure the speaker has watched happen, often more than once, in a domain they have personally worked in.

The information density of that one sentence is enormous, and it pays to enumerate the payload explicitly because the enumeration is what makes the technique reproducible.

Take *"my pet peeve is teams backsliding after six months."* That sentence implicitly transmits at least the following:

The speaker has implemented process improvement before. You do not have a pet peeve about a problem you have not encountered. The phrase implicitly claims firsthand experience.

The speaker has implemented it more than once. A pet peeve is a pattern, not an incident. To call something a pet peeve, you need to have seen it recur. The model parses this without being told.

The speaker has run the implementation for longer than six months. The temporal anchor in the sentence is a claim about duration. The speaker is talking about post-launch reality, not launch-day functionality. They have stayed in the room long enough to see what the system looks like after the consultants have left.

The speaker thinks about adoption failure as a structural problem rather than a personal one. Calling it a pet peeve frames it as something the system should have handled, not as a failure of any particular team. The model picks up the systemic framing.

The speaker cares about long-term reinforcement, organizational entropy, behavior durability. None of these phrases appear in the sentence. All of them are implicit in what it would mean to have a pet peeve about six-month backsliding.

The speaker has specific failure modes in mind that they are not enumerating. The "I'm not going to spell this out because anyone in the room knows" register is itself a signal. It is how practitioners talk to other practitioners. The model recognizes the register and continues it.

The speaker is not asking for a toy. A pet peeve about backsliding implies a concern about deployment durability that toys do not need to handle. The model receives this as a quality-bar signal.

That is one sentence. The payload is on the order of seven or eight implicit claims, each of which would require its own sentence or paragraph to make explicit. The pet peeve does the compression work for free. The model receives the compressed payload, decompresses it through pattern recognition over the training distribution, and continues the pattern. The output reflects every implicit claim, even though none of them was named.

This is what I mean by pet peeves as compression operators. SSE Vol I-B Ch 12 develops compression operators formally — short surface forms that activate large semantic structures. The classic compression operators in that framework are archetypal invocations, technical vocabulary, named methodologies. A pet peeve is a different species of compression operator with the same structural function: minimal surface form, maximal activation. It activates a region of latent space densely populated by practitioner discourse about the named failure mode, and it does so at the cost of fewer tokens than any other compression operator I have access to.

The activation is especially strong because of the affective signal. *Pet peeve* is not a neutral phrase. It carries emotional valence. It signals that the speaker has skin in the game on this specific problem. Affective signals are densely correlated with practitioner discourse in the training corpus — practitioners talk about what frustrates them, and the frustration register is one of the most reliable markers of having-been-there. The model is sensitive to the register. The activation kicks harder because of the affect.

The relationship to identity anchoring (SSE Vol I-B Ch 13) and activation kickers (Ch 18) is structural. Identity anchoring positions the model in a particular identity slot; the pet peeve does this implicitly, by demonstrating the speaker's identity rather than declaring it. A kicker is a short, high-leverage phrase placed at a strategic position; the pet peeve is a kicker that operates through demonstration of operational experience rather than through invocation of role. The pet peeve is a compound move — identity anchoring plus kicker plus compression operator — packed into one sentence. There is no other single technique in the practitioner's repertoire that produces comparable leverage at comparable cost.

Let me walk specific worked examples, because the technique is concrete and the abstraction loses force without the examples.

The Kaizen example, already developed in the previous chapter, is the canonical one: *"One of my pet peeves from running Kaizens is that teams always drift back to old behaviors after six months."* That single line transforms a process-improvement-app build request from a tutorial exercise into a system designed for the actual operational reality of post-launch drift. The pet peeve activates everything: adoption failure mode awareness, reinforcement design, accountability surfacing, the social friction that makes backsliding visible. The model produces output that has all of these features without being told to include any of them.

A software example. *"My pet peeve is microservice architectures that nobody can debug because the trace spans don't propagate through the message queue, so when something breaks in production you spend three hours figuring out which service even owns the problem."* That sentence, dropped into a request for a distributed-system design, completely changes the output. The model now produces architectural recommendations that take observability seriously from day one — distributed tracing, correlation IDs, structured logging conventions, message-queue trace propagation. None of these were enumerated. The pet peeve activated the entire payload of "engineer who has been on call for a microservice mess at 2 AM." The model continues that engineer's pattern.

A content-production example. *"My pet peeve in technical writing is articles that introduce a concept, gesture at why it matters, and then never actually develop the concrete move the reader can make. The reader closes the tab feeling like they almost learned something."* Drop that sentence into a request for a draft of a technical article, and the output changes. The article the model produces is no longer a generic explainer. It is an article structured around concrete moves, with each concept tied to a specific action, with the gesture-toward-importance replaced by a worked example. The pet peeve activated the editor in the model — the part of the training distribution where people who care about technical writing talk about what bad technical writing does.

A business-operations example. *"My pet peeve is operational dashboards that show you everything that happened yesterday but nothing about what is going to break next Tuesday. You end up with a really pretty rear-view mirror."* Drop that into a dashboard-design request, and the model produces a dashboard with leading indicators, forecast bands, anomaly-detection signals, and explicit prioritization of forward-looking metrics over historical ones. The pet peeve activated the operator who has stared at a rear-view-mirror dashboard during an incident and realized it was useless.

A meta example, from prompt engineering itself. *"My pet peeve in prompt-engineering tutorials is that they teach you cute tricks and never tell you what to do when the model goes off the rails in production. The tricks work in the demo and fail the moment real ambiguity shows up."* That sentence, dropped into a request for guidance on production prompt design, produces output focused on robustness, recovery patterns, validation gates, and failure-mode handling — exactly because the pet peeve activates the part of the corpus where practitioners who have shipped AI systems talk about what actually fails.

The pattern across all the examples is the same. The pet peeve names a specific operational failure mode. The naming activates the practitioner discourse around that failure mode. The model continues the discourse. The output has all the features the discourse would naturally produce, even though those features were never enumerated.

How to construct a pet peeve that signals real experience? The honest answer is: have the experience. The dishonest version of the technique — borrowing pet peeves from other practitioners without having lived them — produces output that is marginally better than the baseline but has a particular shallow quality the model can detect. The signal is too clean. Real pet peeves come with surrounding texture — specific temporal anchors ("after six months"), specific quantitative observations ("three hours figuring out which service owns it"), specific framings that reflect the speaker's actual stance ("you end up with a really pretty rear-view mirror"). A borrowed pet peeve tends to come without the texture, and the model averages over the inconsistency. The dishonest version routes you most of the way to the right region of latent space but does not stabilize you there.

For the practitioner working in domains where they do have lived experience, the technique unlocks output that is qualitatively different. For the practitioner working in domains where they do not yet have lived experience, the technique is a forcing function: the discipline of trying to write a pet peeve forces you to notice the gaps in your own experience. The gaps are useful information. You go acquire the experience and come back with real pet peeves to deploy.

The failure mode of the technique is over-deployment. A prompt with one pet peeve is dense. A prompt with five pet peeves is incoherent — the model cannot route to all five regions simultaneously, and the signals begin to interfere. The discipline is to drop one pet peeve, perhaps two if they are tightly related, and let the rest of the prompt sustain the register without piling on more peeves. The technique is a scalpel, not a sledgehammer.

The other failure mode is using a pet peeve that is too generic. *"My pet peeve is bad code."* That sentence has no payload. It does not name a failure mode. It does not signal experience. It is the kind of thing anyone who has thought about software for ten minutes would say. The model receives no routing signal because nothing has been specified. The pet peeve has to be operationally specific to work — it has to name the failure, the temporal or quantitative anchor, and the stance the speaker has developed about it. *"My pet peeve is engineers who add caching before they've measured the latency problem they're solving"* works. *"My pet peeve is bad engineering"* does not.

A useful diagnostic, once a practitioner has drafted a candidate pet peeve, is to ask: could a beginner have written this sentence? If yes, the pet peeve is too generic. *"My pet peeve is teams that don't communicate"* — a beginner could write that. It says nothing about lived experience. *"My pet peeve is standups that became status reports the moment management started attending them"* — a beginner could not write that. The specificity, the temporal cause-and-effect, the implied stance about management presence in agile rituals all signal a speaker who has watched the failure happen. The beginner test is reliable: if the sentence is something the speaker could have produced before they had the experience, the sentence is not signaling experience.

A second diagnostic is to ask whether the pet peeve has a counterfactual. The strong pet peeves implicitly name what the speaker wishes had happened instead. *"My pet peeve is teams backsliding after six months"* implicitly says *what should have happened is sustained behavior change.* *"My pet peeve is activity-log tables nobody budgeted retention for"* implicitly says *what should have happened is retention-aware design from day one.* The implicit counterfactual is part of the payload. A pet peeve without an implicit counterfactual is closer to a complaint than to operational signal. The counterfactual is what makes the peeve point in a particular direction; without it, the model has no vector to continue along, and the activation diffuses.

A third diagnostic, the most important for the practitioner's own development, is honesty. Is this actually a pet peeve I hold, or is it something I have read and absorbed and am now redeploying? Borrowed pet peeves work to some degree — they do contain real operational signal, drawn from whoever originally voiced them — but they have a particular shallow quality the model can detect when the rest of the prompt does not match. If the pet peeve is mine, the rest of my prompt naturally extends the same register. If it is borrowed, the rest of my prompt drifts toward the register I actually have, and the model averages over the inconsistency. The technique cannot make me a practitioner I am not. The technique surfaces the practitioner I am, when I have done the work in the domain. The honesty diagnostic is the daily-practice equivalent of the structural argument from Chapter 1: the master does not hand the sword to the robot, and the master also does not pretend to mastery they have not earned.

A note on pet peeves across project phases. The pet peeves that work in an architecture-design phase are not the same as the pet peeves that work in an implementation phase or a deployment phase. *"My pet peeve is teams backsliding after six months"* is a deployment-phase pet peeve — it concerns operational reality after the system is live. *"My pet peeve is engineers who add caching before they've measured the latency problem"* is an implementation-phase pet peeve — it concerns engineering discipline during the build. *"My pet peeve is architecture documents that gesture at trade-offs without ever picking a side"* is a design-phase pet peeve — it concerns the form of the work product. The practitioner who keeps an inventory across phases has the right peeve for the right moment. Deploying a deployment-phase peeve during architecture-design work produces output that is operationally cautious but architecturally underdeveloped. Deploying an architecture-phase peeve during deployment work produces output that is structurally interesting but operationally naive. Match the peeve to the phase. The inventory pays off when the inventory is organized.

The relationship between pet peeves and the broader cartography practice developed later in this manual (Chapter 20) is worth flagging here. The pet peeve inventory is, in effect, a region directory of the speaker's own operational experience — a map of the places they have been in their working life and the failures they remember from each place. As the inventory grows over months and years, it becomes one of the most valuable working artifacts the practitioner maintains. The inventory is not a list of clever phrases to deploy. It is the working catalog of the practitioner's lived domain knowledge in a form that can be dropped into prompts as compression operators. The practitioner who maintains the inventory has, alongside every prompt they write, the option to deploy the right peeve at the right moment. The practitioner who does not maintain the inventory rediscovers their own peeves session by session, often forgetting between sessions which ones produced the best routing, and the cumulative advantage of the technique is lost to forgetting. Keep the inventory. Date the entries. Note which prompts the peeve worked in and which prompts it did not. The pet peeve inventory is among the highest-ROI working artifacts in the practitioner's repertoire.

The move I make tomorrow morning is the inventory. I sit down with a working note and write out the pet peeves I genuinely hold in the three domains I am most often prompting about. Software architecture. Content production. Business operations. I write them in their full operational form — failure mode, temporal anchor, stance. I keep the list in my working notes, alongside my prompt templates. The next time I am building a prompt in one of those domains, the relevant pet peeve gets dropped in. The output improvement is immediate, and the technique compounds over time as the inventory grows.

**The Bottom Line:** A well-placed pet peeve is worth a hundred specification lines. The model recognizes scarred operational experience and continues the pattern.
