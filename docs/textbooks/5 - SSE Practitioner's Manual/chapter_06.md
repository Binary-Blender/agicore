# Chapter 6: Progressive Constraint Steering

This is the chapter the book exists to deliver. Every other technique in the manual sits in some relationship to this one. The morning calibration sets the target the steering moves toward. The Attune-phase mechanics provide the somatic signals that tell the practitioner when the steering is working. The end-of-day audit catches the specific steering moves that produced the best moments of the session. Practitioner signaling, semantic momentum, harvested hallucination — all of them are flavors and extensions of the move developed in this chapter. The move has a name. Progressive Constraint Steering. Once you have it, the rest of the discipline is variations.

The move is this. When the model produces an imperfect output, you preserve the architectural momentum of what the model produced and you progressively tighten the constraints. You do not reject. You do not restart. You add a constraint. The model evolves the artifact under the new constraint. You evaluate the evolution. You add the next constraint. The artifact evolves again. Each pass tightens the artifact toward the target. The session is not a series of rejections. It is the evolution of a system under a sequence of progressively tightening pressures.

The shape of the conversation looks like this. *Interesting. Let's continue in this direction, but now harden it for scalability.* The model harden-passes the artifact. *Now add governance.* The model adds governance. *Now optimize indexing.* The model optimizes indexing. *Now think about edge cases.* The model walks the edge cases. *Now assume ten million records.* The model rescales the design. *Now add operational telemetry.* The model adds telemetry. *Now enforce stronger validation semantics.* The model tightens the validation. Each move is a constraint. Each constraint operates on the artifact that exists at the moment the constraint is introduced. The artifact at the end of the chain is dramatically more sophisticated than any single one-shot prompt could have produced, and it is built from the architectural momentum of the original output that an inexperienced practitioner would have rejected.

The reason this works is not that the model has emotions or gains confidence over the course of the conversation. Both of those framings are wrong and they obscure what is actually happening. The mechanism is structural. The session is a context window. The context window contains the cumulative conversational state. Each new constraint operates on the state, not on a fresh empty prompt. The constraint says *take the artifact that has just been produced and apply this transformation to it*. The transformation is local and bounded. The model is good at local bounded transformations. The model is bad at synthesizing all the constraints simultaneously from a single prompt. Splitting the synthesis across many turns lets the model do each piece well; concentrating all the constraints into a single prompt makes the model do each piece adequately, which means none of the pieces are good.

The compounding effect is what makes the technique structurally different from prompt refinement. Each turn's output is the substrate for the next turn. The third constraint operates on an artifact that already satisfies the first two constraints. By the seventh turn, the artifact satisfies seven layers of constraint that no one-shot prompt could have specified up front because the practitioner did not know, at turn one, what the constraints needed to be. The constraints emerged from the practitioner's evaluation of each successive output. The trajectory revealed the constraints that the target required. The target was reached by walking, not by jumping.

Let me walk this through the canonical worked example, which I have run dozens of times and is the example I use to teach the technique because it shows the move at its clearest. Imagine I am designing a database for an application that stores user-uploaded images and their metadata. I open a session and ask the model: *Help me design the storage layer for an application that handles user-uploaded images and metadata. Walk me through your initial architecture.*

The model returns the naive answer. It says: *The simplest architecture stores images as BLOBs in a Postgres table alongside the metadata, with a single images table containing the binary data, the filename, the uploader ID, the upload timestamp, and a few descriptive fields. Here is the schema.* It produces a schema. The schema is correct as far as it goes. It is also wrong for any production system at any non-trivial scale. The image BLOBs will balloon the database. Backups will become operationally painful. Queries against the metadata will compete with binary data for disk I/O. The architecture, naively scaled, will produce an outage in eighteen months.

The novice engineer rejects this output. *No, never store images in Postgres. Use object storage.* The model concedes. The model produces the object storage architecture, but the architecture is now produced without the discovery process that would have generated it. The novice has imported the answer they already had and the model has flattered it. The session produced nothing the novice did not already know. The novice's hostility to the model's first output prevented the model from doing the cognitive work that would have made the second output sophisticated.

What I do instead is this. I leave the naive Postgres-BLOB architecture in place. I do not reject it. The next prompt is a constraint. *Okay. Now assume the system needs to scale to ten million records. What changes about this design?* The model thinks about it. The model says: *At ten million records you need to think about partitioning the images table by upload date, adding indexes on the metadata fields that get queried often, paginating the binary fetches, and adding a CDN cache layer for frequently-accessed images. Here is the revised schema.* The model has produced a more sophisticated version of the same architecture. The revisions are useful. They are not yet the answer I am steering toward; they are way-points.

Next constraint. *Now add operational telemetry. What metrics do we need to be capturing in production?* The model walks the telemetry. It names ingestion latency, storage cost per record, query latency on the metadata fields, cache hit rate, CDN edge latency, backup duration, restore duration, disk usage growth rate. The list is good. The list is also surfacing something the model has not yet noticed, which is that the storage cost per record and the backup duration are going to be the dominant operational constraints under this architecture, and they are dominant because of the BLOB-in-Postgres decision. The model has not yet said this. The model has surfaced the data that will make it visible.

Next constraint. *Now design the backup and restore strategy. The system needs to be restorable in under thirty minutes after a complete database failure.* The model walks the backup strategy. The model says: *Backing up a Postgres database that contains image BLOBs at ten million records is going to take several hours on standard backup infrastructure. Restoring it is going to take longer. To hit a thirty-minute RTO, we need to either separate the binary data from the metadata or use a backup approach that does not require copying the entire BLOB volume on each backup.* The model has now said the thing. The architecture's central problem has surfaced from inside the model's own reasoning. I did not import the conclusion. The conclusion emerged from the constraint sequence.

Next constraint. *Given that constraint, evaluate whether object storage is a better fit for the binary data than Postgres BLOBs. Walk through the tradeoffs honestly.* The model walks the tradeoffs. The model concludes: *Object storage is the better fit. The binary data is large, infrequently mutated once written, and benefits from CDN integration. The metadata is small, mutable, and benefits from relational queries. Storing the binary data in object storage and the metadata in Postgres separates the two workloads, drops the database size by an order of magnitude, makes the thirty-minute RTO achievable, and reduces operational cost. The original design's choice to colocate them was a simplification that does not survive the operational constraints we have introduced.*

This is the conclusion the novice engineer would have asserted in turn one. The novice would have been right. But the assertion in turn one would have produced a flat architecture. The conclusion in turn seven is produced by a chain of reasoning that the model itself walked, and the chain of reasoning is what makes the architecture sophisticated. The session now contains the explicit operational justification for the object-storage choice. The session contains the telemetry plan. The session contains the backup strategy that justified the choice. The session contains the partitioning approach for the metadata table. Every one of these is downstream of the constraint chain. None of them would have appeared in the session if the first output had been rejected.

This is Progressive Constraint Steering. The technique is named. The example is walked. The mechanism is structural — the model is good at local bounded transformations, and the practitioner who issues a sequence of local bounded transformations gets better output than the practitioner who issues a single global specification or who rejects-and-restarts in pursuit of the global specification.

The technique connects to two specific arguments from SSE Vol I-B that are worth marking explicitly. The first is Chapter 16's constraints-versus-suggestions distinction. The constraints in Progressive Constraint Steering are working as constraints, not as suggestions — each one operates on the current artifact and binds the next generation to satisfy it. The second is Chapter 22's recursive semantic traversal. The constraint chain is the practitioner's deliberate traversal of the topology of the artifact's design space. Each turn moves the trajectory to an adjacent position; the chain of turns walks a path through the design space; the path ends at a position the practitioner could not have specified up front because the path had not yet been walked. The theoretical structure SSE Vol I-B develops at the level of latent-space topology is the same structure I am developing here at the level of daily working moves.

There are several things this technique is not, and they are worth naming because the technique can be misread.

The technique is not blind trust in the model. The practitioner's judgment is active at every turn. The constraint that gets added next is determined by the practitioner's evaluation of what the artifact still lacks, not by the model's report of what the artifact still lacks. The model is not steering. The model is being steered. The practitioner is making the architectural decisions about what constraints to add and in what order. The practitioner is also evaluating each output to decide whether the constraint was satisfied. The Attune-phase mechanics from chapter three fire continuously throughout the constraint chain. The First-Line Test runs on every output. The Surprise Detector fires when the model surfaces something the constraint chain did not specifically ask for. The Boredom Alarm fires when the trajectory has gone flat and the next constraint is not producing genuine evolution. The What's-Missing Protocol runs before every new constraint to identify what the artifact still lacks.

The technique is also not appropriate for every trajectory. There are unsalvageable trajectories. The signal of an unsalvageable trajectory is that the constraint chain is producing flat outputs rather than genuine evolution — the third and fourth constraints produce artifacts that are not meaningfully different from the second, even though the constraints should be transformative. When that happens, the trajectory has hit a wall. The context window has accumulated too much surface-level material; the model's distribution has flattened; the original framing was wrong and the chain has been refining the wrong artifact. The corrective is to reset deliberately — to start a new session, with a different framing, importing only the specific learnings from the failed trajectory rather than the whole context. The reset is not a failure of the technique; it is the technique's recognition of when continued steering is no longer productive.

The skill of recognizing the unsalvageable trajectory takes time to develop. Early in my practice I would over-steer — I would keep adding constraints to a trajectory that should have been reset, and the constraint chain would produce diminishing returns for ten or fifteen turns before I conceded. The signal I learned to trust is this: if two consecutive constraints produce outputs that do not move the artifact meaningfully closer to the target, the trajectory is unsalvageable and the next move is the reset, not the third constraint. The two-consecutive-flats rule is mechanical and saves enormous amounts of working time.

The technique also has a specific failure mode I want to name. It is possible to run Progressive Constraint Steering as a kind of theater — to issue constraints because the chapter says to issue constraints, without actually steering toward a target. The constraints in that version of the practice are decorations. They do not move the artifact toward anything in particular. The session produces a long chain of competent transformations that converge on a position no better than the starting position because the starting position was already close to the model's distribution center and the constraints were not pulling the trajectory away from the center. The discipline that prevents this failure is the morning calibration's quality bar from chapter two. The quality bar is the target. The constraints are what move the trajectory toward it. Without the target, the constraints are random. With the target, the constraints are a directed walk. The two phases of the practice work together; this technique fails when run without the calibration that gave it a target to aim at.

The compounding effect across long sessions is the most consequential property of the technique and the property that makes it the central single move of the manual. A short conversation with a single constraint chain produces an artifact better than a one-shot prompt would have. A working session with three or four constraint chains, each on a different aspect of a system, produces an integrated artifact that no one-shot prompt could have produced under any specification. A working week of sessions, each running the technique on a different aspect of a larger system, produces a body of work that is structurally different from what an equivalent practitioner produces using the reject-regenerate loop. The leverage is not marginal. It is several-times. Over months it widens. Over years the gap between practitioners who run this technique and practitioners who do not becomes the difference between practitioners who produce infrastructure and practitioners who produce disposable artifacts.

A note about why the technique is the central one specifically — why this move and not some other. The reason is that the model's actual generative competence is enormous and is severely under-used by single-turn or rejection-loop interaction. The model contains an enormous amount of latent capacity to produce sophisticated work. The capacity is not accessed by asking for the sophisticated work in a single prompt; the capacity is accessed by walking the model through the constraint chain that the sophistication requires. The technique is what gets the capacity out. Other techniques in this manual help with adjacent problems — routing the model into expert territory, managing context, building infrastructure that activates cognition — but Progressive Constraint Steering is the move that converts the model's latent capacity into actual output during the session itself. It is the central one because every other technique is in service of the same conversion.

A note on what the technique looks like at the level of the working day. A productive session running Progressive Constraint Steering does not look impressive from outside. The practitioner is typing short prompts at a regular cadence — *now harden it*, *now add the failure case*, *now check the assumptions*, *now consider scale* — and reading the model's outputs at speed. The work is not visibly intense. The output at the end of the session is dramatic. The contrast between the unimpressive working rhythm and the disproportionate output is part of why the technique is invisible to observers who watch other practitioners work and conclude that AI does not produce meaningful leverage. The leverage is in the chain, not in any single prompt. The observer who looks at any single prompt sees a tiny constraint and concludes that nothing is happening. The chain over thirty turns is what is happening. The chain is the technique.

A note about the cadence of the constraints, because cadence matters and I have learned it through enough chains gone wrong. The constraints should be issued at a pace that lets each one actually do its work before the next is added. The temptation, especially for engineers used to writing detailed specifications, is to issue large compound constraints — *now harden scalability and add governance and optimize indexing and walk the edge cases* — in a single prompt. The compound constraint produces a compound output that adequately addresses each piece without doing any of them well. The technique works because each constraint is small enough to be locally bounded and the model is doing each transformation discretely. Compound constraints break the locality. The discipline is to issue constraints atomically. One at a time. Let each one produce a transformed artifact. Then issue the next. The pace is slower than the engineer's instinct says it should be. The output is better.

A note about constraint sequencing, because the order of the constraints affects what the trajectory produces. The general rule I have learned is to issue constraints in the order that matches the order in which a senior engineer would think about the problem when reviewing a junior's design. The first constraints address structural concerns that affect everything downstream — scalability, the dominant failure modes, the operational envelope. The middle constraints address sophistication — governance, telemetry, partitioning, indexing. The later constraints address polish — edge cases, validation semantics, secondary failure modes. Issuing the polish constraints before the structural constraints is wasted work; the polished artifact will be discarded when the structural constraint forces a different architecture. Issuing the structural constraints first means the polish constraints, when they come, are operating on an artifact that already has the right shape. The sequence matters. Practitioners who run Progressive Constraint Steering without thinking about sequence get diminishing returns; practitioners who sequence the constraints deliberately get the full compounding.

A note on the practitioner's escalation discipline within the chain, because the chain has a rhythm and the rhythm has its own discipline. Each constraint should be slightly more demanding than the one before. Not dramatically. Slightly. The model's response to each constraint sets the next constraint's calibration. If the model handled the previous constraint cleanly with room to spare, the next constraint can pull harder. If the model handled the previous constraint at the edge of its capacity, the next constraint should be roughly the same difficulty. The chain works because the model is operating near the upper edge of what it can do well; the constraints calibrate the pressure to keep the model at the edge without pushing it over. Pushing too hard produces a constraint the model addresses superficially; the chain becomes theater. Pushing too gently produces a chain that converges on a position no better than the starting position; the chain wastes turns. The calibration is felt rather than measured. The Attune-phase mechanics from chapter three are what fire on the calibration. The somatic signal of a constraint that landed cleanly is different from the somatic signal of a constraint the model addressed superficially. The practitioner learns to feel the difference; the next constraint adjusts.

The move you make tomorrow is this. Open a session. Issue a deliberately under-specified prompt — *help me design X* or *walk me through Y* — and accept whatever the model returns as a starting position. Do not evaluate it for correctness. Treat it as turn one. Then issue a single constraint: *now harden it for scalability*, or *now add governance*, or *now consider the failure case*. Let the model evolve the artifact under the constraint. Then issue the next constraint. Continue for ten turns. At the end of the ten turns, read the final artifact. Compare it mentally to what your usual one-shot prompt on the same problem would have produced. The comparison is the proof. After the first session in which the comparison is dramatic, the technique installs itself permanently.

**The Bottom Line:** Preserve momentum. Tighten constraints incrementally. The AI is not a chatbot that produces answers. It is a probabilistic cognition substrate that evolves systems interactively.
