# Chapter 21: Build Up, Pare Down, Build Up

Part VI is the longest part of the manual, and it is the longest on purpose. The chapters in front of you stop being about technique inside a single session and start being about what the practitioner builds over months and years on top of the daily moves. This chapter opens the part by naming a pattern I have observed across every serious project I have worked on with AI in the loop. It is one of the few patterns in the book I would call universal. Build up. Pare down. Build up again.

The pattern is not obvious. It looks inefficient on the surface. It looks like you did the work twice. In some accounting sense, you did. The accounting sense is wrong, and the rest of this chapter is the argument for why.

Here is the cycle, stated cleanly. You build something concrete first. The thing is messy, directly operational, connected to a real workflow you actually run, and full of decisions you will later regret. You let it run. You let it touch reality. You watch where it breaks, where it repeats, where the same shape of problem keeps surfacing in slightly different costumes. Once enough of the operational reality has accumulated, you pare down. You extract the recurring patterns into primitives. You name the governance semantics. You give the orchestration shapes proper labels. You promote the duct tape into a small language that describes what the messy system was actually doing under all the surface noise. Then — and this is the part most people skip — you build the concrete capability back up on top of the abstractions. You rebuild the original thing. Sometimes you rebuild more than the original thing. The rebuild is the proof. If it goes faster, cheaper, and more coherently than the original messy build, the abstractions are valid. If it doesn't, the abstractions were academic and you need to go pare differently.

The cycle is universal because it is the only path I have ever seen reliably produce abstractions that are not fake. Upfront design — the practice of sitting down with a clean page and a working theory of what the right abstractions are and writing them down before any operational reality has touched the system — has a name. The name is wishful thinking. The wishful thinking sometimes works. More often it produces architectures that are clean on paper, that compress the wrong things, that leave out the variables reality cares about, and that have to be silently abandoned six months in when the operational truth refuses to fit. I have done this enough times in my own career to be specific about it. The architectures I am proudest of all came from the build-pare-build cycle. The architectures I am embarrassed by all came from upfront design that I was too committed to scrap when reality disagreed.

Here is the worked example. The genealogy of Agicore, which is the project I have worked on longest and which is the cleanest example of the cycle I can point to.

The first generation was a super app. One desktop application, built for a process improvement consultancy I was running, that did everything the consultancy needed. Kaizen tracking. Workflow capture. Customer relationship management for the engagements. Reporting. Document generation. A built-in agent that could answer questions about the running engagements. It was a super app in the literal sense — one application that held everything, with no clean module boundaries, with shared state running everywhere, with abstractions invented in the moment whenever I needed them. The thing worked. It ran real engagements. It saved real time. It produced real reports for real clients. It was also, by any normal software engineering standard, a mess. The mess is the point. The mess is what taught me what reality actually contained.

The second generation was the modular breakdown. I had been running the super app long enough that I could see the recurring shapes. The same flavor of orchestration showed up in three different places. The same governance check kept getting reinvented because I had no good word for it. The same kind of data entity — something that has a current state but also keeps its history — kept appearing in different domains under different names. I broke the super app apart. I separated it into modules with clean boundaries, with proper interfaces, with the recurring shapes given proper names. The modules ran. They ran less well than the super app in some ways — I had introduced overhead and ceremony — but they ran more legibly. I could explain them to another engineer. I could reason about which module owned which piece of behavior. The mess had been organized. The organization was the work the pare-down phase exists to do.

The third generation was the DSL and runtime. Once the modules had been broken apart and the recurring shapes had been named, I could see that the names were not just labels. The names were a language. Workflow had a specific shape. Entity-with-current-state had a specific shape. Governance had a specific shape. AI service invocation had a specific shape. Each shape recurred across modules with enough regularity that I could write it down once, as a declaration, and have a compiler generate the supporting machinery. The DSL was born from this realization. Agicore the runtime grew up around the DSL. Twenty-some declarations now, each one earning its place by appearing in concrete reality often enough to deserve a name. The DSL is the pare-down phase made permanent. The DSL is what cognitive infrastructure looks like when you let the cycle run all the way through.

The fourth generation — the one I am working on now, the one this book is being written alongside — is the rebuilt super app. The original capability, built back up on top of the DSL. Process improvement engagements managed through the DSL. Workflows declared. Entities declared. Governance declared. AI services declared. The runtime generates the machinery. The application code that I used to hand-write into the super app now writes itself out of the declarations. The capability is not just the original capability rebuilt; it is the original capability plus capabilities the original could not afford because the hand-built version cost too much to extend. The rebuild is faster. The rebuild is cleaner. The rebuild does things the first generation could not. The rebuild is the proof.

That is the cycle in one practitioner's working life. Super app. Modular breakdown. DSL and runtime. Rebuilt super app on top of the DSL. The whole thing took years. The whole thing was the necessary path. If you had asked me at the start of generation one to design the DSL that would emerge in generation three, I could not have done it. The DSL was not invented; it was uncovered, by running the messy version long enough that the underlying structure became visible. You don't design cognitive infrastructure. You let it surface. The cycle is how it surfaces.

I want to say something about the rebuild specifically, because the rebuild is the part most people skip and most people who skip it never learn that the abstractions they extracted were wrong. The rebuild is the falsification step. The pare-down phase produces a hypothesis: that the named primitives compress the operational reality correctly. The rebuild tests the hypothesis. If the rebuild becomes faster, the compression was real. If the rebuild becomes more coherent, the primitives were the right primitives. If the rebuild surfaces capabilities the original could not afford, the abstraction layer has earned its keep. If the rebuild crawls — if the original messy code did something the DSL cannot express, if the rebuild requires constant escape hatches to do what the original did naturally — the abstraction layer is wrong and you need to go back to pare-down with different theories. The rebuild is what makes the difference between architecture that compounds and architecture that collects dust.

Most people skip the rebuild because the messy version is already running and they have moved on to other work. This is the failure mode. The messy version keeps running. The abstractions sit in a directory called `architecture/` and nobody calls them. The next project that should have inherited the abstractions doesn't, because the abstractions were never proved by being used. Six months later somebody — maybe you, maybe someone you handed the project to — looks at the abstractions and asks whether they are still relevant, and the answer is unknowable because they have never been operationally tested. The unrun abstraction is worse than no abstraction, because it costs maintenance attention while delivering nothing. The rebuild is the antibody against this.

A pause on the failure mode, because the cycle has a specific way of going wrong and I do not want to develop the technique without naming how it fails. The failure mode is paring down too early. The practitioner gets impatient with the messiness of the build-up phase, decides they can see the recurring patterns after only a handful of cases, and rushes to extract abstractions before the operational reality has supplied enough data to support them. The premature abstractions look right. The practitioner names them, builds the runtime around them, and starts the rebuild. The rebuild is where the failure surfaces. The rebuild requires escape hatches for cases the abstractions did not anticipate. The escape hatches accumulate. The runtime, six months in, is half declarative architecture and half escape hatches, and the escape hatches are doing most of the work. The practitioner has produced an abstraction layer that imposes a tax without providing the leverage that would have justified the tax. The diagnosis is always the same: the build-up phase did not run long enough. The practitioner pared down before the recurring shapes had recurred enough to be trustworthy.

The defense against the failure mode is patience that does not feel like patience. The build-up phase has to run until the practitioner is bored of writing the same workarounds and the workarounds have settled into a clear pattern. Bored is the diagnostic. The boredom is the signal that the operational reality has supplied enough data to support the pare-down. Before the boredom, the abstractions are guesses. After the boredom, the abstractions are extracted from data. The difference matters. The cycle does not run on the practitioner's eagerness to extract abstractions; the cycle runs on the operational pressure that supplies the data the abstractions compress. The practitioner who paces the cycle correctly produces durable abstractions on a slower timeline than the practitioner who rushes. The slower timeline beats the faster one on every measurable axis a year later, because the slower one's abstractions are real and the faster one's abstractions are wrong.

Why has this cycle been impossible until now. The honest answer is the economics. Pre-AI, the rebuild phase was so expensive that almost no one ran it. Tearing apart a working system, extracting frameworks, then rebuilding the original capability on top of the frameworks required teams, years, and rewrites that the business case could rarely justify. The companies that pulled it off — the rare ones — became platform companies, because the cycle produced cognitive infrastructure that compounded across products. Everyone else either skipped the rebuild and accumulated abstractions that aged badly, or skipped the pare-down entirely and lived with the messy version forever. Both failure modes were rational responses to the prevailing economics. The economics no longer prevail.

AI dramatically changes the cost of every phase of the cycle. The first build phase is faster because the AI absorbs the friction of typing out the duct tape. The pare-down phase is faster because the AI can scan across the messy code and surface candidate recurrences for you to evaluate. The rebuild phase is faster because the AI can generate the rebuilt capability against the new abstractions as quickly as you can declare the abstractions. For the first time in the history of software, a single practitioner can run the full cycle in a working timeframe — months instead of years, one person instead of a team, with the rebuild proving the abstractions instead of the abstractions outliving their relevance unproved. The cycle becomes economically tractable at the individual scale. That is the change.

What you should do with the change. Pick a project that is messy. Resist the urge to rebuild it cleanly before the messiness has earned the rebuild. Let it run. Watch where the same shapes keep recurring. When you have enough operational reality to see the recurrences, name them. Promote the duct tape into a small language that describes what the system was doing all along. Then rebuild on top of the small language. If the rebuild is faster and cleaner than the original, your abstractions were real and you have just produced infrastructure that the next project will inherit. If the rebuild crawls, your abstractions were wrong and you have just learned something important about which patterns are real and which were artifacts of one specific implementation. Either outcome is productive. Both outcomes require running the rebuild. The rebuild is non-optional.

A note on the recurring pattern across non-software domains, because the cycle is universal and I want the universality to be visible rather than implied. The same shape shows up in writing. The first version of a piece of long-form work — the first novella, the first textbook, the first long-form essay — is built up concretely, often clumsily, against the actual material the writer has access to. The recurring shapes become visible only after the first version exists. The chapter break that keeps wanting to land in the same structural position. The transition that keeps using the same rhythm. The argument that keeps following the same three-step shape. These are the primitives the writer extracts in the pare-down phase. The next book, written on top of the extracted primitives, is faster and structurally cleaner because the writer is no longer inventing the bones of each chapter from scratch — the bones have been named and the writer can compose against them. The same cycle. The same payoff. The economics are different from software but the structure is identical.

The same shape shows up in organizational work. A first attempt at a process — a customer onboarding flow, an internal escalation protocol, a release procedure — gets built concretely and operates messily for some number of weeks or months. The shapes the process actually contains become visible only after the first version has been pressure-tested by reality. The pare-down phase extracts the actual primitives: the handoffs that matter, the checkpoints that prevent regressions, the escalation conditions that turned out to fire most often. The rebuilt process — version two of the onboarding flow, with the primitives named and the handoffs standardized — runs faster and breaks less. The same cycle. Process improvement work has known this for a century under different names. AI changes the economics of running the cycle, but the cycle itself is not new. The newness is the ability of a single practitioner to run it without organizational support, which is the structural shift this part of the book is about.

One more domain, because the third example clinches the pattern. Research. A first investigation into a research question is built up concretely against whatever the researcher has at hand — methods borrowed from adjacent fields, instruments not quite calibrated for the specific question, analytic frames imported from prior work. The investigation produces a result, often a messy one. The pare-down phase extracts the methodological primitives the investigation actually depended on — the analysis technique that turned out to be load-bearing, the measurement protocol that surfaced the relevant variance, the framing that made the result legible. The next investigation, built on the extracted methodological primitives, produces a result faster and more cleanly. Methodologies in mature research fields are exactly the pare-down output of generations of investigations. The same cycle. Generations to build up a methodology in the pre-AI era; quarters or months to build one up in the post-AI era. The compression is what makes the cycle tractable for individual practitioners in domains where it used to be tractable only for institutions.

I want to close with a smaller version of the same cycle, because the genealogy version is at architectural scale and the same logic operates at session scale too. Inside a single working day, the cycle plays out in miniature. You build up a working approach to today's problem with whatever materials are at hand. The approach is messy. You let it run against the actual problem. You watch what it does. Mid-afternoon, you notice that you have written the same three lines of prompt four different times in slightly different configurations. You pare down. You make the three lines a named primitive — a snippet, a template, a skill doc entry. You build back up: the rest of the afternoon's work uses the primitive. The cycle compounded in one day. By the end of the week, you have a small library of named primitives that did not exist on Monday. By the end of the month, you have a working notebook that contains the primitives most of your work depends on. By the end of the year, you have a personal cognitive infrastructure that the next year's work will inherit. The cycle scales down. The discipline is the same at every scale. Build. Pare. Rebuild. Repeat.

**The Bottom Line:** Build reality first. Extract abstractions second. Rebuild on top, and let the rebuild prove the abstractions. The cycle has been impossible for individual practitioners for the entire history of the field. It is tractable now. Run it.
