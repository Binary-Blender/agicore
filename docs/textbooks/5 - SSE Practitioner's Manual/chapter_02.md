# Chapter 2: Think — Morning Calibration

The first phase of the daily practice happens before I touch the keyboard. It takes about five minutes. It is the highest-leverage five minutes of my working day, and I skipped it for two years before I understood why my output kept feeling random.

The setup is plain. Before I open the model, before I open the project, before I open anything, I sit and answer four questions in writing. I keep a single text file open for this. The questions never change. The answers do. The act of answering them out loud, on the page, in actual sentences rather than ambient intention, is the move. The five minutes is not for thinking; it is for committing the thinking to a form that can constrain the rest of the day.

The four questions are these. What is the most important thing I am creating today. Which framework am I bringing to the work. What does good enough look like. What am I not doing. In that order. Each one does different cognitive work and each one has been refined by my having skipped it on enough days to know what the day looks like when you skip it.

The first question — what is the most important thing I am creating today — is not asking what is most urgent. Urgency will dictate itself if I let it. The question is what is the single architectural piece that represents genuine, non-linear progress. The Slack message is not it. The status update is not it. The morning review of yesterday's output is not it. The question is naming the one move whose completion would let me say, at the end of the day, that the day produced something. Most days have one such move. Some days have none, and naming that honestly is its own kind of useful — if today is a maintenance day, knowing it is a maintenance day before the maintenance starts prevents the maintenance from being smuggled into the project's ledger as forward progress.

The second question is about which framework I am bringing to the workspace. I work in one of three modes, and they require completely different settings inside my head and completely different prompting moves. Production Mode is when I am building something that needs to ship. The quality bar is hardened. The constraints are tight. The exploration radius is small. I am pulling the model toward a known target, refining against a specification, hardening against edge cases. Exploration Mode is the opposite. The quality bar is loose on purpose. The constraints are deliberately under-specified. The exploration radius is wide. I am letting the model surface coherent structures I did not know to ask for, harvesting what stabilizes, discarding what does not. QC Mode is a third mode entirely. I am not generating. I am auditing. The model is reviewing what I produced yesterday, or what I produced this morning, or what some other model produced. The cognitive stance is adversarial in a useful way. Suspicion is on. Surface enthusiasm is off. I am looking for the failure my own taste did not catch.

Each of these modes wants different prompts, different model selection, different temperature, different reading discipline. Production Mode wants tighter constraint chains and lower temperature. Exploration Mode wants looser prompts and a stronger model with a wider context. QC Mode wants a different model than the one that did the generation, because a model auditing its own output tends to ratify its own choices. The reason to name the mode at the start of the day is that the mode determines almost everything about how I read what the model produces. The same output can be acceptable in Exploration Mode and unacceptable in Production Mode. If I do not name the mode in the morning, I find myself accepting Exploration-grade output as Production output, which is the silent quality leak that eats the most expensive practitioners alive.

The third question — what does good enough look like — is the question most people skip, and it is the most important of the four. I write down, in concrete language, what the output has to look like for me to ship it. Not aspirationally. Not the version where the work is beautiful. The actual minimum the artifact has to satisfy to be ready to leave my desk. The reason this question is non-optional is the sunk-cost fallacy, which operates in AI work with brutal efficiency.

Here is the failure mode. I sit down without a quality bar. I prompt. The model produces something. The something is roughly adequate. I have already invested ten minutes. If I reject this and start over, I have to invest another ten minutes. The mind, which is a cost-minimization engine masquerading as a judgment engine, finds reasons that the roughly adequate output is actually fine. It is fine. It is shipping. The quality bar was never set, so the quality bar is whatever the first competent-looking output happened to deliver. The day produces output. The output is not what I would have produced if I had asked, before generation started, what I actually wanted. Over a year of this, the practitioner has thousands of artifacts none of which would meet the standard the practitioner would have set if the standard had been set before the generation began. The practitioner blames the model. The practitioner is wrong; the practitioner blames the model because the practitioner did not set the bar.

The defense is mechanical. Set the bar before the model produces anything. Write it down. Specifically. For a paragraph I am drafting, the bar might be: must include the concrete worked example, must name the failure mode, must not exceed three hundred words, must read aloud cleanly. For a piece of code, the bar might be: must pass the existing tests, must add the new failure mode to the test suite, must not introduce new dependencies. For a feature spec, the bar might be: must name the user behavior under three specific load conditions, must specify the failure-recovery branch, must name the telemetry that will be added. The specifics matter less than the act of writing them down before the model starts producing. The bar set in writing is the bar I will hold to. The bar held in my head is the bar that adapts to whatever the model surfaces.

The fourth question is the Anti-Plan. What am I explicitly not doing today. This is the question I undervalued longest. The Anti-Plan is the protection of cognitive RAM. Every working day has a finite number of high-quality decisions in it. I have come to think of decision capacity less as a battery that drains and more as a single working register that gets occupied by whatever I look at. When my attention is on the project I named in question one, the register is occupied by the project. When my attention slides to the tempting tangent — the small bug I noticed yesterday, the prompt template I wanted to refactor, the documentation page that has been bothering me — the register is now occupied by the tangent and the project is gone from working memory. Bringing the project back into working memory is not free; it costs the kind of attention I had hoped to spend on the project.

The Anti-Plan names the tangents I am explicitly not allowing today. The minor tweak that has been in the back of my head for a week. The interesting idea that surfaced in yesterday's session that I have not yet built into anything. The status of the side project that lives in a different repo. The morning email that I am pretending I will check briefly. The half-finished sketch I want to revisit. All of it written down, all of it banned from working memory for the day. The act of writing them down does most of the work. The mind that has named a tangent and put it on paper stops carrying the tangent as background load. The tangent gets revisited at the end of the day when the Observe phase runs and the day's commitments are no longer at risk.

The Anti-Plan has the second-largest effect on my working output of any practice I run, after the quality bar. The first time I noticed the effect was on a day I had committed to writing four sections of a long technical document. By the time I closed the laptop that evening, I had written one section, refactored a build script, partially fixed two unrelated bugs, opened three browser tabs about a feature I had been considering, and started but not finished a Slack message to a collaborator about a different project entirely. None of those tangents were unreasonable on their own. Together they ate the day. The Anti-Plan the next morning named all five tangents as forbidden. I wrote three sections. The day worked.

Five minutes. Four questions in writing. The output of the Think phase is four lines of text I can return to during the day when I notice that I am drifting. Most days the drift starts somewhere around the second hour and is not about the project at all. The drift is the working register being pulled toward whatever the next email surfaces, whatever the model produces that nudges me sideways, whatever ambient anxiety I happened to bring to the keyboard. The Anti-Plan is the four lines that say no.

A note on production mode versus exploration mode specifically, because I have had to learn this the hard way. The two modes are not equally valuable on every day, but they are equally valuable across a working week. Practitioners who run only Production Mode produce volume but their output starts to taste the same after months because they are never letting the model surface anything they did not already know to ask for. Practitioners who run only Exploration Mode produce interesting fragments but never ship anything because the fragments never get hardened. The discipline of the morning calibration is partly the discipline of letting the right mode be the day's mode rather than letting the previous day's residue determine it. Some days are exploration days. Some days are production days. Some days are QC days. Naming the day's mode in the morning is the move that lets the day's mode actually run.

A small note on the failure mode of the calibration itself. The calibration can become its own form of procrastination if the practitioner lets it grow. The four questions answered in five minutes are the calibration. The forty-five-minute journaling session is something else entirely and is not what this chapter is teaching. If the calibration is taking longer than ten minutes, the calibration is not being run; some other practice is being run that has eaten the calibration's name. Keep it short. The five minutes is the form. Honor the form.

A note on the sunk-cost fallacy in AI work specifically, because the fallacy operates here with a particular cruelty that is worth naming directly. In pre-AI knowledge work, the sunk cost of a piece of output was high — hours of writing, days of design, weeks of code. The high cost made the temptation to ratify mediocre output measurable but rare. In AI-assisted work, the sunk cost of any single output is much lower — usually a few minutes of prompting. The lower cost makes the temptation feel cheaper to resist. It is not. The lower per-output cost means the practitioner is producing many more outputs per day, and the sunk-cost fallacy now operates many more times per day, each time ratifying a small amount of below-bar output. The aggregate is large. A practitioner who ratifies one slightly-below-bar paragraph per hour for a forty-hour week has produced forty paragraphs of below-bar work. The forty paragraphs are not visibly bad; each is competent. The body of work the forty paragraphs constitute is significantly below what the same practitioner would have produced with the bar set in advance. The morning calibration's quality bar is the only structural defense against this aggregation. The bar set in writing in the morning is the bar the practitioner returns to throughout the day; the bar held only in the practitioner's head adapts to whatever the model surfaces and stops being a defense.

A note on why most builders skip this phase entirely, because the skipping is the norm rather than the exception and the reason is worth naming. The default morning shape for most working knowledge-workers is reactive. The day starts with the inbox. The day continues with the messages that accumulated overnight. The first decisions of the day are decisions about what other people have surfaced. The practitioner's own intentions for the day are negotiated in fragments around the surfaced material, and by the time the practitioner gets to their actual work, the working register is already occupied by half a dozen low-stakes contexts the practitioner did not choose. The morning calibration is the deliberate refusal to start the day reactively. Five minutes before the inbox. Four questions before any external surface. The phase happens early specifically so the day's working register is occupied by the practitioner's own priorities before anyone else's priorities have a chance to load. The skipping is not laziness; the skipping is the default current of the working world, and the calibration is a small dam against the current. The dam has to be built every morning because the current resets every night.

A note on the morning calibration's effect over months, because the effect compounds in a specific way I want to mark. After a week of running it, the calibration feels like a small overhead. After a month, the four questions start producing the answers themselves — the practitioner has internalized the shape of the questions enough that the morning thinking is faster than the writing-down. After three months, the calibration file becomes a working record of the practitioner's actual priorities across the quarter. Patterns emerge. The most-important-thing question, answered for sixty days, surfaces what the practitioner's working life is actually committed to versus what they tell themselves they are committed to. The Anti-Plan, accumulated for sixty days, surfaces the tangents that keep recurring — the same minor refactor that has been in the Anti-Plan for two months and never gets done. The calibration is not just the day's protection; it is a long-term diagnostic. The file is private. The diagnostic is for the practitioner alone. The patterns surface honestly because nobody is being performed for.

A practical note about tooling, because the tooling does not matter and people ask. I use a single plain text file. The file is named with the current date. I keep the previous month's files in a folder. I do not use a structured journaling app. I do not use a database. I do not use anything that would let me lose the entries to a vendor change or a subscription lapse. The discipline is the practice; the tool is the simplest possible vessel for it. The practitioner who is choosing between four journaling apps to find the right one for the morning calibration is not yet running the calibration. The calibration runs in any text editor. Run it in any text editor. Optimize the tool later if the practice surfaces a specific need the tool is failing.

The move you make tomorrow morning is this. Open a text file. Write today's date at the top. Write down, in plain sentences, the four answers — most important thing, framework, quality bar, Anti-Plan. Then close the file and start the first session. At the end of the day, open the file again. The Observe phase, two chapters from now, is the practice that turns those four lines into compounding infrastructure.

**The Bottom Line:** The five-minute morning calibration sets the quality ceiling for the entire working day. Skip it and you accept whatever the day surfaces.
