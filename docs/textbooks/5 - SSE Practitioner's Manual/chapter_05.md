# Chapter 5: Steering, Not Policing

The single most expensive habit I see in working engineers approaching AI for the first time is the reject-regenerate-restart loop. The model produces something. The something is not exactly what the engineer wanted. The engineer rejects it. The engineer regenerates. The new something is also not exactly what the engineer wanted. The engineer rejects again. The engineer restarts the conversation. The new conversation produces an output not noticeably better than the first. The engineer leaves frustrated, having done thirty minutes of work and produced nothing they would commit to a repository or send to a colleague.

I have done this. I did it for months. The loop feels productive while you are inside it because each rejection looks like the exercise of judgment. *I have standards. I am not accepting this. I am demanding better.* That story is what the loop sells you. The story is wrong. The loop is not the exercise of judgment. The loop is the systematic destruction of every condition under which the model could have produced what you actually wanted.

The first thing the loop destroys is architectural momentum. When the model produced its first output, there was a context behind that output — assumptions the model had absorbed from the prompt, constraints it had inferred from the framing, a working hypothesis about what kind of thing was being built. Some of that context was wrong; that is why the output was imperfect. But most of it was right. When you reject and restart, you do not selectively discard the wrong parts of the context. You discard all of it. The next generation has to reconstruct the right parts from scratch. It usually fails to reconstruct them as completely as the previous generation had them. The third generation is structurally worse than the first.

The second thing the loop destroys is semantic continuity. Each rejection is a small rupture in the conversation's working frame. The model is trying to figure out what you want from a working frame that is being repeatedly fragmented. The longer the loop runs, the less of a working frame remains, and the more generic the outputs become. By the fifth rejection, the conversation has lost the specific routing that made the first output even nominally relevant, and the model is generating from the broad center of the distribution. The outputs at this point are interchangeable. The loop has produced a session in which the model is no longer responding to your problem at all; it is responding to its best guess about what kind of generic response your fragmented prompt history is consistent with.

The third thing the loop destroys is exploratory flow. The first output had some unexpected element in it — some piece of the response that surfaced something you had not specifically requested. That element was a discovery candidate. It was the most valuable single piece of the response, more valuable than the parts that satisfied your stated requirements, because it was the part you would not have asked for and would not have gotten any other way. Rejecting the output discards the discovery candidate along with the parts you did not want. You never noticed it was there. The loop is structurally biased against the most valuable thing the model produces.

The fourth thing the loop destroys is the trajectory itself. A conversation with a model is a path through a topology of related concepts. The first output established a position in the topology. The next prompt — if it were a steering move rather than a rejection — would move you to a related position, closer to what you wanted. The position after that would be closer still. The trajectory was working its way toward the target through a sequence of incremental positions. The reject-regenerate-restart loop is not a trajectory. It is a series of attempts to land on the target in a single jump, each starting from approximately the same starting position as the last. The starting position never moves closer to the target. The jumps never land. The loop converges on nothing.

I learned to recognize this loop in myself because I noticed the somatic signature of it. There is a specific flavor of frustration that fires when the loop is running. It feels like being right — like the model is being stupid and I am being demanding in a useful way — and that flavor is the diagnostic. The frustration is the signal that I am policing instead of steering. The corrective is not to try harder. The corrective is to stop the loop, leave the imperfect output in place, and write a different kind of prompt — one that takes the imperfect output as a starting position and moves the trajectory toward where I want to be.

This is what I mean by the distinction between steering and policing. Policing treats each output as a binary correctness event. Good output, keep it. Bad output, destroy it. The model is on trial. The session is a series of verdicts. Steering treats outputs as positions in a trajectory. No output is the final answer; every output is a way-point. The next prompt's job is to move the trajectory toward where the practitioner wants it to be, taking whatever is useful from the current position with it. The model is not on trial. The session is an evolving system. The judgment that fires after each output is not is-this-correct; it is what-move-comes-next.

The shift is mental-model-deep. It is not a technique you can install in an afternoon by reading about it. It requires the practitioner to stop treating the AI as a deterministic tool that should produce the right answer on each call. It requires accepting that the AI is a probabilistic cognition substrate whose value comes from how the practitioner moves it through the topology over multiple turns. The practitioner who internalizes this works differently — they think differently about what counts as a successful session, they evaluate outputs differently, they construct prompts differently.

Experienced engineers struggle with this shift more than beginners do. The reason is structural. Decades of deterministic-tooling habits have trained the senior engineer to expect that good inputs produce correct outputs and incorrect outputs indicate incorrect inputs. The reflex is to debug — to find the precise specification that will produce the precise correct output. The reflex is correct for compilers, databases, and APIs. The reflex is wrong for probabilistic cognition systems, because the probabilistic system's outputs are not determined by the input in the way the deterministic system's outputs are. The same input will produce different outputs across runs; the same input on a slightly different context will produce dramatically different outputs; the system is responding to the entire conversational state, not just to the most recent prompt.

The senior engineer's reflex applied to this system is the reject-regenerate loop. *That output is wrong. Therefore my input must be wrong. Therefore I will refine my input and regenerate.* The story makes deep engineering sense. It is precisely how a compiler should be debugged. It is wrong for the model not because the engineer's discipline is wrong but because the system being engineered is structurally different. The probabilistic system does not benefit from input refinement the way the deterministic system does. The probabilistic system benefits from trajectory steering — from accepting the current output as a position and prompting from that position toward the target — in a way the deterministic system does not.

This is why experienced engineers often produce dramatically worse output from these models than less-experienced people who happen to have less deterministic-tooling reflex to overcome. The experienced engineer's reflexes are working against them. The beginner has no reflexes, takes the first output as a starting position, asks a follow-up question, gets a better output, and keeps iterating. The engineer rejects, refines, regenerates, restarts, and produces nothing. The beginner's lack of reflex is, on this specific axis, an advantage that takes the engineer six months to undo.

The mental-model shift I had to make explicit was this. The model is not a function. It is a substrate. A function takes an input and produces an output. A substrate is a medium through which the practitioner moves an evolving artifact. The practitioner's job with a substrate is to direct the evolution, not to debug the function call. Direction happens over many turns. Direction takes the current position as the starting point and moves toward a better position. Direction never restarts from zero unless the current trajectory is fundamentally unsalvageable — which, in my experience, is rare. Most trajectories that feel unsalvageable are trajectories that the practitioner has been policing rather than steering.

A concrete example, because the abstraction is not enough. I was working on a section of documentation. The first draft the model produced was structurally fine but tonally wrong — it had drifted into the formal-spec register when I wanted the working-engineer register. The policing reflex was to reject the draft and reissue the prompt with stronger tone constraints. I caught myself reaching for that move. Instead, I left the draft in place and wrote a different prompt: *That draft is structurally close. The tone is wrong — too formal. Rewrite it in the same structure, in the voice of a working engineer telling another engineer how they actually do this thing.* The next draft kept the structure, fixed the tone, and was usable. The whole exchange took two turns and four minutes. The reject-regenerate version would have taken twenty minutes and produced a tonally-correct draft that had lost the structural quality of the first draft. Steering preserved both axes. Policing would have traded one for the other.

This example is small. The compounding effect across a working day is large. Practitioners who steer produce three to five times the usable output per hour as practitioners who police. Across a working week the gap widens. Across a year the practices produce entirely different working lives.

The next chapter develops the canonical positive move that replaces the reject-regenerate loop. Its name is Progressive Constraint Steering and it is the central single technique of this book. The chapter you just read is its precondition. You cannot run Progressive Constraint Steering while still policing the model — the policing reflex will interrupt the steering trajectory before the steering has had time to work. The work of this chapter is to install the steering stance. The work of the next chapter is to teach the move that the stance enables.

A note on the failure mode of the steering stance, because the stance has its own failure mode and I want to name it before I leave it. The failure is the practitioner who has internalized "steering, not policing" and uses it as license to accept poor output indefinitely. That is not steering. Steering is the active redirection of an evolving trajectory toward a target the practitioner has already defined. The morning calibration's quality bar from chapter two is the target. Steering is the work of moving the trajectory toward the bar. The practitioner who has dropped the bar in the name of "not policing the model" has not learned steering. They have learned to ratify whatever the model produces. The output of that practitioner is no better than the output of the rejection-loop practitioner; it is just produced with less friction.

The distinction is this. The steering practitioner has a target. The policing practitioner is debugging against a target. The ratifying practitioner has dropped the target. The steering practitioner produces output that meets the target through a multi-turn trajectory. The policing practitioner produces nothing through a series of futile single-turn debugging attempts. The ratifying practitioner produces volume of output that no one would publish under their name. All three look superficially similar from outside. The difference is whether the target is present, active, and being moved toward — or absent, abandoned, or fought-with-but-not-moved-toward.

A note on why this chapter is positioned where it is in the book, because the position is deliberate and not arbitrary. The Daily Practice chapters established the operating system the rest of the book runs on top of. The morning calibration sets the target. The Attune-phase mechanics provide the somatic signals that fire when the trajectory is working or failing. The end-of-day audit converts the day's experience into compounding infrastructure. Each of those phases assumes the practitioner is steering rather than policing. The morning quality bar is a target the practitioner steers the trajectory toward; the Attune mechanics are the in-flight signals for adjusting the steering; the audit catches the specific steering moves that worked. The whole framework presupposes the steering stance. The reason this chapter, on the steering stance itself, comes after the Daily Practice chapters is that the practitioner who reads about the stance abstractly will not internalize it. The practitioner who is already running the morning calibration and the Attune mechanics will read this chapter and recognize what they have already started doing. The stance describes itself naturally as the practice runs.

A note on the somatic signature of the steering stance, because the stance has one and learning to recognize it is most of how the stance installs. When I am running the policing loop, my body is leaning forward and tight. I am scanning for errors. The breath is short. The cursor is hovering over the regenerate button. When I am running the steering stance, the body is calmer. I am reading the model's output as information about a position, not as a verdict about competence. The breath is normal. The cursor is moving toward the next prompt rather than the regenerate button. The two stances have different physical signatures and the practitioner can learn to notice which one is firing. The noticing is the diagnostic. If the body is in policing-stance, the next move is to pause, breathe, and consciously shift into steering-stance before issuing the next prompt. The shift takes about ten seconds. It changes the prompt that gets typed.

A note on what makes this shift particularly hard for experienced engineers, beyond the deterministic-tooling reflex named above. The reflex is structural; there is also an emotional component that is worth naming directly. Experienced engineers have spent their careers being correct. The senior engineer's competence is partly the competence of being able to identify wrong outputs immediately and reject them. The competence is real and it is hard-earned. Asking the senior engineer to stop rejecting wrong outputs feels like asking them to stop using the skill that defined their career. The ask is misframed. The skill is not being asked to stop; the skill is being asked to fire in a different way. The senior engineer's quality detection is still firing. The detection is producing the diagnosis that the current output is not at the target. The change is that the diagnosis now produces a steering move rather than a rejection. The skill is intact. The skill's downstream behavior is what changes. Framing the shift this way makes it easier for engineers to accept; the work of installing the shift is still real, but the practitioner is not being asked to discard a hard-won capability.

The move you make tomorrow is this. When the model returns an output that is not what you wanted, do not reject it. Leave it on the screen. Read the four-question audit you wrote this morning, look at the quality bar, and write the next prompt as a steering move — as the move that takes the current output as a starting position and moves the trajectory toward the bar. The first time you do this, it will feel wrong. The reject reflex will fire. Run the steering move anyway. After a week, the steering reflex will start firing first, and the reject reflex will start firing only on the trajectories that are genuinely unsalvageable. That ratio — steering reflex first, reject reflex only for genuine unsalvageability — is what working AI practice looks like when the mental-model shift has actually installed.

**The Bottom Line:** Stop fighting the model. Steer the trajectory.
