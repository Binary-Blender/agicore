# Chapter 5: The Offer

Knox restarted the Wedge.

He backed it down the spur to the Forest Service gate. He waited for a pickup pulling a flatbed of cedar shake to pass on State Route 104, then pulled out west and headed up the Hood Canal. The rain had thickened a notch. The wipers were running on the auto setting at the lower of two intervals. The Hood Canal was a wide flat gray to the right of the Wedge through the wet glass and the Olympic foothills behind it were a darker gray under the cloud deck.

They drove nine miles in silence.

Knox routed off State Route 104 at a turnout sign above the south end of the Dosewallips delta. The turnout was a gravel apron set on the shoulder above a forty-foot drop to the water. Two picnic tables in the rain. One concrete bollard. No other vehicle. Knox pulled in, swung the Wedge around to face the water, killed the ignition. The wipers stopped. The rain came down on the windshield in the slow heavy drops that fell in the cold air off the Olympics in late May.

Knox turned in his seat.

He said *Three things.*

Cole said *Go.*

**Thing one.**

Knox said *I'm loaning you my anomaly investigation team. No charge. No press release. Eleven people. The team is run by my chief reliability engineer, a woman named Soo-Ah Park, Korean-American, MIT then JPL then Vantage from 2009, I poached her off the Mars Science Laboratory team and put her on our first stage-recovery failure investigation in 2011 and she has run every catastrophic-loss investigation at Vantage since, including the one on LC-39A in 2016 that found the COPV failure mode three weeks before SpaceX would have found it on their own, which she did not say at the time and which I am not saying now and which you are going to forget I said the second this drive ends. She is the best in the world at this. She lands at Melbourne at twenty hundred Eastern tonight on the Vantage Falcon. She walks the slag with Reggie's team at oh six hundred Eastern Saturday morning. She gives Reggie everything she finds. She gives me nothing she finds that Reggie has not first cleared as releasable. I do not see a single byte of Vector data from this investigation. Park reports to Reggie. Park reports to Reggie alone. The cover is that she is on personal leave. The actual situation is what I just said.*

Cole said *Why.*

Knox said *Because if your anomaly turns into a six-month Congressional hearing the Artemis IV bid window closes for both of us. NASA picks Boeing on the lander, NASA picks ULA on the heavy. Boeing is not ready. ULA is not ready. Boeing has never been ready. The lunar surface stays empty till the back end of the decade. The Chinese put a flag on the Aitken Basin in 2028. We both lose. I am not in the business of losing to Boeing.*

Cole said *Accepted.*

Knox said *Good.*

Knox pulled his phone from the front pocket of the hoodie. He sent a single message to a contact called *S Park.* The message was four words long. Cole did not see the screen.

Knox said *Park is in the air at twenty-one hundred Eastern. She'll text Reggie direct when she's wheels-down at Melbourne. Reggie will know it's coming because I'm going to text Reggie myself in about ninety seconds.*

Cole said *Text Reggie now. I do not want a surprise on her ground.*

Knox said *Smart.*

Knox sent a second message. He put the phone face down on the console.

**Thing two.**

Knox said *Second thing. I'm posting a public statement at five PM Pacific tonight. Three paragraphs. Vantage publicly backs Vector. Vantage says heavy-lift development is hard, Vector is the most disciplined organization in the industry outside of Vantage, and Vantage will be the first paying commercial customer to fly a payload on Vector Heavy when Vector Heavy returns to flight. Draft is on the phone. I want you to read it. If you tell me not to post it I won't post it. If you tell me to change a word I'll think about changing a word. If you tell me to post it, I post it.*

He picked the phone back up. He swiped to the draft. He turned the screen to Cole.

Cole read the draft.

The draft was three paragraphs. The first paragraph opened with two sentences about heavy-lift development. The second paragraph was the operative one. The third paragraph was the customer-commitment sentence. The draft was dated Friday 29 May Year 0, 17:00 PT, signed by Knox in the position of *Founder & CEO, Vantage Aerospace.*

Cole read it twice.

He looked up.

Cole said *Knox.*

Knox said *Yeah.*

Cole said *Paragraph two. The line about the most disciplined organization in the industry outside of Vantage. Strike outside of Vantage. Leave it the most disciplined organization in the industry.*

Knox looked at Cole.

Knox grinned.

Knox said *No.*

Cole said *Knox.*

Knox said *Cole. I cannot lie. Even to help you. Vantage is more disciplined than Vector. You and I both know it. Reggie knows it. Hiro Tanaka knows it. Wade Ferris knows it. The reason your trench held last night and the reason your pad did not is the same reason — your team has the discipline to win the eleven-month internal fight on the cooling-line spec and not have the discipline to win the eleven-month internal fight on the methane-tank vent geometry, both of which are fights I know about because Soo-Ah has been benchmarking your program against ours since 2022. We're the more disciplined organization. The line is the line. If I take outside of Vantage out, the line is a lie. I'm not going to lie in a public statement on the day after your pad event. The line stands or the statement doesn't go out. Your call.*

Cole looked at Knox for four seconds.

The rain on the windshield was the rain.

Cole said *Post it.*

Knox said *Posting at five.*

He swiped the draft into the queue. The phone went face down on the console again.

**Thing three.**

Knox reached into the inside pocket of the hoodie. He pulled out a single folded sheet of paper, ivory, the embossed sun-rune of Helios in the upper-left corner, no other markings on the page. He unfolded it on the center console between them. On the sheet was one name written in Knox's handwriting. Black ink. Block capitals. Eight letters in the first name. Seven in the second.

Cole looked at the name.

He looked at it for two seconds.

He looked up at Knox.

Knox said *My intel desk has been hearing that name in three places in the last four weeks. The first place is a Cypriot wire structure with a passthrough in Limassol that took an unusual long-dated put position on Carrick at the start of May through a Geneva intermediary that the FINRA monitoring desk in New York is not going to flag because the structure is shaped to clear the FINRA threshold by sixty basis points. The position becomes profitable on a Carrick close below ninety-eight dollars before September. The second place is a contractor crew working out of Vilnius that an old colleague of yours at the FBI Seattle field office flagged for me last December as having taken a contract that did not have a known principal behind it, the colleague's name I am not going to say in this car but you know who I mean, the contract value was high seven figures and the work product was open-ended, the crew has been silent since January. The third place is a private intelligence consultancy in Tel Aviv that has been buying technical reads on Vector's vendor base, specifically on the methane-tank vendor and the GSE software vendor, for the previous six weeks. The consultancy bills its work to a holding company in Limassol. The holding company is named in the wire structure I mentioned in place one. I have no evidence the three threads connect to each other. I have no evidence any of them connect to last night. I have three threads that if you pulled on them might or might not braid. I am giving you the name because if it were me, I would want it.*

Cole read the name a second time.

He did not write it down.

He closed his eyes for two seconds.

He opened them.

Cole said *I have it.*

Knox said *Good.*

Knox pulled a small lighter from the same hoodie pocket. The lighter was a brushed-steel rectangle, no logo, no engraving, the kind of lighter a fastidious man bought at a stationer's in Geneva and replaced once every three years because the wick burned down. Knox flicked the wheel. The flame caught at one inch. He held it to the corner of the sheet of paper. The flame took the embossed sun-rune first, then the ivory of the page, then the ink of the name. The paper curled inward as it burned. Knox set the burning sheet on a small flat steel coaster Cole had not noticed was in the cupholder until Knox set the paper on it. The paper burned down to ash in nineteen seconds. Knox brushed the ash into the cupholder. He put the lighter back in the hoodie pocket. He put the coaster back in the cupholder.

Knox said *That conversation did not happen.*

Cole said *Understood.*

They sat looking at the Olympics across the canal for three minutes without speaking. The rain came harder. The wipers ran once on the auto setting at the higher interval. A bald eagle crossed the airspace above the canal on a slow northeast track. Cole watched it cross.

Knox started the engine.

Knox said *I'll get you back to Boeing by three.*

Cole said *Thank you.*

They drove back south.

Knox ran the countersurveillance suite for the full return. The rolling counters at the bottom of the secondary readout cycled the way they had cycled on the way out. Mara was in the chase eight hundred yards back at the start, two thousand yards back across the ferry, eight hundred again on the run down I-5. The suite flagged two new vehicles at separate points on the return — a black Tahoe that held the same speed for eighty-three seconds before falling away at the Edmonds exit, and a white Sprinter van that held for sixty-one before turning off at Aurora. Neither held long enough to cross the ninety-second threshold. Neither cleared the flag. Knox said nothing about either of them. Cole noted both.

The Wedge pulled back onto the Boeing apron at three oh four PM.

Knox shut the Wedge down. They sat for two seconds in the cabin in the silence of the unpowered vehicle. Knox unbuckled. Cole unbuckled. They got out.

They stood on the apron in the rain. The Vantage Gulfstream was fueled and crewed twenty yards away with the door open and the air-stair down and the pilot at the top of the stair in the doorway in the pilot's hat. Knox put his hand out.

Knox said *Cole. I meant the line. I am sorry about your pad.*

Cole said *Knox. Thank you for the drive.*

They shook hands. The handshake ran a fraction longer than the standard one and a quarter seconds. They released.

Knox walked to the air-stair. He climbed up two steps. He turned back.

He said *Cole.*

Cole said *Yes.*

Knox said *The sixth countersurveillance counter. Over the beer in Bel Air. The first weekend you can clear. Bring Annika. Tahirih makes a Persian rice that you will eat for three days afterward.*

Cole said *I will clear the weekend.*

Knox said *Good.*

He climbed the rest of the stair. The door sealed at three oh nine. The engines spooled. The Gulfstream taxied to the south end of the runway. Wheels-up at three twenty-one PM Pacific in the rain. Cole stood on the apron until the aircraft was out of sight to the south on a heading toward the Bay Area.

Mara's Suburban rolled up to the apron at three twenty-four. Cole got in.

Mara said *Boss.*

Cole said *Mara. Boeing Field FBO. The Gulfstream is wheels-up to Cape at six.*

Mara said *Copy.*

She turned the Suburban around. They left the apron.

At three forty-one PM the chase Tahoe and the white Sprinter van were no longer in any of the surveillance feeds Mara's tablet was running.

At five PM Pacific Knox Vandermeer posted his three-paragraph statement to the public feed. The line in paragraph two read *the most disciplined organization in the industry outside of Vantage.* The Carrick stock closed the regular session at ninety-four dollars and was on a halt to trading in extended hours pending the absorption of the Knox post. Owen Friedlander, in his office in Madison Park, read the post at five oh two Pacific, read paragraph two twice, called his counterpart on the Carrick investor-relations desk on a secure line, and said one sentence: *He held the line.* The IR head, a woman named Priya Bhattacharya who had been at Carrick for nine years and who had not yet been promoted to the general-counsel role she would hold by the events of *Carry,* read the post a third time and said *He held what line.*

Owen said *The one that was true.*

He hung up.

At five seventeen Pacific the Carrick stock opened the extended-hours session at ninety-six fifty. It would close at ninety-eight twenty-five on Sunday night. Karen Yoshida would publish a maintain-buy note at six AM Pacific on Monday morning. The Knox post would be the single line she quoted from the news cycle. The Bloomberg chyron from Thursday night would not be quoted by any analyst at any major desk by Monday at noon.

Cole was on the Gulfstream out of Boeing Field at six PM Pacific.

He was over Idaho at seven thirty.
