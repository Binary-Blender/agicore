# Chapter 6: The Rebuild

The Gulfstream descended into Melbourne at five forty-eight AM Eastern.

Cole had slept three hours and twenty minutes on the bed in the aft cabin, having taken the bed at one AM Eastern over the Mississippi after Mara had told him on the cabin intercom that the consolidated SITREP for Vector East had stabilized and that Reggie was no longer in active triage. He had woken at four fifty Eastern on the descent into the Pensacola weather hold. He had washed in the lavatory. He had put the working clothes on — khaki five-pocket pants, gray Vector polo, brown boots, a thin shell against the rain that would not be falling at the Cape that morning. He had eaten an apple at the galley. He had drunk a glass of water. He had walked forward to the forward cabin at five thirty-eight and looked through the right-side window at the dawn coming up over the Atlantic east of the aircraft as the wing dropped through the cloud deck at twelve thousand feet.

The Gulfstream touched at six oh four AM Eastern.

Mara was in the right-hand seat across the aisle in the forward cabin. The pilot's voice came through on the intercom and said *Mr. Westerlund. Welcome to Melbourne. Local time oh six oh four. Local weather clear, eighty-one degrees and rising, wind out of the east at six knots. The Vector ground team is on the apron.*

Cole keyed the intercom. He said *Thank you, Mike.*

He stood up. He took the working jacket off the hook. He took the canvas field bag off the bench. He went down the air-stair into the Florida heat at six oh six AM.

The heat was the heat that came off the apron at six AM at Melbourne in late May, which was the heat of a coastal-Florida pad at the back end of dry season — eighty-one and climbing, the humidity at sixty percent, the air on the lungs as a wet warmth. The smell was the smell of jet fuel and asphalt and the salt that the easterly carried in off the beach two miles east.

Reggie Pace was at the foot of the air-stair.

She was in a gray Vector polo and khaki cargo pants and steel-toed boots and a Vector cap that had been on her head for the previous thirty-three hours. Her left hand was on her hip. Her right hand was on a black portfolio that she had been holding under her arm. She had not slept.

Reggie said *Cole.*

Cole said *Reggie.*

She did not put the hand out for a handshake. He did not put his out for one either. They had worked together for four years. The handshake had stopped being the greeting in 2023. The greeting was the name.

Reggie said *Suburban is at the gate. Hiro is at the pad. Soo-Ah Park came in on the Vantage Falcon at twenty-one fourteen last night and is in the trailer with the Vector reliability team running through the data set. Park has not slept either. Park said and I quote *Reggie, I'll sleep when we have a fault tree.* The data set is preliminary but the first read is interesting and I do not want to characterize it for you in the open. I want to walk the pad with you and tell you on the perimeter road.*

Cole said *The perimeter road.*

Reggie said *The perimeter road.*

They walked to the Suburban.

The Vector security operator at the wheel was a former Marine named Cesar Vincenzo who had been with the Vector East detail for two years and who knew the perimeter road of Pad 47 in the dark, in the rain, with the lights off, and in three weather conditions Cole had never seen at the Cape. Cesar said *Sir, ma'am,* and drove. The Suburban left Melbourne via the back-gate access road, turned north on Highway 1, and ran twelve miles up the coast to the Vector East south entry.

The Vector East complex sat on a leased parcel inside Cape Canaveral Space Force Station, immediately north of the joint NASA-DoD perimeter and immediately south of the cordoned area that had been the Space Force's Launch Complex 36 in the old configuration and that Vector had taken under a thirty-year lease in 2021 and rebuilt at a cost of three-point-eight billion dollars between 2022 and the current quarter. The complex was three pads — Pad 45, Pad 46, Pad 47 — connected by a perimeter road and serviced by the integration facility, the propellant farm, the firing room, and the mobile command post. Pad 47 was the heavy-lift pad. Pad 45 and Pad 46 were rated for the medium-lift Vector Standard and the small-lift Vector Light respectively and were intact and operational.

Pad 47 was a hole in the ground.

The Suburban came up the perimeter road from the south at six twenty-eight AM. The smell hit the Suburban at the half-mile mark.

The smell was the smell of a methane-LOX heavy-lift static fire that had gone catastrophic at T-plus eight seconds thirty-three hours earlier and that had been venting through the wreckage of a tank farm for the intervening day and a half. Propellant residue. Vaporized GSE seals. Scorched concrete. The iron tang of a flame trench that had had its water-cooling lines opened to the atmosphere and that had been allowed to dry under the Florida sun for two full days. The smell carried half a mile down the perimeter road. Reggie did not warn Cole about the smell. Reggie did not need to.

Cesar stopped the Suburban at the south perimeter overlook at six thirty-one AM.

Cole and Reggie got out.

The pad was a hole in the ground.

The launch table was gone. The four lightning towers stood at three of four — the southwest tower had collapsed inward, the top forty feet of it folded across the deck of the pad like a kicked tripod, the remaining structure twisted at the base where the heat had taken the alloy past its yield. The water-deluge holdpond had been punctured in two places — the south wall and the east — and the pond had drained into the perimeter swale and was a stained brown rectangle of dry concrete. The strongback — the steel A-frame structure that had held Vector Heavy SN-7 vertical against the wind during the fueling load — was in three pieces visible from the overlook. The largest piece was a quarter mile downrange in the scrub. The propellant tank farm at the north end of the pad complex was the worst of it: the methane tank was a charred steel skeleton, the insulation foam burned off in a uniform layer that left the welds exposed and the cylindrical shell standing as a black structural ghost. The LOX tank, two hundred feet to the east of the methane, was intact and white and pressurized and venting a slow continuous plume of vapor that the easterly carried away west over the scrub.

Reggie stood next to Cole at the overlook.

Reggie said *Strongback recovered in three pieces. Launch table gone, will require ground-up rebuild, twelve to sixteen weeks. Lightning towers, three to four months. Water-deluge holdpond, six weeks. Tank farm — the methane tank is a total loss, fourteen weeks on the replacement, the long-lead item is the field-weld certification. GSE about sixty percent loss, the surviving forty will require full recert. Firing room intact. Mobile command intact. Pad concrete deck is the question — Hiro is doing the survey this morning. If the deck took less than two centimeters of thermal deformation we resurface and we are flying SN-8 off this pad inside seven months. If the deck took more than two centimeters we excavate and pour fresh and we are looking at eleven months.*

Cole said *The trench.*

Reggie said *The trench held. The trench is the one structural element on the pad that performed exactly as designed.*

Cole said *The trench was the eighteen million dollar line in 2023.*

Reggie said *It was.*

They walked the perimeter.

They started at the south overlook and went counterclockwise — east face first, north face along the back of the tank farm, west face down the access apron, south face back to the overlook. The walk was a mile and a quarter. The temperature rose six degrees during the walk. The smell stayed.

At the back of the methane tank Reggie stopped. She turned to Cole. She said *Cole. The Park read.*

Cole said *Go.*

Reggie said *Park has been on the data for nine hours. Park is not ready to commit. Park has a preliminary indication that the failure originated in the methane tank's vent geometry — specifically in the vent stack ring that was redesigned in the Q3 cost-reduction last year. The redesign added a flow restrictor that the original spec did not have. The flow restrictor was inside the certification envelope on paper. The flow restrictor was not inside the certification envelope under the specific thermal profile of an integrated stage-one and stage-two hotfire at full mission load. Park thinks the restrictor created a pressure pulse in the vent line that exceeded the vent line's burst rating by a factor of about one point four during the T-plus four to T-plus seven window. The vent line failed. The vent line failure decompressed the methane tank into the engine compartment. The engine compartment was a methane-and-warm-engine environment for approximately one point one seconds before ignition. That is the working hypothesis. It is preliminary. Park is going to spend the next three days proving it or disproving it. If it is right, the fix is straightforward. If it is right, we fly SN-8 in seven months, not eleven.*

Cole said *Reggie.*

Reggie said *Yes.*

Cole said *The Q3 cost-reduction was approved by whom.*

Reggie said *The cost-reduction package was approved by the Vector executive committee in October of last year. The vent-stack redesign was a line item in the package. I signed the package. I am the executive responsible for that signature. The signature was the signature.*

Cole looked at Reggie.

Cole said *Reggie. The signature was the signature. The signature is not the failure. The failure is the system that allowed a flow restrictor outside the certification envelope under a specific thermal profile to be inside the certification envelope on paper. The system is the failure. The system is yours and mine to fix. We fix it. We do not blame the signature.*

Reggie nodded once.

Reggie said *Copy.*

They walked the rest of the perimeter.

At the flame-trench overlook at the south end of the pad complex Reggie stopped for a second time. She did not say anything. She looked down into the trench. The trench was intact. The cooling-line ports on the north and south walls of the trench were closed and were still under pressure. The trench had held a fireball with a peak temperature of approximately twenty-seven hundred degrees Celsius for the full eight seconds of the event, and the steel liner inside the trench was discolored a deep blue-black at the inflection point at the back wall and was otherwise structurally sound.

A man in a Vector polo and a tablet walked up to them from the south.

Reggie waved him in.

Reggie said *Cole — Eli Marquette. Lead pad-systems engineer at Vector East. Twelve years on the program. Eli was the lead engineer on the cooling-line spec fight in 2023.*

Cole turned to Eli.

The chapter shifts to Eli for five paragraphs.

---

Eli Marquette was thirty-eight. He was five foot ten and a hundred sixty-five pounds and he had not slept since Thursday afternoon. He had been at the pad at T-zero on Thursday night, on the catwalk above the firing room console, and had been the second man Hiro had counted in the personnel sweep at T-plus two minutes seventeen. He had been at the pad continuously since. He had taken twenty minutes at the trailer at midnight Thursday to call his wife Lara at the kitchen of the four-bedroom on Riverside Drive in Titusville. Lara had taken eleven stitches in her left forearm at the Cape Canaveral Hospital ED at ten forty-five PM Thursday from the kitchen window the shockwave had blown in. Mia, age six, in the front room watching *Moana* for the eighth time, had been unhurt. Lara had said on the call *Eli. We are fine. Do the work. Come home when the work allows you.* Eli had said *I will.* He had hung up. He had not been to the house since. He had slept three hours in the trailer bunk Friday afternoon. He had been at the perimeter since.

He walked up to Reggie and Cole at the flame-trench overlook at six fifty-two AM in the Florida heat with the tablet under his arm and the polo shirt soaked through at the back from the trailer-air-conditioning-to-outside-air temperature swing of the previous ninety seconds. He stopped two paces back from Reggie. He waited to be acknowledged. Reggie waved him in.

Cole turned to him.

Cole said *Mr. Marquette. One question.*

Eli said *Sir.*

Cole said *What is the one thing you know now that you did not know on Thursday afternoon.*

Eli looked at Cole for two seconds.

Eli said *Sir. The trench held. The trench held because the cooling-line specification we argued for in 2023 against the cost reduction was the right specification. I did not know on Thursday afternoon whether the trench would hold. I knew on Thursday afternoon that the spec was correct. Now I know that I was correct that the spec was correct. The difference matters.*

---

Cole looked at Eli for three seconds.

Cole said *Mr. Marquette. Thank you.*

Eli nodded. Eli looked at Reggie. Reggie nodded once. Eli walked back to the trailer.

Reggie said *He has not been home since Thursday at four PM.*

Cole said *Reggie. I want a Vector aircraft on the ground at Melbourne by noon and a crew on standby. When Mr. Marquette goes home, he goes home on the aircraft. Lara meets him at the house. Mia gets the day with both of her parents at the beach in Cocoa. Bill the cost to my personal account, not the company.*

Reggie said *Done.*

They walked back to the Suburban.

At eight oh four AM the Vantage Falcon touched at Melbourne with the second shift of the Park investigation team. Soo-Ah Park was already on site. Park and Reggie went into the trailer at the south end of the perimeter at eight thirty-eight. They closed the door. They did not come out until two PM. Cole left them to it. He walked the perimeter a second time alone, with Cesar and one operator at sixty yards behind him, and stopped for thirty seconds at the flame trench, at the methane tank, at the launch-table footprint, at the strongback debris field in the scrub. He took the canvas field bag off his shoulder once and pulled a small notebook from it and wrote a single line in pencil on the first blank page. He closed the notebook. He put it back in the bag.

He met with Hiro Tanaka in the firing room at ten. The meeting ran forty minutes. Hiro presented the deck-survey methodology. Cole approved it. Hiro left.

He met with Charlene Bly from the NASA Cape liaison office at eleven. The meeting ran fifty-two minutes. Wade Ferris was coming on Tuesday. The walk-the-perimeter slot was confirmed for fourteen hundred Eastern. Charlene left.

He took the call from Owen at twelve fourteen Eastern. Owen said *Yoshida maintain-buy posts at oh six hundred Pacific Monday. The Knox statement at five PM Pacific last night moved the extended-hours session by two points. The retail desk that read the Vector loss as a Carrick balance-sheet event covered the short on Sunday afternoon. Carrick is up zero point eight in extended hours into the Monday open. We are through it.* Cole said *Owen. Thank you.*

He ate a sandwich at the trailer at one thirty PM that Bear had ice-packed in the canvas field bag at the Bainbridge house on Friday afternoon. The sandwich was brisket on a baguette with horseradish and arugula. He ate it standing up at the trailer counter. He drank a bottle of water with it. He gave the second of the two sandwiches Bear had packed to Cesar Vincenzo, who ate it standing next to him at the counter and who said, after the third bite, *Sir, this is the best brisket sandwich I have eaten in my life.* Cole said *Mr. Doerksen will be pleased.*

He left the perimeter at five oh four PM. Cesar drove. The Suburban came back down Highway 1 to Melbourne. The Gulfstream was on the apron, fueled and crewed. Mara was in the right-hand seat in the forward cabin reading the day's threat-assessment brief.

The Gulfstream taxied at five twenty-eight. Wheels up at five thirty-four PM Eastern off runway nine right in the long horizontal Florida afternoon light.

The pilot's voice came through on the cabin intercom at five forty-one over the climb-out.

The pilot said *Mr. Westerlund. Long day, sir.*

Cole looked out the right-hand window. The Atlantic below the wing was the long flat blue of the Florida coast at six PM in late May. The shadow of the Gulfstream ran the length of a sandbar off Patrick. The light was the late-May light.

Cole keyed the intercom.

He said *It's worth it.*

The Gulfstream climbed into the west.
