# Chapter 3: The Call

The alarm went at four forty-five.

He was already awake. He had been awake since three twelve. He had not stayed in the bed past three thirteen because the math of staying in a bed when the body was not going to sleep again before the alarm was bad math, and he had been raised by Phillip Garber to refuse bad math at any hour. He had gone downstairs at three fifteen and stood in the kitchen and read the consolidated SITREP Reggie had pinged him at seven Pacific the previous evening on the secure tablet. He had read it through twice. He had stood at the kitchen window and watched the moonset behind the western trees. He had drunk a glass of water. He had gone back upstairs at four oh one and lain on the bed without getting back under the covers because Annika was sleeping and the disturbance of pulling back the duvet a second time was a disturbance he did not need to make. He had stared at the ceiling for forty-four minutes. The alarm had gone at four forty-five.

He had risen, taken the wet washcloth from the towel bar, scrubbed his face in the dark, put on the gym kit laid out on the chair the night before, and gone down to the gym.

Bear had the trap bar racked at three forty.

The trap-bar max was three eighty. The trap-bar working set on a Friday was three sixty for three sets of five. Three forty was a notch below. Cole stood next to the bar. He looked at the plates. He looked at Bear.

Cole said *Bear. The bar is light.*

Bear said *Boss. You slept ninety minutes.*

Cole said *I slept three hours.*

Bear said *You slept ninety minutes. The bar is light.*

Cole said *Five sets of five.*

Bear said *Three sets of five.*

Cole said *Five.*

Bear said *Three.*

Cole said *Five.*

Bear said *Three. The bar is three forty for a reason. If you want five sets of five next Tuesday, you do three sets of five today. If you want one set of five next month, you do five sets of five today. Your call.*

Cole looked at Bear.

Cole said *Three sets of five.*

Bear said *Three sets of five.*

Cole loaded in. He set up over the bar. He set the bracing. He pulled the first rep. The first rep moved the way three sixty had moved on a Tuesday in March. The second rep moved the same. He banked the five reps. He racked the bar.

Bear said *Two more sets.*

He took the two more sets.

Conditioning circuit. Bear ran the substitution sheet without commentary. Air bike for the rower because the row was the conditioning movement that bled the most from a short-sleep day. Goblet squat for the Turkish get-up because the get-up required a coordination load the brain on three hours of sleep could not produce safely. Farmer's carry instead of the kettlebell snatch. The substitutions added thirteen seconds per round and Bear rebuilt the rest interval to keep total time at twenty-six minutes.

They went through the circuit.

The satphone in Mara's hand rang at six fourteen.

Mara had been standing in the gym foyer for the previous nineteen minutes — Mara was in the gym foyer for the back half of every gym session because the gym foyer was the room from which Mara could observe both the gravel approach and the trail down to Bear's cottage and the slate path to the south lawn, and Mara had been standing in the gym foyer with the satphone in her right hand and the encrypted tablet in her left hand for nineteen of the previous twenty-six minutes. She walked the satphone onto the gym floor.

Mara said *Cole. Knox Vandermeer.*

Cole was on the second rower at the back wall, two minutes into the row leg of the third round of the circuit. He pulled the handle through the last stroke and let it ride home. He stepped off the rower.

He took the phone from Mara. He walked out the side door onto the deck off the gym. The rain was coming in horizontal off the Sound at six fourteen Pacific on a Friday morning in late May. The deck was wet. The roof of the gym kept the rain off the first six feet of decking out from the door. He stood in the dry strip in his gym kit and the sweat going cool in the wind off the water.

He keyed the phone.

He said *Knox.*

Knox said *Cole.*

There was a half second of cabin noise behind Knox's voice — the soft sustained whisper of a pressurized cabin at altitude, the faint ATC chirp on a handset Knox was holding away from the mouthpiece, the deeper hum of two Rolls-Royce BR725 engines at cruise. Cole had been on enough Gulfstream G700s in the last six years to read the air signature of the cabin and the engines through a satellite line. Knox was at thirty-eight thousand feet over the Mojave on a heading north.

Knox said *Cole. I know we're competing here. But this crosses the line. How can I help.*

Cole listened to the sentence.

He listened to it the way Phillip Garber had taught him in 1992 to listen to the opening sentence of any conversation that mattered, which was to listen for the syllables Garber had called the structural beats — the syllable a man emphasized, the syllable he tucked, the syllable he held a fraction long, the syllable he cut. The structural beats were the truth of the sentence underneath the lexical surface. The lexical surface was negotiable. The structural beats were not.

Knox had emphasized *competing.* Knox had tucked *line.* Knox had held *help.* Knox had cut *how.*

The reading was the reading.

Cole said *Knox. Where are you landing.*

Knox said *Boeing Field. ETA twelve fifteen Pacific. I want a drive. I want you in the right-hand seat of a Wedge by one. Bring nothing. No detail. No phone. Just you.*

Cole said *Mara comes.*

Knox said *Mara can drive a chase in a separate vehicle. Mara does not ride in the Wedge. The Wedge is the conversation. The conversation is one-on-one. I can give Mara a Helios encrypted comm she can run on a channel only her tablet and my dash will see. She can ping me direct if she needs me to stop the car. She'll have eyes on the Wedge from her chase the whole drive. But the right-hand seat is yours alone.*

Cole looked through the gym glass. Mara was in the foyer with the tablet open in her left hand, watching the call posture through the glass, already running the chase-car logistics in her head against the Boeing Field tarmac geometry she had memorized in 2019. She caught Cole's eye through the glass. He nodded once. She nodded back.

Cole said *Knox. Yes.*

Knox said *Good. I'll see you at the Helios hangar at Boeing. North end of the field. The Wedge is gunmetal. You can't miss it.*

Cole said *I will be there.*

Knox said *Cole.*

Cole said *Yes.*

Knox said *I meant the line.*

The connection cut.

Cole stood in the dry strip on the deck. The rain was coming in at the angle it was coming in at. He looked across at the southern point of Bainbridge through the rain. The point was a gray on gray. He keyed the phone off.

He walked back into the gym.

Bear was at the first rower at the back wall, set up for the row leg of his own circuit, which Bear ran in parallel with Cole's at a tonnage Bear had not disclosed in nine years and that Cole had stopped asking about in 2017.

Bear said *Boss.*

Cole said *Bear. Last round of the circuit.*

Bear said *Last round.*

They went through the last round. Cole on the second rower. Bear on the first. The row ended at six thirty-six. The cooldown ran four minutes. The total was twenty-six minutes thirty-eight seconds. Bear racked the gear. Cole drank a glass of water from the gym refrigerator. Mara was in the foyer.

Mara said *Chase car?*

Cole said *Chase car. One operator. You driving. Civilian plates.*

Mara said *Approved.*

Cole said *Tell Sloane and Reyes I am taking the Wedge ride solo on Knox's request. Brief them on the comm channel Knox is opening to your tablet. If the channel drops for more than ninety seconds you call me back on the secondary.*

Mara said *Approved.*

Cole said *Tell Mr. Vandermeer's pilot crew that the FBO at Boeing has the slot for the Gulfstream return at six PM Pacific tonight. Tell them the slot is held under a Carrick partner name, not under Helios.*

Mara said *Done.*

Cole said *Mara.*

Mara said *Sir.*

Cole said *The Knox channel runs to your tablet only. Not the operations desk. Not the Vector duty officer. Not Owen. The channel is a Friday afternoon drive between two CEOs. The world finds out about it Monday or the world does not find out about it at all.*

Mara said *Copy.*

Cole walked up to the house.

He was in the shower at six forty-eight.

The shower in the master bath was tile to the ceiling, dark slate, three heads, a single brass handle that Annika had ordered from a foundry in Antwerp in 2009 because the foundry's brass was the only brass Annika had been willing to look at for forty years without flinching. The water came up to temperature in two seconds. The tile was cold against his back. He stood under the center head for the first ninety seconds without moving and let the water hit the back of his neck. He thought about the call.

The words *crosses the line* sat in the room with him.

He thought about the structural beat on *competing.* He thought about the structural beat on *line.* He thought about the way Knox had said *I meant the line* at the end of the call after he had already said it at the beginning and after he had already paid the price of saying it once on a Vantage satphone that Knox's own security desk would have a log of for the rest of the time Vantage Aerospace existed as a corporate entity. He thought about the math of a Vantage CEO calling a Vector CEO at six fourteen Pacific on a Friday morning seventeen hours after a public pad event and saying the sentence Knox had said. The math did not add up to the public posture Knox had taken on the feed the night before. The math added up to the fifth read.

He turned the shower off.

He shaved.

He dressed.

He went downstairs at seven twenty-two.

Annika was at the kitchen island in jeans and the gray wool sweater she wore on Friday mornings when the schedule had her on the Tacoma site by nine. She was on her second coffee. She was working on the third revision of a courtyard plan for the Bellevue civic project, on a roll of trace paper she had unrolled across the marble, with a four-B pencil in her right hand and a stainless triangle in her left.

Cole said *Annika.*

Annika said *Who.*

Cole said *Knox Vandermeer.*

Annika said *Good.*

She did not look up. She added a line to the courtyard at a forty-five-degree angle off the south face of the lobby. She set the pencil down. She picked the coffee up. She looked up.

She said *He flew up to see you.*

Cole said *He did.*

Annika said *Where.*

Cole said *Boeing Field at twelve fifteen. He wants a drive in a Wedge.*

Annika said *Of course he does.*

Cole said *I will be back tonight by five. I leave for the Cape at midnight on the Gulfstream.*

Annika said *I will be back from the Tacoma site at nine. The brisket is in the warming oven. Bear is going to slice it at eight thirty. There will be brisket sandwiches for Reggie's team when you land Saturday morning if you want them. Bear can ice-pack the whole flat and Mara can take it through Cape security as personal cargo. The Cape security desk knows Bear's brisket.*

Cole said *Brisket sandwiches for Reggie's team.*

Annika said *I'll tell Bear.*

She went back to the trace paper.

She said without looking up *Cole.*

Cole said *Yes.*

She said *Have the drive.*

He left at eleven twenty.

Mara at the wheel of a black Suburban with the civilian Washington plates Mara kept on a quick-swap mount for the days when the standard Carrick fleet plates were too visible for the assignment. The Suburban came off the Bainbridge ferry at eleven fifty-two. State Route 99 south. The Boeing Field perimeter at twelve oh nine. The Helios hangar was at the north end of the field on a leased plot Knox had taken in 2024 for the Seattle service crews. The hangar door was open. The Vantage Gulfstream was on the ramp. The gunmetal Wedge was on the apron in front of the hangar door.

Knox was leaning against the front fender in the hoodie.

Mara stopped the Suburban thirty feet short of the apron.

Cole said *Mara.*

Mara said *Channel is up. I have eyes. Go.*

Cole stepped out of the Suburban.
