# Anomaly

**Author:** Christopher Bender
**Publisher:** AI WIN-WIN Institute
**Companion to:** *Carry* (Vol XI, textbook shelf)
**Position:** Standalone prequel novelette. Set May, Year 0 — sixteen months before the Iona Tran call on the *Coelacanth* that opens *Carry*.
**Length:** ~15,000 words / 6 chapters / single arc
**Genre:** Business thriller (Lewis / Stone / Carreyrou register — *Carry* voice carried verbatim)

---

## Concept

A "ripped from today's headlines" novelette built on the real May 28, 2026 Blue Origin *New Glenn* hotfire explosion at LC-36, Cape Canaveral. In our universe the rocket is the *Vector Heavy*. The program is Cole Westerlund's. The pad is Vector East at Cape Canaveral Space Force Station, Launch Complex 47. The test article is SN-7. The hotfire is the first integrated stage-1 + stage-2 static fire since the FAA cleared the program to return to flight the previous week.

It blows the pad off the map at 9:00 PM Eastern on a Thursday. Cole is in his study on Bainbridge Island, 6:00 PM Pacific, when his Vector president — Dr. Reggie Pace, former NASA Marshall — calls him on the secure line and gives him the two words she has been authorized to say to him before the rest of the brief: *Personnel safe.* Then she tells him the pad is gone.

Cole posts the line that will sit at the top of the social feed for the next eighteen hours — the real Bezos tweet, used verbatim. The fictionalized-Musk analog, **Knox Vandermeer** of *Helios Motors* and *Vantage Aerospace*, posts a four-word reply that will sit second on the feed: *Most unfortunate. Rockets are hard.* The Wall Street take is that Vandermeer has just enjoyed his competitor's failure in public.

The Wall Street take is wrong.

At 6:14 AM Pacific the following morning, Knox calls Cole on the satphone Mara routes through and says one sentence: *Cole. I know we're competing here. But this crosses the line. How can I help.* He is on a Vantage Gulfstream at Boeing Field at noon. He is driving Cole up the Hood Canal in a Helios Wedge prototype by one-thirty. The Wedge has Q-style military and countersurveillance hardware concealed in volumes the brutalist exterior geometry was engineered around. Knox pops the dash panel on an EMP-burster module the size of a paperback and says the line the book exists to land:

*Cole. See how it sits. The chassis is a Faraday box. The geometry isn't styling. The geometry is the requirement. That's why I had to make them so ugly. The shape had to absorb the hardware.*

Cole laughs for three seconds — which is the second-longest he has laughed at anything in eighteen months. Knox grins.

The drive ends at a viewpoint above the Hood Canal. Knox lays out three things he came to offer: a no-charge no-press loan of the Vantage anomaly-investigation team for the Vector failure analysis; a public statement from Vantage backing Vector against the regulatory blowback even though Vector and Vantage are head-to-head on the Artemis IV lander bid; and a name — written on a single sheet of Helios stationery and burned in the center console with a Helios-branded lighter — that Knox's own intel desk has been hearing in connection with the anomaly that may not have been an anomaly. Cole reads the name once. Cole accepts the first two. Cole logs the third.

By Saturday morning Cole is walking the slag at Pad 47 with Reggie Pace at six AM in the Florida heat. The rebuild starts. There is no neat resolution on cause. There is no villain reveal. There is the work.

The novelette ends with Cole on the Gulfstream at sunset Saturday, the pilot asking him *long day, sir,* and Cole, looking out at the Atlantic, answering the question with the closing line of the tweet he had posted thirty-six hours earlier:

*It's worth it.*

## Voice

The same close-third, past tense, kinetic Lewis/Stone register that drives *Carry*. No interior rumination. Sensory and operational precision. Brand names, model numbers, time stamps, exact dollar figures, exact distances. Dialogue moves the plot. Short paragraphs. Set pieces over reflection.

Knox Vandermeer's dialogue runs faster than Cole's. Cole speaks in periods like a military communication. Knox speaks in run-ons with embedded asides — the public Musk register without ever naming him. Bear, Mara, Annika carry over from *Carry* exactly as written there.

The two real tweets — Cole's and Knox's — are reproduced verbatim. They are the anchoring artifacts of the "ripped from headlines" intent. The novelette will not paraphrase them.

## Chapter-by-chapter

### Chapter 1: The Anomaly (Cole POV, ~2,500 words)

Bainbridge study. Thursday May 28, Year 0. 6:00 PM Pacific (9:00 PM Eastern). Cole at the standing desk reading a Vector quarterly forecast on the encrypted laptop. The secure phone rings at 6:02. Reggie Pace from the Vector East control center at Cape Canaveral. The hotfire of *Vector Heavy SN-7* on Pad 47 went catastrophic at T+0:08. The pad is gone. Personnel safe — all 184 on-console and on-perimeter accounted for inside three minutes. Mara comes into the study at 6:04 having heard the secure ring from the corridor. Bear is in the gym. Annika is at a dinner with the Westerlund Mori partners in Belltown.

The set piece is the next forty-seven minutes. Cole on the secure line to Reggie. Cole on a second line to Vector's chief structural engineer at Cape (Hiro Tanaka — referenced, not named on stage). Cole on a third line to the NASA Cape liaison. Cole on a fourth line to Owen at home in Madison Park. The questions Cole asks are operational, in order: *Personnel. Pad. Range. Press. NASA. FAA. Insurance. Tank farm.* Each question gets a one-paragraph answer in the chapter. Each answer goes into the brief on the encrypted laptop that Cole is building in a single open Pages document on the right-hand monitor while the calls run on the left.

At 6:38 PM Pacific Reggie tells Cole the orange sky is visible in Fort Pierce. One hundred fifteen miles south. Cole writes the figure into the brief. At 6:41 the first three news desks have the story. At 6:47 the Cape Canaveral mayor's office calls Reggie. At 6:49 Reggie pings Cole *I have to take the next one. I will call you back inside the hour.*

Cole stands up from the desk. He walks to the window. The Sound is the long horizontal evening light. Mara is in the doorway. Cole says: *Mara. Get me Annika. Tell her she does not have to come home early. Tell her I am fine. Tell her I will explain when she gets home.* Mara nods. Mara leaves.

Cole goes back to the desk. He opens the social-media composer on the laptop. He looks at the cursor blinking in the empty field. He does not type yet.

Chapter ends.

### Chapter 2: The Post (Cole POV, ~2,000 words)

Continuous with Ch 1. 6:51 PM Pacific. Cole writes the post. He writes it once, reads it through, does not edit a word, sends it.

The post is reproduced in the chapter on its own line, in italics, verbatim:

*It's too early to know the root cause but we're already working to find it. Very rough day, but we'll rebuild whatever needs rebuilding and get back to flying. It's worth it.*

The chapter follows what happens in the next four hours.

7:04 PM Pacific — Carrick stock down 1.8% in extended-hours trading on the Vector exposure read-through. Owen calls. *Cole. The retail desk is reading the Vector loss as a Carrick balance-sheet event. They are wrong. I have the talking points ready for Karen Yoshida pre-market.* Cole: *Send them. Do not call Yoshida yourself. Let her call you.*

7:22 PM Pacific — the Knox post lands. The reader reads it on its own line, verbatim:

*Most unfortunate. Rockets are hard.*

The Vantage stock ticks up 0.6% in extended-hours trading on the read that Vantage is the only remaining viable heavy-lift bidder for Artemis IV. Three financial-Twitter accounts with combined eleven million followers screenshot the Knox post next to Cole's post and write some version of the same thing: *Knox enjoys this.* The take is everywhere by 8 PM Pacific.

Cole reads the Knox post twice. He does not respond.

8:14 PM Pacific — Wade Ferris, the NASA administrator, posts a 180-word statement about the unforgiving nature of spaceflight and the difficulty of developing heavy-lift launch capability. The statement does not name Vector. The statement is read by every reporter in Washington as the political weather for the next sixty days.

8:47 PM Pacific — Annika home. She comes into the kitchen in the green silk dress she had worn to the Westerlund Mori dinner. She has heard about the pad on the drive home from her partner Jun Mori on speakerphone. She puts the bag down. She does not ask. She walks to the espresso machine, makes Cole a double, brings it to him at the island. Cole takes the cup. *Annika.* She says: *I know.* They sit at the island for nine minutes in silence. Cole drinks the espresso. Annika drinks a glass of water. At minute ten she puts her hand on his hand. She says: *You will be at the gym at five.* Cole says: *Yes.* She says: *Good.* She goes upstairs.

Chapter closes with Cole at the study window at 9:30 PM Pacific watching the lights of a freighter move north up the Sound and thinking nothing the reader is allowed to see.

### Chapter 3: The Call (Cole POV, ~2,500 words)

Friday May 29, Year 0. 5:00 AM Pacific.

Cole in the Bainbridge gym. Bear has the trap bar racked at 340 instead of 380. Cole loads in. Bear says: *Three sets of five.* Cole: *Five sets of five.* Bear: *Three sets of five.* Cole: *Five.* Bear: *Three.* Cole loads in. Three sets of five. Bear is right.

Conditioning circuit. Air bike for the rower. Goblet squats for the Turkish get-ups. Bear running the substitution sheet without comment, in the same way he runs it on the days after a redeye, or on the day after the credible-threat brief has run two pages instead of half a page. Cole goes through the circuit.

The satphone in Mara's hand rings at 6:14 AM.

Mara walks the phone over to Cole at the rower. *Cole. Knox Vandermeer.*

Cole takes the phone. He steps out onto the deck off the gym, the rain coming in horizontal off the Sound. He says: *Knox.*

Knox is calling from a Vantage Gulfstream G700 climbing through eighteen thousand feet over Death Valley. He is in jeans and a hoodie in the front-facing cabin seat. The reader hears Knox's voice through Cole's ear. The chapter narrates the call in real time — the ambient ATC chirp on Knox's end, the rain on Cole's, the pause between Knox's question and Cole's answer.

Knox: *Cole. I know we're competing here. But this crosses the line. How can I help.*

Cole takes the call. He listens for six seconds. He says: *Knox. Where are you landing.*

Knox: *Boeing Field. ETA twelve fifteen Pacific. I want a drive. I want you in the right-hand seat of a Wedge by one. Bring nothing. No detail. No phone. Just you.*

Cole: *Mara comes.*

Knox: *Mara can drive a chase in a separate vehicle. Mara does not ride in the Wedge. The Wedge is the conversation. The conversation is one-on-one.*

Cole looks at Mara through the gym glass. Mara has been watching the call from inside, reading Cole's posture, already running the chase-car logistics in her head. Cole nods once at Mara. Mara nods back. *Knox. Yes.*

Knox: *Good. I'll see you at the Helios hangar at Boeing. North end. The Wedge is gunmetal. You can't miss it.*

Cole: *I will be there.*

Knox: *Cole.*

Cole: *Yes.*

Knox: *I meant the line.*

The line goes dead.

Cole hands the phone back to Mara. *Chase car. One operator. Yourself driving. Civilian plates.* Mara: *Done.* Cole walks back into the gym. Bear has the second rower set up. Cole gets on the second rower. Bear gets on the first. They row for the last eight minutes of the circuit. Bear does not comment on the call.

Cole in the shower at 6:48. The tile is cold. The words *crosses the line* sit in the room with him.

Annika at the kitchen island at 7:30 in jeans and a wool sweater, working on the third revision of a courtyard plan for the Bellevue civic project. Cole pours coffee. Annika without looking up: *Who.* Cole: *Knox Vandermeer.* Annika: *Good.* She does not look up. Cole sits at the island. They drink coffee. Annika at minute four: *He flew up to see you.* Cole: *He did.* Annika: *I will be back from the Tacoma site at nine tonight. There is a brisket in the smoker. Bear is watching it.* Cole: *Thank you.* Annika: *Have the drive.*

Cole leaves for Boeing Field at 11:20. Mara at the wheel of a black Suburban with civilian plates. The ferry off Bainbridge. The ferry across the Sound. The drive south on State Route 99. The chapter closes with the Suburban turning into the Helios hangar perimeter at the north end of Boeing Field at 12:11 PM. The Vantage Gulfstream is on the ramp. The hangar door is open. The gunmetal Wedge is on the apron.

Knox is leaning against the front fender in the hoodie.

### Chapter 4: The Wedge (Cole POV, ~3,000 words)

Boeing Field. 12:13 PM. The Suburban stops. Cole gets out. Mara stays in the driver's seat. The chase plan is set: Mara behind at 800 yards minimum, civilian plates, Cole on a panic ping that Mara watches on the encrypted tablet on the passenger seat.

Knox pushes off the fender. *Cole.* *Knox.* They shake hands. Knox is six-two, lean, hair receding at the temples, the public-persona hoodie hanging off the frame the way it does on television but in person looks deliberately chosen for the shape of the room he is going to be in. He is wearing black jeans, black low-top sneakers, no watch, no rings. The exact uniform. He looks like the photograph he has been for fifteen years.

The Wedge.

The Wedge is gunmetal. The chapter spends a paragraph on what the Wedge looks like — the angular brutalist shape that has been the subject of every car-design think-piece for two years and that the *New York Times* has called *the ugliest production vehicle ever sold in the United States.* Flat planes. Hard creases. A windshield raked at twenty-two degrees. Bed cover flush with the cab roof. No chrome. No badging. The Helios sun-rune is embossed into the tailgate at one-eighth-inch depth. That is all.

Cole walks around the vehicle once. He stops at the front quarter panel. He says: *Knox.*

Knox: *Yeah.*

Cole: *I have been wondering for two years.*

Knox grins. *Get in. I'll show you in twenty minutes.*

The drive.

Knox at the wheel. Cole in the right-hand seat. They roll off the Boeing apron at 12:24 PM. Knox drives the Wedge onto State Route 99 north. Mara two cars back, four cars back, eight cars back, the spacing varying the way the spacing varies on a real protective detail in non-hostile territory. Knox routes the Wedge onto I-5 north, then off at the Edmonds exit, then onto the Edmonds-Kingston ferry, then up the Hood Canal on State Route 104 west.

The set piece is the drive. Knox shows Cole the cabin tech panel by panel, in the order Knox built them in, between 2023 and 2026, at the Helios skunkworks at the back of the Fremont complex.

**Panel one — the seats.** Knox flips a recessed switch under the right-hand armrest. The right-hand seat reclines an inch and a half. The seat-back interior is a ferrofluid lamination rated to thirty-millimeter rounds at point-blank. *I drive this car in markets where I get death threats every Tuesday,* Knox says. *The seat is the wall.* Cole runs his hand along the seat-back. The fabric is the same fabric the rest of the cabin is. *You would not know.* *That is the point.*

**Panel two — the windows.** Glass laminated to a thickness that adds 240 pounds to the vehicle. Rated to AK-47 sustained burst. UV-tinted to defeat thermal optics from outside. Knox taps the driver's window with the back of a knuckle. The sound is the wrong sound. The sound is the sound of a bank-vault door, not a windowpane.

**Panel three — the suite.** Knox reaches under the dash and flips a guarded toggle. The dash display reconfigures. A second readout comes up: blue field, white text, six rolling counters. *Countersurveillance suite,* Knox says. *Defeats license plate readers in a four hundred meter radius. Blinds drone uplinks in a two hundred meter radius. Reads RF signatures in the surrounding traffic and flags any vehicle that has tried to match our speed for more than ninety seconds. The car is a SCIF.* Cole watches the rolling counters. One of the readouts ticks up by one. Knox looks at the rear camera. *Mara is closing,* he says. *She is the one new signature. She has been at six hundred yards for ninety-two seconds. The car is reading her as a possible tail.* Cole: *She is.* Knox grins.

**Panel four — the stow.** Knox lifts the cover on the center console. Beneath it is a recessed magnetic mount. A Sig Sauer P226 sits in the mount, secured by an electromagnet keyed to Knox's right thumbprint on the steering wheel. *I do not advertise this,* Knox says. *You did not see it.* He drops the cover. The cover seals flush.

**Panel five — the burster.**

Knox pulls the Wedge off State Route 104 onto a Forest Service spur road at one twenty-one PM. He stops the vehicle on a level pad of crushed gravel a quarter mile in from the road. He kills the ignition. He looks at Cole.

*Cole. The one I wanted to show you.*

Knox pops a panel in the dash, the panel below the secondary readout. The panel hinges down. Inside the recess is a black anodized rectangular module the size of a paperback book. Two coaxial leads run from the module into the chassis. A small green LED on the front face of the module is lit. Knox does not touch the module. Knox looks at Cole.

*EMP burster,* Knox says. *Single-shot. Six-meter radius. Designed to brick any electronics in a vehicle that has just rammed me. Or a drone that has just acquired me. Or a pursuer's phone. Single use, ten-second recharge, three rounds in the magazine before the capacitor bank has to be regenerated at a Helios facility.*

Cole looks at the module. Cole looks at the dash recess. Cole looks at the inside curve of the A-pillar. Cole looks at the front quarter panel through the windshield. Cole looks back at Knox.

*Cole. See how it sits. The chassis is a Faraday box. The geometry isn't styling. The geometry is the requirement. That's why I had to make them so ugly. The shape had to absorb the hardware.*

Cole laughs.

He laughs for three seconds, hard enough that it shows in his shoulders, hard enough that Knox sees it and laughs too. Knox's laugh runs longer because Knox laughs at everything. Cole's stops at three seconds because Cole laughs at almost nothing.

Knox closes the panel. The panel seals flush. The green LED disappears behind the seamless dash.

Knox: *Now. About your rocket.*

Chapter closes.

### Chapter 5: The Offer (Cole POV, ~2,500 words)

Continuous with Ch 4. Knox restarts the Wedge. He pulls back out onto State Route 104. He drives another nine miles up the Hood Canal to a turnout above the water at the south end of the Dosewallips delta. He parks the Wedge facing the water. The Olympics are visible across the canal. Cloud cover at four thousand feet. Light rain. He kills the ignition. He turns to Cole.

Knox: *Three things.*

**Thing one — the engineering loan.** Knox tells Cole he is loaning the Vantage anomaly-investigation team to Vector for the failure analysis. No charge. No press. Eleven people. Headed by Vantage's chief reliability engineer, a Korean-American woman named Soo-Ah Park whom Knox has worked with since 2009 and who has run six full anomaly investigations at Vantage including the one on the LC-39A pad fire in 2016. Park is the best in the world at this. Park flies into Cape Canaveral on the Vantage Falcon Friday night. She works the slag with Reggie Pace's team starting Saturday morning. She gives Reggie everything she finds. She gives Knox nothing she finds that Reggie has not first cleared. Knox does not see a single byte of Vector data. Park reports to Reggie. Park reports to Reggie alone. Knox just wants the answer found.

Cole: *Why.*

Knox: *Because if Vector's anomaly turns into a six-month Congressional hearing, the Artemis IV bid window closes for both of us. NASA picks Boeing. Boeing is not ready. Boeing has never been ready. The lunar surface stays empty for another decade. We both lose. I am not in business to lose to Boeing.*

Cole: *Accepted.*

**Thing two — the public statement.** Knox shows Cole a draft on his phone. Three paragraphs. Vantage publicly backs Vector. Vantage says heavy-lift development is hard, Vector is the most disciplined organization in the industry outside of Vantage, and Vantage will be the first to fly a Vector Heavy customer payload when Vector returns to flight. The draft is dated Friday May 29, Year 0, 5 PM Pacific. The draft is signed by Knox.

Knox: *I post this in four hours. Unless you say don't.*

Cole reads the draft. He reads it a second time. He looks up. *Knox. The line in paragraph two about the most disciplined organization outside Vantage. Strike outside Vantage. Just leave it most disciplined organization in the industry.*

Knox looks at Cole. Knox grins. *No.*

Cole: *Knox.*

Knox: *Cole. I cannot lie. Even to help you. Vantage is more disciplined than Vector. You and I both know it. The line stands or the statement does not go out. Your call.*

Cole looks at Knox for four seconds. Cole says: *Post it.*

Knox: *Posting at five.*

**Thing three — the name.**

Knox reaches into the inside pocket of the hoodie. He pulls out a single folded sheet of Helios stationery, ivory, embossed sun-rune in the upper left, no other markings. He unfolds it on the center console between them. There is one name written on the sheet in Knox's handwriting. Black ink. Block capitals. The reader does not see the name on the page in the chapter — the reader sees Cole's eyes go to it, register it, and come back up to Knox.

Knox: *My intel desk has been hearing this name in three places in the last four weeks. Cypriot wire structures. A Limassol holding company that took an unusual short position on Carrick three weeks ago through a Geneva intermediary. And a Belarusian crew that an old colleague of yours in the Vilnius field office flagged for me last December as having taken a contract that did not have a known principal behind it. I have no evidence. I have three threads that, if you pulled on them, might or might not braid. I am giving you the name because if it were me, I would want it.*

Cole reads the name once. Cole does not write it down. Cole closes his eyes for two seconds. Cole opens them.

Cole: *I have it.*

Knox takes a Helios-branded lighter out of the same hoodie pocket. He sets the sheet on fire over the center console. The flame catches the embossed sun-rune. The paper curls. Knox lets it burn down to ash on the steel coaster he has placed under it for the purpose. He brushes the ash into the cupholder. He puts the lighter back in his pocket.

Knox: *That conversation did not happen.*

Cole: *Understood.*

They sit looking at the Olympics across the canal for three minutes without speaking. The rain comes harder. The wipers run once on the auto setting. Knox starts the engine.

Knox: *I will get you back to Boeing by three.*

Cole: *Thank you.*

They drive back south. Knox runs the countersurveillance suite for the entire return. The suite shows Mara's chase behind them, two new vehicles entering and leaving the trailing field, none holding for longer than the ninety-second flag. The Wedge pulls back onto the Boeing apron at 3:04 PM. The Vantage Gulfstream is fueled and crewed. Knox shuts the Wedge down. They get out. They shake hands on the apron.

Knox: *Cole. I meant the line. I am sorry about your pad.*

Cole: *Knox. Thank you for the drive.*

Knox climbs the stairs into the Gulfstream. The door seals. The engines spool. The aircraft taxis. Cole watches the Gulfstream lift off the south end of the runway at 3:21 PM in the rain. He stands on the apron in the rain until the aircraft is out of sight to the south.

Mara's Suburban rolls up to the apron. Cole gets in. Mara: *Boss.* Cole: *Mara. Boeing Field FBO. The Gulfstream is wheels-up to Cape at six.*

Chapter closes.

### Chapter 6: The Rebuild (Cole POV with Eli Marquette inset, ~2,500 words)

Saturday May 30, Year 0. 5:48 AM Eastern. Cole on the Gulfstream's descent into Orlando-Melbourne. Mara across from him. Reggie Pace on the tarmac at Melbourne with a Suburban and two Vector security operators. Cole steps off the Gulfstream into the Florida heat at 6:09 AM. The pad is twelve miles north up Highway 1.

They drive to Pad 47.

The chapter opens with the smell. The smell of a methane-LOX heavy-lift static fire that went catastrophic at T+0:08 thirty-three hours earlier and that has been venting through the wreckage of a tank farm for the intervening day and a half — propellant residue, vaporized GSE seals, scorched concrete, the iron tang of a flame trench that has had its water-cooling lines opened to atmosphere. The smell carries half a mile down the perimeter road. Reggie does not warn Cole about the smell. Reggie does not need to.

Pad 47 is a hole in the ground.

Cole walks the perimeter with Reggie at 6:30 AM. The set piece is the walk. Reggie narrates the damage stand by stand, structure by structure, the way a chief engineer narrates the damage on a ship that has taken a torpedo. The launch table — gone. The lightning towers — three of four standing, one collapsed inward. The water-deluge holdpond — punctured in two places, drained. The strongback — recovered in three pieces a quarter mile downrange. The propellant tank farm — the LOX tank intact, the methane tank breached and burned out. The flame trench — sound. The flame trench held. The flame trench is the one structural element on the pad that performed exactly as designed.

Reggie at the flame-trench overlook: *Cole. The trench held. Whatever it was, the trench was not it.*

Cole: *Reggie. Soo-Ah Park is wheels-down at eight.*

Reggie nods. Reggie has known for nine hours. Reggie has cleared the Vantage anomaly team for full pad access through Vector general counsel overnight. Reggie has a desk set up for Park in the trailer at the south end of the perimeter.

**Eli Marquette inset — five paragraphs.**

Eli Marquette, 38, lead pad-systems engineer at Vector East. Twelve years on the program — joined Vector when the company was 240 people and a leased Mojave hangar. Married to Lara, an RN at Cape Canaveral Hospital. One daughter, Mia, six years old, in first grade at Titusville Elementary. They live in a four-bedroom on a half-acre two miles south of the pad. The shockwave on Thursday night blew the kitchen window in. Mia was in the front room watching a movie and was not hurt. Lara was in the kitchen and took a cut on the forearm from a shard of glass that needed eleven stitches. Eli was at the pad. Eli has been at the pad since.

Eli walks up to Cole and Reggie at the flame-trench overlook at 6:52 AM in a Vector polo shirt over a base layer the heat is already pushing through. Eli has not slept. Eli has the duty roster on a tablet. He stops two paces back from Reggie and waits to be acknowledged. Reggie waves him in.

Cole turns to Eli. Cole says: *Mr. Marquette. One question.*

Eli: *Sir.*

Cole: *What is the one thing you know now that you did not know on Thursday afternoon.*

Eli looks at Cole for two seconds. Eli says: *Sir. The trench held. The trench held because the cooling-line specification we argued for in 2023 against the cost reduction was the right specification. I did not know on Thursday afternoon whether the trench would hold. I knew on Thursday afternoon that the spec was correct. Now I know that I was correct that the spec was correct. The difference matters.*

Cole looks at Eli for three seconds. Cole says: *Mr. Marquette. Thank you.*

Eli nods. Eli walks back to the trailer. Reggie says: *He has not been home since Thursday at four PM.* Cole says: *I want a Vector aircraft on the ground at Melbourne by noon and a crew on standby. When Mr. Marquette goes home, he goes home on the aircraft. Lara meets him at the house. Mia gets the day with both her parents. Bill the cost to my personal account, not the company.* Reggie: *Done.*

They walk the rest of the perimeter. At 8:04 AM the Vantage Falcon lands at Melbourne. Soo-Ah Park is in the trailer at 8:38. She and Reggie close the door. Cole leaves them to it.

Cole back on the Gulfstream at 5:22 PM Eastern. Mara across from him. The aircraft taxis to runway 9 right. Wheels up at 5:34 in the long horizontal Florida afternoon light.

The pilot's voice comes through the cabin intercom at 5:41 over the climb-out. *Mr. Westerlund. Long day, sir.*
