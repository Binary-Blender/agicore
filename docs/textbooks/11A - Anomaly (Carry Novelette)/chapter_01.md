# Chapter 1: The Anomaly

The secure phone on the corner of the standing desk rang at six oh two on a Thursday evening in late May, and Cole Westerlund picked it up on the second ring.

The line was Reggie Pace from the Vector East flight-control center at Cape Canaveral. Reggie had been authorized by the company's crisis protocol — the protocol Cole himself had signed in 2022, sitting at this same desk, three weeks after Vector had stood up its first heavy-lift program office — to say two words to Cole before any other word, in any anomaly event involving live propellant on a populated pad.

The two words were *personnel safe.*

Reggie said them.

Cole said *Reggie.*

Reggie said *Cole. The hotfire went catastrophic at T-plus eight seconds. Pad forty-seven is gone. All one hundred eighty-four on-console and on-perimeter are accounted for as of three minutes ago. No injuries. No fatalities. The pad is a hole.*

Cole was standing at the window of the study on the second floor of the Bainbridge house with his right hand on the secure handset and his left hand still resting on the edge of the standing desk where it had been when the phone began to ring. The encrypted laptop on the desk was open to a Vector quarterly capacity-utilization forecast Reggie had sent him at four PM Pacific. The forecast had been the reason he had come up to the study after dinner instead of going down to the gym. He had been reading column four — pad-availability projections for the second half of the year — when the phone rang.

He said *Hold.*

He set the handset down on the desk. He walked the four steps to the second monitor. He opened a blank Pages document on the second monitor. He created a header at the top of the document: *Vector Heavy SN-7 hotfire anomaly. Thursday 28 May Year 0. 18:02 PT / 21:02 ET. Log start.*

He picked the handset back up.

He said *Reggie. I am taking a written brief on the right-hand monitor. Go.*

Reggie went.

The chief structural engineer at Cape, Hiro Tanaka, had already mobilized the structural team and was at the south end of the perimeter road in the Vector mobile command post, which was a twenty-eight-foot blast-rated trailer parked nine hundred meters from the pad on a dirt access cut. Hiro had counted heads against the duty roster three times in the first ninety seconds and had radioed clear at T-plus two minutes seventeen. The NASA Cape liaison, a woman named Charlene Bly who had been on the Vector East site for eighteen months as the agency's permanent rep, had been in the Vector blockhouse at T-zero and had gone to ground in the blockhouse stairwell with the rest of the on-console team for the first ninety seconds and had come back up to the operations floor at T-plus three minutes and had called her counterpart at NASA headquarters in Washington at T-plus six. The Federal Aviation Administration's permanent on-site safety officer, a former Air Force test pilot named Don Eckles, had been on the catwalk above the firing-room console at T-zero and had been the second person Hiro had counted. The Cape Canaveral Space Force Station range-safety officer had cleared the airspace within fourteen miles of the pad at T-plus eleven seconds. The Brevard County emergency manager's office had been notified at T-plus forty-four. The pad fire was, as of six oh four Pacific time, still burning.

Cole typed. The Pages document on the right-hand monitor filled up under his right hand at the cadence of about eighty words a minute. Reggie's voice in his left ear kept pace.

Mara Quinn came into the study at six oh four.

She had heard the secure phone ring through the corridor wall — the corridor on the second floor of the Bainbridge house ran along the south wall of the study, and the secure phone, which lived on the desk in the southwest corner, was audible from the corridor on a quiet evening at any hour past five. She had been at the head of the corridor in the small adjoining room she used as her on-site office, reviewing the threat-assessment brief for the following Monday morning. She had stood up at the first ring. She had walked to the study door at the second. She had opened the door at the third.

She read Cole at the desk and stopped two paces inside the door.

Cole, without looking up from the monitor: *Reggie. Continue.*

Reggie continued.

Mara stayed inside the door. She closed it behind her. She crossed the room to the window. She stood at the window in the position she always stood at the window, which was three feet back from the glass and one foot off the corner, with her right hand at her hip and her left hand at her side and her chin level and her eyes on the gravel sweep below and her ear on the room behind her. Mara could read a half of a phone call the way Reggie could read a thrust curve. She had been at Carrick for eight years. She had been at Cole's side for the last five of them.

At six oh seven Reggie reached the part of the brief she had been holding until the personnel count was confirmed firm — the propellant inventory at moment of incident. The methane main tank had been at one hundred percent capacity. The liquid-oxygen main tank had been at one hundred percent capacity. The hotfire was the first integrated stage-one and stage-two static fire since the program had been cleared by the FAA to return to flight the previous Wednesday, and the fueling load was the full mission load. Both tanks had been opened to atmosphere by the failure event. The methane tank had burned to empty by T-plus four minutes. The LOX tank had vented as a vapor cloud and was still venting as of the present second. The vapor cloud was not a hazard to personnel. The wind was off the Atlantic at six knots and was carrying the cloud southwest over uninhabited scrub.

Cole typed.

Reggie said *Cole. I have to take Charlene Bly on the second line in two minutes. The NASA Washington side is going to want a direct line to me before seven Eastern.*

Cole: *Take her. Call me back at six twenty Pacific.*

Reggie: *Copy.*

The line clicked off.

Cole put the handset down on the desk. He stood up from the standing desk. He did a single shoulder rotation, right then left. He walked to the window. He stood beside Mara, three feet back from the glass, one foot off the corner on the opposite side. The Sound below the house was the long horizontal pre-evening light, and a Washington State Ferry was a quarter of the way across the channel toward Seattle. The light on the water was the light it was at six oh nine Pacific in late May on a Thursday with cloud cover at twelve thousand feet.

Mara said *How bad.*

Cole said *Bad. The pad is gone. Nobody is hurt.*

Mara said *Bear is in the gym.*

Cole said *Leave him.*

Mara said *Annika is at the Mori dinner.*

Cole said *Get her on a secure text. Tell her she does not have to come home early. Tell her I am fine. Tell her I will explain when she gets home.*

Mara said *Copy.*

Mara walked out of the study. The door closed behind her with the small click the door made when Mara closed a door. Cole stood at the window a moment longer. He watched the ferry. He went back to the desk.

He picked up the handset on the second secure line — the line that ran to Owen Friedlander's house in Madison Park, which Owen kept at his bedside at all hours and which had a distinct ring tone Owen had set in 2019 after the first credible-threat escalation, a single low pulse that Owen had once told Cole sounded like a buoy bell. Owen picked up on the first pulse.

Owen: *Cole.*

Cole: *Owen. Vector East. Pad forty-seven. The hotfire of Vector Heavy SN-7 went catastrophic at T-plus eight seconds at nine PM Eastern. The pad is destroyed. All personnel safe and accounted for. Reggie Pace is running the on-site response. I am building the brief now.*

Owen, for two seconds, said nothing. Then Owen said: *Insurance is twelve hundred million on the pad, eight hundred on the vehicle. Both are intact at Lloyd's. We can file at first light Eastern. The Carrick balance-sheet read-through is zero — Vector's a separate entity and the loss is fully insured at Carrick's parent level. The stock will not understand that for forty-eight hours. The retail desk at Morgan Stanley will sell. Karen Yoshida will not. I have the talking points for Yoshida written in my head right now. I will draft them by eleven. They will be in her inbox at four AM Pacific.*

Cole: *Send them. Do not call her. Let her call you.*

Owen: *Done. Cole — Knox.*

Cole: *I know.*

Owen: *He will post something in the next two hours.*

Cole: *I know.*

Owen: *Cole.*

Cole: *Yes.*

Owen: *Your post matters more than his post. Take ten minutes. Write the right one. I will not call you again until tomorrow.*

Cole: *Owen. Thank you.*

The line clicked off.

Cole stood at the desk. He did not yet sit. He put both hands on the edge of the desk and looked down at the second monitor at the brief he had been building. The brief was at eleven hundred words. He had time-stamped every paragraph. He had cross-referenced each operational call against the moment Reggie or Owen had said it. He read the brief through once at the speed he read everything through once, which was the speed Phillip Garber had taught him to read briefs through at Quine Asset Management in 1991 — top to bottom, no rereading, the eye picking up the structural beats and ignoring the lexical surface. He found two corrections. He made them. The brief was twelve hundred words.

The secure phone rang again at six twenty.

Reggie.

Cole: *Reggie.*

Reggie: *Cole. NASA Washington is briefed. Wade Ferris will post a public statement out of his office within the hour. The statement will not name Vector. Wade himself called me three minutes ago and told me he is not going to use the word Vector in any sentence in any statement until the FAA has opened its formal investigation. He said and I quote *Reggie, I have flown three times and seen one of these and the word for what happened to your pad tonight is not a word I am going to put into the political weather until the investigators tell me what to call it.* He said he is sorry. He told me to tell you he is sorry. He told me to tell you he has a flight slot at the Cape on Tuesday and he would like to walk the perimeter with you if you are there.*

Cole: *Tell Wade I will be at the Cape on Tuesday. Tell him I will walk the perimeter with him at any hour he picks.*

Reggie: *Copy.*

Reggie went on.

The Cape Canaveral mayor's office had called the on-site PIO at six fourteen Pacific. The mayor's office had reported that the orange sky from the explosion had been visible from the city of Fort Pierce, one hundred fifteen miles south of the pad, and that windows had rattled in Cocoa Beach and homes had shaken in Cape Canaveral and Cocoa, and that residents up the Atlantic coast as far north as Charleston, South Carolina — five hundred miles up the seaboard — had reported a glow in the southern sky. Three local television helicopters were in the air over the pad perimeter. Two national networks had cut into prime-time programming. The video of the fireball was on every aerospace newsfeed in the world.

Reggie said *Cole. NSF has the cleanest footage. It is the one that is going to run on every cable cutaway tomorrow morning. The fireball is the size of a city block. The pad lighting towers are silhouetted in the foreground. The shot is the worst shot of a pad event since LC-39A in 2016.*

Cole typed.

Reggie said *Cole. The Brevard County sheriff has closed Route 401 from the south gate to the State Road 528 interchange. The county emergency manager is requesting a Vector spokesperson on the ground at the Cocoa Beach community center at eight AM tomorrow morning. The emergency manager's name is Ruth Calderon. She is reasonable. I have her cell. I would like to send Diego Ferreira from our community-affairs office to meet her at seven.*

Cole: *Send Diego.*

Reggie: *Copy.*

Reggie said *Cole. The propellant tank farm. The methane tank is burned out and structurally lost. The LOX tank is intact. The GSE — ground support equipment — is approximately sixty percent lost, the surviving forty percent will require full recertification before any future test. The launch table is gone. The strongback is in three pieces a quarter mile downrange. The flame trench held.*

Cole said *The trench held.*

Reggie said *The trench held.*

Cole said *Reggie. The trench was the thing we argued about for eleven months in 2023.*

Reggie said *It was. The cooling-line spec we held the line on cost us eighteen million in 2023 dollars. It saved the trench tonight. I have a pad-systems engineer named Eli Marquette who fought that fight harder than any of us. I would like him to know in the morning that you know.*

Cole: *I will be on the Gulfstream out of Boeing at midnight tomorrow night. I will be at the Cape Saturday morning at six. I will walk the perimeter with you. I will tell Mr. Marquette myself.*

Reggie: *Copy.*

Reggie said *Cole. I have to go. I have the FAA's Eckles waiting. I have the Cape Canaveral mayor's office. I have Charlene Bly back on for the NASA Washington briefing. I will ping you the consolidated SITREP at seven Pacific.*

Cole: *Reggie.*

Reggie: *Sir.*

Cole: *You held the room.*

Reggie was silent for half a second. Reggie said *I held the room. I will hold the next one. Talk at seven.*

The line clicked off.

Cole put the handset down. He looked at the brief on the second monitor. He looked at the empty composer window on the social-media client he had opened on the first monitor at some moment he did not remember opening it. The cursor blinked in the empty field.

The light through the study window was the last of the long horizontal pre-evening light, and the cloud cover was beginning to gray over from the southwest with the rain that was forecast for the small hours of the morning. The ferry had completed its eastbound crossing and was off the Seattle waterfront. The water in the channel between Bainbridge and Seattle was the same flat slate it was at six thirty-eight Pacific on any Thursday in late May.

Mara opened the study door. She did not step in. She said *Cole. Annika has the text. She will be home by nine. Bear is on his way up from the gym. He will be at the kitchen at six forty-five. The Brevard County FBI field office has called Cape three times in the last fourteen minutes and Reggie has the lead. The first three national networks are stacking helicopters on the Cape Canaveral airspace perimeter and the Space Force range officer is reading them the line. Two of the three financial-twitter accounts you keep on the watchlist have screenshot the AP wire and posted it. The third is asleep. Carrick stock is down one point eight in extended hours.*

Cole: *Mara. Thank you.*

Mara: *Bear is going to want to know whether to keep the brisket on the smoker.*

Cole: *Keep the brisket on the smoker. Annika will be hungry.*

Mara: *Copy.*

Mara closed the door.

Cole sat down at the desk for the first time since six oh two. He pulled the keyboard tray out one notch. He looked at the empty composer window on the first monitor.

He did not type yet.

He read the brief on the second monitor through one more time, end to end. He found no corrections.

He looked back at the first monitor.

The cursor blinked in the empty field.
