# Chapter 4: The Wedge

Knox pushed off the front fender of the Wedge as Cole crossed the apron.

He was six foot two, lean, the hairline running back at the temples in the same retreat the photographs had been documenting for five years, the hoodie black, the jeans black, the low-top sneakers black, no watch, no rings, the uniform that had been the uniform of the public persona for fifteen years and that read in person as a man who had selected an outfit at twenty-eight for the photograph he intended to be the same photograph at fifty-four. He was Knox Vandermeer. He looked like Knox Vandermeer. The way certain men in late middle age looked exactly like the man their younger photograph had promised. He came across the apron with his hand out.

Knox said *Cole.*

Cole said *Knox.*

They shook hands.

Knox's grip was the engineer's grip, all from the forearm, the wrist locked. Cole's grip was the lifter's grip, all from the shoulder, the wrist neutral. The handshake lasted the standard one and a quarter seconds. They released.

Knox stepped back. He waved one open palm at the Wedge.

He said *So. There she is. The car the *New York Times* called the ugliest production vehicle ever sold in the United States. They were not wrong, that's the funny thing, I read that review when it came out and I sat there in the kitchen in Bel Air at six in the morning with a coffee and I laughed because the guy was right, he was looking at it correctly, he just wasn't looking at it for the right reason — anyway, come on, get in, walk around it first if you want, I always like to let people walk around it first, the thing only makes sense from outside before you understand it from inside, so go ahead, take a lap, I'll wait.*

Cole walked around the Wedge.

He did it once, counterclockwise, in the slow easy walk that he had used as a teenager going around a used car at a Princeton lot on the Saturday he had bought his first car for eight hundred dollars in 1983, which was a slow walk that allowed both eyes to catalogue surface and proportion in parallel. The Wedge was gunmetal. The angular brutalist shape was the shape that had been the subject of every car-design think-piece for two years. Flat planes that met at hard creases. A windshield raked at twenty-two degrees. The bed cover flush with the cab roof so that the whole upper silhouette ran one continuous angled plane from the leading edge of the hood to the tailgate. No chrome. No badging. The Helios sun-rune was embossed into the tailgate at one-eighth-inch depth and was the only mark on the entire exterior surface of the vehicle that identified the manufacturer.

Cole stopped at the front quarter panel on his second pass.

He put his hand on the panel.

He said *Knox.*

Knox said *Yeah.*

Cole said *I have been wondering for two years.*

Knox grinned. The grin was the grin from the photographs. It went all the way to the corners of the eyes.

Knox said *Get in. I'll show you in twenty minutes.*

They got in.

Knox at the wheel on the left. Cole in the right-hand seat. The doors closed with the dull sealed sound of a door that weighed sixty percent more than the door of a comparable production vehicle. The cabin was quiet. The dashboard was a single horizontal slab of matte black composite running the full width of the windshield base, with no analog instruments, two recessed flush displays — one for the driver, one centered above the console — and no switches that were visible without searching for them. The seats were a fabric that felt like nylon and that, on closer look, was not nylon. The headliner was the same fabric. The cabin smelled new the way a cabin smelled new when its components had been installed inside the previous fourteen days.

Knox keyed the ignition. The Wedge came on without the sound of an engine. The HVAC came up at a low whisper. The primary display populated. Knox swiped through the start-up checklist with one finger.

He said *Hold on, let me get the chase channel up for your detail. What's her name. Mara. Right. Hang on.*

He tapped a recessed button under the dash.

He said *Mara Quinn. Knox Vandermeer. You should be reading me clean on the Helios channel. If you are, hit the green check on your tablet. I see the green check. Good. You have eyes on the Wedge through the rear camera I'm streaming to you, you have my live route on the map, and if you need me to stop the car you double-tap the red square and I'll have us on the shoulder inside six seconds. Sound right.*

The console showed a return ping from Mara's tablet at the bottom of the secondary display.

Knox said *Great. Mara, follow us at eight hundred yards minimum, take a four-vehicle gap when the traffic supports it, civilian plates work as cover all the way out to the ferry, after the ferry I'd loose the gap to a quarter mile because the Hood Canal traffic thins out and any car at eight hundred yards out there is going to be reading as a tail. You're driving the chase yourself. Good. I respect that. Sloane and Reyes can run the perimeter at the FBO and the Bainbridge ferry terminal till we get back. We're going to be back on the Boeing apron at three. Sound good.*

The return ping from Mara's tablet came back.

Knox said *Good. Cole. Belt.*

Cole pulled the belt.

Knox put the Wedge in drive. The Wedge rolled off the Boeing apron at twelve twenty-four PM. Knox routed it through the perimeter gate, onto the access road, onto State Route 99 north. The Wedge moved with the silent torque of a vehicle that weighed more than the production specification sheet said it weighed. Knox drove with two hands on the wheel at nine and three, which was not the way Knox drove in the photographs of Knox driving. The photographs of Knox driving showed one hand at six on the bottom of the wheel and the other on the gear lever or the door pillar. Two hands at nine and three was the way an instructor at a precision-driving course in southern Arizona taught a CEO to drive a vehicle that had a Sig Sauer in the center console.

Knox said *OK. Here's the tour. You ready.*

Cole said *Ready.*

**Panel one — the seats.**

Knox flipped a recessed switch under the right-hand armrest with his right thumb without taking his eyes off the road. The right-hand seat reclined an inch and a half on a soft hydraulic at the base. Cole felt the seat-back move into him. The fabric was the same fabric the rest of the cabin was. He ran his hand along the inside curve of the seat-back at his right shoulder. The surface was the same. The interior was not the same.

Knox said *Ferrofluid lamination. Three layers, magnetorheological, rated to thirty-millimeter rounds at point-blank with the field active, the field comes up off the chassis bus when the cabin detects a discharge event within four meters, so the seat is soft until the second a round goes through the window, at which point the lamination locks and the seat is the wall. I drive this car in markets where I get death threats every Tuesday. The seat is the wall.*

Cole said *You would not know.*

Knox said *That's the point. If anyone could tell it was the wall, the wall would not work. The wall works because the wall is the seat.*

Cole rolled the seat back to its neutral position with the switch.

**Panel two — the windows.**

Knox tapped the driver's window with the back of his right knuckle. The sound was the sound of a vault door, not a window. Then he tapped Cole's window. Same sound.

Knox said *Two-forty additional pounds in the glass. Laminate construction, polycarbonate sandwich, full perimeter, rated to AK-47 sustained burst on the side glass and to fifty-cal at angle on the windshield. The tint is the trick, the tint is a multi-spectral coating, it defeats thermal optics from outside the cabin to a range of about three hundred yards in daylight and about a hundred forty at night, you cannot heat-image the occupants of this vehicle from a parking lot across a street, you cannot do it from a helicopter at five hundred feet, you cannot do it from anything anyone outside of three governments has access to.*

Cole said *Which three.*

Knox said *I'll tell you over a beer in Bel Air. Not on this drive.*

Cole said *Fair.*

**Panel three — the suite.**

Knox reached under the dash with his right hand and flipped a guarded toggle that Cole had not seen. The primary display reconfigured. A second readout came up alongside it: a blue field, white text, six rolling counters across the bottom.

Knox said *Countersurveillance suite. The big counter on the left is license plate readers — defeats them in a four hundred meter radius using a polarized scrim on the rear plate and a mirrored modulation on the front, the readers ping the plate, the plate sends back garbage, the readers log a read error, and we move on. The next counter is drone uplinks, blinds anything trying to push or pull a video stream in a two hundred meter sphere, that's harder than the plate readers and it draws a lot more power off the battery so I run it on the lower duty cycle in city driving and bring it up to full on rural transit. The third counter is RF signatures in the traffic around us, the car catalogues every transponder it sees inside a four hundred meter perimeter, fingerprints it, and flags any vehicle that has been holding our speed within five miles per hour for more than ninety seconds. The fourth is acoustic, the fifth is thermal, the sixth is — actually, never mind the sixth, the sixth is the one I tell you about in Bel Air.*

Cole watched the counters. The first three were rolling steadily. At minute four into the drive the third counter ticked up by one. A small amber dot appeared on the rear-camera display.

Knox said *Mara is closing.*

Cole said *She is.*

Knox said *She's at six hundred and fifty yards now, she was at eight hundred, the car is flagging her as the one new signature that's been holding to us for ninety-two seconds. The car doesn't know she's friendly. The car just knows she's there. I'll clear the flag.*

He touched the amber dot with his right index finger. The dot went green. The counter rolled back.

Knox said *Mara's tablet now reads as a known friendly in my suite for the next six hours. The next time she's behind the Wedge on a drive, the car won't ping her.*

Cole said *You can whitelist on the fly.*

Knox said *I had to be able to. There are seven people in the world the suite has to clear instantly. My mother's car is on the whitelist. My sister's car. My pilot's car. My head of security's car. The other three — over the beer in Bel Air.*

**Panel four — the stow.**

Knox tapped the center console cover. It slid open on a soft track. Beneath it was a recessed magnetic mount in a low-pile foam well. A Sig Sauer P226 sat in the mount, the slide racked clear, the magazine seated, a single fifteen-round mag in the well next to it. Knox did not lift the weapon. He did not touch the weapon.

Knox said *Electromagnet keyed to my right thumbprint on the steering wheel. The mount releases the weapon to my hand in about half a second once I grip the wheel with intent. The intent signature is a pressure-and-galvanic combination that distinguishes between a man holding a steering wheel to drive and a man holding a steering wheel because he needs the weapon. I cannot show you the intent signature without releasing the weapon. I am not going to release the weapon in this car on this drive. You did not see it.*

Cole said *I did not see it.*

Knox closed the console cover. The cover sealed flush.

**Panel five — the burster.**

They had been on State Route 104 west for fourteen minutes. The Hood Canal was a long flat gray to the right of the Wedge through the rain. Knox slowed at a Forest Service spur road sign at one twenty PM and turned the Wedge onto the gravel spur. He drove a quarter mile in. He came to a level pad of crushed gravel at a small clearing in second-growth cedar. He stopped the Wedge facing the cedar. He killed the ignition.

He turned in his seat to face Cole.

He said *Cole. The one I wanted to show you.*

He reached under the dash with his right hand. He pulled on a recessed tab Cole had not seen. A panel below the secondary readout — a panel Cole would have sworn fifteen minutes earlier was the same composite slab as the rest of the dash — hinged down. Inside the recess was a black anodized rectangular module the size of a paperback book. Two coaxial leads ran out of the back of the module into the chassis. A small green LED on the front face of the module was lit.

Knox did not touch the module. He looked at Cole.

Knox said *EMP burster. Single-shot, six-meter spherical radius. Designed to brick any electronics in a vehicle that has just rammed me, or in a drone that has just acquired me, or in a pursuer's phone the moment that pursuer crosses inside the six-meter sphere. Single use per discharge. Ten second recharge between rounds. Three rounds in the capacitor magazine before the bank has to be regenerated at a Helios service facility. The bank is back there behind the rear seat in a shielded box about the size of a dorm fridge.*

He looked at the module a moment longer. Then he looked back at Cole.

Cole was looking at the recess in the dash.

He looked at the inside curve of the A-pillar to his right.

He looked at the seam where the A-pillar met the dash slab.

He looked through the windshield at the long flat plane of the hood.

He looked at the front quarter panel through the wet glass.

He looked at the way the hood angle ran into the angle of the windshield ran into the angle of the roof ran into the angle of the bed cover ran into the angle of the tailgate.

He looked back at Knox.

Knox said *Cole. See how it sits. The chassis is a Faraday box. The geometry isn't styling. The geometry is the requirement. That's why I had to make them so ugly. The shape had to absorb the hardware.*

Cole laughed.

He laughed hard, from the diaphragm, the kind of laugh that pulled his shoulders forward an inch and that produced two audible exhalations before the third caught and turned into the laugh proper. He laughed for three seconds. He stopped. He looked at Knox.

Knox was laughing too.

Knox's laugh ran longer, because Knox laughed at everything, but Knox's laugh was different from the photographs of Knox laughing. The photographs of Knox laughing were the public laugh, the on-camera laugh, the laugh Knox produced for the interview and the keynote and the rocket-test livestream. The laugh in the Wedge in the second-growth cedar on a Forest Service spur off State Route 104 at one twenty-three PM Pacific in the rain was a different laugh. It was the private laugh. It was the laugh a man laughed when another man had finally seen the thing the first man had built and had seen it correctly.

Knox stopped laughing. He closed the dash panel. The panel sealed flush. The green LED disappeared behind the seamless slab.

Knox said *Now. About your rocket.*
