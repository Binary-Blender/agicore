# Chapter 2: The Post

He typed the post in one pass.

He wrote it inside the composer field on the first monitor, with the brief from the right-hand monitor still open in his peripheral vision, in the cadence he had taught himself in the late nineteen-nineties to use for any external communication that was going to be read by more than one hundred thousand people, which was the cadence of a man writing a letter to a single specific person whom he respected and did not want to waste the time of. He typed it once. He read it through. He did not change a word. He sent it.

The send confirmed at six fifty-one Pacific.

The post read:

*It's too early to know the root cause but we're already working to find it. Very rough day, but we'll rebuild whatever needs rebuilding and get back to flying. It's worth it.*

He closed the composer. He stood up from the desk. He walked to the window. The light through the glass was the last of the long pre-evening light and the cloud cover was a flat slate. The ferry he had watched cross to Seattle thirty-eight minutes earlier had docked. A second ferry was inbound from the city on the westbound run.

He went downstairs.

Bear Doerksen was in the kitchen at the island, drinking a glass of water from the pitcher in the refrigerator, in a black T-shirt and gym shorts and bare feet, six foot four, two hundred and forty pounds, the conditioning circuit having ended at six twenty-eight which was the time the circuit ended on a Thursday because the conditioning circuit on a Thursday was forty-eight minutes long. Bear had heard the secure-line cadence through the kitchen ceiling. Bear had finished the last twelve minutes of the circuit anyway because Bear was Bear. Bear looked up from the glass of water.

Bear said *Boss.*

Cole said *Bear.*

Bear said *Pad.*

Cole said *Gone.*

Bear said *People.*

Cole said *Safe.*

Bear said *Bar at five tomorrow.*

Cole said *Bar at five.*

Bear nodded once. Bear walked out of the kitchen toward the mudroom and the gravel path that ran down to the cottage on the south end of the property where Bear had been living since 2015. Bear stopped at the kitchen door. Bear turned back.

Bear said *Boss. The brisket has another forty minutes. Hold it at one-ninety-five till Annika is in the kitchen. The crust will set.*

Cole said *Thank you, Bear.*

Bear walked out.

The secure phone in Cole's pocket buzzed.

Owen.

Cole picked up.

Owen: *Cole. Three minutes after your post the AP picked it up off your account and pushed it into the prime evening cycle on the East Coast. CNBC has it on the bottom of the screen. Bloomberg has it as the lead. The Reuters London desk has it as the lead. The post is the story. Your line is the line everyone is going to quote in every print piece tomorrow morning. The line that is going to land hardest is the last sentence.*

Cole: *Owen.*

Owen: *Cole.*

Cole: *Tell me about Carrick.*

Owen: *Carrick is down one point eight in extended. The volume is light. The desks that are selling are the desks that read a Vector loss as a Carrick balance-sheet event. They will be back in by Tuesday. The desks that understand the parent-subsidiary structure are not selling. Yoshida is the bellwether. Yoshida is asleep until three Pacific.*

Cole: *Hold the line.*

Owen: *Holding the line.*

The call ended.

The kitchen at six fifty-seven was the slate gray of the cloud cover off the Sound and the warm low light of the two pendant lamps Annika had hung over the island in 2009 when she had designed the house. The brisket smoker on the south deck was a low thread of smoke at the eaves. The house was quiet.

Cole walked back upstairs.

The first monitor on the desk had been left on the empty composer. He opened the social-media client back up. He went to the feed.

The Knox post was the first thing on the feed.

It had landed at six fifty-six Pacific. Cole's post had been live for five minutes. The Knox post was four words on its own line, replying not to Cole's post but to the AP wire's screenshot of Cole's post, which was the colder route — replying to the wire instead of to Cole was the move that meant the post had been written for the public and not for Cole. Cole read the post through Knox's eyes once. Then he read it through the eyes of the public. The two readings produced different sentences from the same four words.

The post read:

*Most unfortunate. Rockets are hard.*

The reply count was at eleven hundred at six fifty-seven Pacific.

The reply count was at four thousand at six fifty-eight Pacific.

The reply count was at fourteen thousand at seven oh four Pacific.

Three of the financial-Twitter accounts Mara kept on the watchlist had screenshot the Knox post next to Cole's post, side by side, with no caption. The first account had three million followers. The second had four. The third had four. By seven twelve Pacific the screenshot was the third trending image in the United States. The cable cutaway on Bloomberg at seven fifteen ran the two posts in a split panel above a chyron that read *VANDERMEER REACTS.*

The Vantage stock — which traded continuously on a Singapore desk during the New York after-hours session because Knox had personally lobbied the exchange in 2024 to extend Vantage's window into the Asia open — ticked up zero point six percent at seven oh seven Pacific. The read was that Vantage was the only remaining viable heavy-lift bidder for the Artemis IV lunar-lander contract. The read was wrong. The read was wrong because nothing about a Vector pad event in May changed the Vantage program's calendar for the Artemis IV down-select in October. The read was wrong because the lander down-select had never been a heavy-lift bid in the first place — the heavy-lift contracts were a separate procurement on a separate timeline. The read was the read that the retail trader in Houston who had screenshot the post at seven oh five Pacific had gone with, and the read that fourteen thousand replies had then amplified, and the read that the Bloomberg chyron had ratified at seven fifteen, and the read that Karen Yoshida was going to spend twenty minutes of the Carrick analyst call on Tuesday morning correcting.

Cole read the Knox post a fourth time.

He did not respond.

He read the Knox post a fifth time.

The fifth read was the one where the cadence of the four words rearranged themselves into a different sentence than the one the public was reading. The fifth read was the read where *Most unfortunate* was not a flippant gloss but the same word a man whose mother had been a flight nurse used at the kitchen table when he was eleven years old and had broken a glass and meant by it that the glass was broken and that the fixing of it was going to require a broom and a careful hand and time. The fifth read was the read where *Rockets are hard* was not the dismissive shrug of an alpha competitor but the sentence that a man who had spent the previous twenty-four years putting eighteen-thousand-kilogram-thrust engines onto a test stand in the high desert and watching some of them go catastrophic in his face would say to another man who had just had one go catastrophic in his.

Cole did not have evidence the fifth read was the right read.

Cole logged the fifth read in the brief on the second monitor under a single header that he typed at seven twenty Pacific and that read *KV post 18:56 PT — public read vs. alternate read. Hold for forty-eight hours.* He saved the brief. He closed the laptop.

He went back downstairs.

He sat at the kitchen island. He poured himself a second espresso from the machine. He drank it in three sips. The cup went down on the marble. The brisket smoke from the deck came in through the cracked window over the sink.

Wade Ferris's statement landed at eight fourteen Pacific.

Cole read it on his phone at the island.

Wade had used one hundred eighty words. Wade had not used the word *Vector* anywhere in the statement. Wade had used the words *unforgiving* and *extraordinarily difficult* and *get back to launching rockets* and *full evaluation of timelines.* Wade had ended the statement with a line that was, on its surface, a generic line about partnerships, and that was, on its private read, a thirty-year ex-astronaut telling every reporter on the Washington beat that the political weather for the next sixty days was going to be the weather Wade said it was, and not the weather Bloomberg's chyron said it was. The statement was the work of a man who had been on a Space Shuttle that had nearly not made it home in 2004 and who had, since that flight, spent his life inside the politics of who got to make the next rocket. The statement was the third post that mattered on the feed that night. The first had been Cole's. The second had been Knox's. The third had been Wade's. The other forty-one million posts that had gone up about the pad event between six oh two Pacific and eight fourteen Pacific did not matter, because the people who decided whether Vector flew again or did not fly again were not on the other forty-one million posts.

At eight forty-seven the headlights of the Range Rover came up the gravel drive.

Annika.

Cole heard the engine cut. He heard the driver's door open and close. He heard the footfalls on the stone path from the parking pad to the mudroom — measured, unhurried, the way Annika walked from a parked car to a kitchen door when she was carrying something she had been thinking about on the drive. He heard the mudroom door. He heard the second door. He heard the soft sound of a leather bag being set on the bench.

Annika came into the kitchen in the green silk dress she had been wearing to the Westerlund Mori partners' dinner. The dress was the dress she had bought in Stockholm in 2019 on a trip with her sister and that she wore to dinners where she needed to look like the person Jun Mori needed her to look like in a room of architects and clients. Her hair was up. She was carrying a small clutch and the espresso cup she had taken from the kitchen at five PM and not finished. She set the cup on the counter next to the sink. She set the clutch on the island. She looked at Cole at the island.

She did not ask.

She walked around the island to the espresso machine. She made him a double. She brought it to him. She sat down on the stool to his right.

Cole said *Annika.*

Annika said *I know.*

They sat at the island.

For nine minutes neither of them said anything. Cole drank the espresso. Annika drank a glass of water that she poured for herself at minute three from the pitcher in the refrigerator. At minute six the brisket timer in Bear's kitchen — which Bear had set to ping Annika's phone when the meat hit the holding plateau — pinged on the island. Annika reached over, swiped the alert closed, did not look at the screen, did not get up.

At minute ten she put her hand on Cole's hand.

She said *You will be at the gym at five.*

Cole said *Yes.*

She said *Good.*

She kissed him on the temple. She picked up her clutch. She walked out of the kitchen. He heard her on the stairs. He heard the bedroom door upstairs open and close.

Cole sat at the island.

The kitchen was the warm low light of the pendants over the marble and the cracked window over the sink and the brisket smoke from the south deck and the slate of the Sound through the western window. The house was quiet.

At nine thirty Pacific he walked back up to the study. He did not turn on the desk lamp. He stood at the window in the dark. A freighter was moving north up the Sound on the inbound to Seattle, the lights of the deck cranes strung along the long flat horizontal of the ship the way the lights of a small town are strung along a road at night.

He watched the freighter for the length of time it took the bow to clear the southern point of Bainbridge and the stern to come into view at the northern. He did not look at the phone.

He did not type anything.

He went to bed at ten oh seven.

He set the alarm for four forty-five.
