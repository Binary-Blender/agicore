# Chapter 5: The Three Days

Thursday morning the team came back at eight.

Dana had reset the room overnight. She had cleared the table of the coffee cups from the day before. She had taped a new sheet of paper to the whiteboard. *Day One. Good to 10x — The AI Multiplication Doctrine.* She had set out water bottles and pads of paper and the same cookies from the bakery downstairs that Mark had teased her about on Pinnacle day, because that was the joke now between them and because cookies kept people in the room past the difficult parts.

Patricia arrived at seven fifty-five. Yusuf at seven fifty-seven. Theo at seven fifty-nine, in shop clothes — he had been on the floor since six. James drove over from Shop B and was in his chair by eight. Connie sat in the same spot near the door. Linda came in at eight oh-three with her Kindle and a travel mug.

She had read.

Dana could see she had read because the page indicator at the bottom of the screen, when Linda set the Kindle down on the table, showed forty-one percent through book one.

Dana did not say anything about it.

"Good morning," she said. "Book one. *Good to 10x.* The AI Multiplication Doctrine. We have six hours. Two breaks. The question we are working on today is what AI multiplication means in our context. Not in general. In our context. At Bellfield."

She paused.

"Mark has read the book. I have read it. The rest of you have had since yesterday. Some of you have read more than others. That's fine. The point of today is not whether we have all read it. The point of today is what we make of it together."

Yusuf, with his pen on his pad: "Where do you want us to start."

"Page one of chapter one. The premise. *AI doesn't replace your people. It multiplies them.* I want each person around this table to tell me what that means in their own department. One sentence. Not a paragraph. One sentence."

She started with Theo because Theo would not stall.

"In Shop A," Theo said, "it means my engineers stop spending half their time looking up specs and half their time writing the memo nobody reads."

"That's two things."

"It's one thing. Looking up information and writing about it are the same thing."

Dana wrote *Theo: information retrieval + documentation.*

James next. James thought longer. "In Shop B it means the validation paperwork happens in days instead of weeks. If we can do it in days instead of weeks we can run more product lines through the validation pipeline. The bottleneck is not the work. The bottleneck is the writing about the work."

*James: validation paperwork. Days instead of weeks.*

Yusuf. "In finance it means I can run twelve scenarios instead of three. Right now I run three because three is what my analysts have time to run. With twelve I see things I can't see now."

Connie, more quietly, from the seat by the door. "In customer service it means I can respond to a complicated technical question from a customer in twenty minutes instead of two days. Right now I have to chase down an engineer and pull the spec file and look up the change history. With this I do that myself in twenty minutes."

Dana wrote it down.

Linda, across the table, said nothing.

Dana did not call on her.

Patricia spoke last, without being asked. "I don't know what it means in my role because I don't have a role like the rest of you. But I think what it means for this company is that we get a chance to grow into what we already are."

The room was quiet for a moment.

Dana wrote *Patricia: grow into what we already are.*

She underlined that one twice.

---

They worked through chapter one of *Good to 10x* in the first hour. Mark spoke twice. Once to clarify a point about AI multiplication versus AI replacement. Once to push back on Theo, who had said something a little too optimistic about how fast Shop A could adopt new tooling. Theo accepted the pushback without argument. Yusuf wrote everything Mark said in the margins of his pad.

By eleven they were on chapter four. The worked example. The book had a worked example of a regional engineering firm that had pulled fourteen days out of its proposal cycle by using AI to handle the technical-spec assembly. Page after page of before-and-after.

Yusuf put his pen down for the first time.

"Stop," he said. "Go back to that page."

Dana scrolled back on her Kindle. She read it aloud.

The before-state was: a six-person engineering team spending an average of four days per proposal pulling specs from twelve different document sources, formatting them into a customer-facing deck, drafting the technical-overview narrative, and routing the draft through internal review.

The after-state was: the same six-person team, with AI assistance, doing the same work in seven hours.

"Fourteen days to seven hours," Yusuf said.

"That's what it says."

"On what proposal volume."

"Forty-two proposals a year."

Yusuf did the math on his pad. He did not need a calculator. Dana watched him write the numbers.

"Forty-two proposals," he said, "times thirteen days saved, times the fully loaded engineering rate of about a hundred ten an hour times eight, is roughly four hundred eighty thousand dollars a year. In engineering hours alone. Not counting the proposals they would have won by being faster. Not counting what they would have done with the freed time. Four-eighty in engineering hours saved on a single workflow."

"Yes."

"That's a real number."

"Yes."

Yusuf looked at Mark.

"That's the kind of number you can put in a board deck and people stop arguing about whether to do this."

Mark said: "That's why I want you to see this number now."

Yusuf wrote *480K. ONE workflow. ONE department.* on his pad in large letters and circled it three times.

He did not put his pen down again that day.

---

The hard moment came at three-thirty.

They had been working through chapter seven — the chapter about what AI multiplication does to organizational headcount. The book did not dodge it. It said the question plainly. *If one person can do the work of ten, what happens to the other nine?*

Theo had been quiet for twenty minutes. He had finished his cup of coffee. He was holding the Kindle in both hands.

He said: "I want to ask the question everybody is going to ask."

Dana waited.

"If AI multiplies people," Theo said, "do we lose anyone?"

The room went still.

Linda, across the table, looked at Mark.

Mark set his Kindle down.

He took a long moment.

"No," he said. "We are not laying anyone off. That is not what this is. This is what we are doing instead of laying people off. The bet is that if we get this right, we grow into our headcount instead of cutting it. Aerospace is asking us for more capacity. Medical is going to ask us for more capacity in 2027. If we are faster and better with the same people, we keep the same people. If we are slower than the market, we are going to have to cut. I am not going to cut. I am going to make us faster."

Theo nodded slowly.

Yusuf said, "I want to be in the room when you tell the company that."

"I will tell the company that in week nine," Mark said. "On the shop floor. In person. In front of everyone."

"All right."

Linda — Linda had not said anything all day — nodded.

It was a small nod. Maybe two degrees of motion. Maybe she didn't mean it. Maybe she was reacting to Mark, not endorsing him.

Dana saw it.

She wrote nothing on her pad. She did not want to put it down in ink yet. But she saw it.

---

Friday. Day two. *Old Ideas Revisited with AI.*

Dana opened the room at eight with a single question on the whiteboard. *Which framework fits Bellfield?*

The book was structured around six historical organizational frameworks. The Toyota Production System. Lean Six Sigma. The Theory of Constraints. The Hewlett-Packard "next-bench" model. W. Edwards Deming's Fourteen Points. The early-Intel "constructive confrontation" culture. For each one the book argued the same shape: *here is the framework. Here is the human constraint that limited it. Here is what AI removes. Here is what the framework becomes.*

They worked through the first three before lunch.

Patricia spoke less than the day before. She listened. She held the Kindle in her lap. She marked pages with the little ribbon-bookmark feature, which Dana saw her use three times.

After lunch they came back and worked through the Toyota Production System chapter.

The chapter walked through Jidoka. Built-in quality. The andon cord. *Stop the line when something is wrong.* The principle that any worker on any line could halt production to surface a defect — and that the system rewarded the surfacing, not the silence. The chapter explained that Jidoka had worked in Japanese manufacturing for sixty years on the factory floor but had never crossed into white-collar work because white-collar defects were invisible. A bad memo did not show up like a bad weld. A wrong spec citation in a proposal did not stop a line.

The book argued that AI changed that.

AI could see white-collar defects. AI could read the memo and ask whether the citation was real. AI could read the proposal and flag the inconsistency. AI could function as a kind of organizational andon cord across knowledge work — pulled by any worker, surfaced to the organization, addressed before it reached the customer.

The book called it *Jidoka Across the Whole Building.*

Patricia put her Kindle down.

She looked at Mark.

"Robert had a book on the Toyota plant tour by his bed for twenty years," she said.

Mark nodded. He had known the book. He had picked it up off his father's nightstand at the hospital after the stroke.

"He took the tour in 1989," Patricia said. "He came back from that tour and he tried to bring Jidoka into Bellfield for the next two decades. He got part of it. He got the floor part. He never got the office part. He told me once it was because the office didn't make things you could see."

She paused.

"This says you can see it now."

"Yes," Mark said.

Patricia looked at the page. Then she looked at the others around the table.

"I think this is the framework," she said. "I think Robert would have said this is the framework. Jidoka. Across the whole building. Quality built in. Anyone can pull the cord."

Yusuf wrote *Jidoka. Whole building.* on his pad.

Theo said: "I can sell that to my crew. They know what the cord is. They've pulled it. If we tell them this AI thing is the cord for everybody else, they'll understand it."

James said: "It fits the regulated work. Jidoka and validation are the same impulse. I can sell it to my crew too."

Connie wrote in her notebook.

Linda did not say anything. But she was leaning forward.

Mark, in the chair next to Yusuf, did not say anything either. Dana looked at him once and she could see him keeping his face still.

*The matriarch just blessed this,* Dana thought.

Patricia picked up the Kindle again and turned to the next chapter.

---

Saturday. Day three. *The Blueprint Audit.*

They came in at eight. They had agreed Friday night to come in Saturday and stay until they had a plan. Yusuf had brought sandwiches from the deli his wife liked. Theo had brought coffee from the place near Shop A. Patricia had brought cookies from her own kitchen.

The room felt different Saturday.

It felt like the room was working on the same problem.

Dana put the audit framework up on the whiteboard. Department by department. Six columns. *Current state. Bottleneck. AI lift. Pilot or scale. Owner. Start week.*

They went department by department.

Shop A — aerospace — Theo. Bottleneck: information retrieval and quality documentation. AI lift: high. Pilot. Theo owner. Start week 5.

"Pilot," Theo said. "I want it. My crew can absorb it."

"You're the pilot."

Customer service — aerospace accounts — Connie. Bottleneck: response time on technical questions, proposal-support requests. AI lift: high. Secondary pilot. Connie owner. Start week 5.

"Secondary pilot," Dana said. "Alongside Shop A. Same week."

Connie looked at Linda. Linda looked at the table. Then Linda looked at Connie.

"Take it," Linda said.

Connie said: "Yes."

Engineering — Patrick Whitmore's team — Aisha Bell on the ground in Shop A. Start week seven, expand from there.

"We're going to put Aisha in early," Theo said. "She wants in. She's been asking about it for a month."

"Officially she starts week seven."

"Officially. She'll be on the tool informally from week five."

"Fine."

Finance — Yusuf. Month three.

Yusuf said: "I want to come in week nine. I want my analysts using it on the Q1 close."

"Week nine."

HR — Marcia Vega — month three.

Shop B — James — slow.

James said: "I want to do this carefully. The FDA work is the FDA work. We are not deploying NovaSyn for customer-facing or compliance work in week five. I want internal documents and internal analysis only at first. I want to write a validation protocol with Dana before I move it any further."

"That's right."

"Month two internal use. Month three we revisit whether we expand."

"That's the plan."

Sales — Linda direct.

The room paused.

Dana looked at Linda.

"Voluntary," Dana said. "No pressure. The door is open. We will not roll anything out in your direct team without your sign-off. Connie's secondary pilot is customer-service operations. That's different from your sales work."

Linda nodded slowly.

"That's fair," she said. "I'm not in yet. I'm not opposed yet either. I'll see what comes back at six weeks."

It was the most she had said in three days.

Dana wrote *Sales: voluntary. Linda's call.*

---

By four they had filled the whiteboard.

By five they had condensed it onto a single sheet of paper. Dana had drawn the grid by hand with a ruler. Six columns. Eight rows. Every department. Every owner. Every start week.

Patricia signed her name at the bottom of the page in her small careful handwriting. Then Mark. Then Dana. Then Yusuf, Theo, James, Connie. Then Linda. Linda was the last to sign. She signed *L. Kowalski* in the same blue pen she had been using all day.

Dana taped the page to the boardroom wall, beside the original single-sentence agenda from Thursday morning.

"Take a picture," Mark said.

Dana took a picture with her phone. She took three, in case one came out blurry. She took one with everyone standing in front of the wall. Patricia in the middle. Mark next to her. The rest of them around them.

It was six oh-seven on Saturday evening.

"All right," Dana said. "We have a plan."

Yusuf was already packing his pad.

Theo shook her hand on the way out. So did James. So did Connie.

Patricia stopped at the door. She put her hand on Dana's arm.

"You did this well," Patricia said.

"Thank you."

"I mean it. The room was a hard room. You ran it carefully."

"Thank you, Patricia."

Patricia walked out. Mark followed her down to her car and walked her to the driver's side door, the way he had been doing since his father had died.

Linda left next. She stopped at the boardroom door. She turned around.

"Dana."

"Yes."

"The Kindle thing on Thursday."

"Yes."

"That was the right call. Don't make me say it again."

Dana said nothing. She just nodded.

Linda left.

---

Dana sat in the boardroom alone for ten minutes.

She looked at the wall. The agenda from Thursday. The single-page plan beside it. Eight signatures at the bottom of the plan. *L. Kowalski.* Patricia. Mark.

She took her phone out.

She texted Eliza — Mark's wife, whose number Dana had only because Eliza had texted her once during the snowstorm in February when Mark had been stuck in Chicago. The text thread had three messages in it from nine months ago and then nothing.

Dana typed: *We did it. Photo attached.*

She attached the photo of the team in front of the whiteboard. She hit send.

She gathered her things and walked down to the parking lot. She got in her car.

She sat with her hands on the steering wheel.

Her phone buzzed.

Eliza: *Did you actually do it?*

Dana looked at the phone. She thought about Patricia signing first. She thought about Linda signing last. She thought about Connie saying *yes* across the table. She thought about Yusuf circling 480K. She thought about Theo saying *I want it.* She thought about Mark, sitting in the chair next to Yusuf, not at the head of the table.

She typed: *We actually did it.*

She hit send.

She started the car.

She drove home in the dark.
