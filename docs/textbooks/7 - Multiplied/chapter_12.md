# Chapter 12: The Board Meeting

Patricia Bellfield arrived at the Bellfield Industries headquarters at eight forty-five on the Friday morning of Week 12, twenty minutes earlier than she needed to. She was seventy years old. She had been driving herself to Bellfield board meetings for thirty-one years and she had been early to every one of them.

She parked in the visitor lot, because she did not believe in taking an employee spot. She walked through the lobby. Jeannie at the reception desk looked up.

"Mrs. Bellfield."

"Jeannie. Good morning."

"They're set up in the boardroom. Coffee's on the credenza."

"Thank you, Jeannie."

She took the elevator to the third floor. She walked the long hallway past her late husband Robert's old office — which Mark had not changed, even after four years, even though the carpet was the original 2003 carpet and the blinds were the original 2003 blinds. She paused at the doorway. She did not go in. She kept walking.

She carried a leather portfolio under her arm. Inside the portfolio was a single sheet of paper with notes for the meeting written in fountain-pen ink in her own slow careful hand.

The boardroom door was open. She stepped in. She set the portfolio at her usual seat — head of the table, opposite Mark — and walked to the credenza for coffee.

She had eight minutes before anyone else was scheduled to arrive.

She used them to look out the boardroom window at the loading dock.

---

The board assembled at five minutes to nine. The other four directors filed in: Janet Holloway, who had been on the board since 1998 and ran a regional bank; Daniel Pierce, who had been Robert's lawyer and was now Mark's; Sam Kennedy, the outside director Patricia had recruited five years ago from a Cleveland industrial firm; and Ed Burroughs, the senior independent director, eighty-one years old, the longest-serving member of the board, who had known William Bellfield personally and who came to every meeting in a brown suit that had not been new since 2002.

Mark, Yusuf, Dana, and Linda came in at nine sharp.

Patricia opened the meeting.

"We have a long agenda today," she said. "Mark will walk us through the ninety-day rollout. Yusuf will walk us through the numbers. Dana will walk us through operations. Linda will walk us through the customer pipeline. I will speak at the end. Mark, please."

Mark stood up.

He did not use slides. He used a single sheet of paper that Dana had printed for him with the ninety-day timeline laid out. He walked the board through it month by month. Month 1: leadership alignment, the Saturday with the toolkit, the three-day boardroom working session with the strategic-foundation books, the implementation plan taped to the wall. Month 2: the pilot in Shop A, Aisha Bell's fastener fix in Week 6, the iteration phase in Weeks 7 and 8, Walt Bayer's safety memo. Month 3: the organization-wide rollout, James Tanaka's validation protocol for Shop B, Linda's Sterling Aerospace win, the all-hands meeting.

He gave credit specifically. He named names. Reggie Vance for the recommendation. Dana for the execution. Theo Park and Aisha Bell for the pilot wins. Linda for landing Sterling. Yusuf for the financial dashboard. James for the FDA validation work. Connie Sutter for being the operational adopter who pulled customer service through.

"And Patricia," Mark said, "for the framework choice."

The board looked at Patricia. Patricia did not look up from her notes.

"It was Robert's framework," she said. "From his Toyota plant tour book."

"It was your framework, Patricia," Mark said. "Robert had the book. You pointed at the page."

Patricia did not respond. She wrote two words on the page in her portfolio.

Mark continued for another five minutes. He closed by saying that the implementation, in his judgment, was complete in the sense that the methodology was now embedded in how the company operated, but that the work of using the methodology was permanent.

He sat down.

Yusuf stood up.

---

Yusuf had brought a one-page financial summary. He laid a copy in front of each board member.

"I'm going to walk through nine numbers," he said. "I'll take questions at the end."

He went down the page.

"Total productivity gains across pilot departments, measured by output per labor hour over the trailing eight weeks against the comparable period last year: twenty-three percent.

"Reduction in customer-side rework requests, same window: thirty-eight percent.

"The Aisha fastener fix in Week 6, direct savings against the prior fourteen months of carrying cost: six hundred and eighty-nine thousand dollars.

"Sterling Aerospace contract Linda landed in Week 11, annual contract value: four point six million.

"Overtime reduction in Shop A, Q4 against Q3: thirty-one percent.

"Customer-credit issuance year-to-date, against the prior nine months: down forty-four percent.

"TAO toolkit, Kindles, and API spend year-to-date, all-in: forty-seven thousand two hundred dollars.

"Pinnacle Consulting engagement quote, October: two point four million, declined.

"Return on the TAO investment, not yet annualized but using direct savings only against direct cost: seventy-three to one."

He looked up.

Janet Holloway said, "Yusuf, you're saying seventy-three times return on direct savings alone."

"I am, Janet. The number gets larger when you add the Sterling contract, the overtime savings, the reduction in customer credits, and the productivity gains. I have not annualized it because annualizing it at this point would be irresponsible. We've had ninety days. The number I just gave you is the floor."

Janet wrote on her notepad.

Sam Kennedy said, "Yusuf, what's the comparable number for the Pinnacle alternative if you'd signed?"

"Sam, we would have been in Week 12 of a seventy-two-week engagement. The Pinnacle deck does not project savings inside the engagement window. The Pinnacle deck projects platform delivery at Week 72 and savings beginning Year 2. The comparable number at Week 12 of the Pinnacle engagement is zero in savings and approximately seven hundred thousand dollars in cumulative billed engagement fees."

"And the cumulative billed engagement fees at Week 12 of TAO?"

"Forty-seven thousand two hundred. The toolkit, the Kindles, and the API spend."

The room was very quiet.

Ed Burroughs, eighty-one years old, spoke for the first time.

"That's not a close call," Ed said.

"No, Ed," Yusuf said. "It is not."

Yusuf sat down.

Dana stood up.

---

Dana's report was shorter. Operational status. Adoption rates. Ninety-six percent of office staff onboarded with NovaSyn. Seventy-eight percent of shop-floor staff active users — defined as logging in at least three times per week. One hundred and nineteen prompt-library entries vetted and shared across departments. Three field manuals built using the Step 0 publishing pipeline: Shop A safety, Shop B FDA-internal documentation, customer-service playbook. One EPUB published company-wide — the internal Bellfield AI operations handbook, written collaboratively by the leadership team and distributed to every Kindle in the company.

"The handbook," Dana said, "is two hundred and forty pages. It is the operating doctrine. It is what we will give to new hires on their first day."

Janet asked who had written it.

"All of us," Dana said. "Mark wrote the introduction. Theo wrote the Shop A section. James wrote the Shop B section. Linda wrote the customer-service section. Yusuf wrote the finance section. Patrick wrote the engineering section. Marcia wrote the HR section. I edited it. The whole thing took about three weeks because we had NovaSyn helping us draft it. Without NovaSyn it would have taken six months and we would not have done it."

She sat down.

Linda stood up.

---

Linda's report was the shortest. New-customer pipeline. Sterling Aerospace, signed Week 11, three-year MSA, four point six million annual. A new medical-device customer named Halcyon Surgical, a smaller account signed last week, eight hundred thousand annual. Two more aerospace opportunities in active proposal stages, both at customers Linda had been working for over two years, both moved into the proposal phase in the last six weeks because the customer-service team had freed up enough of her time to focus on them.

"Six weeks ago," Linda said, "I did not believe what we were doing here was the right path. I want the board to know that I have changed my view. I have been wrong on bigger things, but not many."

She sat down.

Janet Holloway smiled.

Daniel Pierce wrote *Linda — note for posterity* on his notepad.

---

Patricia opened her portfolio. She did not read from it. She did not need to.

She set her pen down on top of the page.

"I want to tell you a story," she said.

The room was quiet.

"In 1987 my husband Robert took over this company from his father William. Robert was forty-one years old. William was sixty-eight. The handover happened in this room. I was here. Mark was six years old. Mark, you would not remember it. You were downstairs in the lobby with a coloring book."

Mark watched her.

"Three weeks after the handover," Patricia said, "Robert stood up in this room, at the seat Mark is sitting in now, and he told the board that he was going to spend one hundred and seventy thousand dollars — which was a great deal of money for this company in 1987 — to buy our first CNC machine. A Mazak. A vertical machining center. Robert had been to a tour of a competitor's shop in Indiana two months earlier and he had come back convinced that the future of this business was CNC and that the shops that did not get into CNC in the next five years were going to be gone in ten."

She paused.

"The board fought him. Ed, you were on the board then. Tell them what you said."

Ed Burroughs cleared his throat.

"I said it was a foolish use of capital," Ed said. "I said the manual machining tradition at Bellfield was our competitive advantage and that we should not abandon it for a piece of equipment we did not yet know how to operate."

"And what did Robert say to you?"

"He said the manual machining tradition would not be replaced by the CNC machine. He said the manual machining tradition would be served by the CNC machine. He said the operators would still be operators. The work would still be Bellfield work. The CNC would multiply what we could do."

Ed paused.

"He used that word. *Multiply.*"

Patricia nodded.

"Robert bought the Mazak," Patricia said. "I voted yes on it. Ed voted no. The motion passed three to two. Within five years CNC was standard across the industry. Within ten years the shops that had not adopted were gone. Bellfield grew from forty-one employees in 1987 to two hundred and twenty in 1997. Robert ran this company for twenty-six years. The Mazak we bought in 1987 is still on the shop floor. It is in Shop A. It is at the back near the breezeway door. Theo runs a part on it twice a year for nostalgia."

She turned to Mark.

"Mark, your grandfather and your father each had one of these moments. The moment where the company became something a little different because the world had become something a little different. Your grandfather had it in 1956 when he started the company in the streetcar barn with a single lathe. Your father had it in 1987 when he bought the Mazak."

She paused.

"You almost signed with Pinnacle in October. I would not have stopped you. I would have voted no on this board, and I would have made the case to the other directors, but I would not have stopped you. The decision was yours to make. You found the other thing on your own. That is the part that matters."

She closed the portfolio.

"I am proud of you, Mark."

Mark looked down at the table.

"Thank you, Patricia."

"This board," Patricia said, "is adjourned at eleven-fifteen AM. I move that we ratify the operational direction Mark has laid out. Second?"

"Second," Janet said.

"All in favor."

Five hands went up.

"Adjourned."

---

The directors stood up. Coats came off the rack. Daniel Pierce shook Mark's hand. Sam Kennedy clapped Mark on the shoulder. Ed Burroughs walked the long slow walk to the door with the help of his cane. Janet stopped to thank Yusuf for the dashboard.

Patricia stayed at her seat for a moment after the others left. Then she stood up, picked up the portfolio, and walked to the boardroom door.

She stopped beside Mark.

"Mark."

"Patricia."

"Come over for dinner Sunday. Bring Eliza and the kids. I want to cook for you."

"Yes."

"I haven't cooked for you since Robert."

"I know."

"Six o'clock."

"Six o'clock, Patricia."

She put her hand on his sleeve for a moment. She did not say anything else. She turned and walked out.

Mark stood in the empty boardroom.

He walked to the window.

Outside, the snow had started. Light, slow flakes coming down past the high windows of Shop A and settling on the rail spur where the steel deliveries arrived. The kind of snow that meant, by morning, the city would be quiet.

He stood there for a long time.

Then he gathered the printouts off the table, turned off the lights, and went back to his office to start on the work for next week.
