# Chapter 9: The Iteration

Dana Reyes came into Connie Sutter's office on a Monday morning in November with a paper cup of coffee and a notebook. Connie was at her desk. The screen in front of her had NovaSyn open and a Word document open and an Outlook window open. Connie was looking at all three with the expression of someone solving a small puzzle.

"You have ten minutes," Dana said.

"For you, I have fifteen."

"I'll take it."

Dana sat in the chair across from Connie's desk. The customer-service area was busy this morning — three of Connie's reps on calls, two more typing, the coffee machine in the corner cycling.

"Talk to me about prompts," Dana said.

Connie looked up.

"You read my email."

"I read your email."

"I'm watching my team reinvent the same prompts every week. Jess writes a prompt to summarize a customer thread. The next day Maria writes a prompt to summarize a customer thread. The prompts are not the same. Some of them are better than others. The good ones aren't being shared. We are losing all the institutional learning."

"Right."

"NovaSyn has a prompt-library feature. It's built into the sidebar. I haven't used it. Nobody on my team has used it. I want to start using it. I want every prompt that works to go into the library, with a label, so the next person who needs to do the same thing can pull it down and modify it instead of starting from scratch."

"Yes."

"That's a yes?"

"That's a yes. Today."

Connie laughed.

"Dana. I love you and I love that you say *today.* But this is going to take more than today. I need to set up the categories. I need to figure out who curates. I need to decide whether we're going to vet prompts before they go in or whether we're going to let everything in and clean up later."

"What do you want."

"I want a curator model. I think Jess should curate. She's been at this the longest. Three reps submit a prompt. Jess reviews. Jess pushes it to the library with a label. The label tells you what it does. Then anyone on the team can grab it."

"Do it."

"Today."

"Today."

Dana wrote *prompt library / customer service / Jess curates* in her notebook.

"I'll come back Tuesday afternoon and see how it's going."

"Bring coffee."

"I will bring coffee."

---

Dana came back Tuesday afternoon at three o'clock with coffee.

Connie was at her desk with her prompt library open. The sidebar showed a folder structure. Customer summarization. Proposal language. Quality memos. Customer credit responses. Customer onboarding. Each folder had between two and six prompts in it.

"You did all this yesterday."

"My team did all this yesterday. I just sorted it."

Dana sat down.

"Show me."

Connie opened the customer-summarization folder. She clicked on a prompt labeled *long-thread engineering extraction.* The prompt was four sentences. It was the prompt Aisha had used to find the three engineering questions in the forty-seven-email thread, six weeks earlier. Aisha had submitted it. Connie had reviewed it. Jess had pushed it to the library.

"Aisha's prompt."

"Aisha's prompt. With a label and a description. Anyone on my team can click it, modify it, and run it on whatever customer thread they need to summarize."

"How many prompts in the library so far."

"As of an hour ago — forty-seven."

"Forty-seven."

"We've been at this since yesterday morning. We'll have a hundred by Friday. We'll have two hundred by the end of the month."

Dana watched Connie organize for a minute. Connie was renaming a folder. *Quality memos* had been split into *quality memos (internal)* and *quality memos (customer-facing).* Connie added a note to the customer-facing folder: *VERIFY ALL SPECS BEFORE SENDING.*

"You added that note."

"I added that note this morning. Because I keep telling the reps to verify before they send and the reminder needed to be in the library, not in my head."

Dana wrote that down.

"Connie."

"Yeah."

"This is the institutional memory we never had."

Connie looked at her.

"We've been writing the same proposals for fifteen years and reinventing the language every time," she said. "We've been writing the same credit-memo responses for fifteen years and reinventing them every time. Linda's team has the muscle memory but the muscle memory does not transfer. When somebody leaves we lose the muscle memory. Now it's in here."

"Yeah."

"That's what this is."

"Yeah."

Connie went back to her folders.

"I'm going to keep going," she said.

"Go."

Dana finished her coffee at Connie's desk in silence for another five minutes, watching the prompt library grow.

---

Friday morning Brandon Klein, a junior engineer in Patrick Whitmore's group, sat in Mark Bellfield's office with his hands on his knees and a piece of paper folded in his pocket and the look of a person who had not slept.

Brandon was twenty-six. He had been at Bellfield for fourteen months. He had a degree in mechanical engineering from Cincinnati. He had been added to the pilot two weeks ago when Patrick had accelerated engineering's onboarding. He was sharp. He was eager. He had used NovaSyn enthusiastically since the moment he had been given access.

On Wednesday he had sent an email to a customer's lead engineer about a thread-tolerance question for a non-standard fastener. In the email he had cited *MIL-STD-1515 Section 4.3.2.* The citation looked authoritative. He had pulled it from a NovaSyn response that had drafted the technical paragraph for him. He had not verified the citation. He had read the response, decided it sounded right, and pasted it into his email.

MIL-STD-1515 Section 4.3.2 did not exist.

The customer's quality engineer — they had a thorough quality team, which was part of why they were Bellfield's customer — had looked up the citation Thursday morning, found nothing, and emailed Connie asking for the actual reference document.

Connie had called Brandon. Brandon had looked up the citation himself, in a panic, and found nothing. He had walked it back to Connie. Connie had walked it back to the customer. The customer had been professional but pointed. Connie had emailed Mark Thursday afternoon.

Now it was Friday morning at nine.

Mark, Dana, and Theo were in Mark's office. Brandon was in the chair across from Mark's desk.

"Brandon," Mark said.

"Yes, Mr. Bellfield."

"Mark."

"Mark."

"Walk me through what happened."

Brandon walked through what happened. He spoke in the careful sentences of a person who had rehearsed his explanation. He owned the error. He did not blame the tool. He did not blame anyone else. He said he had been excited about the speed at which he could draft the email. He said he had read the response and it had sounded authoritative. He said he had not verified the citation. He said he should have. He said he was sorry.

Mark listened.

When Brandon finished Mark looked at Dana. Dana looked at Theo. Theo nodded once.

Mark turned back to Brandon.

"Brandon," he said. "I want to tell you a few things. First. You're not in trouble in the way you're afraid you're in trouble. You are not getting fired. You are not getting written up. We are going to talk through what happened and you are going to go back to work."

Brandon exhaled.

"Second. What happened to you is going to happen to other people. AI tools generate plausible-sounding output that is not always accurate. This is a known failure mode. We're going to fail on this failure mode several more times this year as a company. We just got the first one. I'd rather get the first one from a junior engineer in an internal pilot than from a senior person in a contract bid."

Brandon nodded.

"Third. We are going to institute a rule, company-wide, today. Any AI-generated content that is going to a customer — any of it — has to be verified before it leaves the building. Citations get checked against the actual standard documents. Specifications get checked against the actual spec sheets. Numbers get checked against the actual source data. Verify before customer-facing. Dana is going to write it up. Theo is going to put it in the Shop A training material. Patrick is going to put it in the engineering training material. I'm going to send a calm email about it to the leadership team. Connie is going to put a reminder in the prompt library. We are going to over-communicate this rule until everyone has it."

"I understand."

"Fourth. The reason I want everyone to know about what happened is that you did the company a service. You did it first. You did it on something small enough that we could walk it back. The company learns the lesson because you got the lesson. I'd rather have this lesson cost a customer apology than cost a contract."

Brandon looked at his hands.

"I'm going to send you back to work in a minute. Before I do — Theo, anything you want to say."

"Brandon," Theo said. "Aisha asked me to tell you something."

"Okay."

"Aisha said: tell Brandon I have verified the wrong thing too. Last month. Twice. I verified after I sent. She caught it the second time and made me fix it before it went out, which is why nobody knows about it. Verify before. Always before."

Brandon nodded.

"Dana," Mark said. "Anything?"

"Brandon," Dana said. "Welcome to the part of the rollout where we learn what the tool does badly. Now you're useful to us in a way you weren't before."

Brandon looked up.

"That's a real thing I'm saying," Dana said. "Tell the next junior engineer who joins the pilot what happened to you. Tell it without softening it. That's how the training material gets real."

"I will."

Mark stood up.

"Get back to work."

Brandon stood up. He shook Mark's hand. He shook Dana's. He shook Theo's.

"Thank you, Mark," he said, at the door.

He left.

---

Mark sat back down.

Theo exhaled.

"That was the right way to handle that," Dana said.

"I hope so."

"It was."

"What do we put in the email to leadership."

Dana opened her notebook.

"Subject: New rule, verify before customer-facing. Body: a junior engineer used an AI-generated citation in a customer email this week. The citation was incorrect. The customer caught it. No contract impact. Effective immediately, all AI-generated content that is going to a customer must be verified before it leaves the building. Specifics: citations against standards documents, specs against spec sheets, numbers against source data. Dana will distribute updated training material by Monday. No blame on the engineer involved. We expect this failure mode and we are now operationally prepared for it."

"That's the email."

"Send it under your name."

"Yes."

He turned to his keyboard.

He sent the email at nine forty.

---

Dana sat with the training-course curriculum team Friday afternoon and Saturday morning. They added a section to the onboarding module for all NovaSyn users. The section was titled *Always Verify AI-Generated Citations Before They Leave the Building.* The section was three pages. It included Brandon's case as the lead example. Brandon had given permission for his name to be used. Dana had asked him directly. He had said yes.

The section made it into the training material before Monday.

---

Theo walked the shop floor Tuesday afternoon and stopped at Walt Bayer's bench at three o'clock.

Walt was running a calibration. He looked up when Theo's shadow crossed the bench.

"Theo."

"Walt. Got a minute when you're done with that."

"I'll be done at three ten."

"I'll be back at three ten."

Theo came back at three ten with two coffees from the break room. He set one on Walt's bench.

"Thank you."

"Walt."

"Yeah."

"You haven't used it."

Walt looked at his bench. He looked at the workstation monitor. He looked at the NovaSyn icon in the corner.

"No."

"I want to know why."

Walt did not answer for a full minute.

He drank his coffee.

He set the cup down.

"I don't trust it," he said.

"Okay."

"And I don't want to look stupid."

"Okay."

Walt looked at Theo.

"I am sixty years old," he said. "I have run a lathe since I was nineteen. I know what I am doing on a lathe. I am the best on this floor on a lathe. I do not want a piece of software to make me look like an old man who does not know what he is doing."

"Walt."

"Yeah."

"What would make you trust it."

Walt thought about that for a long time. He drank more of his coffee. He looked at his bench.

"I have been trying to write a safety memo," he said. "For two months. About the cleaning procedure on the swarf collection system on Machine 4. The way it is now, the operators are doing it three different ways. One of them is the right way. Two of them are not. The two wrong ways are not dangerous yet but they will be dangerous eventually. I have rewritten the memo four times. The version I have makes it sound like a routine maintenance procedure. It is not a routine maintenance procedure. It is a safety thing. I cannot get the memo to read like a safety thing."

"Show me."

Walt opened the drawer of his bench. He pulled out a manila folder. Inside were four printed drafts of the memo, with handwritten notes in the margins of three of them. Walt's handwriting was precise and small.

Theo read them.

"You want this to land harder."

"I want it to land like a safety thing. Not like a how-to."

"Okay."

Theo sat down on the stool next to Walt's bench.

"I'm going to do a thing," he said. "Tell me to stop if you want me to stop."

He pulled the workstation keyboard over. He opened NovaSyn.

"I'm going to type. You talk. Tell me about the swarf collection system. Tell me about the three ways. Tell me about why the two wrong ways are dangerous. Don't worry about the memo. Just tell me."

Walt watched him for a moment.

"You're going to put it in there."

"I'm going to put it in there."

Walt sat down on his own stool.

"Okay," he said.

He talked for twenty minutes. Theo typed. Walt described the swarf collection system on Machine 4 in detail — the chip conveyor, the bin, the magnetic separator, the cleaning frequency, the three operator behaviors he had observed, the failure mode that one of them would eventually produce, the specific safety risk to the operator's hand and forearm. He used his hands. He used the actual swarf collection system fifteen feet away as a reference, walking Theo over to it twice during the conversation.

Theo typed everything into NovaSyn. He included Walt's exact phrasing in places. He added a request at the end: *Draft this as a safety memo, not a procedure memo. The tone should make clear that this is a safety issue with two specific incorrect cleaning behaviors that need to be corrected immediately. Reference the operator-safety implications. Use the standard Bellfield safety-memo format.*

He clicked Send.

NovaSyn took thirty seconds.

The draft came back.

The opening paragraph said: *This memo addresses an operator safety risk on the swarf collection system on Machine 4. Two cleaning behaviors currently in use on this system create a foreseeable risk of operator injury. Effective immediately, all cleaning of the Machine 4 swarf collection system must follow the procedure outlined below.*

The rest of the memo was Walt's content, structured in the format he had been trying to find for two months, with the safety language threaded throughout instead of buried in a final paragraph.

Walt read it. He read it a second time.

He read it a third time.

"Okay," he said.

"What do we change."

Walt pointed at the second paragraph. "This sentence. I would not use the word *foreseeable.* I would say *real.* *Foreseeable* sounds like a lawyer wrote it. *Real* is what it is."

Theo edited *foreseeable* to *real.*

Walt pointed at the procedure list. "The third step. The way it is written here, it sounds like step three is optional. It is not optional. It is the step everybody is skipping. We need it to read like the step that is the whole point."

Theo rewrote step three with Walt watching.

They edited for thirty more minutes. Theo did the typing. Walt did the editing.

At four oh-five they had a memo Walt was happy with.

"Print it," Walt said.

Theo printed it.

Walt picked it up. He read it one more time standing at his bench.

"Theo."

"Yeah."

"This is the memo I have been trying to write."

"Yeah."

"This is exactly the memo I have been trying to write."

Walt folded the memo once. He put on his jacket. He walked out of the shop, down the breezeway, into the office building, and to Dana's office.

Dana was on a phone call. He stood in the doorway until she ended the call.

"Walt."

"Dana."

He handed her the memo.

She read it.

"This is the cleaning memo."

"This is the cleaning memo. I want this in front of every operator on the floor by tomorrow."

She looked at him.

"Consider it done, Walt."

"Theo helped me with it."

"I figured."

"I used the AI."

Dana set the memo down.

"How was it."

Walt thought about that.

"It was a tool," he said.

He walked out of her office.

Dana picked up the memo. She walked it down the hall to Marcia Vega in HR for safety-protocol distribution. She came back to her office. She sat down at her desk.

She wrote *Walt: converted Tuesday* in her notebook.

---

By Friday Walt had used NovaSyn three more times. Once for a calibration log entry. Once for an incident report on a near-miss with a chip ejection on Machine 6. Once to summarize a manufacturer's bulletin on a tool-life issue that Walt had been meaning to read for a month. He did not announce that he was using it. He just used it.

On Wednesday Walt had stopped at Aisha's workstation on his way back from lunch. He had said, looking at her screen rather than at her: *if you set up one of those context-project things for the older machinists, I would use it.*

Aisha had said: *I will set one up tomorrow.*

She had set one up by Wednesday evening.

By Thursday Walt was using it.

By Friday three of the senior machinists were using Walt's context project.

---

Dana wrote the week-eight status memo to Mark on Friday at five fifteen.

She wrote it at her desk with the office quiet and the light outside the window already going to that early-November gray. She wrote it short.

*Mark — Week 8 status, Shop A and customer-service pilot.*

*Pilot status: working.*

*Adoption: 94 percent in Shop A (one operator on medical leave; one operator on jury duty; remaining three intermittent but engaged). 100 percent in customer service. 100 percent in the subset of engineering that came in early — six engineers under Patrick.*

*Total queries through NovaSyn in the pilot to date: 4,247.*

*First-win impact: $689K identified and being remediated (the M8 cluster). Corrective-action plan on Patrick's desk; humidity sensor permanent install next week. Expected scrap-rate normalization within sixty days.*

*Prompt library: 119 vetted prompts as of today, curated by Jess Foreman in customer service and by Aisha Bell in Shop A. Growing roughly fifteen prompts per week. Most-used prompt this week: long-thread engineering extraction.*

*Field issues this period: one hallucination event (Brandon Klein, engineering, citation error in customer email). Handled in person Friday morning. New rule instituted company-wide: verify before customer-facing. Training material updated. No customer-relationship damage. Brandon back at work and contributing to the updated training section.*

*Holdouts: zero. Walt Bayer converted Tuesday. Senior machinists onboarding behind him.*

*Recommendation: full-organization rollout begins Monday Week 9.*

*— Dana*

She read it. She read it twice. She sent it at six oh-one.

Mark replied at six oh-four.

*Approved. Let's go.*

Dana closed her laptop.

She sat at her desk for a moment longer. The office was empty. She could hear the cleaning crew in the hallway.

She thought about Walt walking into her office Tuesday with the memo folded in his hand.

She thought about Brandon at nine o'clock Friday morning saying *thank you, Mark.*

She thought about Connie's prompt-library folders.

She thought: *the methodology held.*

She got up. She put her coat on. She walked out to the parking lot. The sky was already dark. She drove home.

She thought, on the drive: *Monday we go everywhere.*
