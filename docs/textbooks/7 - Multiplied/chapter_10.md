# Chapter 10: The Rollout

Dana Reyes came into the office at six forty-five on Monday morning of Week 9 with a printout of the rollout schedule in one hand and a coffee in the other. She set both on her desk. She opened her laptop and pulled up the master tracker.

Three hundred and eighty employees. Forty-one workstations to be re-imaged with NovaSyn Chat Lite. Sixty-two Kindle Paperwhites loaded with the AI WIN-WIN strategic foundation books and the Mastering AI Prompts training course. Five training cohorts scheduled across two weeks. One all-hands meeting Friday at one PM.

The pilot had run for four weeks. The leadership had read the books. The numbers were on Yusuf's dashboard. Now they were going to do the thing.

Dana drank half the coffee in one pull and started her morning.

---

James Tanaka, the plant manager for Shop B, was in his office at seven-thirty when Dana knocked. James was fifty-one, regulatory-bound, careful by professional habit. Shop B made orthopedic implant components and surgical-instrument substrates. Shop B was FDA-regulated end-to-end. Shop B was the reason James had not slept particularly well in October when the TAO rollout was first announced.

"James."

"Dana. Come in. Close the door."

She closed the door. She sat down. She set the printout on his desk.

"I want to spend the morning with you on the validation protocol," she said. "I don't want to walk out of this office until you're comfortable with what NovaSyn touches and what it doesn't touch in Shop B."

James exhaled. It was a real exhale, the kind that comes from a person who has been holding something for ten weeks.

"Thank you," he said.

They started at the top of a blank legal pad. Dana wrote two columns. The left column was *NovaSyn permitted.* The right column was *NovaSyn prohibited.*

James talked first. Dana wrote.

Permitted: internal SOPs for non-customer-facing process work. Training-material preparation for new hires. Scheduling and shift-coverage analysis. Analysis of historical maintenance logs. Drafting of internal memos to leadership. Summarizing email threads with internal stakeholders. Pulling reference data from already-validated specifications. The Step 0 publishing pipeline for internal handbooks.

Prohibited: any output that would land in a customer-facing document. Any output that would land in a FDA submission. Any output that would land in a Design History File. Any output that would land in a Device Master Record. Any output that would touch the QC validation paperwork — the inspection reports, the lot-release records, the calibration logs.

"The validation paperwork stays exactly the way it is," Dana said. She wrote it on James's pad and underlined it. "NovaSyn does not touch the paperwork. NovaSyn helps you not have to write the analysis memo about the paperwork at nine PM on a Tuesday."

James looked at her for a long moment.

"Dana," he said. "I was afraid you were going to tell me to use it on the QC validation paperwork."

"We'd never do that. You'd never let us. We wouldn't even ask."

James looked at the page.

"I can work with this," he said.

He looked up at her.

"I genuinely can. This is the line I would have drawn myself. I just wasn't sure anybody else was going to draw it."

"Mark drew it in the leadership session in Week 3," Dana said. "James, you weren't in that session because you were at the FDA inspection in Indianapolis. The line was drawn before you knew it was being drawn."

"Send me the notes from that session."

"I'll have them on your desk by lunch."

They worked the rest of the morning on the specific cases. The borderline ones. The one where a process engineer might want NovaSyn to help draft an internal failure-mode analysis that would *inform* the validation work but not *be* the validation work. They wrote that one down too. They wrote the escalation path: if any Shop B employee was unsure whether a task was permitted or prohibited, they emailed James directly. James would adjudicate. If James was unsure, he emailed Dana. If Dana was unsure, she emailed Mark.

"Three layers," James said. "That works."

"That works."

She stood up.

"James," she said. "Thank you for being patient with us."

"Dana," James said. "Thank you for being patient with me."

She walked back to her office. She put the validation protocol into the master tracker. She marked Shop B *green.*

---

Tuesday morning. Finance.

Yusuf Okafor had been gnawing at the bit since the Sunday phone call at the end of Week 6, when he had looked at the $689,000 number from Aisha's fastener fix and called Mark and said *I want to accelerate.* He had been waiting two and a half weeks.

Dana walked into the finance bullpen at eight AM and Yusuf was already there with his five accountants and his two analysts sitting around the conference table. There were Kindles in front of each of them, charging cables snaking across the table. There was a printout in front of Yusuf with three columns: *prompt name, use case, sample input.* Dana glanced at it as she sat down.

"Yusuf," she said. "Did you build prompt-library entries before the rollout?"

"Three of them. Over the weekend."

"Of course you did."

"Variance analysis prompt. Aging-receivables summary prompt. Month-end accruals reconciliation prompt. I tested all three against last quarter's data."

"And?"

"The variance analysis one is going to save Janelle six hours a month. The aging summary is going to save Marcus four. The accruals reconciliation might be the biggest win — it takes Sarah twelve hours every month-end and the prompt got it down to about ninety minutes including her review."

Janelle, sitting at the table, looked at Yusuf. "Yusuf, you used my Q3 data?"

"I anonymized the account numbers, Janelle. The pattern is the same."

"You could have asked."

"I should have asked. I'm sorry."

Janelle smiled despite herself. "Show me the prompt."

Yusuf turned his laptop around.

Dana sat back and watched Yusuf onboard his team for the next forty minutes. He did not need her in the room. She stayed anyway because she wanted to see it. Yusuf was a good CFO and a careful man and now he was something else — he was the kind of operator the User's Manual described, the person who learned the tool by using the tool on real work and then taught the next person by sitting next to them.

She left at nine.

---

Wednesday. HR.

Marcia Vega, VP of Human Resources, fifty-two, fourteen years at Bellfield, met Dana in the second-floor conference room at ten AM. Marcia had brought her three direct reports and a yellow legal pad with eleven questions written on it in tight blue ink. Dana sat down. Marcia handed her the pad.

"Go ahead, Marcia."

Marcia worked through the questions one by one. The candidate-screening question. The performance-review-language question. The compensation-confidentiality question. The PII question. The employment-law-citation question — Marcia did not want anyone in HR pulling legal citations from a chatbot without verification.

Dana answered the ones she could. She wrote down the ones she couldn't. She told Marcia she'd have a memo on the legal-citation question by Friday and that the HR team would not use NovaSyn for anything legally adjacent until the memo was signed off.

Marcia nodded. She put a checkmark next to seven of the eleven questions.

"The other four," Marcia said, "we hold until the memo."

"That's the deal."

"Then we're in."

Dana walked out of the room at ten forty-five. Marcia was already showing her senior HR generalist how to log into NovaSyn.

---

Thursday. Engineering.

Patrick Whitmore, VP of Engineering, forty-nine, eleven years at Bellfield, had been a quiet ally of the TAO rollout since Week 2 — quiet because he had been running a department fire involving a customer change-order for the entire month of October and had not had time to be vocal about anything else. Patrick met Dana in his office at eight AM.

Aisha Bell was already there. Aisha had been operating in NovaSyn since Week 5. She had cracked the fastener-tolerance issue in Week 6. She had been informally helping the other engineers in Shop A since Week 7. She was twenty-eight and had three years at Bellfield and she was about to be made, formally, engineering's NovaSyn lead.

Patrick said it himself.

"Aisha," Patrick said. "I want you to run point on engineering's NovaSyn workflow. Title is yours to name. Budget is whatever we need. You report to me, but for the rollout phase you also have a dotted line to Dana. We good?"

Aisha looked at Dana. Dana nodded.

"We're good," Aisha said.

"Tell me what you need."

Aisha pulled out a one-page outline. She had clearly been thinking about this. She walked Patrick and Dana through it. A weekly engineering NovaSyn office hour for the first six weeks. A shared prompt-library workstream specifically for engineering specs and tolerancing. A protocol for loading the right reference docs into a NovaSyn context project for each active program. A monthly engineering rollup of NovaSyn-assisted wins so Yusuf could put them on the dashboard.

Patrick read the outline twice.

"Run it," he said. "All of it. I'll back you on the budget at the next leadership review."

Aisha stood up. Dana stood up. They walked out together.

In the hallway, Aisha exhaled.

"Dana," she said. "Six months ago I was the youngest engineer in Shop A and I was the third person to talk in any meeting I went to."

"You're still the youngest engineer in Shop A, Aisha."

"I know. I'm just saying."

Dana smiled.

"Run it," she said. "Same as Patrick said. Run it."

---

Friday. One PM. Shop A.

Mark Bellfield walked out onto the shop floor at twelve fifty-eight with no notes. The whole company was there. The CNC machines had been powered down for the hour. Folding chairs had been brought in from the cafeteria for the office staff. The shop-floor crews were on their feet. Speakers had been wired into Shop B across the breezeway so James's people could hear. Two cordless microphones sat on a folding table for the Q&A.

Dana, at the back of the room, counted heads. Three hundred and twenty-one in Shop A. Forty-nine in Shop B. Ten more at the satellite distribution center on the speakerphone link. Patricia was there, in person, sitting in one of the folding chairs in the second row. Linda Kowalski was there, standing at the back, arms uncrossed for the first time in ten weeks.

Mark started at one o'clock exactly.

"Thank you for being here," he said. "I'm going to talk for about twenty minutes. Then I'm going to take questions for as long as it takes. Nobody leaves until everybody who wants to ask something has asked it."

He looked around.

"In October a consulting firm came to this building and pitched me a two-point-four million dollar engagement called an AI Transformation. The deck was beautiful. The case studies were real. The partner was good at his job. I almost signed."

He paused.

"I didn't sign. I'm going to tell you why."

He told them. He told them about the Reggie Vance phone call. He told them about the weekend with the User's Manual. He told them about the three days the leadership team spent in the boardroom in Week 3 reading the strategic-foundation books together. He named the books by name. He told them they could borrow any of the leadership Kindles for a weekend if they wanted to read them themselves. He held up his own Kindle for the room to see.

"What we chose instead is called TAO. Transformative AI Operations. It is a framework that says one thing very clearly. AI does not replace people. AI multiplies people. If we ever use AI here to do something other than multiply you, we are doing it wrong."

He talked about the pilot. He named the work that had already been done. Aisha Bell, in Shop A, had cracked a fastener-tolerance issue in Week 6 that had been on the books for fourteen months. The issue had cost the company six hundred and eighty-nine thousand dollars. It was solved now. He named Aisha by name. Aisha, in the third row, looked at the floor.

He named the prompt library. He named Walt Bayer's safety memo. Walt, near the back, took his hat off when his name was called and put it back on.

He named James Tanaka's validation work in Shop B. He explained what NovaSyn would and would not touch in regulated work. He said the words *the validation paperwork stays exactly the way it is.*

He talked for nineteen minutes.

"Questions," he said.

A hand went up in the middle of the floor. Marcus Brand, thirty years old, four years at Bellfield, a machinist on the second-shift line. Mark did not know his name. Dana, at the back, did, and she wrote it on her pad in case Mark asked her later.

Mark walked the microphone over to him.

"Your name?"

"Marcus, sir."

"Marcus. Go ahead."

Marcus held the microphone for a second before he spoke.

"What happens to my job," he said, "if the AI is doing my job better than I am."

The room did not move.

Mark looked at him.

"Marcus," he said. "The AI is not doing your job. The AI is helping you do your job. The day we use AI to replace people instead of help them is the day this company stops being Bellfield. If you ever see that happening — if you ever see somebody on this leadership team make a decision that smells like that — you come to me directly. My door is open. I mean that."

The room stayed quiet for a moment.

Then a hand went up at the very back.

Mark looked over. It was Hector Salazar — fifteen years at Bellfield, loading dock, the man on the forklift Mark had watched out the boardroom window in October.

"Mark," Hector said.

"Yeah, Hector."

"I'm using it for the loading-dock memos now. The bills-of-lading reconciliation. The safety flags."

"Yeah."

"Used to take me forty minutes a shift. Takes me eight."

"That's good, Hector."

"I'm not doing less work."

The room waited.

"I'm doing more work," Hector said. "I'm just doing different work. Stuff I wanted to do for ten years and never had time for."

There was a pause.

Then the laughter started. Scattered at first. Then real. Then the applause.

Mark let it happen. He did not interrupt it. He waited for it to settle.

"Thank you, Hector," he said.

Hector nodded once. He sat back down.

The Q&A went for another forty minutes. The questions got easier after Hector. People asked about training schedules and Kindle pickup and which workstations would be re-imaged when. Dana, at the back, fielded most of those from where she stood, calling out times and dates without a microphone. Mark answered the ones about principles. By two-twenty PM there were no more hands.

"Thank you," Mark said. "Back to work."

The room broke up. The machines started coming back on. The folding chairs got carried back to the cafeteria.

---

Friday. Five PM. Shop A, empty.

The crews had finished the second shift's first hour and were settled back in. The office staff had gone home. The light through the high windows had gone the low orange of late autumn. Dana stood next to Mark at the back of the shop floor where the all-hands had been four hours earlier.

"That was the right answer," Dana said.

"Hector saved me."

"Hector was the answer for a reason. He's the one everybody trusts."

Mark looked at her.

"You knew he was going to do that?"

"I knew there was a chance. I asked him on Wednesday if he was planning to come."

"Dana."

"I didn't ask him to say anything. I just told him if he wanted to say something, he should."

Mark laughed.

"You're going to make a hell of a COO someday."

"I'm going to make a hell of a COO right here."

She turned. She walked toward the breezeway. Mark stood for another minute on the empty shop floor, looking up at the rafters and the high windows and the dust hanging in the orange light.

Then he followed her out.
