# Chapter 11: The Conversion

Linda Kowalski sat at her desk at six forty-five on Tuesday morning of Week 10 with a legal pad in front of her, a black ballpoint pen in her hand, and the Sterling Aerospace RFP open on her laptop screen. She had been at it since Monday morning. She had eleven pages of the thirty-eight done. The deadline was Thursday at noon.

She was sixty years old. She had been VP of Sales and Customer Relations at Bellfield Industries for nineteen of her twenty-five years at the company. She knew every aerospace and medical-device customer's main contact by their kids' names and their kids' colleges. She wrote her proposals longhand on yellow pads and re-typed them into Word because she did not trust the way her brain worked when she typed first. She had done it that way since 1994.

The RFP on the screen was the largest opportunity she had worked in five years.

Sterling Aerospace. Aerospace prime. Tier-one supplier to the major airframe manufacturers. She had been working Gerald Westin, their VP of Procurement, since 2019. Five coffees in three years. Two Christmas cards. Gerald was sixty-three, ex-Air Force, careful and quiet and not given to handing his business to people he had not built a five-year relationship with.

Two weeks ago Gerald had called her.

*Linda, I want to issue an RFP to Bellfield. Your numbers are competitive. Your quality story is strong. The deciding factor is going to be the technical depth of your proposal and whether you can show us you understand our supply-chain context. Get me something good by Thursday noon.*

She had thanked him. She had hung up. She had sat in her office for ten minutes staring at the wall.

Then she had started writing.

She had been writing since Monday morning. By Tuesday noon she had eleven pages. She needed thirty-eight. She needed technical specs cross-referenced from twelve historical Bellfield jobs. She needed case-study language in a register she found tedious. She needed customer-context analysis that she, personally, did not enjoy writing.

She was exhausted.

---

Connie Sutter knocked on her door at one PM Tuesday.

Connie was forty-seven. Customer Service Lead, aerospace accounts. Linda's right hand for nine years. The operational adopter of NovaSyn before Linda was. The reason Linda had a customer-service team that had been quietly hitting its numbers for six straight weeks despite Linda herself never having logged into the tool.

"Linda."

Linda did not look up.

"Connie."

"Have you tried using NovaSyn for the technical assembly?"

Linda waved her hand.

"Not now, Connie. I have to get through this."

"Linda —"

"Not now."

Connie stood at the door for another two seconds. Then she left.

Linda kept writing.

---

By four PM Tuesday she had thirteen pages. She had been writing for six hours and she had added two pages. She set the pen down. She stood up. She walked to the window of her office and looked out at the parking lot.

She was sixty years old.

She was tired.

She had forty-four hours left until the deadline. She had twenty-five pages still to write. She had a customer she had been working for three years who was finally — *finally* — giving her a real shot. She had a proposal that was, at her current pace, not going to be finished in time. Or it would be finished in time and it would be bad. Or it would be finished in time and it would be mediocre and Gerald Westin would shake her hand at the next industry dinner and tell her the contract had gone to the Pennsylvania shop.

She thought about Connie.

She thought about Mark in the all-hands meeting on Friday afternoon. *AI does not replace people. AI multiplies people.*

She thought about her daughter Megan, who was twenty-eight and a nurse practitioner in Cleveland, who had said to her at Easter dinner over wine: *Mom, you should at least try the AI thing. You can hate it after you try it.*

She walked back to her desk.

She opened her laptop. She closed Word. She found the NovaSyn icon on her desktop — Connie had installed it for her in Week 9 and Linda had not opened it once. She double-clicked it.

The window opened.

She stared at it for a moment.

She typed her first prompt with two fingers.

*I am writing a technical proposal for an aerospace supplier RFP. I need to assemble a section that demonstrates our experience with high-tolerance fastener systems by drawing on twelve historical Bellfield jobs. I will paste the job summaries below. Please draft a three-page narrative organized chronologically that emphasizes tolerance, on-time delivery, and customer-side rework rate. Use a formal but plainspoken register.*

She pasted the twelve job summaries from her Word document.

She hit enter.

She waited.

The response came back in forty seconds.

Linda read it.

She read it again.

It was — not perfect. It was close. The chronology was right. The tolerance numbers were right. The register was tedious in exactly the way she had not wanted to write it, which meant it sounded like a proposal and not like Linda. That was good. That was correct. The proposal was supposed to sound like a proposal.

She edited it. She moved a paragraph. She fixed a number. She added a sentence Gerald Westin would specifically recognize about a 2022 job for a customer he had previously worked with.

She had three pages.

She had been at it for nine minutes.

She sat back.

"Huh," she said out loud.

She kept going.

---

By six PM Tuesday she had fourteen pages of NovaSyn-assisted draft. By seven PM she had the case-study language drafted in a register that needed only her editing pass — and the editing pass was what she was good at, what she had been doing for thirty years. By eight PM she shut her laptop. She put on her coat. She walked out to her car in the parking lot. She drove home.

She did not call Connie.

She did not call Mark.

She had dinner with her husband. She watched the second half of a basketball game. She went to bed at ten.

---

Wednesday morning. Six-thirty AM.

Linda came in early. The cleaning crew had not finished. The hallway smelled faintly of floor polish. She made coffee in the break room and carried it to her office and sat down at her desk and opened the proposal.

She did her editing pass.

This was the work she had been doing for thirty years. This was the work she was good at. The technical assembly was solid; the case-study language was solid; the customer-context analysis was solid. None of it was in her voice yet. By eleven AM she had moved it all into her voice. She had cut three pages. She had added two. She had rewritten the executive summary from scratch because the AI version was correct and bland and the executive summary was not allowed to be bland — it had to land in Gerald Westin's chest the way the right opening line of a proposal always did.

She wrote the executive summary herself, in longhand, on the yellow pad, and re-typed it.

By eleven-thirty the proposal sang.

She read it through end-to-end. Thirty-six pages, not thirty-eight. Two pages tighter than projected. The right two pages tighter.

She set the pen down.

She looked at the clock.

She was a full day ahead of deadline.

---

She did not send it Wednesday.

She let it sit. She had learned, in twenty-five years, never to send a proposal the same day it was finished. She read it one more time Wednesday evening with a glass of wine in her kitchen at home. She found three typos. She fixed them.

Thursday morning at nine AM she sent it to Gerald Westin.

At nine twenty-three AM, Gerald Westin emailed back.

*Linda — strongest proposal we've seen from Bellfield. Get on my calendar Friday afternoon to discuss next steps.*

Linda read it twice.

She closed her laptop.

She sat at her desk for a minute.

Then she got up and walked down the hall to Connie's office.

---

Connie was at her desk eating yogurt out of a cup. She looked up.

"Linda."

Linda closed the door.

She sat down in the chair across from Connie's desk. She had not sat in that chair in maybe four years. They had always talked in Linda's office, with Connie standing.

"Connie," she said. "I'm sorry I dismissed you for six weeks."

Connie set the yogurt down.

"Linda," she said. "You didn't dismiss me. You were waiting for the right moment to try it."

"That's generous."

"It's true."

Linda looked at her.

"I want you to teach me everything you've learned. From the beginning. The prompt library. The context-project workflow. The customer-research patterns. Everything."

"Linda."

"Everything, Connie. From the beginning."

Connie sat back.

"Come over to my office after work tonight," she said. "I'll show you the prompt library. We'll go through it entry by entry. It'll take an hour and a half."

"That's perfect."

"And tomorrow at lunch we'll go through the context-project workflow. I'll set up Sterling Aerospace as your first project so you have a live one."

Linda nodded.

"Thank you, Connie."

"You're welcome, Linda."

Linda stood up. She paused at the door.

"Connie."

"Yeah."

"You've been carrying this team for six weeks while I sat in my office refusing to look at it."

"Linda, I wasn't carrying it. I was running it. There's a difference."

Linda thought about that.

"There is," she said. "I'll remember that."

She walked back to her office.

---

Thursday evening she sat with Connie for an hour and forty minutes going through the prompt library, entry by entry. There were a hundred and nineteen entries. They got through forty-three of them. Linda took notes on the yellow pad.

Friday at lunch they went through the context-project workflow.

Friday at three PM Linda took the elevator up to the third floor and walked down to Mark Bellfield's office. His door was open. Mark was on a phone call. He looked up. He saw her. He held up one finger. She waited.

He hung up.

"Linda."

"Mark."

"Come in. Close the door."

She closed the door. She sat down.

"I want to be in the rollout report next month," she said. "I want my numbers in the dashboard. I want the sales team formally onboarded with my full sponsorship. I want Connie listed as the operational lead and me listed as the executive sponsor."

Mark sat back in his chair.

He smiled. It was not the careful smile she had seen on his face for the last ten weeks. It was a different one.

"Welcome aboard, Linda."

"I should have done this in Week 1."

"Linda. You did it the right week."

"I sat in my office for ten weeks refusing to look at this."

"You did it on your own terms. Means more this way."

Linda looked at him.

"Mark," she said. "That's a generous read."

"It's the read I have."

She nodded.

"I landed Sterling," she said.

Mark went still for a second.

"Sterling Aerospace?"

"Gerald Westin emailed yesterday morning. We're meeting this afternoon to discuss next steps."

"Linda."

"I know."

"Linda, that's a five-year customer you've been working."

"Three. Almost three."

Mark stood up. He walked around the desk. He held out his hand. Linda stood up and shook it.

"That's a hell of a week," he said.

"It was a hell of a Tuesday afternoon."

He laughed.

She left his office and walked back to hers. She had her Friday afternoon call with Gerald at four. She had forty minutes. She sat down at her desk and pulled up her NovaSyn context project — the one Connie had set up for her at lunch — and started drafting talking points.

The talking points came together in fifteen minutes.

She edited them by hand on the yellow pad in the other twenty-five.

At four PM she picked up the phone.

---

Two weeks later, in the last week of Week 11, Sterling Aerospace signed a three-year master service agreement worth four point six million dollars annually.

The contract paperwork came across Linda's desk on a Thursday afternoon. She signed it. She made a copy. She walked it down to Mark's office and laid it on his desk without saying anything.

Mark looked at the cover page. He looked at the annual contract value.

He picked up the phone.

He called her from the desk phone twelve inches away. The handset rang in her hand. She looked at him. He gestured for her to answer. She picked up.

"Linda."

"Mark."

"I knew you'd land them."

Linda smiled.

"I landed them with help, Mark. That's the part I want to be honest about."

Mark sat back.

"Linda. That's the part that matters."

She hung up the phone.

She set the handset back in the cradle. She walked out of Mark's office. The hallway light, late November afternoon, came in at the long horizontal angle she had been watching slant through the Bellfield third-floor windows for twenty-five years.

She walked back to her own office.

She had work to do.
