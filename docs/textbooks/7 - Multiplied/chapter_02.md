# Chapter 2: The Phone Call

Saturday morning at eight forty-five Mark Bellfield was sitting in his home office on the second floor of the house on Edgecliff Drive with a coffee on the desk and a yellow legal pad open in front of him. He had not slept well. He had written six lines on the legal pad and crossed out four of them. The remaining two were *Pinnacle: 3.4M all-in* and *Reggie 9AM*.

The house was quiet. Eliza had taken the kids to their Saturday morning swim practice. Through the window Mark could see the maple in the side yard had gone the color it went every October, which was the color of a brake light.

At eight fifty-eight his phone rang.

"Mark."

"Reggie."

"You good to talk?"

"I'm at my desk. I've got coffee. I've got a legal pad. Go ahead and ruin my Saturday."

Reggie Vance laughed, which was not a loud laugh but the kind that came from somebody who had spent forty years in a machine shop and learned to laugh under hearing protection. Reggie was fifty-six. He owned Vance Tool and Manufacturing in Indianapolis. Three hundred and twenty employees, third generation, mostly Tier 2 automotive and some Tier 1 defense work. Mark had known him for nine years through the Midwest Precision Manufacturing Association. They had served on the workforce-development committee together. Reggie had been the guy who, two summers ago, had stood up at the annual meeting in Columbus and given a fifteen-minute talk about something called TAO that Mark had only half-listened to because at the time Mark had been on his phone trying to put out a fire about a delayed aluminum shipment.

He wished now he had listened harder.

"Tell me about the pitch," Reggie said.

"Pinnacle. Bryce Templeton out of their Cincinnati office. Two o'clock yesterday in the boardroom. Eighteen months. Two point four million on the line item. Yusuf has it at three four all in once you load it up."

"How was the deck?"

"It was beautiful, Reggie. It was the best pitch deck I've seen in three years."

"It always is."

There was a pause. Mark heard Reggie shift in his chair. Reggie's chair always squeaked. Mark had been in Reggie's office once, in Indianapolis, in 2022, and the chair had squeaked then too.

"Mark," Reggie said. "Before I tell you what I'm going to tell you, I want to say one thing. I almost made the wrong call. Honest to God I almost signed with McAlister."

"McAlister Group?"

"McAlister Group. Out of Chicago. Two years ago. They sent a guy named Pritchard. Pritchard had three associates. The deck was beautiful. The number was two point one. The pitch was eighteen months. You with me?"

"I'm with you."

"I sat in my office for three weeks looking at that proposal. I had it printed. I had it bound. I had Bev — Bev's my CFO — I had Bev build me the financial model six different ways. Every way she built it, it penciled out. Every way. I was going to sign on a Wednesday morning. The Tuesday before, I drove down to Greenwood for the workforce committee meeting and I sat next to a guy named Don Pelletier who runs a stamping operation south of Lafayette. Don and I had served together fifteen years. I had never once seen Don Pelletier excited about a software product in his life. Don is the guy who still keeps his run sheets on a clipboard. You know the type."

"I know the type."

"Don was excited about this. I trusted him."

"What did Don tell you?"

"Don told me what I'm going to tell you. He told me there was a thing called TAO. Said his nephew had told him about it. The nephew works in IT for some hospital system in Indy. Don bought the thing for his shop, paid for it out of pocket on a Saturday afternoon, and three months later his cycle time on the small-batch work was down nineteen percent and his quote-to-order turnaround was three days instead of nine. He had not laid anyone off. His people loved him. He showed me the dashboard on his phone."

"And you bought it."

"I bought it that night. Tuesday night. Cancelled the McAlister meeting Wednesday morning. Pritchard called me three times in the next four weeks. I let it go to voicemail."

Mark wrote *Don Pelletier* on the legal pad. He underlined it.

"Reggie," he said. "What is it. What did you actually buy."

Reggie took a breath. Mark could hear him doing it. Reggie was the kind of guy who took a breath before he answered something hard.

"It's a toolkit," Reggie said. "That's the right word. It's a complete toolkit. One-time purchase. You own it outright. Nobody bills you again."

"How much."

Reggie told him.

Mark wrote the number on the legal pad. He looked at it. He looked at the number above it, the three point four million from Pinnacle. He looked at the ratio. The ratio did not seem possible.

"Reggie. That's three orders of magnitude off from Pinnacle."

"I know it is."

"That can't be right."

"It's right, Mark. I have been paying for it for two years and I have not paid for it more than once. They don't charge a subscription. They don't charge maintenance. They don't charge per seat. The thing is yours. You download it on a Saturday and you own it on Sunday."

"Then what are you getting."

"Four things. You're getting four folders."

"Walk me through them."

"All right. Hang on. I'm going to make this clean for you."

Mark heard Reggie set the phone down for a second and then pick it up. He heard the chair squeak again.

"It's called TAO," Reggie said. "Transformative AI Operations. The whole thing's structured as a four-step process. Step Zero, Step One, Step Two, Step Three. They number it that way because Step Zero is the publishing engine — it's how you take everything else and turn it into Kindle books your people can actually read. Step One is the strategic foundation. Step Two is the training library. Step Three is the tool."

"Slower," Mark said. "Step Zero is what."

"Step Zero is a set of scripts and what they call skill documents. You feed them into an AI — I use Claude, you can use whatever — and the AI helps you produce professional-grade Kindle books. Training manuals. Field manuals. SOPs. Onboarding wikis. Whatever your company needs to put in front of its people in a format they'll actually read. You distribute it on Kindles. The Kindle thing is important. I'll come back to it."

"Okay."

"Step One is three books they wrote. Call it the strategic foundation. The first book is called *Good to 10x*. The premise is the whole premise of the toolkit — AI doesn't replace your people, it multiplies them. The second book is called *Old Ideas Revisited with AI* — it's about proven organizational frameworks reborn through AI. Toyota Production System, that kind of thing. The third book is called *The Blueprint Audit*. That one is the practical how. Department by department, you run an audit on your own company and the book walks you through the questions. You come out the back end with an implementation plan."

"And the leadership team reads these."

"Your leadership team reads these. You buy Kindles. You hand each of your VPs and plant managers a Kindle. You sit them in a room and you read the books together. Three days, one book a day. By Saturday night you have a plan."

"Three days."

"Three days. We did it in three days. I had eight Kindles. I bought them at Best Buy in Greenwood the day after I bought the toolkit. Loaded the books on them. Called my team in for a Thursday-Friday-Saturday working session. I'll send you the photo of the plan we taped on the wall Saturday night."

"Send it."

"I'll send it when we hang up."

Mark drew a box around *Step 1: three books*. He drew a line out of the box and wrote *Thursday-Friday-Saturday*.

"Step Two," Reggie said.

"Go."

"Step Two is a training library. They call it that. It's twenty-some courses they've already built. Mastering AI Prompts is the first one most people start with. There's one called Stop Being the Bottleneck which I personally think is the best course in the whole toolkit. They're in markdown — which is just text — and you load them into an AI and the AI remixes them for your company. Your name, your industry, your terminology, your voice. Then you build the EPUBs using the Step Zero pipeline. Then you put them on Kindles. Then you hand the Kindles to whoever's getting trained."

"My people don't read."

"Your people will read on a Kindle. That's the Kindle thing I was going to come back to. There's no Slack on a Kindle. There's no email. There's no LinkedIn. There's no notifications. You hand somebody a Kindle and you say *this is your training material* and they read it. I've seen guys who haven't read a book since high school finish a Kindle in three weeks. It's the device. It's a different experience than handing somebody a PDF."

Mark wrote *Kindles* and underlined it twice.

"Step Three," he said.

"Step Three is the tool. It's called NovaSyn Chat Lite. It's a Windows executable. You don't install it. You just copy it to a workstation and run it. It's a unified front end to all the AI providers — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Grok. Conversations stay local on the machine. You can organize them in folders, search them, tag them. There's a prompt library so your people share the prompts that work. There's a context project feature so you can load reference documents and ask questions against them. That last one is the part where the magic happens."

"How."

"Aisha — sorry, I keep using my people's names — I have an engineer named Daria. Twenty-six years old. Two years on staff. We had a recurring tolerance issue on a defense subassembly we couldn't crack. Engineering had been chasing it for eleven months. Daria loaded the quality reports, the maintenance logs, and the temperature data from the shop sensors into a context project in NovaSyn. Spent a Tuesday morning with it. By Tuesday afternoon she had a hypothesis nobody had thought of. By Friday the maintenance team had verified it. The issue was solved. We had been bleeding money on that line for eleven months."

"How much."

"In our case it was a quarter million in scrap and credits. Plus engineering time we'd been writing off."

"Solved in three days."

"Solved in three days by a twenty-six-year-old with a subscription that costs my company twenty bucks a month per seat and a tool that came out of the toolkit. That's TAO. That's what the toolkit lets your people do."

Mark wrote *NovaSyn* and underlined it. He drew a line from *NovaSyn* to *Daria* and from *Daria* to *$250K*.

He looked at the legal pad. The whole thing was starting to look like a wiring diagram.

"Reggie," he said. "Slow down on me for a second. I'm a methodical guy. I have to ask you some hard questions."

"Ask them. That's why I'm on the phone."

"Question one. Who maintains it after you bought it. Pinnacle's whole pitch is that they don't leave you holding a deck. Their pitch is they build you a capability and then they stay involved through the optimization phase. Who's doing that for you with TAO."

"Nobody's doing it for me with TAO. We're doing it ourselves."

"That sounds like the worst-case Pinnacle outcome. The thing where the consultants leave and nobody knows how to maintain it."

"That's not the same thing, Mark. Pinnacle would build us a custom enterprise platform. The maintenance burden on a custom enterprise platform is the platform itself — the code, the integrations, the data pipelines. There's nothing like that here. NovaSyn is an executable. It doesn't need maintenance. ChatGPT and Claude are services somebody else maintains. The training materials are markdown files in a folder. The strategic books are books. There is nothing to maintain because there is nothing custom. There is no thing we own that has dependencies. What we own is the toolkit and the toolkit doesn't need babysitting."

Mark wrote *no custom platform* on the legal pad. He drew a small triangle next to it.

"Question two," he said. "Who trained your people. You said you and your leadership team did the books in three days. Fine. After that — who actually ran the courses for your three hundred and twenty employees. You don't have a learning and development department."

"We don't have an L and D department. We have me, Bev, and Marv my plant manager. The way we did it: we picked Marv's shop as the pilot. Eighteen guys plus three engineers plus two QC techs. Twenty-three people. We handed them all Kindles with Mastering AI Prompts loaded on them. We told them: read this in the next two weeks, you can read it on the clock. We installed NovaSyn on their workstations. We made me available for one hour every Friday at lunch to answer any question they had. Marv was on the floor every day anyway."

"That's it."

"That's it. After six weeks the pilot was running. We expanded. By the end of the quarter we had everyone in the building on NovaSyn. The training material was the training material. The tool was the tool. The leadership was the leadership. There wasn't a separate training organization. There didn't need to be."

Mark wrote *one hour Friday lunch*.

"Question three."

"Go ahead."

"What did you actually spend. End to end. Toolkit plus Kindles plus API budget plus any internal time you'd call out as material. I want a real number."

Reggie thought about it.

"The toolkit, I told you. Kindles I bought thirty of them at one forty a pop, call it forty-two hundred. The API budget for the first year for three hundred and twenty people running through ChatGPT and Claude organization accounts, full year, was twenty-eight thousand. We've spent more on coffee. Internal time — Bev and I and Marv spent maybe two weeks of our own time over the quarter that I'd call out as opportunity cost. Call it another fifty thousand fully loaded if you want to be honest. End to end first year, eighty thousand dollars. Order of magnitude."

"And what did it return."

"Revenue up fourteen percent year over year. EBITDA up nineteen. Three big customer wins I credit at least partially to the proposal work the team is doing with NovaSyn. The Daria fix paid for the whole thing forty times over by itself. And — Mark, listen to me — my people are happier than they have ever been. In thirty-one years of this business I have not had a year where the floor was as good as it was last year. Voluntary turnover dropped from twelve percent to six. We had two guys retire who told me on their way out it was the best place they'd ever worked."

Mark stopped writing.

He looked at the legal pad.

"Question four," he said. "How did you decide what to do first."

"The Blueprint Audit. Book three. You sit your leadership team down and you go department by department and you ask the same set of questions. What does this department actually produce. Where is the bottleneck. Who are the people. What would change if every person here had a competent assistant who never slept and never got tired and could read a thousand pages in a minute. You answer those questions honestly and the plan writes itself. We picked Marv's shop because Marv was ready and because his bottleneck was the clearest. Your situation will be different. The book walks you through it."

"Okay."

Mark looked at the clock. Nine fifty-two. They had been on the phone fifty-two minutes.

"Reggie. Hold on a second."

"I'm holding."

Mark opened his laptop. He pulled up the text Reggie had sent him at eight fifty-six that morning. The text was a URL. Mark had not opened it yet. He clicked it now.

The page that loaded was simple. A clean white page. The headline read *TAO — Transformative AI Operations*. The subhead read *The Complete Toolkit*. There was a single line of body copy. There was a price. There was a button.

Mark read the page. He read it twice.

"Reggie."

"Yeah."

"I'm on the page."

"What do you think."

"I think it looks like a one-page Shopify site."

"It is a one-page Shopify site."

"That doesn't make me less interested. That makes me more interested. There's no funnel. There's no pop-up. There's no chat widget."

"There's a button."

"There's a button."

"Push the button, Mark."

Mark put his phone on speaker and set it on the desk. He clicked the button. The checkout page loaded. He typed in his card number. He typed in the billing address. He clicked *Complete Purchase*. The page refreshed and showed a download link and an order confirmation.

He clicked the download link.

A zip file began downloading. Eighty-four megabytes. The bar moved fast. The download finished in maybe nine seconds.

"It's downloaded," Mark said.

"You bought it."

"I bought it."

"Welcome to TAO."

Mark laughed. It surprised him to laugh. He hadn't been planning to laugh.

"Reggie. I just bought it while you were still on the phone."

"You did."

"Yusuf is going to ask me how long the procurement evaluation was."

"Tell him it was an hour."

"Tell him it was an hour."

"Tell him Don Pelletier's nephew vouched for it through Don and Don vouched for it through me and I vouched for it through you and now you own it. Your procurement evaluation was three references and a Shopify checkout."

"That's exactly what I'm going to tell him."

Mark unzipped the file. The folder structure was clean. Four folders, just like Reggie had said. *0. Book Creation Skills*. *1. AI WIN WIN*. *2. Training Courses*. *3. NovaSyn Chat Lite*. At the top of the parent folder was a single markdown file named *USERS_MANUAL.md*.

"Reggie. There's a User's Manual at the top level."

"That's where you start."

"Tonight."

"Tonight. Read it cover to cover. It's not long. Maybe thirty pages. Then tomorrow, the rest of the weekend, you spend with the four folders. You don't have to read everything. You just have to walk through each folder once and understand what's in there."

"Got it."

"Mark."

"Yeah."

"One more thing before we hang up. You want me to come up next Saturday? I'll drive up Friday night. We can sit at your kitchen table Saturday morning and I'll walk you through how we ran the three-day leadership working session. I'll tell you what worked and what didn't. I'll tell you the things I got wrong the first time so you don't get them wrong."

Mark blinked at the laptop screen.

"You'd do that."

"I'd do that."

"I'd say yes."

"Saturday morning. Eight o'clock. I'll bring coffee."

"I'll be here."

"And Mark."

"Yeah."

"Don't tell Pinnacle yet. Don't call them on Monday. Get through the weekend first. Read the User's Manual tonight. Read the books tomorrow. Be sure of what you're doing before you make any call you can't take back."

"That's the plan."

"Good. Talk to you Saturday."

"Saturday."

Mark hung up.

He sat at the desk for a long moment without moving. The coffee in the mug to his right was cold. The legal pad in front of him was full. The laptop in front of him showed an unzipped folder with four subfolders and a markdown file at the top. The October light through the window was the color of brake lights again.

He opened the User's Manual.

The first line read *This is not a book. It is not a course. It is a complete organizational transformation toolkit.*

He started reading.

He had been reading for maybe four minutes when he heard the front door open downstairs and Eliza's voice in the entryway calling out to one of the kids about wet swim bags on the floor. He heard footsteps on the stairs. Eliza appeared in the doorway of the office and looked at him over the rim of her own coffee mug.

She had the look she gave him when she could tell something had shifted. She had given him that look maybe six times in twenty years of marriage. He had given it back to her maybe twice.

"What happened," she said.

Mark looked up from the laptop.

"I think I just bought the thing."

"Bought what?"

"The way out of the Pinnacle pitch."

Eliza came into the office. She set her coffee mug on the corner of his desk. She read the screen over his shoulder for a moment. She read the legal pad.

"Reggie?"

"Reggie."

"How much."

He told her the number.

She did not say anything for a moment.

"That's less than the bathroom remodel," she said.

"That's less than the bathroom remodel."

"And it does what Pinnacle's three million was going to do."

"Reggie says yes. Reggie's revenue is up fourteen percent. His people are happier than they've ever been."

Eliza put a hand on the back of his neck and left it there for a few seconds. She did not say anything. She did not have to. She had been married to him long enough.

"I'll be downstairs," she said.

"I'm going to read this."

"Read it."

She picked up her coffee mug and walked out.

Mark turned back to the laptop. The cursor was blinking at the top of the User's Manual. He scrolled down. The four-step process was on page two. *Step 0: Book Creation Skills. Step 1: AI WIN-WIN. Step 2: Training Courses. Step 3: NovaSyn Chat Lite.*

He picked up the legal pad and turned to a fresh page.

At the top he wrote, in capital letters: *TAO.*

Then he started reading.
