# Multiplied: A TAO Implementation Novel

**Author:** Christopher Bender
**Publisher:** AI WIN-WIN Institute
**Volume:** 7 (Textbook Shelf)
**Genre:** Business fiction / business fable (Phoenix Project / The Goal lineage)
**Length target:** ~38,000 words, 13 chapters, 5 parts
**Companion to:** *TAO: The Way of AI Orchestration* (handbook); *Anchor* (literary case study); *Meridian* (Agicore case study)

---

## Concept

A business novel that walks the reader through a complete 90-day TAO implementation at a fictional mid-size manufacturer. The implementation is the protagonist. Characters get arcs. Methodology becomes visible through scenes and dialogue rather than sidebars or chapter-end summaries. Reads like *The Phoenix Project* (Gene Kim) or *The Goal* (Eli Goldratt) — fiction that teaches.

Where *Anchor* is literary novella demonstrating TAO through the story of a recovery (insurance carrier course-correcting after AI failure), *Multiplied* is business fable demonstrating TAO through the story of a greenfield implementation (manufacturer choosing TAO instead of the wrong path). Different register, different shape, same operational substrate.

The book closes with the implementation finished and the business measurably stronger. The methodology is the hero. The CEO is the vehicle.

## Setting

**Bellfield Industries.** Cincinnati, Ohio. 380 employees. Third-generation family-owned. Founded 1956 by William Bellfield in a converted streetcar barn in Norwood; expanded by his son Robert through the 1980s; currently led by William's grandson Mark Bellfield. Patricia Bellfield (Robert's widow) is board chair. The company produces precision machined components for two principal markets: aerospace (subassemblies, fastener systems, engine-housing tolerancing) and medical devices (orthopedic implant components, surgical instrument substrates).

The physical plant is two production shops separated by a covered breezeway plus the office building and the customer-service / engineering wing. Shop A (Theo Park, manager) handles aerospace work. Shop B (James Tanaka, manager) handles medical-device work. The customer-service and engineering teams sit in the office wing alongside finance, HR, and the executive suite.

Bellfield is a real company in the industrial heartland sense: known to its customers, invisible to the broader world, profitable but not lavishly so, technically excellent in narrow domains, behind on software and digital tooling. The kind of company that wins by being trusted and on-spec and on-time for forty years and then suddenly has to figure out what to do about AI.

## Cast

**Mark Bellfield** — 45. CEO. Took over four years ago after his father Robert's stroke forced early retirement. Engineering degree from Purdue, MBA from Ohio State (evening program while running the shop floor as plant manager in his early thirties). Married to Eliza, two kids (high school and middle school). Pragmatic, methodical, conscientious to a fault. Has been losing sleep over AI for eighteen months because his customers — particularly the aerospace primes — have been asking questions Bellfield doesn't have answers to. *Primary protagonist; POV in Chs 1, 3, 6, 12, 13.*

**Dana Reyes** — 38. VP Operations. Joined Bellfield two years ago from Procter & Gamble's industrial division. Mark's hire, against the advice of two board members who wanted someone with manufacturing-only experience. Master's degree in industrial engineering from Georgia Tech. Married to a high school teacher, no kids by choice. Quietly ambitious. The execution owner for the TAO rollout. *Major POV: Chs 4, 5, 9, 10.*

**Yusuf Okafor** — 52. CFO. Joined ten years ago. Family in Lagos and London. Detroit accent from forty years in the auto-supply orbit. Skeptical of the Pinnacle consulting pitch; more skeptical of TAO until he sees the numbers. *POV: Ch 8 (the first-win chapter, observed through the CFO's eyes).*

**Linda Kowalski** — 60. VP Sales and Customer Relations. Twenty-five years at Bellfield. Knows every customer-side contact by their kids' names and college choices. Open hostility toward AI early in the book — and the eventual convert when the tool proves it can amplify her relationships rather than replace them. *POV: Ch 11 (her conversion).*

**Theo Park** — 44. Plant Manager, Shop A (aerospace). Korean-American, second-generation. Father owned a machine shop in Toledo. Theo is the pilot manager. Calm, technical, deeply trusted by his crew. *POV: Ch 7.*

**James Tanaka** — 51. Plant Manager, Shop B (medical devices). Older than Theo, regulatory-bound by FDA requirements that make experimentation slower. Initially uneasy about TAO because of validation overhead. Wins later. *Significant supporting role in Chs 9-10; no POV chapter.*

**Aisha Bell** — 28. Production Engineer in Shop A. Black, Cincinnati native, Xavier University engineering. Three years at Bellfield. Quiet, careful, very good. Becomes the breakout voice of the rollout when she uses NovaSyn to crack a long-running quality investigation in a single afternoon. *POV: Ch 8 (alongside Yusuf, in alternating scene sequence).*

**Patricia Bellfield** — 70. Board Chair. Robert's widow. William Bellfield's daughter-in-law. Lived in Cincinnati since 1973. Conservative on most questions; loyal to the company's identity over its growth trajectory. Mark's strongest skeptic on AI and his strongest ally on the family's legacy. *POV: Ch 12 (the board meeting).*

**Connie Sutter** — 47. Customer-Service Lead, aerospace accounts. Linda's right hand. Important supporting role: she's the operational adopter of NovaSyn before Linda is, and her usage is part of what eventually convinces Linda.

**Reggie Vance** — 56. Outside industry peer. Owns Vance Tool & Manufacturing in Indianapolis. Forty-two-year manufacturing veteran. Mark calls him at the right moment in Ch 2 and Reggie is the one who points Mark at TAO. Recurring presence by phone and text throughout the book. Not a consultant, not a vendor — just a peer who has done it. *POV: none, but his phone calls structure several chapters.*

**Pinnacle Consulting** — The "easy path" antagonist. A Big-Four-tier consulting firm pitching Bellfield a $2.4M, eighteen-month "AI Transformation Engagement" in Ch 1. Embodied principally by **Bryce Templeton**, the partner who delivers the pitch. Pinnacle reappears at chapter intervals: the follow-up phone calls, the assistant who keeps trying to schedule the next meeting, the LinkedIn announcements about other clients who DID sign — the constant pull of the conventional path. Pinnacle is not evil. Pinnacle is what most companies pick. The book's central decision is Mark choosing the other thing.

## Voice / register

This is **NOT** the close-third literary register of *Anchor* and *Meridian*. This is the business-fable register: faster pacing, dialogue-forward, scene-driven, accessible. The audience is a board member, a CEO, a department head, an operations VP — not a literary-fiction reader.

**Reference voice:** *The Phoenix Project* (Gene Kim, Kevin Behr, George Spafford), *The Goal* (Eli Goldratt), *The Five Dysfunctions of a Team* (Patrick Lencioni). All three:

- Third-person past tense, single POV per chapter, but the POV is **transparent** rather than literary — the reader sees through the character rather than into them
- Short paragraphs (often 2-4 sentences)
- Lots of dialogue, including dialogue tags ("Mark said," "Dana said") with sparing description
- Characters introduced with explicit roles when they first appear ("Dana Reyes, his VP of Operations")
- Methodology articulated **in dialogue** by characters — when someone explains TAO, it's because a character is asking and another character is answering
- Stakes visible — every chapter ends with something at stake or something changed
- Scenes have clear locations and time markers ("Tuesday morning," "the third-floor conference room")
- Numbers and operational specifics are concrete (production rates, cycle times, dollar figures, headcounts)

**What to avoid (versus *Anchor*):**
- Long literary observational paragraphs
- Sensory grounding for its own sake (the harbor, the rain)
- Restrained interior consciousness ("the question would not move in her mind")
- Single thematic image carrying a chapter
- Sentences that resist paraphrase

**What to embrace:**
- "Mark hung up the phone and stared at the ceiling for a moment."
- "Dana came in Tuesday morning with four Kindles and a list of names."
- "*Three days.* *Three days* and we have the audit done. Or we don't."
- Characters who say the methodology in their own words
- Pacing that pulls the reader through

## The 90-Day Rollout (the book's spine)

The TAO User's Manual lays out a 90-day rollout for a mid-size organization:

- **Month 1:** Foundation. Leadership alignment (Weeks 1-2). Training prep (Weeks 3-4).
- **Month 2:** Pilot. Pilot department launch (Weeks 5-6). Iterate and expand (Weeks 7-8).
- **Month 3:** Scale. Organization-wide rollout (Weeks 9-10). Institutionalize (Weeks 11-12).

The book maps to this directly. Each part covers a stage. Each chapter is roughly a week or a discrete event within a week.

## Chapter-by-chapter

### Part I: The Crisis (Chs 1-3, ~9,000 words)

**Chapter 1: The Pitch** (Mark POV, ~3,000 words)
The Pinnacle Consulting pitch. Cincinnati, October, Friday afternoon. Bryce Templeton and two associates in the Bellfield boardroom for two hours. $2.4M engagement, eighteen months, "AI Transformation." The deck is gorgeous. The case studies are real. Bryce is good at his job. Mark feels the gravity. Cost is large but not insane for a $94M-revenue company with the runway and the customer pressure. After Pinnacle leaves, Mark stays in the boardroom alone for an hour, drinking lukewarm coffee, looking at the deck. The chapter establishes: the AI pressure on Bellfield (aerospace customers asking pointed questions), the family stakes (Patricia's quiet skepticism, Robert's framed photo in the lobby), the financial stakes (Yusuf's spreadsheet of the engagement cost vs. EBITDA projection), the personality (Mark's methodical conscientiousness), the antagonist (Pinnacle is not bad, they're the conventional path), the question (what should Mark do). Closes with Mark calling Reggie Vance from his car on the drive home.

**Chapter 2: The Phone Call** (Mark POV, ~3,000 words)
Saturday morning. Mark and Reggie on a long phone call. Reggie owns a similar-sized shop in Indianapolis and went through his own AI question two years ago. He looked at three consulting pitches (Pinnacle's main competitors), almost signed one, and at the last minute someone in the trade association introduced him to "this thing called TAO." Reggie walks Mark through what TAO actually is: not a consultancy, not a SaaS subscription, not a course — a complete toolkit ($X, one-time, owned outright) with strategic foundation books, a training library, a tool, and a publishing pipeline. Reggie's company implemented it themselves in a quarter. Reggie's revenue is up 14% year over year. Reggie's people are happier than they have ever been. Reggie offers to walk Mark through it. Mark, on the phone, opens his laptop and buys the toolkit. The download finishes while they're still on the call. Reggie says: *read the User's Manual first. Tonight. The rest of the weekend you spend with the four folders.* Chapter closes with Mark thanking Reggie, hanging up, opening the User's Manual on his laptop in his home office, his coffee cooling, his wife Eliza glancing in from the hallway with the look she gives when she can tell something has shifted.

**Chapter 3: The Saturday** (Mark POV, ~3,000 words)
Saturday afternoon and evening, into Sunday. Mark with the toolkit. Reading the User's Manual cover to cover. Browsing each of the four folders. The Phoenix Project trick: the methodology becomes visible to the reader through Mark's discovery. Mark reads *The AI Multiplication Doctrine* (Book 1 of AI WIN-WIN) on his back porch with a beer. He reads *Old Ideas Revisited with AI* (Book 2) in bed. He runs the Blueprint Audit's first questions against his own company in his head. He sees the shape of it. He emails Dana late Saturday night: *we need to talk Monday morning. I think I know what we're doing. We're not signing with Pinnacle.* Sunday morning he walks his neighborhood. He thinks about his grandfather William, who built the company by hand. He thinks about Patricia. He drafts the message he'll send to Bryce Templeton on Monday — short, professional, declining the engagement. He doesn't send it yet. He puts it in drafts. Closing image: Mark at his kitchen table, the User's Manual open, a yellow notepad full of names — every department head, every plant manager, the leadership team he's going to ask to do this with him.

### Part II: Foundation — Month 1 (Chs 4-6, ~9,000 words)

**Chapter 4: The Kindles Arrive** (Dana POV, ~3,000 words)
Week 1. Monday morning. Mark calls Dana into his office and shows her the toolkit. Dana, the operations VP, the executor, the one who actually has to make this happen — she's intrigued but immediately practical. She asks Mark hard questions: what's the budget, what's the timeline, who's running it, what happens if it fails, how do we measure success. Mark, having done his weekend homework, has answers for most. The ones he doesn't, they work through together. By Tuesday afternoon Dana has ordered eight Kindle Paperwhites (Mark, Dana, Yusuf, Linda, Theo, James, Patricia, plus Connie as the surprise eighth — the customer-service lead who Dana thinks should be in the room). By Wednesday the Kindles arrive. By Thursday the AI WIN-WIN books are loaded on each one. Dana calls the leadership team into the boardroom Friday for what she calls "a different kind of working session." Patricia attends in person — she lives twenty minutes away and Mark called her specifically to make sure she came. The meeting agenda is one sentence: *we are spending three days reading three books together and we will leave Saturday with an implementation plan or with a decision not to do this.* Yusuf raises an eyebrow. Linda crosses her arms. Theo nods once. Dana opens the first book.

**Chapter 5: The Three Days** (Dana POV, ~3,000 words)
Thursday-Friday-Saturday, the leadership working sessions. Compressed, scene-driven. Each book gets one day. Thursday: *Good to 10x*. The team works through what AI multiplication means in their context. Linda is visibly resistant. Yusuf is taking notes. Patricia listens. Theo asks one question late in the day that breaks the meeting open: *if AI multiplies people, do we lose anyone?* Mark answers: *we are not laying anyone off. That is not what this is. This is what we are doing instead.* Friday: *Old Ideas Revisited with AI*. The team finds the framework that fits Bellfield: the Toyota Production System / Jidoka model, reborn as AI-assisted quality system. Patricia, whose husband Robert had a Toyota plant tour book on his bedside table for twenty years, is the one who points at the framework. Saturday: *The Blueprint Audit*. The team produces the actual department-by-department plan. Shop A becomes the pilot (Theo agrees). Customer service joins the pilot (Linda objects; Connie volunteers). Engineering joins later. Finance and HR get their own track. Shop B (medical, FDA-regulated) gets a slower, more careful rollout. By Saturday evening they have an implementation plan on a single page taped to the boardroom wall. They take a photo of it. Dana texts the photo to Mark's wife Eliza, who texts back *did you actually do it.* Mark, sitting in the parking lot in his car, replies: *we actually did it.*

**Chapter 6: The Pilot Pick** (Mark POV, ~3,000 words)
Week 2. The pilot department decision and the prep work. Theo's Shop A is chosen as the pilot. Connie's customer-service group joins as the secondary pilot. Mark walks the shop floor with Theo on Tuesday afternoon — explains it to Theo's crew in person, not in a memo. The crew's reaction is mixed: the older machinists are wary, the younger ones are openly excited, one mid-career operator named Hector says *am I going to lose my job over this* and Mark says *no. We are going to use this to make your job better. If anyone asks you to do something that doesn't make sense, you tell me directly.* Dana spends the week setting up the API accounts (organization-wide ChatGPT and Claude billing through Yusuf), distributing the NovaSyn Chat Lite executable to the pilot group's machines, and selecting the first training course (Mastering AI Prompts) from the toolkit's training library. The chapter has a key scene: Linda comes into Mark's office Thursday afternoon and says she thinks this is a mistake. Mark listens for thirty minutes. He doesn't argue. He thanks her and says: *I want you in the room when the pilot data comes back. If you still think it's a mistake after six weeks, we stop.* Linda nods. Closing scene: Friday afternoon, Theo's crew getting their Kindles, the training course loaded, Mark in the back of the shop floor watching, the kind of clarifying nervousness he hasn't felt since the week he took over from his father.

### Part III: Pilot — Month 2 (Chs 7-9, ~9,000 words)

**Chapter 7: NovaSyn Day One** (Theo POV, ~3,000 words)
Week 5. The Monday morning the pilot launches. Theo's shop floor. NovaSyn Chat Lite is installed on every workstation. The Mastering AI Prompts training course is loaded on every Kindle. Theo runs a thirty-minute kickoff with his crew at 7 AM. Then they start working. The chapter follows three people through their first day: Hector (the wary mid-career operator), Aisha Bell (the young engineer who turns out to be a natural), and a third character named Walt Bayer (60, the oldest machinist, plate steel and Cohiba cigars on weekends, no patience for software) who is going to be the hardest sell. By end of day: Aisha has used NovaSyn three times — once to draft a quality memo, once to summarize a customer email thread, once to look up a thread-tolerance spec from her engineering notes. Hector has used it once and was surprised it worked. Walt has not used it and made one joke about it that landed flat. Theo, debriefing with Dana at 4:30 PM in his office, says: *day one is fine. Day five is the question.* Dana asks if there's anything they need to fix before tomorrow. Theo says: *the training course is good but it's not specific to manufacturing. Aisha asked if we could load some of our spec docs as references. I think we should let her try.* Dana, on the spot, says yes. The chapter establishes the rhythm: small experiments, fast iteration, the methodology working through specific people.

**Chapter 8: The First Win** (alternating POV: Aisha Bell + Yusuf Okafor, ~3,000 words)
Week 6. The first significant win. Aisha has spent the week loading Bellfield's quality-control documentation into a NovaSyn context project. She's been investigating a recurring micro-tolerance issue on an aerospace fastener line — a problem that's been on the books for fourteen months and that the engineering team has not been able to nail down. On Thursday morning Aisha sits down with NovaSyn and walks through the case. She asks the AI to cross-reference the quality reports with the maintenance logs and the temperature data from the shop-floor sensors. She asks it to surface any pattern the engineering team might have missed. By Thursday noon she has a candidate hypothesis: the tolerance drift correlates with humidity changes after the building's HVAC retrofit in 2024, specifically on the machines closest to the loading dock door. By Thursday afternoon she has run the hypothesis past Theo. By Friday morning the maintenance team has installed a humidity sensor and confirmed it. By Friday afternoon Theo has emailed Mark. Saturday morning Mark calls Yusuf. *This is one example,* Mark says. *I want you to see what it cost us in the form of scrapped parts, customer credits, and overtime trying to chase it.* Yusuf, who has been quietly building a financial-impact dashboard for the rollout, runs the numbers Sunday. The fourteen-month micro-tolerance issue cost Bellfield $387,000 in scrap, $112,000 in customer credits, and roughly $190,000 in chargeable engineering time. Total: $689,000. Solved in three days by a 28-year-old production engineer with a $20-per-month AI subscription and three hours of training. Yusuf's POV: he has been a CFO for two decades. He has watched a lot of consulting engagements deliver on promises that turned out to be smoke. This is not smoke. The chapter closes with Yusuf in his kitchen Sunday night, looking at the dashboard, calling Mark, and saying: *I want to accelerate. I want NovaSyn deployed in finance next month, not Q2.*

**Chapter 9: The Iteration** (Dana POV, ~3,000 words)
Weeks 7-8. The iteration phase. The pilot has been running for a month. Some things are working. Some things aren't. Dana is running the operational tightening. The chapter is structured around three specific problems and how they're solved:

1. *The prompt-library problem.* People in the pilot are reinventing the same prompts every week. Dana institutes a "prompt-library" workflow — Connie's customer-service team starts contributing a shared collection of vetted prompts to NovaSyn's prompt-library feature.
2. *The hallucination problem.* A junior engineer uses an AI-generated spec citation without verifying it and submits it to a customer. The citation was wrong. Customer catches it. Connie has to walk it back. Mark, Dana, and Theo handle it together — no blame, but they institute a "verify before customer-facing" rule. The chapter doesn't gloss this — the methodology has to handle real failure modes.
3. *The Walt problem.* Walt Bayer, the 60-year-old machinist, has still not used NovaSyn. Theo sits down with him at his bench on a Tuesday afternoon and asks him directly. Walt says he doesn't trust it. Theo asks what would make him trust it. Walt thinks for a long moment and says: *if it helped me write the safety memo I've been trying to write for two months and I haven't gotten right.* Theo opens NovaSyn on Walt's bench computer. They work on the memo together for forty minutes. Walt walks away with a draft he can edit. By the end of the week Walt is using it daily. The chapter closes with Dana writing the week-8 status memo to Mark: *the pilot is working. Recommend full-organization rollout Monday Week 9.*

### Part IV: Scale — Month 3 (Chs 10-12, ~9,000 words)

**Chapter 10: The Rollout** (Dana POV, ~3,000 words)
Week 9. The organization-wide rollout. Compressed, kinetic chapter. The chapter is structured around the rollout's logistics: distributing the NovaSyn executable to every workstation, distributing Kindles loaded with training materials to the rest of the company, opening up the API budget, scheduling the training cohorts. James Tanaka's Shop B (the medical-device, FDA-regulated shop) is the slow, careful rollout — they're not deploying NovaSyn for customer-facing or compliance work, only for internal documentation and analysis. James and Dana spend most of a day together working out the validation protocol for the regulated work. Finance gets NovaSyn under Yusuf. HR gets it under their VP (briefly introduced: Marcia Vega). Engineering gets it under their VP (briefly introduced: Patrick Whitmore). Customer service rolls it out under Linda (still grudging but now operationally committed because of Connie's results). The chapter has one big set-piece: Friday afternoon all-hands meeting where Mark addresses the entire company in person on the shop floor — speakers in both shops, mics for questions. He explains what TAO is. He explains why they chose it over Pinnacle. He explains the principles. He answers questions. The hardest question comes from a 30-year-old machinist Mark doesn't know by name: *what happens to my job if the AI is doing my job better than I am?* Mark answers: *the AI is not doing your job. The AI is helping you do your job. The day we use AI to replace people instead of help them is the day this company stops being Bellfield. If you ever see that happening, you come to me directly.* The room goes quiet. The chapter closes with the all-hands ending and people filing back to their work and Dana standing next to Mark on the empty shop floor and saying: *that was the right answer.*

**Chapter 11: The Conversion** (Linda Kowalski POV, ~3,000 words)
Weeks 10-11. The Linda Kowalski conversion arc. Linda has been the central skeptic of the entire rollout. Her sales and customer relations team has been using NovaSyn for six weeks now, mostly through Connie. Linda herself has not. The chapter opens with Linda in her office on a Tuesday morning, working on a proposal for a new aerospace prime — a customer she's been trying to land for three years. The proposal is long, technical, requires assembling specs from twelve different jobs, requires writing case-study language in a register Linda finds tedious, and is due Thursday at noon. She has been dreading it. Connie comes in and asks if Linda has tried using NovaSyn for the technical assembly. Linda waves her off. By Tuesday afternoon Linda is alone in her office with the deadline looming and the technical assembly still not done. She opens NovaSyn for the first time, types in her query awkwardly, and gets back something useful. Useful enough that she keeps going. By Wednesday morning she has the entire technical assembly drafted. By Wednesday afternoon she has the case-study language drafted in a register that needs only her editing pass — and the editing pass is what she's good at, what she's been doing for thirty years. By Thursday morning the proposal is done two hours ahead of the deadline. She sends it. She calls Connie and tells her: *you were right. I'm sorry I dismissed you for six weeks.* Connie says: *you didn't dismiss me. You were waiting for the right moment to try it.* The chapter closes with Linda walking down to Mark's office Friday morning and saying: *I want to be in the rollout report next month. I want my numbers in the dashboard.* Mark, who has been waiting for this moment for ten weeks, says: *welcome aboard, Linda.* The proposal Linda submitted lands the customer two weeks later.

**Chapter 12: The Board Meeting** (Patricia Bellfield POV, ~3,000 words)
Week 12. The Q4 board meeting. The first board meeting since the TAO rollout. Patricia, the board chair, walks into the conference room on a December Friday morning carrying a leather portfolio with her hand-written notes for the meeting. Mark presents. Yusuf presents the numbers — productivity gains per department, reduction in cycle times, savings from the Aisha fastener fix, reduction in overtime, customer-retention metrics. Dana presents the operational status — adoption rates, prompt-library size, training completion. Linda presents the new customer Mark and the team don't fully realize Linda has landed yet — the aerospace prime from Ch 11 plus a smaller medical-device order that came in last week. Patricia, who has been quiet throughout, finally speaks at the end. She talks about Robert, her late husband — about the day in 1987 when he took over from William, his father, and stood in the boardroom and announced that they were buying their first CNC machine. The board fought him. He did it anyway. Within five years CNC was standard. Patricia draws the parallel without underlining it. She turns to Mark. *Your grandfather and your father each had one of these moments,* she says. *The moment where the company became something a little different because the world had become something a little different. You just had yours.* The chapter closes with the board meeting adjourning and Patricia stopping Mark at the door and asking if he'll come over for dinner Sunday. Mark says yes. Outside the boardroom window the snow has started.

### Part V: Result (Ch 13, ~3,000 words)

**Chapter 13: The Spring Open House** (Mark POV, ~3,000 words)
Six months later. April. Bellfield is holding an open house for its largest aerospace customer — the VP of supply chain for one of the major primes, plus three of his deputies, plus their quality-systems chief, are visiting for a tour. This is the customer that almost a year ago started asking Mark the pointed AI questions that had kept him up at night. Mark, Dana, Theo, and Aisha lead the tour. They walk the visitors through Shop A. They show how NovaSyn is integrated into the quality-control workflow. They show the prompt library. They let the visitors talk to Hector at his bench and to Aisha at her workstation. They explain that no one was laid off. They explain what the books taught the leadership team. They give each visitor a Kindle preloaded with *The AI Multiplication Doctrine* and *The Blueprint Audit* and a copy of *Anchor* — because the customer's quality chief asked about insurance-industry parallels.

The aerospace VP — a tall man in his late fifties named Carter Reyes, no relation to Dana — listens to the entire two-hour tour and asks careful questions throughout. At the end, in the parking lot, before getting into his rental car, he says to Mark: *I have toured eighty manufacturing facilities in the last twenty-four months. I have heard sixty AI pitches from CEOs in that time. I have not heard one of them describe what you just described. We are increasing your share of our 2027 production schedule. The contract paperwork will be in your hands next week.*

Mark thanks him. Carter Reyes shakes Dana's hand and Theo's hand and asks for Aisha's email address so his quality chief can stay in touch. The visitors get in their car and leave.

Dana, Theo, Aisha, and Mark stand in the parking lot for a moment.

Aisha says: *did that just happen?*

Theo says: *that just happened.*

Mark walks back into the building. He goes to the lobby. The framed photo of his grandfather William is on the wall. His grandfather is standing in front of the converted streetcar barn, in 1958, with one hand on the lathe and the other holding a wrench. Mark looks at it for a long moment. He thinks about Patricia. He thinks about the day Reggie Vance called him back. He thinks about the Saturday afternoon he spent on the back porch with the User's Manual.

He thinks: *we did it.*

He goes back to his office. Dana follows him in with three things on her list that she wants to start in May. They sit down to plan.

The chapter closes with that ordinary working scene. The implementation is over. The company has changed. The next thing is starting.

## Cross-references

- *Anchor* is named explicitly once, in Ch 13, when Mark gives the aerospace customer a copy. The narrative parallel between the two books is preserved without underlining.
- *TAO: The Way of AI Orchestration* (the handbook) is named explicitly in Ch 2 (Reggie tells Mark to buy it) and used throughout.
- The four-step process from the User's Manual structures Months 1-3 directly (Step 0 publishing pipeline / Step 1 AI WIN-WIN strategic foundation / Step 2 Training Courses / Step 3 NovaSyn Chat Lite). Each step is shown in use.
- The 90-day rollout is the book's structural spine.

## What this book is for

A board considering AI transformation. A CEO who has been pitched by a Big Four consultancy and is wondering if there's another path. An operations VP who has been asked to lead an AI rollout and doesn't know what good looks like. A department head wondering whether AI will eliminate their team or amplify it. A reader who wants to know what it actually looks like, week by week, when a company gets this right.

The book teaches by showing.

## Title note

*Multiplied* names the central premise of the AI Multiplication Doctrine: AI does not replace people; it multiplies them. The title is the answer to the question every employee in the book asks at some point — *what happens to me?* Single-word title rhymes stylistically with *Meridian* and *Anchor* on the shelf, even though the genre differs.
