# Chapter 4: The Kindles Arrive

Dana Reyes pushed open the door to the Bellfield Industries lobby at six fifty-three on Monday morning with a coffee in each hand. The lobby was empty except for Jeannie at the front desk, who looked up, saw the two coffees, and raised one eyebrow.

"He's already up there," Jeannie said.

"I know."

"He's been here since six."

"I know that too."

Dana took the back stairs because the back stairs were faster and because she did not want to spill the coffee. She had gotten Mark's text at nine forty-seven on Sunday night. *Come in at seven. Bring two coffees. I need to show you something.* She had not slept particularly well after that, which was unusual for her. She had been at Bellfield two years and she had gotten exactly four texts from Mark on a Sunday night during that time. The first one had been about a fire in the breezeway between the shops. The other three had been about meaningful things.

She knocked on the doorframe of Mark's office.

"Come in."

Mark was at his desk with his laptop open and a yellow notepad next to it. The notepad was full of names in Mark's small precise handwriting. Dana set one of the coffees down on the corner of his desk and sat down in the chair across from him with her own.

"Thanks for coming in early," he said.

"You sent a Sunday-night text. I came."

"I'm going to show you something. I want you to ask me hard questions about it. I don't want you to be polite. Yusuf is going to do the financial questions on Tuesday. I want you to do the operational ones now."

"Okay."

Mark turned the laptop around. On the screen was a folder structure. Four folders. *0. Book Creation Skills. 1. AI WIN WIN. 2. Training Courses. 3. NovaSyn Chat Lite.* Below the folders was a markdown document titled *USERS_MANUAL.md*.

"This is what we're doing instead of Pinnacle," Mark said.

"This is a folder."

"It's a toolkit. I bought it Saturday morning after I talked to Reggie. It's the complete strategic framework, the training library, the desktop tool, and the publishing pipeline. All four pieces. One-time cost. We own it outright."

"What's the one-time cost?"

"Under two thousand dollars."

Dana drank from her coffee. She did not say anything for a moment.

"Mark."

"Yeah."

"Pinnacle was two-point-four million."

"I know."

"This is two thousand."

"I know."

"What's the catch."

Mark smiled for the first time since she had walked in. "The catch is we have to actually do the work. The toolkit doesn't do it for us. The toolkit gives us the strategy, the training, and the tool. We have to run it ourselves."

"That's not a catch. That's the price."

"Right."

Dana set the coffee down on the desk and pulled the notepad toward her. She turned to a fresh page.

"Okay," she said. "Five questions."

"Go."

"What's the budget. All in. For everything we're going to spend on this in the first quarter."

Mark had clearly been waiting for this one. He did not even look at his notes. "Under fifty thousand. Toolkit is two. Kindles for the leadership team are about a thousand. API budget for the first quarter — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, the works — call it twenty thousand for org-wide accounts at the volume we'll start at. Hardware nothing. We already have laptops. The rest is operating overhead. Fifty is the ceiling. I'd be surprised if we touch forty."

"Fifty against three-four million for Pinnacle."

"Yes."

"What's the timeline."

"Ninety days per the User's Manual. Month one is foundation. Month two is the pilot. Month three is the scale-out. Ninety days from when we start to when we are running across the company."

"That's tight."

"It's tight but it's not made up. Reggie did it in a quarter. The manual is calibrated for a company our size. We can be honest if it slips. But ninety is the target."

Dana wrote *90 days* and underlined it.

"Third question. Who is running it."

"I am. Strategically. You are the execution owner. We are doing this together. I don't want to put a consultant in the middle of us. There is no consultant. There is no project manager from outside. There is you and me and the leadership team and the people in the shop. That's the team."

"You're going to be in it day-to-day."

"I'm going to be in it day-to-day. I'm not going to sit upstairs and look at status reports. I am in the room. I am at the pilot kickoff. I am at the all-hands. I am in the meetings where we figure out what isn't working. If this is the thing we're betting on, I am in the room while we bet."

Dana wrote *Mark in the room. Dana exec owner. No consultant.* She underlined the last three words.

"Fourth question. What happens if it fails."

Mark sat back in his chair. He drank from the coffee Dana had brought him.

"We stop," he said. "We tell people. We tell the board. We try again or we pivot. We don't pretend. That's the rule. If we hit week six and the pilot is not working, we say so. If we hit week ten and the rollout is going sideways, we say so. If at any point this is the wrong thing, we admit it and we change course. We are not going to be the company that runs a transformation program for eighteen months because nobody wants to be the one to say it isn't working."

"Are you going to be able to do that. Be the one to call it."

"Yes."

"With the board."

"Yes."

"With your mother."

Mark looked at her for a moment.

"Patricia is going to be in the room from day one," he said. "She is going to see what I see. If she thinks at any point we should stop, she will tell me. If I think at any point we should stop, I will tell her. We are not going to surprise each other."

Dana wrote *fail mode: stop, tell, try again or pivot. No pretending.*

"Fifth question. How do we measure success."

Mark turned the laptop back toward himself and clicked into the *1. AI WIN WIN* folder. He scrolled past two of the books and opened the third. *The Blueprint Audit.* He turned the laptop again so Dana could see.

"Book three is the audit framework," he said. "Department by department. For each department we map what they do now, what they spend their time on, where the bottlenecks are, and what AI could multiply. Then we set targets. Cycle time. Throughput. Quality measures. Customer-response time. Time-to-proposal. Things we already measure. We aren't inventing new metrics. We are taking the metrics we already track and we are asking whether AI moved them. Six weeks into the pilot we look. Twelve weeks in we look again. If the metrics moved, we keep going. If they didn't, we go back to the audit and ask why."

"That's clean."

"It's clean because we already measure the things. The book just says: pick the things, set the targets, look at them on a schedule."

"What's the first target."

"The pilot department. Shop A. Theo's shop. The aerospace work. The micro-tolerance issue on the fastener line that engineering has been chasing for fourteen months. If AI can get us one inch closer on that one in six weeks, we have our proof point."

Dana wrote *Shop A. Fastener line. 14-month problem. 6-week test.*

She looked at the notepad. She looked at Mark. She looked at the screen.

"Okay," she said.

"Okay what."

"Okay I'm in."

Mark exhaled. It was the smallest sound. Dana had known Mark for two years and she had never heard him exhale like that.

"Good," he said.

"What do you want me to do first."

"I want you to order Kindles."

---

Tuesday afternoon. Dana sat at her desk with a browser tab open to Amazon and a list on her notepad.

The list had started at six names. Mark. Dana herself. Yusuf. Linda. Theo. James. Then Patricia. Seven.

She tapped the pen against the desk.

She was thinking about Connie Sutter.

Connie was the customer-service lead for the aerospace accounts. Forty-seven. Linda's right hand. The person who actually talked to the customer-side counterparts on the phone three or four times a week. Linda owned the relationships. Connie ran the work.

Dana had been at Bellfield long enough to know that the work was where the AI would actually land. The relationships sat above the work. The work was where the multiplication happened. If anyone in customer service was going to use this tool on day one, it was going to be Connie, not Linda.

Linda had said she didn't want in. Dana had heard her say it.

But Connie hadn't said anything.

Dana wrote *Connie* on the bottom of the list and looked at it for a moment. Eighth name. Customer-service lead. Linda's right hand. Operational adopter before Linda was.

She walked down to Mark's office and showed him the list.

Mark read it. He stopped on the eighth name.

"Connie."

"She's the one who'll actually use it first. Linda will come around when she sees Connie's numbers. If we wait for Linda we'll lose three months."

"Linda is going to know we added her."

"Linda is going to know I added her. That's fine. I'd rather have the conversation about why I added Connie than wait for permission."

Mark looked at the list again. Then he looked up.

"Order them."

Dana ordered eight Kindle Paperwhites that afternoon. She paid for one-day shipping. She paid for it on the company card and she put *Leadership reading initiative* in the description field because she did not yet know what to call this and *Leadership reading initiative* was a thing nobody would ask a question about.

---

Wednesday morning. Eight Amazon boxes on Dana's desk.

She unboxed all eight after lunch. She lined them up in a row on her conference table. She plugged them into the wall along the baseboard, all eight at once, on the power strip she had borrowed from Yusuf's assistant. They took half an hour to charge to the level where she could start loading them.

She had built the EPUBs the night before.

She had used the Book Creation Skills pipeline from the toolkit — Step 0, the publishing engine. The three AI WIN-WIN books in the toolkit were already in markdown. She had run the `build_epub.py` script three times in her home office on Tuesday night with her husband watching the basketball game in the next room. Three EPUBs had appeared in the output folder, properly formatted, with covers and tables of contents and chapter breaks.

*Good to 10x — The AI Multiplication Doctrine.*
*Old Ideas Revisited with AI.*
*The Blueprint Audit.*

She had also pulled two of the training courses from Step 2. *Mastering AI Prompts* and *How to Use Your Strategic AI.* She had built EPUBs of those too, with the same script.

Five EPUBs total. Onto each Kindle. By USB cable, one at a time, the way the manual recommended.

It took her three hours.

She left the office at seven thirty Wednesday night with the eight Kindles in a canvas bag from a manufacturing trade show she'd attended in 2023. She drove home with the bag on the passenger seat.

In her driveway, before she went inside, she sat for a moment.

*Eight Kindles,* she thought. *Eight people. Five books. One toolkit. Ninety days.*

She went inside.

---

Thursday afternoon. Two o'clock. Dana hand-delivered the Kindles.

Mark first. He was already expecting his. He took it from her without comment and set it on his desk next to his laptop. "Thank you," he said.

Yusuf next. Yusuf turned the Kindle over twice in his hands, asked her what was loaded on it, asked her how to charge it, and put it in his briefcase. "I'll read tonight," he said.

Theo in the shop-floor break room. Theo had been expecting it because Mark had mentioned it to him on Monday. Theo opened the box right there at the table, took out the Kindle, looked at the home screen, scrolled through the five book titles, and said: "Good. I'll start tonight."

James in his office in Shop B. James asked which of the books was most relevant to FDA-regulated work. Dana said book three, the audit, because it was the one that would tell him how slow they had to go. James nodded and put the Kindle on his desk.

Patricia at her house. Dana had called ahead. Patricia received her in the front sitting room with coffee and a plate of shortbread. Patricia held the Kindle in both hands, looked at the screen, and said, "Robert read on one of these for the last two years. I haven't picked one up since." She set it on the side table. She did not promise to open it. Dana did not press.

Connie at her desk in the customer-service wing. Connie said, "I wasn't expecting one," and Dana said, "I added you. Mark approved." Connie looked at the Kindle and looked at Dana and said, "Thank you for adding me." Her voice was very quiet.

Then Linda.

---

Linda Kowalski's office was on the second floor at the south end of the building, in a corner with two windows. One window looked out at the parking lot. The other looked at the trees. Linda had been in that office for nineteen years. She had decorated it herself, with photographs of customers' children, framed thank-you notes, a small ceramic dog her granddaughter had made her in kindergarten.

Dana knocked on the open door.

Linda looked up. She looked at the Kindle in Dana's hand. Her expression did not move.

"Dana."

"Linda."

"I told Mark I didn't want in on this."

"I know. I heard you say it."

"And you brought me a Kindle anyway."

"I did."

Linda set down her pen. She crossed her arms.

"Explain."

Dana stepped into the office and closed the door behind her. She did not sit. She held the Kindle in both hands.

"You're getting the Kindle anyway," Dana said. "You don't have to open it. You don't have to read it. You don't have to come to the meeting tomorrow. If you don't open it in two weeks I'll come back here and I'll take it back. But you're going to get to say you turned this down knowing what it was. Not knowing what we wanted you to think it was. Knowing what it actually is."

Linda did not answer.

Dana set the Kindle on the corner of Linda's desk, beside the ceramic dog.

"That's all," she said. "It's loaded with five books. Three are short. The other two are training courses. Nobody is going to ask you whether you read them. I'm just leaving it here."

Linda looked at the Kindle.

She did not pick it up. She did not push it away.

"Dana."

"Yes."

"Why did you take this job."

Dana paused. The question was not the one she had expected.

"I took this job because Mark hired me. And because I thought he was going to do interesting things."

Linda nodded slowly. "He's going to do interesting things."

"Yes."

"All right."

Linda did not say anything else. Dana let herself out and closed the door behind her.

In the hallway she stood for a moment.

*She didn't open it,* Dana thought. *But she didn't push it back either.*

She walked back to her office.

---

Friday morning. Ten o'clock. The third-floor boardroom.

Patricia arrived in person at nine fifty. She lived twenty minutes away. Mark had called her on Tuesday to make sure she came. She wore a navy sweater and slacks and she carried a leather portfolio Dana recognized as the one she'd carried to the last three board meetings. She put the portfolio on the table at the seat closest to the window and the Kindle Dana had given her on Thursday next to it.

Patricia had charged the Kindle.

Dana noticed that.

Yusuf came in next, then Theo, then James (who had driven over from Shop B specifically). Connie came in at nine fifty-six and sat in the chair nearest the door, as if she was not entirely sure she was supposed to be in the room. Linda came in at nine fifty-nine with her Kindle in her hand.

The Kindle was charged.

Dana noticed that too.

Mark came in last. He sat in the chair next to Yusuf, not at the head of the table.

"Good morning," Dana said.

The room looked at her.

"Thank you for being here. Before I say anything else I want to say what this meeting is. The agenda is one sentence. Here it is."

She had taped a piece of paper to the whiteboard behind her. Single sentence in thick black marker.

*We are spending three days reading three books together and we will leave Saturday with an implementation plan or with a decision not to do this.*

Yusuf raised one eyebrow. Linda crossed her arms. Theo nodded once. Patricia said nothing. Mark looked at the floor for a moment and then looked back up.

"Dana's running it," Mark said. "I'm in the room as a participant. Not as the boss. If she calls on me I'll answer. If she doesn't I'll listen with the rest of you."

Connie, by the door, took out a small notebook.

Dana picked up her own Kindle from the table.

"Open to book one," she said.

She watched them.

Theo opened his Kindle first. Then Yusuf. Then James. Then Connie. Then Mark.

Patricia set the leather portfolio aside. She picked up the Kindle. She turned it on with the small button on the bottom. The home screen came up. She looked at the five book titles. She tapped the first one.

Linda was last. Linda uncrossed her arms. She picked up the Kindle. She held it for a moment without turning it on. Then she pressed the button. The screen came alive in her hands.

Dana stood at the front of the room and watched them all open the same book.

*Here we go,* she thought.

She sat down.
