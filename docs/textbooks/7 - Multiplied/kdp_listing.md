# KDP Listing — Multiplied: A TAO Implementation Novel

## Description

In October, a major consulting firm walks into the boardroom of Bellfield Industries — a 380-person family-owned precision manufacturer in Cincinnati — and pitches a $2.4 million, eighteen-month AI Transformation Engagement. The deck is beautiful. The case studies are real. The CFO has done the math. The price is affordable. The framework is sound.

The CEO almost signs.

Instead he calls a peer in Indianapolis who has done it differently. Within seventy-two hours he has bought a $X toolkit and committed his leadership team to ninety days of work. The toolkit is called TAO — Transformative AI Operations. The peer is the only reason Mark Bellfield is reading these books in his back yard on a Saturday afternoon with a beer in his hand, thinking about his grandfather.

*Multiplied* is the story of what happens next.

Thirteen chapters. Ninety days. Three months: foundation, pilot, scale. Eight Kindles. One CFO who comes around. One VP of Sales who refuses to come around — and then does. One twenty-eight-year-old production engineer who solves a fourteen-month, six-hundred-thousand-dollar problem in a single afternoon. One sixty-year-old machinist who won't open the software for six weeks and then writes a safety memo he'd been trying to write for two months. One board chair, the founder's daughter-in-law, who watches her son make the same kind of decision her husband made in 1987 when he bought the company's first CNC machine. One major aerospace customer who, six months after the implementation is done, stands in the parking lot in April and says he has toured eighty-four manufacturing facilities in the last twenty-four months and heard sixty-one AI pitches and never once heard what he just heard.

This is a business novel in the lineage of *The Phoenix Project* (Gene Kim) and *The Goal* (Eli Goldratt). The implementation is the protagonist. The methodology is the hero. The characters are the vehicle.

It is also, structurally, a teaching document. Every step of the four-step TAO process is shown in use. Every phase of the ninety-day rollout is dramatized. Every common failure mode — the hallucinated citation that goes to a customer, the holdout who won't adopt, the FDA-regulated department that needs a careful validation boundary, the VP who is sure the whole thing is going to ruin her customer relationships — is shown being handled by people who do not have perfect information and who make mistakes and who solve them together.

There is no villain. The consulting firm is not bad. The skeptics are not bad. The wary machinists are not bad. The book is a story about a company choosing the other path because the right peer made the right phone call at the right time, and then doing the work.

*Multiplied* is the operational companion to *Anchor* — Christopher Bender's literary novella demonstrating TAO through the story of an insurance carrier recovering from an AI-automation failure. Where *Anchor* dramatizes restoration, *Multiplied* dramatizes implementation. Both stand alone. Both are companions to *TAO: The Way of AI Orchestration*, the handbook to which *Multiplied* serves as worked example.

Hand this to a board considering an AI transformation. Hand it to a CEO who has been pitched by a Big Four consultancy and is wondering if there is another path. Hand it to an operations VP who has been asked to lead an AI rollout and does not know what good looks like. Hand it to a department head wondering whether AI will eliminate their team or amplify it. Hand it to any reader who wants to see, week by week, what it looks like when a company gets this right.

The book teaches by showing.
