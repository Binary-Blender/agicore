# Chapter 3: The Saturday

By one o'clock Saturday afternoon Mark had read the User's Manual twice. The first time straight through, the way Reggie had told him to. The second time slower, with the legal pad open beside the laptop and a pen in his hand.

He had filled four more pages.

The manual was short. Reggie had said thirty pages and Reggie was about right — maybe twenty-eight on the screen, maybe a forty-minute read the first time through. But the thing it contained was not short. The thing it contained was an entire operational philosophy compressed into bullets and tables and a single closing line that Mark had circled twice on the legal pad.

*The only variable left is execution.*

He had written it on his pad with the date next to it. Saturday, October 18.

He pushed back from the desk and stretched. His back hurt. He had been hunched over the laptop for almost three hours.

Downstairs Eliza was making lunch for the kids. He could hear her in the kitchen talking to their daughter about a college essay that was due Tuesday. He could smell soup.

He took his laptop and the legal pad and went down to the kitchen.

"You eating?" Eliza said.

"I'm eating."

She put a bowl in front of him at the island. He sat. He opened the laptop next to the bowl.

"Working through lunch is not allowed at the bathroom remodel," Eliza said.

"This isn't a remodel."

"What is it."

"It's a different kind of building."

She raised an eyebrow but did not press. She knew the look. She had seen the look six times.

Mark ate the soup with one hand and scrolled with the other.

The four-step process. He went back to it for the third time.

Step Zero: Book Creation Skills. The publishing engine. Skill documents and Python scripts that took markdown content and produced professional EPUBs you could load onto a Kindle. The point of Step Zero was that the company eventually published its own internal books — its own training manuals, its own field manuals, its own SOPs — and put them in front of its people in a distraction-free format.

He had not seen this part coming. He had assumed the toolkit was a fixed set of books. It was not. It was a set of books plus the means to make more books indefinitely. The company could grow its own library over time.

He wrote on the pad: *we eventually publish our own field manuals. The shop floor. QC. Customer service. We become the source of our own training material.*

Step One: AI WIN-WIN. Three books. *Good to 10x*. *Old Ideas Revisited with AI*. *The Blueprint Audit*. The strategic foundation Reggie had walked him through on the phone.

Step Two: Training Courses. Twenty-plus courses. Mastering AI Prompts. How to Use Your Strategic AI. Stop Being the Bottleneck. Others. Each one in markdown, ready to be remixed for Bellfield's specific operations and rebuilt as an EPUB.

Step Three: NovaSyn Chat Lite. The tool. The Windows executable in the `release` folder.

And then the rollout. The ninety-day plan. He read the Month 1, Month 2, Month 3 schedule a second time.

Month 1: Foundation. Weeks 1-2 leadership alignment. Weeks 3-4 training prep.

Month 2: Pilot. Weeks 5-6 pilot department launch. Weeks 7-8 iterate and expand.

Month 3: Scale. Weeks 9-10 organization-wide rollout. Weeks 11-12 institutionalize.

He wrote on the pad: *90 days. Three months. Not eighteen.*

He underlined it.

Pinnacle's engagement was seventy-two weeks. TAO's rollout was twelve. The ratio there was almost as large as the ratio in the dollars.

He closed the laptop and finished the soup.

"You're not going to be social today, are you," Eliza said.

"I'm not going to be social today."

"That's fine. I'm taking the kids to my mother's at three. We'll be back tomorrow afternoon."

"Okay."

"Read your books. Drink a beer at some point. Don't sleep in your office."

"I won't."

She kissed him on the forehead and went to find the kids.

---

At two thirty Mark carried his laptop and a beer out to the back porch and sat down in the wicker chair his father had given him for his fortieth birthday. The October light came through the maple in long slanted bars. The neighbor's dog was barking at something behind the fence two houses down.

He opened *Good to 10x*. Book one of AI WIN-WIN.

He had set up his Kindle but the books were still on the laptop. He could read them as markdown for now. He would build the EPUBs later, when he understood what he was doing.

The book was not long. He read the first chapter on the porch. Then the second. Then he kept going.

The premise was the title. The premise was the entire book. *AI does not replace your people. It multiplies them.*

The argument was structured around a single image the author returned to: every employee in a knowledge-driven business had a band of effective output. The bottom of the band was what they produced on a bad day, when they were tired or distracted or had to do a kind of work they were not naturally good at. The top of the band was what they produced on a good day, when the work suited them and they were focused and rested. Most employees lived most of the time in the middle of their band. Almost nobody operated at the top of their band consistently, because nobody could.

What AI did, the book argued, was take work the employee was bad at and lift it out of their hands. What AI did was take work the employee was slow at and accelerate it. What AI did was take the parts of the job that pulled the employee down toward the bottom of their band and remove them — leaving the employee free to spend more time near the top of their band.

The compounding was the multiplication. An employee who spent forty percent of their time near the top of their band instead of twelve produced not three times as much but seven times as much, because the top of the band was where the leverage lived. Top-of-band work was where the customer relationship deepened, where the design problem got cracked, where the institutional knowledge accumulated.

Mark put the book down on the porch table.

He thought about Hector Salazar on the loading dock.

Hector, who was excellent at the physical operation of the forklift, at the spatial puzzle of fitting pallets into a truck, at the running conversation with the drivers, at noticing when something on the dock was about to become a problem. Hector, who hated the paperwork. Hated the bills-of-lading reconciliation. Hated the maintenance log. Hated the incident reports. The paperwork was the bottom of Hector's band.

What if Hector had a tool that did the paperwork for him.

Mark wrote on the pad: *Hector — kill the paperwork. He keeps the dock running. The tool keeps the records.*

He thought about Aisha Bell, the young production engineer in Shop A, whom Theo Park had been talking about for the last six months. Aisha was twenty-eight. Aisha had a Xavier engineering degree and three years on the floor. Theo had said in their last one-on-one that Aisha had the kind of intuition for quality patterns that he had only ever seen in one other engineer in his career — but that she was getting buried under the volume of the documentation work nobody else wanted to do.

Aisha's band, Mark thought, was wide. The top of her band was the pattern recognition. The bottom of her band was the form-filling.

He wrote: *Aisha — pull the documentation work out. She investigates. The tool documents.*

He thought about Linda Kowalski.

Linda's band was narrower because Linda had been in customer relations for twenty-five years and her band had compressed around the parts of the job she was great at. The top of Linda's band was the human relationship — the lunch, the call, the read of the customer's mood, the thirty-second decision about whether to push or wait. The bottom of Linda's band was the technical writing — the long proposals, the spec assemblies, the case-study language. Linda hated that work. She had told him so a dozen times.

He wrote: *Linda — keep her on the relationships. The tool drafts the proposals. She edits.*

He read the rest of *Good to 10x* on the porch with the beer next to him. He read until the light started to go.

By the time he finished the book he had filled three more pages of the legal pad. He had not written notes from the book. He had written about Bellfield. He had written down the names of nineteen people and what each of them was best at and what each of them was bad at. The book had stopped being a book around chapter five and had become a lens.

He closed the laptop.

He sat for a long time with the empty beer bottle and watched the light go.

---

Eliza had left at three for her mother's. The house was quiet when Mark came back inside at six thirty. He made himself a sandwich, drank a glass of water, took the laptop and the legal pad upstairs, and went to bed early with both of them propped on the comforter.

He opened *Old Ideas Revisited with AI*. Book two.

The premise of book two was different from book one but it built on book one. Book one had been about what AI did to an individual. Book two was about what AI did to an organization. Specifically: book two argued that the great organizational frameworks of the twentieth century — the systems-thinking books, the lean books, the quality books, the management books — had been written for a world in which the human bottleneck was fixed. Every framework, the book argued, had a place where it ran into the limit of what a person could read, write, remember, or compute. AI removed that limit.

The book worked through six frameworks. Toyota Production System and Jidoka. Theory of Constraints. The OODA loop. Total Quality Management. Lean. Agile.

The chapter on the Toyota Production System and Jidoka stopped Mark cold.

His father had kept a book about the Toyota plant tour on his bedside table for twenty years. The book had been a hardcover, blue dust jacket, photographs in the middle. His father had taken him to read passages of it out loud at the kitchen table when Mark was twelve and thirteen. His father had been fascinated by the andon cord — the cord that ran along the assembly line at Toyota, the cord any worker could pull at any time to stop the entire line if they saw a quality defect. *Imagine,* his father had said. *Imagine giving every guy on the line the authority to stop the whole plant. That's how much they trust their people.*

Mark read the chapter twice.

The argument was that the andon cord had a hidden constraint. The constraint was the diagnostic capacity that activated when the cord was pulled. When a worker pulled the cord, a small army of engineers and supervisors had to converge on the station and figure out what was wrong, fast, before the line stayed down too long. Toyota had built that capacity. Most companies could not. Most companies could not afford to keep a diagnostic squad on standby. That was why most companies could not run Jidoka — not because they did not trust their people, but because the cost of the diagnostic response was too high.

What if the diagnostic squad was AI.

What if any worker on any line in Bellfield could, in effect, pull the cord — and what arrived was not a squad of engineers but an AI that had read every quality report, every maintenance log, every spec sheet, every prior incident, and could in two minutes produce a candidate diagnosis the engineers could then verify.

Mark sat up in bed.

He picked up the legal pad. He wrote, in capital letters at the top of a fresh page: *AI-AUGMENTED JIDOKA.*

Under it he wrote: *Every operator can flag a quality concern. NovaSyn runs the diagnostic against the historical record. Engineering verifies. Decision in hours, not weeks.*

Then he wrote, smaller, underneath: *this is our framework. This is the one.*

He read the rest of book two with the legal pad on his knees. He took notes on every chapter. He went back to the Toyota chapter once more before he fell asleep.

The light on the bedside table was still on when he finally closed the laptop a little after midnight.

---

He was up at five forty.

He made coffee. He sat in the kitchen with the User's Manual open on the laptop in front of him and read book three.

*The Blueprint Audit.*

This was the practical one. Reggie had said so. The book started with a single instruction in bold type at the top of chapter one: *do not read this book passively. Read it with your own company in mind. Answer each question for your own company before you read the example answers in the book.*

Mark answered.

The audit was structured around departments. For each department the book asked the same six questions.

*What does this department actually produce?*

*Where is the bottleneck?*

*Who are the people, and what is each person best at?*

*What is the work that nobody likes to do, that everyone has to do, and that AI could do instead?*

*What is the work that requires human judgment that AI could augment but not replace?*

*If this department had a competent assistant for every person — an assistant that never slept, never got tired, could read a thousand pages in a minute, and never asked for credit — what would change first?*

Mark went department by department.

The loading dock. What did the dock produce. Shipped product on time. Where was the bottleneck. The reconciliation between the load list and the bills of lading and the maintenance log — the paperwork. Who were the people. Hector. Two other dock leads. Six rotating dock hands. What did Hector hate doing. The paperwork. What would change first if Hector had an assistant. The paperwork would write itself.

He wrote *Hector — bills of lading, maintenance memos, incident reports* and underlined it.

Shop A. Theo Park, manager. Aerospace production. What did Shop A produce. Precision machined components on tight tolerance. Where was the bottleneck. The quality investigations. Not the day-to-day quality — Theo's crew was good. The deep investigations. The fourteen-month tolerance drift problem on the fastener line that Aisha had been quietly trying to crack on her own time. Who were the people. Theo. Aisha. Six lead machinists including Walt Bayer, the sixty-year-old. Fourteen operators. What was the work nobody liked doing. Documentation. Cross-referencing across logs. What was the work requiring human judgment AI could augment. The pattern detection in quality — the kind of work Aisha was already doing, but slower than the company needed.

He wrote *Shop A — the pilot. Aisha leads the investigation work with NovaSyn. Theo runs the floor.*

Shop B. James Tanaka, manager. Medical devices. What did Shop B produce. FDA-regulated implant components and surgical instrument substrates. Where was the bottleneck. The validation overhead. Every change had to be documented and approved through the quality system. AI could not be deployed for any customer-facing or compliance-critical work without a validation protocol Bellfield did not yet have. The work nobody liked doing. The validation documentation. The work requiring human judgment AI could augment. Internal analysis. Trend detection. Process improvement opportunities surfaced from the validation data.

He wrote *Shop B — slow, careful rollout. Internal documentation only. Validation protocol first. James leads that himself.*

Customer service. Linda. Connie underneath her. Six reps. What did customer service produce. Customer relationships and retention. Where was the bottleneck. The technical proposal writing. The long spec assemblies. The case-study language. The work nobody liked doing. The proposal writing. What would change first if every rep had an assistant. The proposal turnaround would drop from weeks to days.

He wrote *Customer service — Connie pilots. Linda holds back until she sees Connie's numbers.*

Engineering. Patrick Whitmore, VP. Twelve engineers. He wrote what they needed.

Finance. Yusuf. Eight people. He wrote what they needed.

HR. Marcia Vega. Three people. He wrote what they needed.

By eight in the morning he had been through every department in the company.

The plan was on the legal pad. Not a complete plan. A first plan. A plan he could take into a room with his leadership team next week and put on the wall and argue over.

He looked at it.

It made sense.

He had not felt this way about a plan in a long time.

---

He emailed Dana at one fifteen in the morning when he had been finishing the Toyota chapter. The email had been three sentences.

*Dana. We need to talk Monday morning. I think I know what we're doing. We're not signing with Pinnacle.*

She had replied at six thirty Sunday morning.

*7 AM Monday. Your office. I'm bringing coffee.*

He smiled at the reply. He had hired Dana two years ago over the objection of two board members who had wanted somebody with a pure-manufacturing background. He had hired her because she answered emails like that.

He drafted a second email at eight that morning. This one was to Bryce Templeton at Pinnacle. He kept it short.

*Bryce. I appreciate the time and the thoroughness of the pitch on Friday. After review with my team and our board chair, we've decided to pursue a different approach to our AI strategy. We won't be moving forward with the engagement. Thank you for the work you put in, and I hope our paths cross again.*

He read it twice. He did not send it. He moved it to drafts.

He would send it Monday afternoon. After he had talked to Dana. After he had called Patricia. After he was sure.

He closed the laptop and went for a walk.

---

The neighborhood was quiet at nine on a Sunday morning. The maples were lit from inside. A few people were out — a woman in a Bengals sweatshirt walking a Labrador, a man Mark did not know jogging on the other side of the street, an older couple at the corner reading the paper on their porch.

Mark walked.

He walked past the house on Hawthorn where he had grown up. He walked past the elementary school. He walked down to the park where his father had taken him fishing on Saturday mornings the year before the stroke.

He thought about his grandfather William.

William had built Bellfield Industries by hand in 1956 in a converted streetcar barn in Norwood. William had bought the first lathe with money he had borrowed from his brother-in-law. William had hired three machinists in the first year. William had stayed on the floor for forty-one years. The framed photograph in the lobby was William at fifty-three, standing in front of the streetcar barn with one hand on the lathe and the other holding a wrench, and the photograph had been taken in 1958.

He thought about his father Robert.

Robert had bought the first CNC machine in 1987 over the objection of the board. Robert had told Mark the story when Mark was sixteen. *The board told me we couldn't afford it. The board told me the old guys on the floor would never use it. The board told me CNC was a fad. I bought it anyway. Five years later we had eight of them. The old guys learned. The young guys flourished. We doubled in size in the decade after.*

Mark stopped on the sidewalk.

He thought: *my father had a CNC moment. My grandfather had a streetcar barn moment. This is mine.*

He walked the rest of the way home with that in his head.

---

He came back to the kitchen at ten thirty. He poured a fresh coffee. He set the legal pad on the kitchen table and opened it to a clean page.

At the top he wrote, in capital letters: *THE LIST.*

Under it he started writing names.

*Dana Reyes.* VP Operations. Hire her into the execution lead role for the rollout. She would say yes.

*Yusuf Okafor.* CFO. Skeptical but fair. He would say yes once he saw the numbers.

*Theo Park.* Shop A manager. The pilot manager. He would say yes.

*Connie Sutter.* Customer service lead. The customer-service pilot lead. She would say yes.

*Aisha Bell.* Production engineer. The breakout. She would say yes.

*Patrick Whitmore.* VP Engineering. Steady and curious. He would say yes.

*Marcia Vega.* HR VP. Aligned with him on culture. She would say yes.

*James Tanaka.* Shop B manager. He would say yes but slowly. He would want the validation protocol nailed down before anything moved.

*Patricia Bellfield.* Board chair. His mother. The conservative voice on the board, his strongest skeptic on AI and his strongest ally on the family legacy. She would say yes if he made the case in the language of the company her husband had built.

*Linda Kowalski.*

He looked at her name.

He drew a small box around it.

Eliza walked into the kitchen at eleven. She had come back from her mother's an hour earlier than she had said. She set her bag on the counter and came over to the table and looked over his shoulder.

"What are you doing."

"I'm making the list."

"List of what."

"The people I'm asking to bet on this with me."

Eliza read over his shoulder for a moment. She read the names. She read the small box around Linda Kowalski.

She put a hand on his back, the same way she had done in the office yesterday.

"They'll say yes," she said.

"Most of them will," Mark said. "Linda won't."

"Linda might surprise you."

He looked up at her.

"You think."

"I think Linda has been at that company longer than you've been alive at it. I think Linda watched your grandfather hire her father. I think Linda is going to fight you for six weeks and then turn around and become the loudest person in the room about it."

"Maybe."

"I've watched her at Christmas parties for fifteen years. I know what she looks like when she's protecting something she loves. She's going to fight because she loves it. Not because she doesn't."

Mark looked at the name in the box.

He did not say anything for a long moment.

"Six weeks," he said.

"Maybe four."

He closed the legal pad.

He set it on the table beside the laptop.

Through the kitchen window the October light was clean and high. The maple in the side yard moved a little in a wind he couldn't hear. Somewhere up the street a kid was shouting at another kid about a soccer ball.

He looked out the window.

"Monday morning at seven," he said. "Dana's bringing coffee."

"You ready for it."

"I'm ready for it."

"Then send the Pinnacle email."

"Monday afternoon. After I talk to Dana. After I call my mother."

"Okay."

She kissed the top of his head.

She walked out of the kitchen and up the stairs.

Mark sat at the kitchen table with the closed legal pad in front of him and the empty coffee mug to one side and the laptop dark to the other side. He sat there for a long time. He did not open anything. He did not write anything more.

He looked out the window at the maple.

He thought: *Monday morning. Seven o'clock. We start.*
