# Chapter 1: The Pitch

Bryce Templeton arrived at the Bellfield Industries headquarters in Norwood at one fifty-five on a Friday afternoon in October, in a charcoal suit that did not have a single visible wrinkle. He was thirty-eight years old and a senior partner at Pinnacle Consulting's Cincinnati office. He had two associates with him, both in their late twenties, both with laptop bags slung diagonally across their bodies. They had parked their rental SUV in one of the visitor spaces nearest the door. The receptionist, Jeannie, called up to Mark at one fifty-seven.

"Your two o'clock is here."

"Send them up," Mark said.

He stood up from his desk and walked to the boardroom on the third floor. The boardroom was the nicest room in the building, which was not saying much. It had been redone in 2003 by his father with a long oak table, twelve leather chairs, a portrait of his grandfather William on the south wall, and a window that looked out over the loading dock and, beyond that, the rail spur where the steel deliveries arrived. The carpet was the original carpet from 2003. The blinds were the original blinds from 2003. Mark had been planning to redo the room for two years and had not gotten to it.

Dana Reyes, his VP of Operations, was already in the boardroom when he walked in. She had set out three water bottles and a tray of cookies from the bakery downstairs.

"You bought cookies," Mark said.

"Yusuf told me they always bring an associate who hasn't eaten lunch."

"Yusuf has been in too many of these."

"That's why I trust him."

Yusuf Okafor, the CFO, walked in a minute later with a folder under his arm. He nodded at Mark. He took the chair at Mark's right. He set the folder down and aligned the edges with the table edge. Mark had been working with Yusuf for ten years and had never once seen the folder set down at an angle.

Linda Kowalski was not at the meeting. Linda had been emphatic about not being at the meeting. *I want nothing to do with any of this until I have to,* she had told Mark on Tuesday, in his office, with the door closed. Mark had let it go. He needed Linda's customer numbers more than he needed her in the room.

Bryce Templeton walked in at exactly two o'clock with his two associates a half-step behind him.

"Mark," he said, holding out his hand. "Thank you for the time today."

"Bryce. Glad to have you here."

The associates introduced themselves. Whitney, who had an MBA from Tuck. Marcus, who had a master's in operations research from Columbia. Both of them had names tags clipped to their bags. Both of them said *pleased to meet you* in the slightly too-rehearsed cadence of people who had been told, multiple times, exactly how to introduce themselves at client engagements.

They sat. Bryce opened a laptop and connected to the boardroom's HDMI cable on the first try, which was the kind of small operational competence Mark always noticed because it almost never happened with vendors.

"I'm going to walk you through the engagement," Bryce said. "It will take about an hour. Then I'd love thirty minutes of questions. Sound good?"

Mark nodded.

The deck was beautiful. Mark could see that immediately. The Pinnacle brand mark in the bottom-right corner of every slide. Photography that did not look like stock photography. Animations that did not look like animations. The first slide was a single sentence in sixty-point serif type: *AI Will Transform Bellfield Industries. The Question Is How.*

"I want to start with the question," Bryce said, "because it's the question every CEO we work with in the industrial sector is asking. The question is not whether AI will reshape your operations. We're past that. The question is whether you reshape on your terms or whether the market reshapes you. And every quarter you wait, the answer to that question becomes more expensive to change."

Mark listened.

Bryce was good. There was no other word for it. He had the cadence of someone who had given this deck eighty times and had refined every transition. He moved through the slides without looking at them. He addressed Mark by name three or four times in ways that did not feel like sales technique but probably were. He addressed Dana once and Yusuf once and got their roles right without looking down. He referenced two of Bellfield's recent contract wins from a press release that had been published the previous month. He had clearly done his homework.

The deck walked through Pinnacle's framework for AI transformation in mid-market industrial firms. Discovery phase, eight weeks. Architecture design phase, ten weeks. Implementation phase, twenty-six weeks. Optimization phase, twenty-eight weeks. Total engagement length, seventy-two weeks, with a ten-percent buffer for scope adjustments. Pinnacle would bring a team of fourteen consultants on rotation. They would integrate with Bellfield's operations team and engineering team. They would build a custom AI roadmap calibrated to Bellfield's specific customer mix and production model. At the end of the engagement Bellfield would have, in Bryce's words, *an enterprise-grade AI operations platform tailored to your business, with the institutional capability to maintain and evolve it.*

The case studies were real. Mark recognized one of the company names on the slide as a competitor in the medical-device space — a Pennsylvania shop that had announced an AI partnership eight months ago in an industry-trade press release that Mark had read three times. The Pennsylvania company's quote on the Pinnacle slide was generous and specific: *Pinnacle didn't just bring us AI. They brought us a way of thinking about AI that's now embedded in how we operate.* Mark could not tell, looking at the slide, whether the Pennsylvania company was happy or whether they had been told to be happy.

The numbers came toward the end. Twenty-three slides in. Pinnacle's recommended engagement for Bellfield, calibrated to Bellfield's revenue band and operational complexity, would be priced at two-point-four million dollars over seventy-two weeks, payable on a milestone schedule that Bryce walked through with practiced clarity. Bryce paused on the pricing slide for several seconds and let it sit. He did not push. He did not editorialize. He let Mark and Dana and Yusuf read it.

Yusuf, beside Mark, had not moved. Mark could feel him doing math without writing anything down.

Bryce closed the deck thirty-eight minutes after he had opened it.

"Questions," he said.

Yusuf went first. Yusuf asked about the milestone schedule, about the change-order provisions, about who owned the IP at the end of the engagement, about what happened to the custom-built platform if Pinnacle and Bellfield parted ways midway through. Bryce answered all four questions in the specific, comfortable way of someone who had answered them eighty times before. Yusuf wrote down four words on his legal pad and did not look up from them.

Dana went second. Dana asked operational questions. Who exactly would be on the team. How much Bellfield staff time the engagement would require. What the discovery phase actually looked like — what would Pinnacle ask Bellfield's people to do. How disruptive the eight weeks of discovery would be on the shop floor. Bryce answered her too. Whitney, the Tuck MBA, jumped in twice with specifics. Dana wrote nothing down. She listened.

Mark went last.

"Bryce," Mark said. "Walk me through how this is different from the standard Pinnacle deck I've seen pitched to manufacturers ten times in the last two years. What's different about it for us."

Bryce smiled. It was a real smile, Mark thought. Not a salesman's smile.

"That's a fair question," he said. "The framework is similar across our industrial engagements because the framework is what works. The deck is similar because we don't reinvent the deck for every client. What's different is the design — the architecture, the implementation, the optimization. Those are calibrated to Bellfield. They're not boilerplate. The boilerplate is the methodology. The custom part is everything that comes out the other side."

Mark nodded.

"And the seventy-two weeks?"

"Mark, I'm not going to tell you that seventy-two weeks is fast. It's not fast. It's the length of time it takes to do this right. The companies that try to do it in twenty-six weeks are the companies that have to come back and do it again in eighteen months. The companies that do it in seventy-two weeks build something that lasts."

"Sure," Mark said.

He did not say anything else for a moment.

Dana glanced at him. Yusuf did not glance at him. Bryce waited.

"Bryce," Mark said. "Thank you. Genuinely. This is the most thorough pitch I've gotten from anyone in the last year. I want to take a couple of weeks to talk to my board and my team and come back to you with a real answer."

Bryce nodded. "Of course. The team and I are here for whatever you need. We can put together a more detailed engagement scope if that would help. We can come back for a follow-up with the board if that's useful. I'll have Whitney email you a calendar hold for next Friday in case you want to use it."

"That would be helpful."

They stood up. They shook hands. Whitney and Marcus closed their laptops and slipped them into their bags. Bryce thanked Dana and Yusuf by name. Mark walked them down to the lobby personally and shook Bryce's hand a second time at the door.

"I appreciate the visit, Bryce."

"I appreciate the consideration, Mark."

The three of them got into the rental SUV and pulled out of the visitor parking. Mark watched the SUV until it was out of sight on Reading Road.

He went back upstairs.

Yusuf was still in the boardroom, in the same chair, with the folder still aligned to the table edge. Dana was standing at the window looking down at the loading dock. Neither of them was looking at the other. Mark closed the door behind him and sat down at the head of the table.

"Yusuf," he said.

"Two-point-four million," Yusuf said. "Plus the change-order exposure, which I'd put at another four hundred thousand based on the standard provisions. Plus our internal labor cost, which I'd estimate at maybe six hundred thousand of full-loaded time across operations and engineering over the eighteen months. Total all-in: three-point-four million. On a hundred and twelve million in revenue this year. That's three percent of revenue, Mark."

"I know."

"It's affordable. If the return is what they say it is, it's a no-brainer. If the return is half of what they say it is, it's a wash but defensible. If the return is a quarter of what they say it is, it's the worst capital allocation I've seen at this company in ten years."

"I know."

Yusuf looked at the folder.

"They're not bad people," Yusuf said. "Bryce is good at this. The framework is real. They'll deliver what they say they'll deliver."

"But?"

"But I don't know that what they deliver is what we actually need."

Mark waited.

"Mark," Yusuf said, "I sat in the same kind of meeting at Stevens Industries in 2018 when I was on their advisory board. They signed. They paid two-point-one million over fourteen months. They got an AI roadmap. They got a custom-built analytics platform. The platform was decommissioned in 2022 because nobody at Stevens knew how to maintain it. Stevens still doesn't know what to do about AI. They paid for a thing and the thing did not turn into a capability."

"Why didn't it turn into a capability?"

"Because the consultants left. The platform left with them, in the sense that the people who understood it left with them. The methodology left with them. Stevens was left holding the deck."

Mark looked at the ceiling.

Dana, at the window, turned around.

"I want to make sure I understand what you're saying, Yusuf," she said. "You're not saying don't sign. You're saying if we sign, we have to do something different about how we keep the capability after they leave."

"That's part of what I'm saying," Yusuf said. "The other part of what I'm saying is that I'm not sure they're the right people to build the capability in the first place. Pinnacle's value-add is that they have done this before. But they have done this before in a way that produces deliverables, not in a way that produces operators. Mark, I don't want a deck. I want our people to actually know how to do this."

Mark nodded slowly.

"That's where I am too," he said.

He stood up. He walked over to the window where Dana was. The afternoon light was already starting to slant. The loading dock had two of the company's trucks backed up to it. A pallet of finished aerospace housings was being loaded onto the second truck. The forklift operator was Hector Salazar, whom Mark had known for fifteen years. Hector backed the forklift up with the same small precise correction he always used, the one he had learned from his father, who had also worked at Bellfield, in the same shop, on the same loading dock, in the 1980s.

Mark looked at Hector.

"Dana," he said.

"Yeah."

"What does Hector need from AI?"

Dana thought about it.

"He needs a faster way to check that the load list matches the bills of lading. He needs the maintenance memos written for him instead of by him. He probably needs an easier way to flag a safety issue when he sees one, because right now the form is a pain in the ass. He doesn't need a custom enterprise AI platform built around a three-year roadmap."

"Right."

Mark stood at the window for another minute.

"Yusuf," he said. "I want you to keep building the financial case for the Pinnacle engagement. Don't kill it yet. Build it out completely. I want to know exactly what saying yes looks like."

"I'll have it by Tuesday."

"And I want to ask around. Industry people. People who have actually done this. I want to know what other paths exist before I spend three million dollars on the path Pinnacle is showing me."

"Yeah," Dana said. "That's the right move."

"I want to talk to Reggie," Mark said.

Yusuf, for the first time in the meeting, smiled.

"Reggie is the right call."

"I'll call him on the way home."

They left the boardroom. Mark went back to his office. He sat at his desk for ten minutes without doing anything. Then he opened his laptop and looked at his calendar for the next two weeks. He had eighteen meetings. He had a board call on Thursday. He had Patricia coming in for lunch on Monday. He had a dinner with the head of supply chain for one of the major aerospace primes on Wednesday — the customer who had been asking the pointed AI questions for the last eighteen months, the customer he had been dreading the dinner with because he did not have the right answer yet.

He closed the calendar.

He got up, took his jacket off the back of the door, walked down to his car in the underground garage, and drove out onto Reading Road.

He was on I-71 South, twelve minutes from home, when he picked up the phone in the car and said: *Call Reggie Vance.*

The phone rang twice.

"Mark," Reggie said. "Tell me what's happening."

"You got an hour Saturday morning?"

"I got the whole day Saturday."

"I just had Pinnacle in. The pitch."

"How much?"

"Two-point-four. Plus change orders. Plus our time. Probably three-four all in."

Reggie was quiet for a moment.

"Mark," he said. "Before you sign anything, call me back tomorrow morning. There's something I want to walk you through. It's going to take an hour. Maybe two. I want you to give me the time before you decide anything."

"That's why I'm calling, Reggie."

"Smart man. Tomorrow morning. Nine?"

"Nine."

"And Mark."

"Yeah."

"Don't sign anything tonight."

Mark laughed for the first time all day.

"I won't, Reggie. I promise."

"Good. Talk to you in the morning."

Mark hung up.

The light was going down over Cincinnati. He could see, ahead of him on the highway, the spire of the cathedral downtown and behind it the river and the bridges. He had grown up in a house twelve miles south of where he was driving. He had walked into Bellfield Industries for the first time when he was four years old, holding his father's hand. He had stood in the lobby looking up at the photograph of his grandfather, the one of William in front of the streetcar barn. He had run his hand along the lathe in the photograph because he had thought, for a long time as a child, that the lathe in the photograph was the actual lathe that was in the lobby, that you could touch the photograph and somehow touch the lathe.

He thought about Hector on the loading dock.

He thought about the question every machinist on his shop floor would ask him within a year, when the AI rollout was visible and real: *am I going to lose my job.*

He thought about what he wanted to be able to say.

He pulled into his driveway. Eliza's car was already there. He sat in his own car for another minute with the engine off and the keys in his hand, and he watched the kitchen window through the trees, and he thought: *I don't know yet what we're doing. But I know I'm not signing two-point-four million dollars on Tuesday.*

He went inside.

Eliza, in the kitchen, looked up at him.

"How was the pitch?"

"It was beautiful."

"Are you signing?"

He set his keys down on the counter.

"I don't think so," he said. "I think I'm doing something else. I don't know what yet."

Eliza poured him a glass of wine without being asked. She slid it across the counter.

"Reggie?"

"Tomorrow morning at nine."

"Good," she said. "Reggie's a smart man."

Mark picked up the glass.

Outside the kitchen window the light had gone the color of the steel he had grown up around — dark gray, with the faintest edge of orange where the sun was finishing its work behind the trees.

He took a long drink.

He thought: *whatever this turns out to be, it starts tomorrow morning at nine.*
