# Chapter 7: NovaSyn Day One

Theo Park was on the shop floor at six twenty Monday morning, forty minutes before the first shift started, with a thermos of black coffee and a yellow legal pad on which he had written, the night before, six words: *tool, like the machines, try it.*

That was the kickoff. He had spent Sunday afternoon trying to write something longer and had given up around four o'clock. The longer he made it, the more it sounded like a memo from corporate. He was not corporate. He was the guy who ran Shop A. He needed to sound like the guy who ran Shop A.

He walked the aisle between the CNC bays. The new NovaSyn icon was on every workstation screen — a small blue square with a white waveform inside. Dana's team had pushed the install Friday after hours. The Kindles were stacked in their charging cradle on the wall outside Theo's office, twenty-three of them, with the operators' names labeled on the cases in white tape.

He stopped at Walt Bayer's bench. Walt was sixty years old and had been a machinist for forty-one of those years. The workstation at Walt's bench had the NovaSyn icon in the lower-left corner of the screen, the same as every other workstation. Theo looked at it for a moment.

*Day five is the question,* he thought.

He went back to the front of the shop and waited for his crew to arrive.

---

They came in starting at six forty-five. Aisha Bell, the production engineer, came in at six fifty with a thermos and her laptop bag. Hector Salazar came in at six fifty-three carrying a hard hat. Walt Bayer came in at six fifty-eight, on the dot, the way he had come in for forty-one years. The rest of the crew filtered in over the next ten minutes.

At seven o'clock Theo stepped up onto the platform at the front of the shop, the one the safety inspector used when she did the quarterly walk-throughs, and clapped his hands once.

"Morning, everyone."

The crew gathered.

"You all know what today is," Theo said. "We're starting the pilot. I'm going to take thirty minutes and then we're going to get to work."

He looked at his crew. Twenty-three faces. Some of them he had hired himself. Some of them had been at Bellfield longer than he had. Hector had a slight frown. Walt was looking at a point three feet above Theo's head. Aisha was holding a pen.

"Here's what I want you to understand," Theo said. "This thing — NovaSyn — is a tool. That's all it is. It's like the CNC machines. It's like the calipers. It's like the spec sheets. It is a thing we are putting on the floor because we think it might make some of your jobs easier. Some of it will work. Some of it won't. The way we figure out which is which is by you trying it."

He paused.

"I want you to try it every day this week. I don't care what you try it on. Try it on something that frustrates you. Try it on something boring. Try it on something hard. At the end of the day I want you to tell me — or tell Aisha, or tell whoever you're closest to — what worked and what didn't. That's the whole assignment. Try it. Tell me."

Hector raised his hand.

"What if it gives me a wrong answer?"

"Then it gave you a wrong answer. You catch it the same way you catch a wrong reading on a caliper. You don't trust the tool blindly. You verify. If you can't verify it, you don't use what it told you."

"Okay."

Walt did not raise his hand. Walt did not move.

"Anything I tell you to use it for, I will tell you directly," Theo said. "Anything else, it's optional this week. Nobody's getting graded on this. Nobody's getting docked if you don't touch it. But I'm going to ask you Friday what you tried. And I want you to have tried something."

He looked at his crew.

"Last thing. If at any point this week you think this tool is being used to replace any of you, you come to me directly. That's what Mark said when he was down here. I'm saying it again. This is not what this is. This is what we are doing instead of that."

He stepped down off the platform.

"Let's get to work."

The crew dispersed. Theo went back to his office, set his thermos on the desk, and exhaled once.

---

Aisha Bell sat down at her workstation at seven thirty-five with a cup of coffee and the engineering log she had been keeping since she had started at Bellfield three years ago — a Moleskine notebook, the third one, with a rubber band around it.

There was a quality memo she had been meaning to write for two days. A tolerance variance on a recent fastener batch — Lot 4471 — had come in at the upper edge of acceptable on the QC report, and she wanted to document the contributing factors before she forgot them. Connie down in customer service would need it for the customer's quality file. Aisha had been putting it off because writing memos was the part of her job she liked the least.

She opened NovaSyn.

The interface was simple. A text box. A button that said *Send*. A sidebar with what looked like a folder structure.

She typed: *I need to draft a quality memo about a tolerance variance on Lot 4471 of the M8x1.25 fastener line. Variance was within spec but at the upper boundary on three of the twelve sampled parts. Contributing factors I noted: coolant temperature ran two degrees high during the second hour of the run, the machine had not been recalibrated since the previous Wednesday's run, and the bar stock came from a new lot from the supplier that had a slightly different surface finish. Customer is in our aerospace fastener accounts. Format as a standard internal quality memo.*

She clicked Send.

NovaSyn took about eleven seconds. The output came back as a draft memo with a subject line, a summary paragraph, three numbered contributing factors, a recommended action, and a notation that the variance was within spec. The language was clean. The format matched the standard memo template she had been using since she started.

She read it twice.

She edited for fifteen minutes. She corrected one fastener size notation. She rewrote the recommended action in her own register because the NovaSyn version was a little too formal for what was really a recalibration note. She added a sentence about the new bar-stock supplier that referenced the lot number from the receiving paperwork.

She sent the memo at eight oh-five.

She looked at the clock.

*Eight minutes to draft. Fifteen to edit. Twenty-three minutes total.*

She had been dreading that memo since Thursday.

She wrote in her Moleskine: *NovaSyn first try, memo, ~25 min from start to send. Would have been 90+ min.*

She underlined it.

---

Hector Salazar was at the loading dock by seven thirty. He had two pallets of aerospace housings to load before nine and a maintenance memo to write about the forklift hydraulics that he had been avoiding for a week.

The hydraulics on Forklift 2 had been bleeding slowly since the previous Tuesday. Not dangerous yet. Not even out of spec. But bleeding, and he could tell, because the lift response was a fraction slower than it should have been, and Hector had spent fifteen years on forklifts and knew the way they were supposed to feel. He needed to put it in writing for maintenance. He had been putting it off because he didn't like writing memos. The form was three pages long and asked for things he could not always articulate.

At ten thirty he came back into the dock office and sat down at the workstation. The NovaSyn icon was on the screen.

He looked at it for a minute.

Aisha had walked by twice that morning. He had seen her at her workstation looking pleased about something.

He opened NovaSyn.

He typed slowly. He was not a fast typist. He typed: *I need to write a maintenance memo about Forklift 2. Hydraulics seem to be bleeding. Lift response is slow. Not dangerous yet but should be looked at.*

He clicked Send.

NovaSyn came back not with a draft but with three questions: *(1) Can you estimate how long the lift response is delayed, in seconds or fractions of a second? (2) Have you noticed any visible hydraulic fluid loss in the area where you operate the forklift? (3) When was the last scheduled maintenance on Forklift 2's hydraulic system?*

Hector stared at the screen.

The questions were the right questions. They were the questions he would have had to answer on the three-page form anyway. He had been avoiding the form partly because he didn't know how to phrase the answers in the form's language.

He spoke the answers out loud, half to himself, as he typed them. *Lift response is maybe half a second slower than normal on a full lift. No visible fluid on the floor or on the housing. Last scheduled maintenance was — I think it was in August. I'd have to check the log.*

He clicked Send.

NovaSyn drafted a maintenance memo in the standard Bellfield format. It included his estimates as he had given them. It flagged the August maintenance date as a follow-up to verify. It recommended a hydraulic-system inspection within the next five working days.

Hector edited for accuracy. He pulled the maintenance log book down off the shelf and checked the date — August fourteenth. He corrected NovaSyn's placeholder. He read the memo a third time. He sent it to maintenance and copied Theo.

He sat back.

*Huh,* he thought.

He went back out to the dock.

---

Aisha used NovaSyn twice more that day.

At two fifteen Connie Sutter from customer service forwarded her a customer email thread that had grown to forty-seven messages between three people across two companies over the previous six weeks. Connie's note said: *Aisha — there are engineering questions buried in here. I cannot find them. Can you take a look.*

Aisha had looked at the thread in the morning and given up after the first ten messages. The customer's project manager had a habit of replying-all to everything and forwarding adjacent threads inline.

She copied the entire thread into NovaSyn and asked: *Extract every question in this thread that requires an engineering answer. Ignore the project-management and scheduling discussion. Group the engineering questions by topic.*

NovaSyn took about thirty seconds.

It came back with three questions. The first was about thread-tolerance requirements for a non-standard fastener in a high-vibration environment. The second was about coating compatibility on a stainless-steel substrate. The third was about whether Bellfield could accommodate a tighter parallelism callout than the original spec on a particular subassembly.

Three questions. Buried in forty-seven emails.

Aisha had the answers by two thirty-five. She wrote them up and sent the response to Connie and the customer's lead engineer. The response was four paragraphs.

She wrote in her Moleskine: *47-email thread, 3 actual questions, answered in 20 minutes.*

---

At three forty-five she opened NovaSyn for the third time.

She had a thread-tolerance question she had been meaning to look up since the previous Friday. She knew she had a note about it somewhere in her engineering log. She did not remember which Moleskine.

That morning she had loaded all three of her Moleskines — she had digitized them last year, during the slow week between Christmas and New Year's — into a NovaSyn context project labeled *Aisha eng notes 2022-present*. The context project was a folder on the side panel. NovaSyn could read everything in it.

She typed: *In my engineering notes, find the entry about thread tolerance on titanium-alloy fasteners in cryogenic service. I think it was from a job in the spring.*

NovaSyn took about four seconds.

It came back with the entry. June 2024. A note about a customer job in the aerospace cryogenic line where the standard MIL-STD tolerance had been tightened by request and Aisha had documented the parameters they had used and the QC outcomes.

She read it.

She thought: *I just did a thing in thirty seconds that would have taken me an hour and a half.*

She wrote in the Moleskine: *Context project / engineering notes lookup. 30 seconds. Would have been 90 minutes of digging.*

She underlined it twice.

---

Walt Bayer did not open NovaSyn.

He was at his bench by seven o'clock the way he always was. He ran his shift the way he always ran it. He ran the morning batch of pinion components for the medical-device order. He ran the calibration at ten. He took his break at ten thirty in the corner of the shop where the coffee machine was. He ate his lunch in his truck. He came back at one and ran the afternoon batch.

At three twenty he looked over at Aisha's workstation. Aisha was at her screen with her notebook open beside her. The NovaSyn window was on her screen. He could see the blue waveform icon in the corner.

He watched her for maybe ten seconds.

He went back to his bench.

He shut down at four thirty-five, which was five minutes later than he was supposed to. He wiped down his bench. He put on his jacket. He walked out to the parking lot. He got in his truck. He drove home.

He had not opened NovaSyn.

He had not said anything about it all day.

---

Theo and Dana sat in Theo's office at four thirty.

Dana had a paper cup of coffee from the break room. Theo had his thermos. The shop floor outside the office was quiet — the second shift was a skeleton crew on Mondays and they were already at their benches.

"Day one is fine," Theo said.

"Tell me."

"Aisha used it three times. She's already onto something with a context-project idea — she loaded her engineering notes into it and she's using it as a reference index. Hector used it once for a maintenance memo and was surprised it worked. I got a copy of the memo. It's a good memo."

"And Walt?"

"Walt didn't touch it."

Dana nodded slowly.

"How do you feel about that?"

"Day one. I'm not worried about day one. The training course is good but it's not specific to manufacturing. Aisha asked if we could load some of our spec docs as references in her context project. I think we should let her try."

"Yes," Dana said. "Today. Tonight if she wants."

"Okay."

"What else?"

Theo thought.

"Hector wrote a maintenance memo in twenty minutes that he had been putting off for a week. Aisha wrote a quality memo in twenty-three minutes that would have taken her ninety. She summarized a forty-seven-email customer thread in thirty seconds. She found a buried note in her engineering log in thirty seconds. That's day one. With the version of the tool we have today, with no manufacturing-specific training material, with no internal documentation loaded yet."

Dana was writing.

"And Walt didn't use it."

"Walt didn't use it."

"Give it the week," Dana said.

"That's what I'm doing."

She closed her notebook.

"Theo," she said. "Good kickoff this morning. I heard the audio from the safety camera."

"You listened to the kickoff?"

"I listened to the kickoff."

"That feels invasive."

"I will not do it again. But it was good."

He half-smiled.

She stood up.

"I'm going to go tell Mark," she said. "Then I'm going home. Tell Aisha she can load whatever she wants in her context project. Anything she wants. If she has questions about IP or customer confidentiality, send her to me directly. We'll work it out."

"Will do."

Dana left.

---

Theo stayed in his office for another ten minutes finishing his coffee. Then he walked the shop floor one more time.

The lights were on low. The CNC machines were silent. The benches were clean. He walked past Walt's bench. The workstation monitor had gone to sleep, but he knew the NovaSyn icon was on it.

He walked past Aisha's workstation. Her Moleskine was still on the desk, closed, with the rubber band around it.

He walked past the loading dock. Hector's forklift maintenance memo was printed and lying in the maintenance inbox.

He thought about his father.

His father had run a machine shop in Toledo for thirty-five years. His father had refused to put computers on the shop floor for fifteen of those years. *I don't need a computer to make a part,* he had told Theo, in 1998, when Theo had brought up the idea of CNC programming software. *I need hands and steel.* In 2003 his father had finally put one computer in the office. By 2005 there were three. By 2008 the shop was running CAD-driven CNC across all six of its lathes. Within eighteen months of the first computer being installed his father's shop had tripled its volume on the same crew, won two new contracts, and turned down a third because there was not enough floor space.

His father had never said *I was wrong about computers.* His father had said, once, in 2010, sitting in his pickup outside the shop with Theo at the end of a Friday: *I should've started this in 1990.*

Theo locked the front door of the shop at five fifteen.

He stood in the parking lot for a moment.

The October sky was the color of unfinished steel.

He got in his truck and drove home.

He thought: *day five is still the question. But day one is fine.*
