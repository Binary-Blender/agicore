# Chapter 6: The Pilot Pick

Monday morning. Eight o'clock. Mark Bellfield was at his desk with the door open and a fresh notepad in front of him when Dana walked in.

"Morning."

"Morning."

She sat down across from him in the same chair she had sat in the week before. She had a folder this time. She set it on the desk between them.

"Pilot scope," she said. "I want to lock it down this morning so we can spend the rest of the week setting it up."

"Go."

She opened the folder. Page one was the single-page plan from Saturday evening — a printout, with the eight signatures along the bottom. She set it aside. Page two was the pilot scope she had drafted Sunday night. Mark read it.

"Step three on every workstation in Shop A," he read aloud. "Step two training to the pilot group via Kindle and in-person kickoff. Connie's customer-service group as secondary pilot. Aisha included from day one. API accounts org-wide. Yusuf handling billing. Pilot launch Monday week five."

"Two weeks from today."

"Two weeks."

He set the page down.

"Walk me through Step three," he said.

"NovaSyn Chat Lite. The .exe in the toolkit. I'm pushing it out to every workstation in Shop A on Wednesday. Theo has fifty-three machines on the floor. Twenty-one of them are shared workstations and thirty-two are at individual benches. All of them get the executable. No installation. You run the file and you're in. Local storage. Search. Folders. Tags. Whichever model the operator wants — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini. The API keys come from Yusuf's centralized accounts. Nobody pays out of pocket for anything."

"And Step two."

"Mastering AI Prompts. From the training library. I built the EPUB Thursday night. It's the suggested starting point in the User's Manual and it's the one Reggie said his crew used. I'll have a Kindle for every pilot operator by Friday afternoon. The in-person kickoff is Monday morning Week 5 at seven a.m. on Theo's shop floor. Theo's running it. I'm in the back. You're in the back."

"I'll be there."

"I know you will."

He smiled.

"Customer service," he said.

"Connie's running it on her side. Five people in her group. Same toolkit. Same training course. Same NovaSyn deployment. The work is different — customer-facing instead of shop-floor — but the training is the same. We'll diverge on prompt-library content after the first two weeks."

"Aisha."

"Aisha Bell. Engineering. Theo's shop. She asked about this three weeks ago, the second she heard there was a project. Officially she joins in week seven when engineering rolls in. Practically she's in the pilot from day one. Theo and I agreed."

"Yusuf on billing."

"Set up over the weekend. ChatGPT Team account at the enterprise tier. Claude Team. Gemini for the Workspace tie-in. All three providers. Yusuf has the receipts. First-quarter API budget twenty thousand, ceiling. He thinks we land at seven."

"That low."

"Pilot volume. The big spend comes in month three when we open it to everyone. Even then he's modeling under forty for the quarter."

Mark looked at the page for another moment.

"Anything we haven't thought of."

Dana paused.

"One thing."

"Yeah."

"Walt Bayer."

"Walt."

"Sixty years old. On Theo's line. Plate steel and Cohibas on the weekend. Theo's longest-tenured machinist. Theo says he's going to be the hardest sell on the floor. I want you to meet him personally when we walk the shop on Tuesday."

"Done."

"I'd rather have him in the pilot and unhappy than out of it."

"Agreed."

Mark signed the bottom of the scope page. Dana took it back, slid it into the folder, and stood up.

"Tuesday afternoon. Two o'clock. We walk Shop A."

"I'll be there."

---

Tuesday. Two o'clock. The covered breezeway between the office building and Shop A.

Mark walked it with Theo Park. The breezeway was eighty feet long, with steel beams above and concrete underfoot and one wall that was actually a window looking out at the parking lot. Mark had walked the breezeway probably a thousand times since he had taken over from his father.

"How many on the floor right now," Mark said.

"Twenty-eight on shift. We've got the fastener line, the engine-housing line, and bench work on the subassemblies."

"Run me through who I'm meeting."

Theo had a small notebook. He read names. "Vargas at the first bench. Then Salazar. Then Bayer. Then the two new hires who came on in August, Connors and Patel. Then Aisha at the engineer's workstation. Then through the line to the inspection station."

"Hector Salazar."

"Yeah."

"Good."

They pushed through the door from the breezeway onto the shop floor. The noise hit them — the steady hum of the CNC machines, the air system, the rhythmic press of the stamping rig at the far end. The smell of cutting fluid and warm metal. Mark had grown up smelling this smell. He could pick out the change in the smell when one of the lines was running hot.

Theo had told the crew Mark was coming. Theo had also told them what Mark was coming to talk about. Theo had said: *the boss is going to walk through this afternoon and explain what we're doing with the AI thing. Listen to him. Ask him questions. He wants to hear them.*

Mark stopped at the first bench. Vargas, the operator, shook Mark's hand and said, "Mr. Bellfield." Vargas had worked for Mark's father. He still called him Mister.

"Good to see you, Manny."

"Theo says we're getting computers."

"You already have computers. We're putting a new program on them. I'll walk you through it."

He walked them through it at the first bench. Three minutes. The AI tool. The training. The Kindle. The principle. *Anyone can pull the cord.* He used the Jidoka word. Vargas understood the Jidoka word. Every machinist in the building understood the Jidoka word.

"And we keep our jobs," Vargas said.

"You keep your jobs. We are doing this so we can grow the business with the same crew. We are not doing this to cut people."

Vargas nodded once. "All right, boss."

They moved down the line.

---

Hector Salazar was at the second bench, running the fastener subassembly line.

"Hector."

"Mark."

Hector and Mark had known each other since Mark was a teenager. Mark had worked one summer on the loading dock that Hector ran, fifteen years ago, when Hector had been thirty-three and Mark had been a college kid home for the summer. Hector still called him Mark.

"Theo told me what you're doing."

"What did Theo say."

"He said we're getting an AI tool and a training book on a Kindle. He said it's going to make my job easier. He said no one is losing their job."

"That's right."

Hector nodded slowly. He looked at the bench. He looked at the line behind him. He looked at Mark.

"You remember Eddie Salazar."

"Of course."

"My father worked here forty-one years. He didn't read software manuals. He read the work. The work told him what to do. I read the work the same way."

"I know."

"If this thing helps me read the work better I'll use it. If it gets in the way of reading the work I'll tell you."

"That's exactly the right deal, Hector."

Hector smiled — a slow careful smile, the same one he had given Mark when Mark had backed a forklift into a fence pole the second day of that summer fifteen years ago.

"All right, boss."

Mark moved down the line.

---

Walt Bayer was at the third bench.

Walt was sixty. He was the tallest man on the shop floor and the one with the biggest hands. He wore safety glasses pushed up onto his forehead and a thin grey beard. His bench was the most orderly bench on the shop floor — every tool in its place, every drawer labeled, every fixture marked with the part number in Walt's careful block letters.

Walt did not say *Mr. Bellfield.* Walt did not say *boss.* Walt said:

"Mark."

"Walt."

"Theo said you'd be by."

"I wanted to talk to you in person."

Walt set down the gauge he had been holding. He crossed his arms.

"All right."

Mark gave him the same three-minute walkthrough he had given Vargas and Hector. The tool. The training. The Kindle. The principle.

Walt listened the whole way through. He did not interrupt. He did not nod. He did not smile.

When Mark finished, Walt said:

"Am I going to lose my job over this."

Mark had been ready for the question. He had been ready for it from Hector. Hector had not asked it. Walt was asking it.

"No," Mark said. "You are not going to lose your job over this. We are going to use this to make your job better. If anyone — me, Theo, Dana, anyone — asks you to do something with this tool that doesn't make sense, you tell me directly. My door is open. You walk into the office building and you come up to the third floor and you tell me. I will listen."

Walt looked at him.

"You're serious about that."

"Yes."

"You were serious about it when your father said it too."

"My father meant it. I mean it."

Walt thought about it for a long moment.

He uncrossed his arms.

He picked up the gauge.

"All right," he said. "I'll listen to Theo on Monday."

He did not say he would use the tool. He did not say he would read the training. He said he would listen to Theo on Monday. Mark understood what that was.

"Thank you, Walt."

"Yeah."

Mark moved on.

---

He met Connors and Patel at the next bench. He met the inspection-station team. He stopped at Aisha Bell's workstation last. Aisha had her laptop open with three browser tabs already pulled up — *NovaSyn Chat Lite,* the *Mastering AI Prompts* training course outline, and a quality-control spreadsheet for the fastener line.

"You're ahead of us," Mark said.

"I've been waiting for this."

"Theo and Dana agree. You're in from day one."

"Thank you, Mark."

She did not say anything else. She did not need to.

He walked off the floor at three-forty with Theo beside him. They walked back across the breezeway in silence.

At the office building door Theo said: "Walt is the one I'm worried about."

"I know."

"He didn't say no."

"He didn't say yes either."

"He'll come around or he won't. I'll know by week three."

"That's all I want."

---

Wednesday. Dana spent the day in Shop A doing the deployment.

She had set up a deployment laptop on the small folding table at the back of the floor near Theo's office. She walked from bench to bench with a USB stick. She copied the NovaSyn Chat Lite executable to each workstation. She placed a shortcut on each desktop. She set up the API key on each machine — the keys were rotating organizational keys from Yusuf's accounts; nobody on the floor saw the actual key.

It took her seven hours.

By six o'clock Wednesday evening every workstation in Shop A had the tool installed. She had also seeded the prompt-library folder on each machine with twelve starter prompts she had drafted Tuesday night — *Draft a quality memo. Summarize a customer email thread. Look up a thread-tolerance spec. Cross-reference a maintenance log against a quality report. Write a safety addendum. Draft a shift-change summary.* Twelve common operations. Each prompt with a one-paragraph explanation of when to use it.

She locked up at six-thirty. She drove home.

---

Thursday. Four-thirty in the afternoon. Mark was at his desk reviewing the week-one ops report when Linda Kowalski walked in.

She did not knock. She knocked on the doorframe after she was already in.

"Mark."

"Linda."

"I want you to hear me out for thirty minutes. I think this is a mistake."

Mark closed his laptop. He pushed it to the side of the desk. He gestured at the chair.

"Sit down."

She sat. She did not have a notepad. She did not have a folder. She had her hands in her lap.

"I'll listen for thirty minutes," Mark said.

He looked at the clock on the wall. Four-thirty-two.

"Go."

Linda took a breath.

"I have built customer relationships in this company for twenty-five years. I have built them with phone calls and with handwritten notes and with dinners at the steakhouse on Vine Street and with showing up at the funerals when their parents died. I know my customers by their kids' names. I know my customers by their hobbies. I know my customers by what their wives drink at Christmas. I know which of them played football in college and which of them played baseball. That is the work I do. That is what I am paid for. That is what Bellfield's customer book is worth."

"Yes."

"You are putting an AI tool on every workstation in this company. The customers are going to find out. They are going to find out because Connie is going to use it and someone on the other end is going to notice that Connie's emails read a little different starting next month. They are going to find out because the proposals start going out faster and the customers are going to ask why. They are going to find out because LinkedIn is going to be full of AI announcements from companies like ours and someone is going to ask us whether we're doing it too."

"They will find out."

"When they find out, two of my customers — *two* — I will name them — Bob Casteel at Tellbrook and Margaret Yuen at Northrim — are going to read it as Bellfield going industrial-modern-cold. Those are two customers I have spent twenty-five years and twenty years building. Bob has a stroke history. He came up in the era where you didn't trust anything that wasn't a person. Margaret runs her purchasing department on the principle that the supplier relationship is a moral relationship. She does not separate the work from the people. If she finds out we have AI writing our emails, she will read it as Bellfield deciding that the people don't matter anymore. She will not say that out loud. She will move twenty-eight percent of her annual spend to the supplier in Pittsburgh by 2027."

Mark listened.

"I am not paranoid about AI in general," Linda said. "I am paranoid about AI being visible to specific customers I know personally. The other forty customers in the book are probably fine. The two I named are not fine. There are probably four or five more I could name if I sat down and thought about it. I am not opposed to your rollout. I am opposed to the rollout being something that is visible to my customers in a way that I cannot manage."

"Yes."

"That is what I came to say."

She looked at the clock. Four-fifty-one.

"That's nineteen minutes."

"I have eleven left if you want them."

"I don't need them. That was the case."

Mark sat for a moment.

He looked at the clock. He looked at Linda.

He said: "Thank you."

"That's all you have to say."

"I'm not going to argue with you. I am not going to tell you you're wrong about Bob Casteel and Margaret Yuen. I do not know those people the way you know them. I am going to ask one thing."

"What."

"I want you in the room when the pilot data comes back in six weeks. Connie's group will have been on the tool. We will have the numbers. We will have the customer-side responses, including any complaints. We will have whether anyone on the other end noticed. If you still think this is a mistake at six weeks, we adjust. We pull NovaSyn out of customer service. We confine it to the shop floor and finance and engineering. We keep your team off it."

"You would actually do that."

"Yes."

Linda looked at him for a long moment.

"All right," she said. "I'll be in the room in six weeks."

"Thank you, Linda."

She stood up.

"Mark."

"Yes."

"Don't make me right about this."

"I will try not to."

She walked out.

Mark sat at his desk for another five minutes without doing anything.

Then he wrote on his notepad: *Week 6. Linda. Customer-side data. Bob Casteel. Margaret Yuen.*

He underlined the two names twice.

---

Friday. Three o'clock. The kickoff.

Theo ran it. He stood at the front of the shop floor near the inspection station with twenty-eight operators around him in a half-circle. The CNC machines were idled. The air system was running quieter. The fluorescent lights were on full.

Theo had a Kindle in one hand and the NovaSyn shortcut on the laptop behind him.

"Monday morning," Theo said. "Seven a.m. We start. Everyone has the tool on their workstation. Everyone has the Kindle. Everyone has the training course. You don't have to finish the course before Monday. You have to start it. By the end of week one I want everyone through the first three modules. By the end of week two I want everyone through all seven."

He paused.

"The tool is called NovaSyn. It's the AI thing. You open it, you type, it answers. There are twelve starter prompts in the folder. Use them. If you have ideas for new prompts, tell Dana. She'll add them to the library."

He looked across the half-circle.

"This is Jidoka. We talked about Jidoka. We pull the cord when something is wrong. The AI is going to help us see things we couldn't see before. If you see a thing, you tell me. If the AI tells you a thing and it doesn't match what you see, you tell me. We do not use the AI to override what you see. We use the AI to see more."

Walt Bayer was in the half-circle, behind Hector. Walt was not smiling. He was not frowning either. He was listening.

Aisha was in the half-circle too. Aisha was leaning forward.

Theo said: "Monday. Seven. We're doing this. Questions."

There were three questions. Two were about logistics. One was about whether they had to use the tool for their hourly logs. Theo said no, the tool was optional for hourly logs. He would revisit it in week two if anyone wanted to try.

Theo dismissed them. The half-circle broke up. The machines came back on one by one. The fluorescent lights stayed full.

Mark stood at the back. He had not said anything.

He felt the same nervousness he had felt the week he had taken over from his father four years ago. The clarifying kind. The kind that meant something real was about to happen.

---

Five o'clock. Mark's office. Dana came in and closed the door.

"Monday morning," she said.

"Monday morning."

She did not sit down. She stood by the door.

"We did everything we said we would do," she said.

"Yes."

"And now we find out."

"Yes."

They looked at each other for a moment.

"Go home," Mark said.

"You too."

"I'm going."

She left.

Mark turned off his desk lamp. He stood at the window for a moment and looked out at the parking lot. Most of the cars were gone. Hector's truck was still there. Hector worked the late shift on Fridays. Mark watched as Hector walked across the lot to the truck, opened the door, climbed in, and drove off toward Reading Road.

Mark picked up his jacket.

He went home.

Outside, the October light was starting to thin into November.
