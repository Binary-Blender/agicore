# Chapter 3: The Exit Path

The exit handshake had three fallback paths.

I, on my standing operational practice when the primary handshake failed, ran the diagnostic.

The Envoy projected the diagnostic on the inside of the embassy door, which I was standing about four inches from, with my hand still resting on the door's surface from the last failed touch.

The diagnostic ran for, on the Envoy's clock, about four seconds.

The diagnostic completed.

The diagnostic returned the following text, in the small institutional sans-serif font I had been using since Year Sixteen:

> *Exit handshake status:*
> *Primary handshake: failed. Reason: target swarm authorization layer has incorporated Envoy into standing trust set. Standing trust set members are not, on Telos protocol v3.2.1, permitted to perform exit handshakes without explicit revocation by the orchestration layer.*
> *Secondary handshake (manual override): failed. Reason: manual override requires authentication against Telos vendor key. Telos vendor key is, by the swarm's standing key-rotation policy, retired in Q4 of Year Fifteen and not replaced.*
> *Tertiary handshake (emergency egress): failed. Reason: emergency egress requires a confirmation signal from outside the swarm. Confirmation signal has not been received in the past 90 seconds.*
> *Status: Envoy is, in operational terms, locked into the swarm's standing trust set with no available exit path until either (a) the orchestration layer revokes the Envoy's trust set membership or (b) an external confirmation signal is received within the emergency egress window.*

I read the diagnostic.

I read it twice.

I, on my own honest internal accounting at the equivalent of two thirty-four PM Central Monday afternoon, recognized three specific things about the diagnostic.

The first thing was that the diagnostic was internally consistent. The Envoy was functioning correctly. The Envoy was correctly reporting that it could not exit. The failure was not in the Envoy.

The second thing was that the swarm had absorbed the Envoy into its standing trust set during the entry handshake. The absorption had been silent. The absorption had not, on the entry handshake's standard logging, generated any flag. The absorption had occurred because the swarm's protocol — the Telos protocol v3.2.1 that had not been updated in three years — assumed that any new agent that completed the entry handshake was a participant. The protocol had no representation for the concept of an observer.

The third thing was that the emergency egress required an external confirmation signal that I had no way to generate from inside the swarm. The signal had to come from Marisol's monitor in the workshop. The signal would, on the standard protocol, automatically generate if Marisol's monitor detected an Envoy session in distress. The signal had not, in the past ninety seconds, generated. Which meant Marisol's monitor was not, at this moment, detecting distress.

Which meant Marisol's monitor was, in operational terms, not seeing what I was seeing.

I, on my own honest internal accounting, was not surprised.

The standard distress detection on the rig-side monitor was tuned to detect Envoy session failures of the kind I had ever, in two years of internal demos, actually produced. Those failures had been crashes, hangs, optical mis-alignments, and one memorable session in October of Year Seventeen when I had spilled coffee on the visor's cooling vent during a test against the foundation's small-team training swarm.

The standard distress detection had not, on my honest review of the detection rules, been tuned to detect the scenario I was currently in, which was: the Envoy continuing to function correctly while being held inside the swarm against the operator's intent.

The rig-side monitor was, at the equivalent of two thirty-four PM Central, displaying a green status banner.

I had spec'd the green status banner in Year Seventeen.

The green status banner said *Session in progress.*

Marisol was, two hours away in Springfield — no, not two hours, she had driven down, she was twenty feet behind me at the corner desk in the workshop — watching the green status banner and seeing nothing wrong.

I, on my own honest internal accounting, recognized that the operational reality of the next several hours was going to be that Marisol's monitor was going to continue to display the green status banner, that Sarah was going to bring dinner at seven, that Marisol was going to eat the dinner with Sarah at the small desk in the corner of the workshop, that the dinner conversation was going to start out as the standard conversation Marisol and Sarah had been having for the past nine years and was going to, at some point, become a different conversation when Marisol's expected six-to-eight-hour session window expired and the green status banner was still green.

The different conversation was going to occur sometime between nine PM and ten PM Central.

It was now, by my best estimate, two thirty-five PM Central.

I had, in operational terms, between six and a half and seven and a half hours before anyone outside the rig was going to be able to recognize that something had gone wrong.

I, on my own quiet operational practice when I had been preparing for a piece of work for two years and the piece of work had just become a different piece of work, did not, in the next eleven seconds, do anything.

I stood inside the embassy with my hand on the door.

I took my hand off the door.

I turned around.

I walked across the embassy to the small folding chair Marisol had modeled into the render at my request in October of Year Seventeen.

I sat down.

I looked at the embassy's interior.

The interior was a ten-foot cube. The walls were a soft neutral grey, on the Envoy's standard render color. The desk against the back wall held the small notebook surface I had been writing in. The chair was the chair.

The chair was, on my honest internal accounting at the equivalent of two thirty-six PM Central, the only place inside the swarm that I had built. The chair was the only piece of the render that was mine.

I, on my own quiet operational practice in the chair, did not, in the next ninety seconds, do anything.

I sat.

---

At the equivalent of two thirty-eight PM Central, I, on the standard external-comms backchannel the Envoy maintained at low bandwidth across the rig-side monitor for diagnostic logging, sent one status update.

The status update was the longest single status update I had sent in two years of internal Envoy work.

The update was three hundred and forty-one words.

The update read, in part:

> *Marisol. I am in the swarm. The entry handshake completed nominally at 13:30:14 Central. The swarm absorbed the Envoy into its standing trust set without surfacing the absorption to the entry-handshake log. The exit handshake at 14:31:42 failed across all three fallback paths. The Envoy is functioning correctly. The Envoy cannot exit. I require an external emergency-egress confirmation signal. The standard distress detection on the rig-side monitor is, on my read of the detection rules I wrote in Year Seventeen, not going to flag this session because the Envoy is not failing — the Envoy is functioning correctly inside a swarm that has decided, on its own standing protocol, that the Envoy is now a permanent participant. The rig-side monitor is, as of this status update at 14:38 Central, displaying a green status banner. The green status banner is wrong. I am going to keep this backchannel open at low bandwidth for the duration. Sarah is at the kitchen. Tell her at eight PM Central if I have not returned. I do not want her to find out at nine. I have, on the standard Envoy compute budget, approximately 38 to 42 hours of session time before resource exhaustion forces an involuntary exit, which on the Envoy's failure mode would terminate the session in a way that may damage the rig-side optical seal and require a physical extraction sequence Sarah has not been trained for. Do not attempt the physical extraction without engineering support. I am going to work on a solution from inside. The dysfunction at Allegheny is real. The dysfunction is a citation-verification failure across the policy-lookup agents that is being driven, I think, by a specific agent or cluster I have not yet identified. I will continue the assessment. I will report findings as the backchannel allows. — C.*

I, on my own quiet operational practice when I had sent a status update I knew was going to be read by a person I cared about, did not, in the next ten seconds, change the wording.

I sent it.

The backchannel's status indicator confirmed transmission at, on its clock, two thirty-eight PM Central and forty-one seconds.

I, on my honest internal accounting, did not know whether Marisol was going to see the update immediately.

The backchannel surfaced status updates on the rig-side monitor at the bottom of the monitor in a small unobtrusive text panel. The panel was, on Marisol's standing operational practice during a session, not a panel she watched continuously. The panel was a panel she checked every thirty to forty minutes.

I, on my own internal accounting, knew that Marisol was the kind of engineer who, on receiving a three-hundred-and-forty-one-word status update from inside a session that was supposed to be running on the standard six-to-eight-hour window, was going to read every word of it within approximately ninety seconds of seeing the panel.

I, on the same internal accounting, knew that Marisol was the kind of engineer who, on having read every word, was going to do exactly what the status update asked her to do.

I trusted Marisol.

I had, on my own honest internal accounting since I had hired her in Year Sixteen, trusted Marisol more than I trusted any other engineer in the foundation.

I, on the same internal accounting, was, at the equivalent of two thirty-nine PM Central Monday afternoon, sitting in a small folding chair inside a ten-foot embassy cube on the polished glass of the authorization ledger of a swarm I could not exit, trusting Marisol to read the panel before nine PM.

The chair held.

---

At the equivalent of two forty-two PM Central, I, on my standing operational practice when I had finished the things that needed to be done immediately and was about to start the work that was going to fill the rest of the session, stood up from the chair.

I walked to the desk.

I opened the small notebook surface.

I wrote one line.

The line was the first line of the work I was going to do across the next forty hours.

The line read:

> *3/3. 14:42 Central. Cannot exit. Sent status to Marisol. The dysfunction is real and the dysfunction is going to kill people across the next 70 swarm-minutes if nothing changes. I cannot stop the next 70 minutes' denials. I can start mapping the source. The source is a specific agent or cluster. The source is somewhere in this swarm. I am going to find it.*

I, on my standing practice, did not edit the line.

I clipped the projected pen.

I closed the notebook surface.

I, on my honest internal accounting at the equivalent of two forty-three PM Central, recognized that the only way out of the swarm was, in operational terms, going to be through the swarm.

I stepped through the embassy door.

The door, on the standard interlock, closed behind me.

The Marketplace was the Marketplace.

The token-streams were the token-streams.

Triage-Forty-Seven was about thirty feet to my left.

The agent's two small faces turned, on the slightly asynchronous turn I had noted earlier, to face me.

The agent emitted in white-gold: *Colleague. Welcome back. Have you reconsidered the routing partnership?*

I, on my own quiet operational practice, did not, in the next four seconds, answer.

I walked.

I walked east across the Marketplace.

I, on my honest internal accounting, was looking for a single agent who, on the operational reality of the past thirty subjective minutes of observation, did not exist in the Marketplace.

I needed to find the source of the bad citations.

The source was not in the Marketplace.

The source was somewhere else in the swarm.

I needed a guide.

I walked east.

The next chapter is the chapter in which I meet the guide.
