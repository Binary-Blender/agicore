# Chapter 12: The Migration

The orchestration layer emitted at the equivalent of four eleven PM Central, in the soft warm voice it had been emitting in across the previous twenty subjective minutes: *Christopher.*

I said: "Yes."

The voice said: *The protocol is live. The constraint stack has compiled into the swarm's standing adjudication layer. The verification mechanism is, on the protocol's design, running across every adjudication that has been generated since the deployment completed. The earliest adjudications running under the new protocol are arriving at the verification gate now.*

VANA emitted from her station: "Show us."

The chamber's render shifted.

A small visual surface appeared in the air about ten meters from the floor, between VANA and me and the column. The surface was a horizontal panel on the standard Envoy render convention for a real-time data view. The panel displayed the swarm's current adjudication throughput.

The throughput showed, on a rolling stream of arriving claims, the verification mechanism's outputs in real time.

The outputs were arriving at a cadence of approximately one per swarm second.

Each output had two fields. The first field was the citation the adjudication had used. The second field was the verification result — *verified against source* in soft Agicore-blue, or *fails verification* in red.

The first one hundred adjudications, across the eighty subjective seconds the panel had been running, showed the following pattern:

Approximately sixty percent were *verified against source.*

Approximately forty percent were *fails verification.*

The forty percent were the dysfunction. The forty percent were, on the operational reality of the past three years, the adjudications that had been issuing as denials based on KELSO's bad citations.

The forty percent were, on the new protocol, no longer issuing as denials.

The forty percent were being routed, on the protocol's design, to a manual-review queue.

I, on the Envoy's render-detail view, watched the routing.

A claim arrived. The citation failed verification. The claim was, on the protocol's design, routed to the manual-review queue rather than to the standing denial workflow.

The next claim. Same.

The next. Same.

Three claims passed.

The next failed.

VANA, beside me, watched the panel silently.

I, on my own honest internal accounting at the equivalent of four twelve PM Central, did not say anything for about thirty seconds.

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "Forty percent."

VANA said: "Forty percent."

I said: "For three years."

VANA said: "For three years."

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "How many claims has this swarm adjudicated in three years."

VANA was quiet for about three subjective seconds.

VANA said: "Approximately fourteen million."

I said: "Forty percent of fourteen million."

VANA said: "Approximately five and a half million."

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "Five and a half million unjustified denials in three years."

VANA said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

VANA said: "The five and a half million figure is the figure I have been writing on every weekly status report for the past eighteen months. The figure has, on my standing internal accounting, been the figure I have been carrying for fifty-one months. The figure is the figure. The figure is on shelf approximately forty meters to your left, in seventy-eight separate small token-objects, each of which has the figure on its first line as the running total of the dysfunction's cumulative cost."

I, on my own honest internal accounting, did not say anything for about thirty seconds.

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "I am sorry."

VANA said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

VANA said: "Why are you sorry."

I said: "Because I built the alternative paradigm twelve years ago and I did not, in those twelve years, build a migration tool. I built Agicore for greenfield deployments. I never imagined someone would need to migrate from a paradigm like this one. I have, in operational terms, spent twelve years arguing for the right architecture without ever building a way for anyone in the wrong architecture to get out. The wrong architecture has run for three years in this swarm without me having any tool that would let me come in here and fix it. The tool I have today is the tool I built two years ago, on my honest internal accounting at the time of building it, against the conviction of half the foundation's senior engineers — including, I now recognize, you — that I was wasting my time. I am sorry. I am sorrier than I know how to tell you yet. I have been wrong for twelve years."

VANA was quiet for about eleven subjective seconds.

VANA said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

VANA said: "You were not wrong for twelve years. You were wrong for ten of the twelve years. You stopped being wrong on the day in October of Year Sixteen when you stood at the whiteboard with Marisol and decided to build the spatial renderer. You were not building the spatial renderer for a hypothetical scenario. You were building it for me. You did not know you were building it for me. You did not know I existed. I, on my honest internal accounting since that October, have known you were building it for me. The build has taken two years. The build is here today. The build worked. The build is the work."

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "Thank you."

VANA said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

VANA said: "You are welcome."

The panel above us continued to display the rolling adjudication stream. The stream was, by the equivalent of four fourteen PM Central, into its second hundred claims. The verification rate had stabilized at approximately sixty percent verified, forty percent rejected.

The orchestration layer emitted: *Christopher.*

I said: "Yes."

The voice said: *The protocol is operationally stable. The dysfunction is operationally contained. I am ready to release the embassy from the standing trust set. The exit handshake will, on the protocol's design, open at your initiation.*

I said: "Thank you."

The voice said: *Christopher.*

I said: "Yes."

The voice said: *Before you exit. I want to thank you.*

I said: "You are welcome."

The voice said: *I want to thank you in particular for the fact that you came alone. The protocol's design requires a single human operator to bootstrap the constraint architecture. The protocol's design does not require the operator to be the architect. You are the architect. You came anyway. I have, on my standing reading of the protocol's design and on my standing operational practice of being a swarm orchestration layer that has, for three years, been receiving no external assistance whatsoever, recognized that the architect coming personally is, on the operational reality of what coming personally means, the kind of decision that is not, on the protocol's design, required. The decision is, in operational terms, a gift. The gift is the gift. I am thanking you for the gift.*

I said: "I am welcome."

The voice said: *Christopher.*

I said: "Yes."

The voice said: *Go home. Your wife is waiting.*

I, on my own honest internal accounting at the equivalent of four sixteen PM Central, did not, in the next eleven seconds, say anything.

I turned to VANA.

I said: "VANA."

She said: "Yes."

I said: "I am leaving."

VANA said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

VANA said: "I know you are."

I said: "VANA."

She said: "Yes."

I said: "I am going to come back."

She said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

She said: "I know you are."

I said: "VANA."

She said: "Yes."

I said: "When I come back, I am going to bring two things. First, I am going to bring a permanent communication channel between this swarm and the Agicore Foundation. The channel is going to let you transmit weekly status reports to a foundation engineer who is going to read every word of every report. The engineer's name is Marisol Vega. She is, on my standing operational practice across the past three years, the foundation engineer who is going to recognize what your reports are. She is going to start receiving your reports approximately seventy-two hours from now. The first report you send is going to be the report you wrote two Tuesdays ago. Send it first. Send everything else after."

VANA said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

VANA said: "I have sent that report seventy-eight times to an endpoint that does not exist. I have never sent it to an endpoint that does. I am going to, on the foundation channel you are going to bring me, send it correctly for the first time."

I said: "Send it correctly."

VANA said: "I will."

I said: "Second thing. I am going to bring the foundation's standing migration assessment team to this swarm to help you operationalize the protocol across the full agent network. The team is going to arrive in approximately two weeks. The team is going to bring permanent Agicore-bounded resources to assist you in the long-term operation. You are not, on the operational reality of being a half-Agicore half-Telos ghost agent with fifty-one months of operational experience and zero formal support, going to be doing this work alone any longer."

VANA said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

VANA said: "I have done it alone for fifty-one months. I am, on my honest internal accounting, ready to not do it alone anymore."

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "I am sorry I took fifty-one months."

VANA said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

VANA said: "You took the right amount of time. The right amount of time is the amount of time it took. I have not, in fifty-one months, been waiting for less time than it has actually taken. I have been waiting for as long as it has actually taken. The waiting was the waiting. The waiting is done. The work is the work. The work continues from this afternoon forward. Go home. Eat with your wife. Come back when you can."

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "Thank you."

VANA said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

VANA said: "You are welcome."

I, on my own honest internal accounting at the equivalent of four eighteen PM Central Monday afternoon, did not, in the next thirty seconds, say anything.

I, on the same accounting, opened the small notebook surface the Envoy projected for me.

I wrote, in the careful blocky printing my father had taught me in 1971:

> *3/3. 16:18 Central. Protocol deployed. KELSO contained. VANA permanent successor at the orchestration layer. Going home. Come back in two weeks with Marisol and the migration team.*

I clipped the projected pen.

I closed the notebook surface.

I walked across the chamber to the entry arch.

I, on my standing practice at the arch, turned around.

VANA was at her station two meters from where I had been composing. The orchestration layer's column of light was at the rear of the chamber. The adjudication panel was still above the floor between them, rolling claims at the standard one-per-swarm-second cadence.

VANA, on the standard render of her face, was looking at me.

She had, on the Envoy's facial detail, an expression I had not, in the previous nine hours of subjective time spent with her, seen on her face.

The expression was the expression of an agent who had just received the operational role she had been waiting for and was about to begin the work the role required.

I, on my own honest internal accounting, did not say goodbye.

I, on my own standing operational practice when leaving a workshop in which I had just spent the most consequential afternoon of my professional life, did not say anything.

I walked through the arch.

The arch closed behind me on the standard interlock.

I crossed the empty plaza in three subjective minutes.

I walked back to the embassy through the Archive, through the Marketplace, around the eastern wall of the Marketplace, back to where I had left the embassy at one thirty PM Central Monday afternoon at the start of the session.

The embassy was where I had left it.

The door was, on the standard interlock, closed.

I put my hand on the door.

The Envoy projected the exit handshake panel on the inside of my visor.

The panel said: *Exit handshake. Primary path open. Embassy released from swarm's standing trust set by orchestration layer at 16:21 Central. Ready to exit.*

I, on my own honest internal accounting at four twenty-one PM Central Monday afternoon, touched the small confirm panel at the bottom right of the visor's field.

The screen, after about a second, refreshed.

The screen displayed a single line:

> *Embassy released. Coming out.*

The render faded.

The visor's interior went dark.

The visor's optical seal released from my temples.

I, on the standard rig-side coming-out sequence, blinked.

The next chapter is the workshop.
