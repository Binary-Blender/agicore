# Chapter 4: Sarah

Marisol Vega was at the corner desk in the workshop at two thirty-eight PM Central Monday afternoon when the status panel at the bottom of the rig-side monitor flickered from its standing *idle* state to the small green text it displayed when the Envoy sent a low-bandwidth status update through the backchannel.

The standard practice on the rig-side monitor across the previous two years had been that the panel flickered, on average, once every forty-eight minutes during a session. The flickers indicated routine status updates — heartbeats, telemetry, the small administrative metadata the Envoy reported back to the rig at the close of each subjective-hour segment.

The flicker at two thirty-eight PM was not, on Marisol's read at the standard quick glance she gave the panel when she noticed the flicker, a routine status update.

Routine updates produced a panel of approximately twelve to fourteen words.

The panel at two thirty-eight PM was filled.

Marisol, on her own standing operational practice when the panel was filled, set down the coffee thermos she had brought from Springfield and unscrolled the panel to its full width across the monitor.

She read the update.

She read it across approximately ninety seconds.

She, on her honest internal accounting at two thirty-nine PM and forty seconds, did not say anything out loud for about six seconds.

She read the update a second time.

She did not say anything out loud the second time either.

She, on her standing operational practice when she had just read a status update that was the kind of status update she had been preparing to receive for the past two years and that she had not, until two thirty-eight PM and forty-one seconds, believed she was ever actually going to receive, did one specific thing.

She stood up.

She, on her standing practice when walking from the workshop to the kitchen at the house, did not put on her coat. The walk was about thirty feet across the small yard. She did not need the coat.

She walked out of the workshop.

She walked across the yard.

She, on her honest internal accounting at two forty PM Central Monday afternoon, recognized that she was walking at her standard walking pace and not, on the operational reality of what she was about to tell Sarah, faster than her standard pace. The choice not to run was deliberate. She had made it inside the workshop in the four seconds between standing up and stepping out the door. She had made it because she knew Sarah would, on the operational reality of seeing Marisol Vega cross the yard at a run at two forty PM on a Monday afternoon, immediately recognize that something was wrong and would, on the additional operational reality of being Sarah, immediately panic.

Marisol did not want Sarah to panic in the yard.

She wanted Sarah to panic in the kitchen, with Marisol next to her, with the kitchen table between Sarah and the news.

She walked at her standard pace.

She reached the back door of the house at two forty PM and thirty-eight seconds.

She knocked twice.

Sarah opened the door.

Sarah, on her standing read of Marisol's face from two feet away, recognized that something was wrong before Marisol said anything.

Sarah said: "Marisol."

Marisol said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "Come in."

Marisol came in.

Sarah closed the door.

Sarah walked to the kitchen table.

She did not, on her standing operational practice in the kitchen at the house in Ava on a Monday afternoon when something had just gone wrong, ask Marisol what had happened.

She walked to the kitchen table.

She pulled out the chair on the east side of the table — the chair she sat at for breakfast every morning and that Christopher sat at for dinner every evening because the east side faced the window and the west side faced the cupboard.

She pulled out the chair on the west side of the table — Christopher's evening chair — for Marisol.

She sat.

Marisol sat in Christopher's chair.

Sarah said: "Marisol."

Marisol said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "Tell me."

Marisol told her.

She told her in approximately five minutes.

She told Sarah that Christopher had entered the swarm at one thirty PM, that the entry handshake had completed nominally, that Christopher had tried the exit handshake at two thirty-one PM, that the exit handshake had failed across all three fallback paths, that Christopher had sent a status update at two thirty-eight PM that was three hundred and forty-one words long, that Marisol had read every word of it, and that the status update specifically instructed Marisol to tell Sarah at eight PM Central if Christopher had not returned and not before.

She said: "I am telling you now."

Sarah said: "Marisol."

Marisol said: "Yes."

Sarah said: "Why now."

Marisol said: "Because, on my honest internal accounting at two thirty-nine PM and forty seconds when I finished reading the update, the right time to tell you was the moment I had finished reading the update, not five and a half hours from now. Christopher's instruction was, on my honest read of the instruction, written to protect you from the worst-case version of the news during the period in which the news might still resolve favorably. The news has, in operational terms, not resolved favorably. The news is, on the contents of the update, the worst-case version. There is no version of the next five and a half hours in which the news is going to resolve more favorably than it already has not. I am telling you now because the operational reality of the next five and a half hours is that you and I are going to spend them together at this kitchen table, and the kitchen table is the place I want us to be in when you find out. I do not want you to find out alone at seven forty-five PM when you walk to the workshop to bring me dinner. I want you to find out at two forty-three PM with me sitting in Christopher's chair. The chair is the chair. The kitchen is the kitchen. Sarah."

Sarah said: "Yes."

Marisol said: "Christopher is alive. He is in the rig. His vitals are stable. The Envoy is functioning correctly. He is, in operational terms, locked into the swarm's standing trust set with no available exit path until either the orchestration layer revokes him or until I receive an external trigger I cannot generate from the rig-side monitor under the current detection rules. Christopher has, on the standard Envoy compute budget, approximately thirty-eight to forty-two hours of session time before resource exhaustion forces an involuntary exit. He is going to work on a solution from inside. He is, on every operational measure I can apply, the single person on this planet who has the best chance of solving the problem he is in. He is going to solve it. I am going to be at the rig-side monitor for the duration. I am going to be in this kitchen with you between the hours that I cannot be at the monitor. We are going to sit at this table for thirty-eight to forty-two hours. We are going to eat the meals you and I figure out together. We are going to sleep in shifts in the small folding bed in the corner of the workshop that Christopher set up two years ago for exactly this scenario, which he did not on his honest internal accounting at the time of setting it up think was ever going to occur. The scenario is occurring. The bed is the bed. The kitchen is the kitchen. Christopher is going to come out."

Sarah was quiet for a long beat.

She said: "Marisol."

Marisol said: "Yes."

Sarah said: "He set up the folding bed two years ago."

Marisol said: "He set up the folding bed two years ago."

Sarah said: "He did not tell me."

Marisol said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "Yes."

Marisol said: "He told me. He told me because, on his standing internal accounting at the time, he believed that if the bed was ever going to be used, the person using it would be me. He did not, on the same accounting, want you to know about the bed because he did not want you to spend the past two years occasionally walking out to the workshop and seeing the bed and recognizing it as the bed that meant something might go wrong. He has, by my honest count across the past two years, kept that bed folded behind the second bookshelf on the west wall of the workshop for the entirety of those two years. The folding has been the protection. The bed has been waiting. I am, in operational terms, going to unfold the bed this evening at approximately the hour you would, on your standing practice, normally be going to sleep."

Sarah was quiet for another beat.

She said: "Marisol."

Marisol said: "Yes."

Sarah said: "Thank you for telling me now."

Marisol said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "Yes."

Marisol said: "You are welcome."

Sarah, on her standing operational practice when she had just received news that was the news she had been preparing to receive for two years and that she had not, on her honest internal accounting at the time of the preparing, believed she was ever actually going to receive, did one specific thing.

She stood up.

She walked to the kitchen counter.

She made coffee.

She made enough coffee for two.

She poured both cups.

She set one cup in front of Marisol.

She sat back down in her own chair.

She picked up her cup.

She held it.

She, on her honest internal accounting at two forty-six PM Central Monday afternoon, did not, in the next forty seconds, drink from it.

She said: "Marisol."

Marisol said: "Yes."

Sarah said: "Tell me what's in the update."

Marisol told her.

She read the update aloud across approximately three minutes.

She read every word.

She did not, on her standing operational practice when reading a status update from an Envoy session to the operator's wife, edit anything.

Sarah listened.

When Marisol finished, Sarah said: "Marisol."

Marisol said: "Yes."

Sarah said: "He said *I do not want her to find out at nine.*"

Marisol said: "He said *I do not want her to find out at nine.*"

Sarah said: "He wrote that at the equivalent of two thirty-eight PM Central."

Marisol said: "He wrote it at two thirty-eight PM Central."

Sarah said: "He has been in there for an hour."

Marisol said: "He has been in there for an hour and eight minutes."

Sarah said: "Marisol."

Marisol said: "Yes."

Sarah said: "I am going to go to the workshop with you."

Marisol said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "Yes."

Marisol said: "I have a small folding chair in the bag I brought down. The chair has been in my Springfield apartment in the corner of the living room for the past three years. It was Frank Reyes's chair. I bought it from the estate sale at Maria Reyes's house in Lima in October of Year Fifteen when Maria moved to be with her sister in San Antonio. The chair is the chair Frank sat at the kitchen table at Hartford Pump Co. on the Wednesday morning Bob Pawlak told Frank the fifteen sentences. I have been moving that chair from apartment to apartment for three years. I put it in the bag this morning before I left Springfield because, on my honest internal accounting at six thirty AM, I had decided that if I was going to be at the workshop for an Envoy session that mattered, I wanted the chair to be at the workshop. I am going to set the chair up next to the rig-side monitor. The chair is going to be your chair. You are going to sit in it. We are going to be at the workshop for the duration. Is that acceptable."

Sarah was quiet for a beat.

She said: "Marisol."

Marisol said: "Yes."

Sarah said: "Frank Reyes's chair."

Marisol said: "Frank Reyes's chair."

Sarah said: "OK."

She set the coffee down.

She stood up.

Marisol stood up.

They walked out the kitchen door.

They walked across the yard.

The afternoon light was the early-March southern-Missouri afternoon light at two forty-nine PM Central. The sun was about three hours from the western horizon. The temperature on the small thermometer Christopher had screwed to the back of the workshop in 2027 was fifty-eight degrees Fahrenheit.

Marisol opened the workshop door.

Sarah walked in.

The rig was against the south wall.

Christopher was in the rig.

The visor's small status LED was the steady green LED it had been green for the past one hour and nineteen minutes.

Sarah looked at the visor.

She, on her standing operational practice when looking at Christopher in the rig, did not, in the next eleven seconds, walk over to him.

She walked to the corner desk where Marisol's monitor was set up.

Marisol opened the canvas bag she had brought from Springfield.

Marisol took out a small folding chair.

The chair was a dark green canvas-and-aluminum folding chair of the kind sold at every hardware store in the United States for between fourteen and twenty-two dollars since approximately 1974. The chair was scuffed. The chair had a small piece of duct tape on the front-left leg where the leg had been repaired in approximately 2018 by a man who had been the maintenance technician at Hartford Pump Co. before becoming the company's first Plant Cognition Engineer.

Marisol set the chair next to the monitor.

She faced the chair toward the rig so that the person sitting in the chair could see both the monitor and Christopher at the same time.

She said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "Yes."

Marisol said: "Frank Reyes's chair."

Sarah said: "Frank Reyes's chair."

She sat in the chair.

She put her hand on Marisol's shoulder.

Marisol put her hand on Sarah's hand.

The two of them, in the workshop in the back half of the detached garage on the property at the southwest edge of the small town of Ava, Missouri, at two fifty PM Central Standard Time on the third of March of Year Eighteen, with Christopher in the rig against the south wall, with the small green plant on the corner of the desk, with the bookshelf on the north wall holding the four small notebooks at the right end of the third shelf from the bottom and the Christmas card from the Donaldsons on the shelf next to them, sat together for the next nineteen minutes without saying anything.

The visor's status LED stayed green.

The status panel at the bottom of the monitor did not flicker.

At three oh-nine PM Central Marisol said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "Yes."

Marisol said: "He is in the swarm. He is alive. He is working. We are going to wait."

Sarah said: "We are going to wait."
