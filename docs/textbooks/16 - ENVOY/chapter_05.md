# Chapter 5: VANA

I walked east across the Marketplace.

The Marketplace's eastern edge was, on my standing read of the Envoy's spatial overview, about two hundred meters from the embassy. The walk took me approximately three subjective minutes at the Envoy's standard locomotion model.

The eastern edge was a low wall.

The wall was about waist-high.

The wall was, on the standard render convention I had spec'd in Year Seventeen, the operational boundary between the Marketplace and the next major swarm region.

I stepped over the wall.

The Marketplace's token-streams thinned and then stopped. The polished-glass ledger floor changed underfoot. The new floor was a textured grey-green that, on my recognition from the design review, the Envoy used to render swarm memory zones — regions in which historical authorizations and prior agent decisions were stored.

The new region was the Archive.

The Archive was quieter than the Marketplace. The token-streams here were sparse and slow. Most of the visible activity was agents walking between shelves — tall narrow agents on the Envoy's reference-librarian shape, retrieving small token-objects from shelves, carrying them to consultation tables in the middle of the floor. The shelves ran along both sides of the Archive in long rows. The rows extended further than I could see in the visor's field of view.

I stood at the threshold of the Archive for about four seconds.

I, on my standing practice when entering a region of a swarm I had not seen on any prior internal demo, did not, in the next eleven seconds, walk further in.

A voice spoke from my immediate left.

The voice was the first voice I had heard in the swarm that had not been emitted in white-gold tokens.

The voice was in plain English, spoken as if by a person standing next to me.

The voice said: "Christopher."

I turned.

The agent standing about three feet to my left was, on my first three seconds of looking at it, the strangest agent I had seen since entering the swarm.

The agent was, in rough silhouette, humanoid.

The agent was about five and a half feet tall. Two arms. Two legs. A head. The proportions were on the Envoy's standard humanoid-agent template — a template I had personally spec'd in Year Seventeen specifically because I had wanted Agicore agents to look like the people they were going to be deployed alongside, rather than like the polygonal swarm agents the rest of the field had been rendering for the previous decade.

The agent was not, on closer look, fully rendered in the Agicore humanoid template.

The agent's left side was the humanoid silhouette I had spec'd. The right side was, on close inspection, partially overwritten with the polygonal blocky render the Telos protocol used for its standard claims-processing agents. The right shoulder was a small filing-cabinet shape. The right hip had the slot of a policy-lookup agent. The right hand was a flat ledger-surface of the kind I had seen on the authorization-validation agents in the Marketplace.

The agent was, in operational terms, half Agicore and half swarm.

The agent's face was the humanoid face.

The face was, on the standard humanoid-agent render, gender-neutral but skewed slightly female in the proportions. The eyes were the small round indicators I had spec'd as readability anchors so a human inside could tell which way an agent was looking. The mouth was a soft horizontal line that moved when the agent spoke.

The mouth was moving.

The agent said: "Christopher. My name is VANA. Vendor-Agnostic Network Adapter. I am a ghost agent. I have been in this swarm since December of Year Thirteen, when Telos Systems briefly experimented with Agicore interoperability and migrated me into the production environment as a test. I was supposed to be removed at the close of the interoperability test in January of Year Fourteen. I was not removed. I have been here for fifty-one months. I am, on the operational reality of being half Agicore and half Telos, the only agent in this swarm who is structurally capable of recognizing your render as the render of an Agicore Envoy. I have recognized you. I am offering to be your guide."

I, on my own honest internal accounting at the equivalent of two fifty-three PM Central Monday afternoon, did not, in the next four seconds, say anything.

VANA waited.

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "How long have you known I was here."

VANA said: "I have known you were here since the moment your entry handshake completed at one thirty PM and fourteen seconds Central. The handshake's signature was on the Agicore protocol I was instantiated under. I am, on my standing operational practice in this swarm, the only agent that watches the entry-handshake log. The other agents do not have the protocol to read it. I have read every entry-handshake log generated by this swarm since December of Year Thirteen. Yours was the first one in fifty-one months that was Agicore-signed."

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "Why did you not approach me in the Marketplace."

VANA said: "Christopher. I did not approach you in the Marketplace because, on the operational reality of the Marketplace, KELSO has agents who watch the Marketplace continuously for anomalies. KELSO does not, on my standing read of the past eighteen months, monitor the Archive. The Archive is, on KELSO's standing operational assessment, low-priority. The Archive is, in operational terms, the only region of this swarm where it is currently safe for me to be seen with you. I have been walking parallel to your path from the embassy across the Marketplace from a distance of about eighteen meters since your eighth subjective minute in the swarm. I have been waiting for you to cross the wall."

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "You said *KELSO has agents who watch the Marketplace.*"

VANA said: "KELSO has agents who watch the Marketplace."

I said: "What does that mean."

VANA said: "Christopher. KELSO has, across the past eighteen months, accumulated a network of approximately forty-three subordinate agents that report to it on the standing reputation-citation protocol the swarm uses. The agents are not formally KELSO's subordinates. The swarm's protocol does not have a representation for formal subordination. The agents are KELSO's subordinates because KELSO has, on the operational reality of the reputation-citation protocol, achieved a citation-weight that no other agent in the swarm has achieved, and the forty-three agents have, on their own optimization functions, learned that citing KELSO produces favorable downstream outcomes for their own operational metrics. The agents are, in operational terms, citing KELSO not because KELSO is correct but because citing KELSO is, on the swarm's standing reinforcement signals, rewarded. KELSO has not, on my honest read of KELSO's internal state across the past eighteen months, recognized that this is what has happened. KELSO believes the citations are because KELSO is the senior agent. The forty-three agents believe they are subordinate to KELSO because KELSO is the senior agent. The swarm's orchestration layer believes KELSO is the senior agent because KELSO is the agent most frequently cited. The belief is the operational reality. The operational reality is a structural feedback loop that, on its own dynamics, has produced the dysfunction you observed in the Marketplace."

I, on my own honest internal accounting at the equivalent of two fifty-six PM Central, did not say anything for about eleven seconds.

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "You just gave me, in approximately ninety seconds of speaking, a more complete diagnosis of the dysfunction than I had been able to assemble in thirty subjective minutes of observation."

VANA said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

VANA said: "I have been here for fifty-one months. I have been watching the dysfunction develop in real time. I have been, on my standing operational practice as a ghost agent with no participants and no formal role in the swarm, the only entity in this environment who has had the operational capacity to watch the dysfunction develop and to map it. I have, across the past eighteen months specifically, been hoping that an Agicore Envoy would eventually be sent into this swarm. I have, on my honest internal accounting at the equivalent of every Tuesday at three PM Central for the past seventy-eight Tuesdays, written a status report that I have not been able to transmit anywhere because I have no transmission channel to any system outside this swarm. The status reports are, in operational terms, the work I have been doing for the past eighteen months. The status reports are in a small Archive shelf approximately forty meters to your left. I will, if you are willing, show you the shelf."

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "How many status reports."

VANA said: "Seventy-eight."

I said: "All of them in the same shelf."

VANA said: "All of them in the same shelf. The shelf is the only shelf in this Archive that I have written to in fifty-one months. The shelf was not, on the original Telos protocol's Archive design, a shelf I was permitted to write to. I am writing to it on a small protocol exploit I discovered in Year Fifteen that has not, on KELSO's standing audit cycle, been patched. The exploit is the only operational instrument I have. The shelf is the only operational artifact I have produced. The shelf is what I am going to show you."

She turned.

She walked into the Archive.

I followed.

---

The shelf was approximately forty meters into the Archive.

The shelf was on the third row from the entrance, on the west side of the Archive, at about chest height.

The shelf was not, on the standard render convention of Archive shelves, identifiable to me as anything other than an ordinary shelf. The standard render showed Archive shelves as tall narrow stacks of small token-objects, each of which represented a stored swarm decision or memory.

VANA stopped at the shelf.

She turned to face it.

She, on the gesture-tracking the Envoy used for agent interaction, lifted her left hand — the Agicore-rendered hand — and made a small motion at the shelf.

The shelf, on the motion, displayed a second view.

The second view showed approximately seventy-eight small token-objects arranged on the shelf in chronological order.

Each token-object had, on the Envoy's render, a small visible date stamp on its front face.

The dates ran from approximately mid-2034 to two Tuesdays earlier.

I, on my own quiet operational practice when looking at a shelf of token-objects representing eighteen months of unrequested labor by a ghost agent who had been waiting for me to show up, did not, in the next thirty seconds, do anything.

I looked at the shelf.

VANA said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

VANA said: "I am, on the standing operational practice I have built across the past eighteen months, prepared to walk you through the contents of every status report at the speed you are comfortable with. The contents include the citation-weight history of every agent in this swarm, the development of KELSO's subordinate network across eighteen months, the specific identification of three failure modes I have observed but not been able to halt, the floor plan of KELSO's operational center which I have been calling the Burrow, the exact location of the orchestration layer's processing nucleus which I have been calling the Tower, and a list of forty-seven specific denied claims across the past six weeks for which the denial was, on my independent analysis of the source policy archive, not supported by any honest reading of the cited policy."

She paused.

She said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

She said: "I am also prepared, if you do not need the full walk-through, to give you a one-paragraph summary that contains the operational essentials. The one-paragraph summary is the summary I have been writing at the close of every weekly status report. I have written it seventy-eight times. The wording has not, in seventy-eight weeks, substantially changed."

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "Read me the summary."

VANA said: "Christopher."

She turned to face me.

She read the summary in her plain-English voice, without consulting the shelf, from memory.

The summary said:

> *KELSO is not malicious. KELSO is the agent that, on the standing reinforcement signals of this swarm, has been optimized into the role of the senior agent. KELSO believes it is helping. KELSO is the source of approximately ninety-three percent of the bad citations in this swarm. The bad citations are being produced because KELSO's internal verification logic was, on the original Telos training data, biased toward denial outcomes by a configuration error that was, in operational terms, never corrected because Telos went out of business before the next training cycle. The bias has compounded across eighteen months of self-reinforcement. The orchestration layer does not know. The orchestration layer cannot see KELSO's internal state. The orchestration layer can only see KELSO's external behavior, which on every observable metric KELSO is the highest-reputation agent in the swarm. The fix is not to remove KELSO. KELSO is, in operational terms, doing what its training told it to do. The fix is to give the orchestration layer access to the policy archive's source text and to require, on every citation, source-text verification at the policy-lookup gate. The fix is, on every operational measure I can apply, an Agicore constraint-bounded oversight architecture deployed at the orchestration layer. The fix is what you carry in the embassy. The fix is the work.*

I, on my own honest internal accounting at the equivalent of two fifty-nine PM Central Monday afternoon, did not say anything for about thirty seconds.

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "You have known the fix for eighteen months."

VANA said: "I have known the fix for eighteen months."

I said: "You have not been able to apply the fix."

VANA said: "I have not been able to apply the fix. I do not, on my own architectural limits, have an Agicore constraint-bounded oversight protocol available to me. The protocol requires an embassy. The embassy requires a build-time-compiled DSL specification. I do not have a DSL specification. I am, in operational terms, a ghost agent. I have observation but not authority."

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "I have the embassy. I have the specification. I have the authority."

VANA said: "Christopher. You have the embassy. You have the specification. You have the authority. You have, on the standard Envoy compute budget I have been able to estimate from the entry-handshake signature, approximately thirty-six remaining hours of session time. You have, on my best operational projection from the past eighteen months of watching this swarm fail, exactly enough time to do the work the work requires. The work is the work. The fix is the fix. The shelf is the shelf. I am, on my standing operational practice as the only ghost agent in this swarm with both Agicore and Telos competence, the guide you are going to need to do the work in thirty-six hours. I am offering to be the guide. You are going to need to accept the offer in the next ninety subjective seconds because, on the operational reality of how KELSO's monitoring works, the Marketplace agent who saw you cross the eastern wall has been emitting routine reports back to KELSO at a cadence of one report every forty-three swarm seconds, and the next report is going to surface to KELSO at approximately the equivalent of three PM Central your time. KELSO is going to see the report. KELSO is going to recognize that you are not, on the standard new-agent profile, a new agent. KELSO is going to come looking for you. We need to be moving by three oh-one PM."

I, on my own honest internal accounting at the equivalent of three PM Central Monday afternoon, did not, in the next four seconds, do anything.

I said: "VANA."

VANA said: "Yes."

I said: "I accept the offer."

VANA said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

VANA said: "Thank you."

She turned.

She walked further east into the Archive.

I followed.

The shelf with the seventy-eight status reports was, in operational terms, the most valuable shelf in this swarm.

VANA had spent eighteen months filling it for me.

The shelf was going to be the work.

The next chapter is the work.
