# Chapter 7: Pittsburgh

The first call from Dale Heller came in at four eleven PM Central.

Marisol was at the corner desk in the workshop watching the status panel at the bottom of the rig-side monitor. The panel had flickered three more times since Christopher's two thirty-eight PM update. The three flickers had been short tactical reports — each between thirty and seventy words — that Christopher had sent at irregular intervals as he moved through the swarm. Marisol had read each one within ninety seconds of its arrival and had read the most recent one out loud to Sarah, who was still in Frank Reyes's chair next to her at the corner desk.

The most recent report had been at the equivalent of three twenty-one PM Central. It had said:

> *Met a ghost agent. Half-Agicore, half-Telos. Name: VANA. Vendor-Agnostic Network Adapter. She has been here since Year Thirteen. She has been writing weekly status reports for 18 months. She has the dysfunction mapped. The dysfunction is a structural feedback loop centered on an agent called KELSO. Fix is at the orchestration layer. Going to the Tower. Will report from the Tower. — C.*

Sarah had read the report twice and had then said, quietly, *thank God for VANA,* which Marisol had recognized at the time as the first sentence Sarah had spoken in approximately forty-three minutes.

The status panel had not flickered since three twenty-one PM.

It had been forty-eight subjective minutes since the last report.

At four eleven PM Central the cell phone on the corner desk — Christopher's cell, which Marisol had on her own standing operational practice moved from the workshop bench to the corner desk at two forty-six PM so that she could see who was calling — rang.

The screen said: *Heller, Dale — Allegheny Health Partners.*

Marisol looked at Sarah.

Sarah said: "Marisol."

Marisol said: "Yes."

Sarah said: "Answer it."

Marisol answered it.

She said: "Dale."

He said: "Marisol."

He paused for about one second.

He said: "I expected Christopher."

She said: "Christopher is in the rig. He has been in the rig since one thirty PM Central. I am the foundation engineer on the rig-side monitor. I am authorized to discuss the session with you."

There was another small pause.

Dale said: "Marisol Vega."

She said: "Yes."

He said: "Aaron's sister."

She said: "Aaron's sister."

Dale said: "I have not seen Aaron in five years. He is the Plant Cognition Engineer at the Adrian plant of the parts manufacturer that was, in Year Twelve, one of the five sponsoring plants of Bob Pawlak's first night class."

Marisol said: "He is."

Dale said: "Marisol."

She said: "Yes."

Dale said: "Tell me about the session."

Marisol took a breath.

She said: "Dale. The session has gone wrong. The entry handshake completed nominally at one thirty PM. Christopher began the standard observation across the Marketplace. At two thirty-one PM he attempted the exit handshake. The exit handshake failed across all three fallback paths because the swarm's authorization layer had absorbed the Envoy into its standing trust set during entry without surfacing the absorption to the entry-handshake log. The Envoy is functioning correctly. Christopher is alive. His vitals are stable. He has been working on a solution from inside since two thirty-eight PM. He has, as of three twenty-one PM, identified a ghost agent who has been mapping the dysfunction for eighteen months and who is now serving as his guide. He has identified the structural source of the dysfunction. He is heading to the swarm's orchestration layer to attempt a fix from inside. His compute budget on the Envoy is approximately thirty-six remaining hours."

The line was quiet for about eleven seconds.

Dale said: "Marisol."

She said: "Yes."

Dale said: "He is trapped."

She said: "He is trapped."

Dale said: "I am the reason."

She said: "Dale."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "Christopher is the reason. The Envoy is the reason. The Telos protocol is the reason. You are not the reason. You asked. He said yes. He is now working. You and I are now on the same operational track, which is to do what we can from outside while he does what he can from inside. You and I are not, on the operational reality of the next thirty-six hours, going to be useful to him by relitigating who is the reason."

The line was quiet for another six seconds.

Dale said: "Marisol."

She said: "Yes."

Dale said: "What do you need from me."

Marisol thought about it for about four seconds.

She said: "Dale. Three things. First. Stay at the office. Do not, in the next thirty-six hours, leave Pittsburgh. Christopher's status updates have been mentioning specific claim denials that he has been observing inside the swarm. I want you to cross-reference the claim metadata against your live denial feed in real time. If Christopher describes a denial that has not yet issued externally, I want you to flag it on your end and prevent the external notification from sending to the member or to the treating clinician. The flagging is going to require a temporary manual gate on the swarm's outbound notification queue. Your engineering team has the authority to install the gate. The gate is going to slow the swarm's notification throughput, which is going to attract the state commissioner's attention. You are going to need to be prepared to explain the slowdown when the commissioner notices, which on the standard cadence of the commissioner's review will be inside seventy-two hours. Are you operationally willing to install the gate."

Dale said: "Yes."

She said: "Second. The mortality data your chief medical officer assembled in October. I want you to send Sarah a copy of the data via the foundation's secure channel. Sarah is in Frank Reyes's chair next to me at the workshop. Sarah is going to read the data tonight. Sarah is, on the operational reality of being the spouse of the person inside the swarm right now and the person who has spent the previous fourteen months adjacent to the news that fourteen members have died from this swarm's denials, entitled to the data. Christopher will, when he comes out, want her to know what she is looking at. Are you operationally willing to send the data."

Dale said: "Yes."

She said: "Third. Get in your car. Drive to your house. Tell your wife what is happening. Eat dinner with your family tonight. You are going to be the operational counterpart of this session at Allegheny for the next thirty-six hours. You are not going to be useful as the operational counterpart if, at hour thirty-five, you have not slept since this morning. Christopher's status updates have been mentioning specific members by anonymized ID. Some of those members are, on his read of the underlying medical-necessity profile, members whose denials are going to kill them. You are going to be reading those updates as they arrive. You are going to want, on every operational impulse you have as a human being, to fly to St. Louis tonight on a charter and drive down to the workshop. Do not do this. The workshop is staffed. The workshop has two people in it who are the right two people. The workshop does not have room for a third. You are going to be most useful to Christopher and to the members at Pittsburgh, in your office, with the gate installed, with your engineering team coordinated, and with the operational decisions about external notifications routing through you. Are you operationally willing to stay in Pittsburgh."

The line was quiet for about fourteen seconds.

Dale said: "Marisol."

She said: "Yes."

Dale said: "I am operationally willing to stay in Pittsburgh."

She said: "Good."

Dale said: "Marisol."

She said: "Yes."

Dale said: "Tell Sarah I am sorry."

She said: "Dale."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "Tell her yourself. The phone is on speaker. She has been listening for the past nine minutes."

There was a small pause.

Dale said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "Dale."

Dale said: "I am sorry."

Sarah said: "Dale."

Dale said: "Yes."

Sarah said: "He took the call from you because he is the man who takes the call from you. He went in because, on his own honest internal accounting at nine forty-six AM this morning when he told me he was going in, the Envoy was the only instrument that had any operational chance of preventing forty to sixty additional member deaths. He told me about the forty to sixty at lunch. He told me about them because he wanted me to know what he was working on. He has been in there for two hours and forty-three minutes. He is going to come out. Dale. The sorry is the sorry. The work is the work. Install the gate. Send the data. Drive home. Eat dinner with your wife."

Dale said: "Sarah."

She said: "Yes."

Dale said: "Thank you."

She said: "Dale."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "You are welcome."

The line was quiet for another four seconds.

Dale said: "I am hanging up. I am driving to my house. I will be back at the office at five thirty AM Eastern tomorrow with the gate installed and the engineering team coordinated. Marisol, the secure-channel password is the password I used to use in 2031. Sarah will have the data by midnight Eastern. Stay on this number for the duration. I am leaving the cell on my desk. The cell will be answered by the night-desk operator. If the situation changes, call this number. The night-desk operator will route to me regardless of the hour."

Marisol said: "Dale."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "Thank you."

He said: "Marisol."

She said: "Yes."

He said: "Thank you."

He hung up.

---

The workshop was quiet for about three minutes after Dale hung up.

The status panel did not flicker.

Sarah, in Frank Reyes's chair, did not say anything for the three minutes.

Marisol, at the corner desk, did not say anything either.

At four twenty-three PM Central, Sarah said: "Marisol."

Marisol said: "Yes."

Sarah said: "He has been in there for two hours and fifty-three minutes."

Marisol said: "Two hours and fifty-three minutes."

Sarah said: "He has thirty-five hours and seven minutes of compute budget remaining."

Marisol said: "Approximately."

Sarah said: "Marisol."

Marisol said: "Yes."

Sarah said: "Are you sure."

Marisol turned in her chair to face Sarah.

She, on her own honest internal accounting at four twenty-three PM Central Monday afternoon, did not, in the next four seconds, say anything.

She said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "Yes."

Marisol said: "No."

Sarah said: "Marisol."

Marisol said: "Yes."

Sarah said: "Are you sure he is going to come out."

Marisol said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "Yes."

Marisol said: "I have been at the foundation for four years and three months. I have, in those four years, watched Christopher fail to figure out exactly four operational problems that he had told me, before he started on each one, that he was going to figure out. He figured out the four of them. He was wrong about how long each one was going to take. He was wrong about which approach was going to work. He was wrong about who was going to help him. He was not wrong about the figuring out. He has, on the operational record I have been keeping in my own small notebook for four years, never once told me he was going to figure something out and then failed to figure it out. I am not, on the operational reality of the past four years, going to be the engineer who tells his wife that he is not going to figure this one out. I do not, on the operational reality of being a sister of Aaron and a daughter of the woman who taught me to be the person Sarah needed at the corner desk this afternoon, get to be that engineer. Sarah."

Sarah said: "Yes."

Marisol said: "I am not sure. But I trust him."

Sarah was quiet for about thirty seconds.

She said: "Marisol."

Marisol said: "Yes."

Sarah said: "OK."

She, on her standing operational practice at four twenty-five PM Central in Frank Reyes's chair, did the thing she had been thinking about doing for the past three minutes and that she had not, until Marisol had said *I trust him,* committed to doing.

She stood up.

She walked to the small refrigerator Christopher had installed in the workshop in Year Sixteen.

She opened it.

She took out two small plastic containers.

The containers held the dinner she had made on Sunday afternoon and that Christopher had asked her not to bring to the workshop tonight because, on his standing operational practice on the night of an Envoy session, he ate dinner at the kitchen table afterward and not in the workshop.

She had brought the dinner anyway, at twelve fifteen PM, when she had brought him the lunch.

She had brought the dinner because, on her own honest internal accounting at twelve fifteen PM, she had recognized that the standing practice was the standing practice and that the standing practice was, in operational terms, not going to hold for tonight even though she did not, at twelve fifteen PM, know why.

The dinner was beef stew with carrots and potatoes.

There was enough for three.

She brought the containers to the corner desk.

She set one in front of Marisol.

She set one at the empty workbench between them where Christopher was not.

She set the third at her own chair.

She sat down.

She picked up her fork.

Marisol picked up her fork.

The dinner was the dinner.

The chair at the workbench was empty.

The status panel did not flicker.

Outside the workshop window, the Missouri afternoon was the Missouri afternoon.

Inside the rig, Christopher was, on his own internal accounting at the equivalent of four twenty-six PM Central Monday afternoon, walking with VANA toward the Burrow.

The next chapter is the Burrow.
