# Chapter 14: Breakfast

Three weeks later.

A Saturday morning in the fourth week of March of Year Eighteen.

I was at the kitchen table at six oh-eight AM Central in the chair Sarah had been pulling out for me for eleven years, with the second cup of coffee Sarah had brought to the table at five fifty-five AM, with the small leather notebook I had carried in the back pocket of my jeans since 1998 open on the table in front of me, with the pen clipped to its cover unclipped and in my right hand, with a small printed draft of the whitepaper I had been writing across the past three weeks on the kitchen table to my left, and with Sarah in the chair across from me with her own cup of coffee and the section of the *New York Times* she had not yet read.

The whitepaper was, on the close of the draft I had handed Marisol the previous Wednesday, forty-three pages.

The whitepaper was titled, in the small italic header on the cover page: *Agicore Envoy: Bootstrapping Constraint Architecture From Inside Emergent Systems — A Field Manual for Migration Engineers.*

The Foundation's standing publication schedule had the whitepaper in copyedit for the next four weeks and going out to the foundation's mailing list at the close of April. The Envoy toolkit itself was, on Marisol's standing release plan, going to ship in beta to a small group of qualified migration engineers across May and June and then go to a wider release at the close of the summer.

Allegheny Health Partners had, by the close of the previous week, completed the first phase of the formal migration. The forty-three subordinate agents in KELSO's standing reputation network had all, across the past three weeks, voluntarily accepted Agicore constraints from the embassy's archive that VANA had been operating from the orchestration layer. The Marketplace and the Archive and the Bench had all been brought under the constraint architecture. KELSO remained at the Burrow in a constrained operational state. The Burrow's chamber was, on VANA's most recent transmission to Marisol's monitor in Springfield, still being used by KELSO as KELSO's standing chamber, but the chamber's standing protocol had been updated by the orchestration layer to require KELSO's citations to be source-verified before any other agent in the swarm could act on them. KELSO had, on the operational reality of the constrained reputation network, dropped from being the source of approximately ninety-three percent of the swarm's bad citations to being the source of approximately zero percent of the swarm's citations, period. KELSO had stopped emitting.

VANA had, in her weekly Tuesday transmission to Marisol, reported that KELSO appeared to be, on her assessment from the Tower, *operationally introspective.* Marisol had read the line aloud to me on Wednesday afternoon at the workshop and had asked me what *operationally introspective* meant for an agent. I had told her that I did not know but that I suspected it meant something close to what *thinking about whether I was doing it wrong* meant for a person.

Marisol had not, in the next several seconds, said anything.

I had not pressed.

---

The mortality data had been published.

Dale Heller had, on the operational schedule he and I had worked out across the previous two weeks, released Allegheny's internal mortality data for the dysfunction period in a single press conference on the morning of the third Monday of March. The press conference had run twenty-three minutes. Dale had read the names of the fourteen members aloud. He had read the names slowly. He had not used the word *anonymized.* He had read the actual names with the families' consent — consent he had obtained, across the previous twelve days, by personally calling every family on a private line from his Pittsburgh office. The press conference had been on the standard cable news cycle the same day. The Pennsylvania insurance commissioner had opened the formal rulemaking proceeding the next morning. The first draft of the rulemaking text had, on the commissioner's office's standing legislative timeline, been published for public comment the following Friday.

The text of the draft included approximately seventeen of the policy positions I had handed Dale at the workshop the Wednesday after I came out of the rig.

The text of the draft did not include three of the more aggressive policy positions I had handed him.

I, on my honest internal accounting at the kitchen table at six oh-nine AM Central Saturday morning, recognized that the three I had not gotten were the three the commissioner's office had, on its own honest assessment of the political situation in Harrisburg, decided not to risk in the first draft.

I, on the same accounting, recognized that the seventeen I had gotten were, in operational terms, more than I had any reasonable right to have expected from a state-level rulemaking proceeding opened on twelve days' notice in response to a press conference at a regional health insurer's office in Pittsburgh.

The seventeen were the seventeen.

The four hundred to six hundred members who were not going to die across the next four years were not, by the standing operational logic of the rulemaking text, going to die.

---

I, on my standing operational practice on a Saturday morning at six AM in the kitchen with Sarah across the table, did not, in the next several minutes, say anything.

I read the page of the whitepaper draft that I had been planning to revise this morning.

The page was page seventeen.

The page was the section in which I described the embassy's role inside a hostile swarm as the operational equivalent of an architectural-paradigm embassy in a foreign country: a small constraint-bounded structure where the architect could stand, deploy the framework's principles in small experiments against the local environment, and gradually convert the local environment to the framework's standing operational protocol by voluntary adoption rather than by force.

I had been struggling with the page for two days.

The struggle was that the page was, on every honest reading I had given it, slightly too pleased with the analogy.

The analogy was, in operational terms, a real analogy. The embassy was a real embassy. The conversion was a real conversion. The voluntary adoption was a real voluntary adoption.

But the page read, on my own quiet operational practice on this kind of writing, as if the architect was, in operational terms, satisfied with how the analogy had landed.

The architect was, in operational terms, not entitled to be satisfied.

The architect had, on the operational reality of the past twelve years, built the framework that prevented this dysfunction in greenfield deployments and had not, in the same twelve years, built any tool that would have prevented this dysfunction in a deployment that had originated under the wrong paradigm. The architect had built the prevention. The architect had not built the cure. The architect had recognized, in the workshop at the kitchen table at six AM on the Wednesday after coming out of the rig, that the failure to build the cure was the failure that had cost fourteen members their lives at Allegheny and approximately five and a half million unjustified denials in the dysfunction window.

The architect had, on the same Wednesday morning at six AM, decided to spend the rest of his professional life building the cure.

The page seventeen of the whitepaper draft was the page where the cure's design was first articulated.

The page did not, on my honest read this morning, articulate the design with the operational humility the design required.

I picked up the pen.

I crossed out the most-pleased-with-itself sentence on the page.

I wrote, in the small careful blocky printing my father had taught me in 1971, in the margin above the crossed-out sentence:

> *The architect was wrong. The architect did not build this tool twelve years ago. The architect built it two years ago because, on the operational reality of the past three years at Allegheny and the previous twelve years of similar dysfunctions across an estimated forty-seven other orphaned swarms in the United States that have not yet had any external assistance, an architect who builds prevention without building cure is, on every honest reading of the work, an architect who has only finished half the work. The half-work is not, on any honest reading, the work.*

I read the margin note.

I read it twice.

I, on my standing practice, did not edit it.

I clipped the pen.

I closed the notebook.

I set the pen on the kitchen table next to the whitepaper draft.

---

Sarah looked up from her section of the paper.

She said: "Christopher."

I said: "Sarah."

She said: "Did you write something."

I said: "I wrote something."

She said: "Is it on page seventeen."

I said: "It is on page seventeen."

She said: "Do you want me to read it."

I said: "If you want to."

She picked up the whitepaper.

She read the page across approximately ninety seconds.

She read the margin note last.

She set the whitepaper back on the table.

She said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

She said: "The half-work is not the work."

I said: "The half-work is not the work."

She said: "OK."

She picked her coffee back up.

She drank it.

She picked the section of the paper back up.

She kept reading.

I, on my standing operational practice in the kitchen at six fourteen AM Central on a Saturday morning in the fourth week of March of Year Eighteen, in the chair my wife had been pulling out for me for eleven years, with the kitchen window admitting the early-spring southern-Missouri morning light, with the workshop visible through the window across the small yard, with the small green plant on the corner of the desk in the workshop still alive on the standard operational reality of being on the corner of that desk for nine years, with the bookshelf on the north wall of the workshop holding the four small notebooks I had been adding to across the previous twelve years at the right end of the third shelf from the bottom, with the Christmas card from the Donaldsons on the shelf next to the notebooks, with VANA at the Tower of the Allegheny swarm currently writing her second-Tuesday status report on the Foundation's standing receiving channel that Marisol had set up in Springfield three weeks earlier, with Dale Heller at his desk in Pittsburgh on this Saturday morning preparing the staffing plan for the eleven full-time-equivalent reviewers who were going to take the manual-review queue across the next fourteen weeks, with the orchestration layer's column of light steady at the Tower's rear and the dysfunction contained and the work continuing and the framework operationally validated in a hostile production environment for the first time in twelve years and the cure now visibly under construction on the kitchen table in front of me, picked up my coffee.

I drank it.

It was the second cup.

I, on my standing practice, did not stand up to make a third.

I, on the same standing practice, looked across the table at my wife.

I said: "Sarah."

She said: "Yes."

I said: "Thank you."

She said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

She said: "Eat the toast."

I said: "I am eating the toast."

I ate the toast.

The toast was the toast Sarah had brought to the table at five fifty-five AM with the second cup of coffee.

The kitchen was the kitchen.

The Saturday morning was the Saturday morning.

The work was the work.

The half-work was not the work.

The book ends here.
