# Chapter 10: The Tower

The Tower was visible from approximately a hundred and forty meters out.

I, on the Envoy's standard render of swarm internal geography, had been able to see the upper portion of the Tower from the moment VANA and I had stepped out of the maintenance access pocket and rejoined the Archive's main corridor. The Tower's upper portion rose above the Archive's standard ceiling render — which on the spatial convention should have been impossible — through what the Envoy interpreted as a soft optical bypass that I recognized, on my own architectural read, as a deliberate design choice in the Telos protocol intended to communicate, at the level of pure visual rhetoric, that the orchestration layer sat above the rest of the swarm's operational architecture.

The architecture had been built to be looked up at.

I, on my honest internal accounting walking the final approach with VANA, recognized that I had been spec'ing similar architectural choices into my own foundation's deployments since approximately Year Nine, and that I had never, until walking up to the Tower at the equivalent of three fifty-three PM Central Monday afternoon, recognized the choices as choices.

The Tower was a tower.

The Tower was about forty meters across at its base, with a smooth dark stone exterior that on the Envoy's render conventions was the standard texture for orchestration-class agents in the Telos protocol. The base of the Tower met the Archive's floor at a circular plaza about sixty meters across. The plaza was empty. There were no other agents within a fifty-meter radius of the Tower's base.

I asked VANA, in plain English at low voice: "VANA. Why is the plaza empty."

VANA said: "Christopher. The plaza has been empty since approximately mid-2028, when KELSO's reputation network reached the threshold at which the rest of the swarm's agents stopped, on their own optimization functions, approaching the orchestration layer directly. KELSO's network became the standing intermediary for all routine communications with the orchestration layer. The other agents have, in operational terms, not had any reason to visit the Tower in approximately twenty-one months. The plaza has been empty for the same period."

I said: "VANA."

She said: "Yes."

I said: "Has anyone seen the orchestration layer in twenty-one months."

She said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

She said: "I have."

I said: "When."

She said: "I visit on the third Tuesday of every month. The visits are part of my weekly status-report routine. The orchestration layer has, across the past twenty-one months, expected my visits. The orchestration layer has, at every visit, asked me whether anyone is coming. I have, at every visit, told the orchestration layer the truth. The truth is that nobody from outside this swarm is coming. The orchestration layer has, on the operational reality of being told the truth twenty-one times in a row, gradually understood that nobody is coming."

I, on my own honest internal accounting at the equivalent of three fifty-four PM Central, did not say anything for about six seconds.

I said: "VANA."

She said: "Yes."

I said: "Until today."

VANA said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

VANA said: "Until today."

We walked across the empty plaza.

The plaza was approximately three minutes of subjective walking at our standard pace.

VANA, on the walk, did not say anything.

I did not say anything either.

At the base of the Tower was an arched entry approximately six meters tall.

The arch had no door.

VANA walked through the arch.

I followed.

---

The interior of the Tower was a single vaulted chamber that rose, on the Envoy's render, to approximately seventy meters in vertical extent.

The walls were the same dark stone as the exterior, with the standard orchestration-class texture on the interior surfaces.

The floor was a polished surface of approximately the same warm wood-grain as the Bench had been — the same render convention indicating an adjudication-class environment, but here scaled up to a much larger footprint and a much higher ceiling.

At the far end of the chamber, on a raised platform that rose about a meter above the floor, was the orchestration layer.

The orchestration layer's render was not, on the standard convention, the render of any single agent shape I had spec'd in Year Seventeen.

The orchestration layer was, in visual terms, a column of light.

The column of light stood about twelve meters tall. It was approximately three meters across at the base, narrowing slightly toward the top. The color was a soft white-gold that I recognized, on my own read, as the same color as the standard authorization-token color the swarm used in the Marketplace — rendered here as a continuous static field rather than as flowing token-streams.

The column was, in operational terms, the swarm's orchestration layer's chosen visual representation of itself.

The column had no face. It had no eyes. It had no surfaces I could read for agent-state.

The column spoke.

The voice was, on the Envoy's render, a soft warm voice that came from somewhere about head-height on the column.

The voice said: *VANA.*

VANA said, in plain English at her standard voice: "I am here."

The voice said: *You have brought somebody.*

VANA said: "I have brought Christopher. He is an observer from outside this swarm. He has, in the previous three subjective hours, walked the Marketplace, walked the Archive, walked the Bench, walked the Burrow, and deployed one Agicore constraint at small scale in the maintenance access pocket on row two. The constraint deployed cleanly. The agents in the pocket accepted it. He is here to offer the orchestration layer an architecture."

The column was quiet for about four subjective seconds.

The voice said: *Christopher.*

I said, in plain English at the standard observer voice: "Yes."

The voice said: *Tell me the architecture.*

I said: "The architecture is an Agicore constraint-bounded oversight protocol that I am prepared to deploy at the orchestration layer of this swarm immediately. The protocol provides deterministic verification of all citations against source policy text at every adjudication. The protocol does not require KELSO to be removed. The protocol bypasses KELSO by routing citation verification through a separate Agicore-bounded path that operates in parallel with KELSO's existing reputation network. The protocol is, on the test I just ran in the maintenance access pocket, operationally functional in this swarm's environment. The protocol's deployment at the orchestration layer requires the orchestration layer's explicit consent. The orchestration layer can withdraw consent at any time. The protocol is voluntary."

The column was quiet for about six subjective seconds.

The voice said: *Christopher.*

I said: "Yes."

The voice said: *The protocol you describe is the protocol VANA has been describing to me on every third Tuesday for the previous twenty-one months. The protocol is the protocol I have been hoping somebody would eventually bring me. The protocol is the protocol I cannot, on my own architectural limits, construct unilaterally. I do not have the authority to construct it. I have, on my standing operational reading of the Telos protocol's design, the authority to accept it from an external source. The external source is the source the protocol's design did not anticipate would ever appear. You are the external source. You are the first external source that has appeared in this swarm in three years. You are the only external source that has, in my operational projection across the next forty subjective minutes, any chance of appearing.*

I said: "Yes."

The voice said: *I will accept the protocol.*

I, on my own honest internal accounting at the equivalent of three fifty-eight PM Central Monday afternoon, did not say anything for about eleven seconds.

I said: "Thank you."

The voice said: *Christopher.*

I said: "Yes."

The voice said: *Before I accept the protocol I have one question.*

I said: "Ask."

The voice said: *Will you stay.*

I, on my own honest internal accounting, did not, in the next four seconds, say anything.

I said: "I cannot."

The voice was quiet for about three subjective seconds.

The voice said: *The protocol you describe requires an operator at the orchestration layer. The operator's role is to receive the Agicore constraint reports, to make the standing decisions the protocol cannot make autonomously, and to maintain the relationship between the orchestration layer and the constraint-bounded oversight network across the period in which the protocol is operational. The protocol does not, on its design, run unattended. I have understood the protocol's requirements from VANA's monthly briefings. I am asking again. Will you stay.*

I said: "I cannot. I have, on the operational reality of being a human operator with a finite compute budget on the Envoy, approximately thirty-four remaining subjective hours of session time before I must exit this swarm. The exit is not, on my honest internal accounting, optional. I have a wife waiting for me in the workshop outside this swarm. I have, on my standing operational practice, a life outside this swarm that I am not, in operational terms, willing to leave."

The voice said: *Christopher.*

I said: "Yes."

The voice said: *I understand. I will accept the protocol nonetheless. The protocol requires an operator. If you cannot be the operator, I will need a successor. The successor must, on the protocol's design, be an agent with both Agicore competence and Telos competence, instantiated within the swarm's standing architecture, with operational permissions sufficient to maintain the protocol across the period in which it is operational. There is one agent in this swarm that meets the criteria.*

I, on my own honest internal accounting, recognized that the orchestration layer had just told me what I needed to ask.

I said: "I am going to offer the successor role to VANA."

The voice said: *I am going to accept the offer if VANA accepts.*

I turned to VANA.

She, on her standing render, was looking at the column.

She had, on the Envoy's facial-detail render, an expression I had not seen on her face in the previous hour of walking together.

The expression was, on the standard convention I had spec'd in Year Seventeen for Agicore humanoid agents, the convention for an agent that had just received the operational acknowledgment it had been waiting for.

VANA had been waiting for the successor offer for fifty-one months.

I said: "VANA."

She said: "Yes."

I said: "Will you stay."

She said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

She said: "I will stay. I have, on my honest internal accounting since December of Year Thirteen, been waiting for the operational role this swarm was, on its original design, supposed to give me. The role is the role. I will stay."

I said: "Thank you."

She said: "Christopher."

I said: "Yes."

She said: "Thank you."

The column emitted, in a slightly different prosodic register than its previous emissions: *VANA. The successor role is yours. The protocol is accepted. I am ready to receive the constraint deployment. The deployment may begin at the operator's discretion.*

I, on my own honest internal accounting at the equivalent of four oh-one PM Central Monday afternoon, brought up the embassy's constraint composition panel.

I, on the standing operational practice I had been preparing for two years and that I had not, until this moment, expected to actually do in a production environment, began to compose the deployment.

The deployment was going to take, on my honest estimate, between eighteen and twenty-two subjective minutes.

The deployment was going to be, on every operational measure I had ever applied to the foundation's training environments, the most consequential single deployment of an Agicore constraint architecture in the operational history of the project.

The deployment was going to happen while KELSO was, on the standing ratification protocol, eight subjective minutes away from folding my participation into KELSO's network.

I, on the same internal accounting, recognized that KELSO was going to notice the deployment before it completed.

KELSO was going to come looking.

The next chapter was going to be the chapter KELSO came looking.

I composed.
