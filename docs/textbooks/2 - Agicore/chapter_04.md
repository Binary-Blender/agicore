# Chapter 4: The DSL as Constraint Boundary

The central technical claim of the Agicore architecture, on which the integrity of every operational property depends, is that a domain-specific language with a finite grammar is a trust mechanism. The claim is sharper than the analogy it inherits from compiler theory and stronger than the casual usage of the word "trust" implies. It is not the claim that grammar-conformant output is more likely to be correct than non-conformant output, though that is also true. It is the claim that grammar-conformance is the mechanism by which untrustworthy AI output becomes the input to a trustworthy production system. The grammar admits what conforms; what does not conform cannot be expressed; what cannot be expressed cannot be executed. The trust is not located in the AI that produced the output. It is located in the constraint that the output had to satisfy to reach execution. The architectural shift this enables is the precise one: you are not asking AI to be correct; you are building a system in which incorrect output cannot reach production. The two stances differ in everything that follows from them.

The framing that gives this claim its precise theoretical weight is Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety, stated by Ashby in 1956 as a result in classical cybernetics: a regulator can only absorb the variety it possesses. Applied to the engineering of cognitive production systems, the law says that any mechanism for absorbing the variety of possible AI outputs must itself possess at least as much variety as the outputs it absorbs. A regulator with too little variety cannot discriminate among the outputs it must respond to; it collapses cases that ought to be distinguished and treats them uniformly, or it rejects cases that ought to be admitted and produces operational friction without informational gain. The implication for the design of constraint mechanisms on AI output is severe: the constraint must be expressive enough to capture the distinctions the discipline cares about, and rigid enough to reject distinctions it does not care about.

A DSL grammar is the right shape for this requirement because it can be tuned, at the time of its design, to admit exactly the variety that the discipline considers legitimate. The grammar's productions enumerate the declaration types; the field-level constraints enumerate the legitimate properties of each declaration; the type system enumerates the legitimate relationships between declarations. What the grammar admits is the discipline's positive vocabulary. What the grammar refuses is the disciplinary judgment that some expressions, no matter how plausible they might appear to an AI, do not correspond to anything the runtime can do. The grammar is, in Ashby's terms, the regulator whose variety is calibrated to the variety of the discipline. Outputs that exceed the constraint are rejected mechanically, before the rest of the pipeline runs.

The contrast with prompt engineering is instructive because it isolates the structural difference. Prompt engineering attempts to convince a probabilistic system to produce specific kinds of outputs by careful phrasing of the request. The engineer studies the model's behavior, identifies prompts that produce the desired outputs more often than alternatives, and iterates toward prompts that produce the desired outputs reliably enough for the use case. The discipline has real content; the work is real. But the structural property of the system it produces is that the trustworthiness of the output is a statistical property of the model's response to the prompt. A model update may change the response distribution; a slight rephrasing of the input may change the response distribution; an edge case the engineer did not anticipate may produce a response that is outside the desired distribution. The constraint is in the persuasion, and the persuasion is best-effort.

Grammar enforcement places the constraint at a different point in the pipeline. The AI is permitted to produce whatever it produces; the engineer does not need to coax it into specific outputs. What the AI produces is then submitted to a parser that either accepts the output as a valid `.agi` artifact or rejects it. Rejected output does not reach the next stage of the pipeline. The trustworthiness of the artifact that does reach the next stage is not a statistical property; it is a categorical property of having passed the parser. The constraint is in the grammar, and the grammar is mechanical. A model update does not affect the constraint. A rephrasing does not affect the constraint. An edge case the engineer did not anticipate may produce a parse failure, which is the system telling the engineer that the case is not in the grammar — a request for either a grammar extension or a different formulation of the case, but in either event a mechanical signal rather than a silent degradation.

This is the structural difference between trying to make AI produce correct output and building a system in which incorrect output cannot be consumed. The first effort is unbounded — there is always another edge case, another phrasing, another model release to recalibrate against. The second is bounded — the grammar is finite, the parser is finite, and the work of extending them is the work of formalizing what the discipline has decided is admissible. The first effort is paid as an operational tax for as long as the system runs. The second is paid as a one-time design investment, with operational dividends that compound over time.

The question of why a DSL beats a JSON schema for this purpose is worth answering carefully, because JSON schemas are the most common alternative in contemporary AI engineering and the comparison illuminates what a DSL contributes beyond shape constraint. A JSON schema constrains the structural shape of an output: which fields are present, what their types are, what value ranges are admissible. A schema can express that a `User` object has a `name` field of type string and an `age` field of type integer between zero and one hundred fifty. It can express that a list of users contains zero or more User objects. It can express discriminated unions, optionality, format constraints on strings. It is genuinely useful for shape validation, and the OpenAI structured-output, Anthropic tool-use, and similar mechanisms in other model families rely on schemas of this kind to constrain the model's output to a parseable shape.

A DSL goes further in three structural dimensions that schemas do not natively address. The first is cross-reference resolution. A declaration in an `.agi` file may refer to another declaration by name: an `ACTION` declaration may reference an `AI_SERVICE` by name; a `WORKFLOW` step may reference an `ACTION` by name; a `RULE` may reference a `FACT` by name. The grammar admits these references syntactically; the static validator verifies that the referenced declarations exist, are of the correct type, and are accessible in the relevant scope. A JSON schema cannot natively express that the value of one field must match the name of a declaration elsewhere in the document; the validation must be done by custom logic outside the schema. The DSL absorbs this validation into the language definition, where it can be enforced uniformly across every declaration that uses cross-references.

The second structural dimension is type unification across heterogeneous outputs. An `ACTION` declares the type of its `INPUT` and the type of its `OUTPUT`. A `WORKFLOW` step that calls the action passes the input and consumes the output; the step's surrounding context must be type-compatible with both. A `PIPELINE` chain of typed nodes connected by `->` requires that the output type of each node match the input type of the next. A JSON schema can validate that each individual artifact is well-formed; it cannot natively validate that a composition of artifacts is type-coherent. The DSL's type system performs this composition-level validation as part of static analysis. A type mismatch between a workflow step and the action it calls is caught at the validation stage, not at runtime.

The third structural dimension is the legality of nesting. An `ENTITY` declaration may contain a `STAGES` block, which may contain `TRANSITIONS` and `GUARDS`, which may reference `RULE` names. The grammar specifies which blocks may appear inside which other blocks and which fields are legitimate at each nesting depth. A `WORKFLOW STEP` may contain `ON_FAIL stop`, but `ON_FAIL` is not legitimate as a top-level field on the workflow itself; the nesting matters. A JSON schema can express nesting depth and required fields per nesting depth, but the result is verbose and brittle. The DSL grammar expresses nesting as part of its production rules, with field-level legality enforced by the parser. The result is a constraint that is easier to maintain, easier to read, and produces parse errors whose messages cite the precise nesting context.

In sum, a schema constrains shape; a DSL constrains meaning. The meaning of an `.agi` artifact includes not just its surface structure but the semantic relationships among its declarations, the type-coherence of its composition, and the disciplinary legitimacy of its nesting. A schema is the right tool when shape is all that needs to be enforced. A DSL is the right tool when the discipline has accumulated enough structure that shape constraint is necessary but not sufficient.

The two-stage validation pipeline in Agicore realizes the constraint discipline as defense in depth. The first stage is the parser: a recursive-descent parser that consumes a token stream produced by the lexer and either produces an abstract syntax tree conformant to the grammar or fails with a precisely located error. Parser errors are syntactic: a missing brace, an unrecognized keyword, an ill-formed field, a type annotation that violates the grammar's type vocabulary. Parser failures abort the pipeline; nothing downstream sees the failed input. The parser is the first mechanical gate, and its strictness is the architectural defense against malformed AI output reaching any stage that might propagate it. An AI assistant authoring `.agi` files iterates against the parser, in the same way that a human programmer iterates against a compiler: the parser tells the AI whether the artifact conforms, with no ambiguity and no negotiation.

The second stage is the static validator. The validator runs after the parser has produced an AST and before any code generator runs. It performs twelve semantic checks that the parser is structurally unable to perform, because the checks span across declarations rather than within a single declaration. The validator verifies that every cross-reference resolves to an existing declaration of the correct type. It verifies that types are coherent across composition boundaries — workflow step inputs and outputs, pipeline node chains, action input/output unions. It verifies exhaustiveness where the language requires it: a `STAGES` block must enumerate every state the entity can be in; a discriminated-union output must have a handler for every variant. It verifies that no two declarations share a name in a namespace that does not permit duplication. It verifies that `BELONGS_TO` references point to entities that exist. It verifies a dozen such properties, each of which is necessary for the generated code to compile and run.

Validator errors abort generation. Validator warnings — properties that are unusual but not incorrect — allow generation to continue with a notice. The distinction between errors and warnings is itself a disciplinary judgment: errors are the cases where continuing would produce incorrect output; warnings are the cases where the output is admissible but might be inadvertent. The two-stage pipeline — parser, then validator — converts the categorical question "is this artifact admissible" into a precise positive answer, with the failure modes named precisely enough that an AI assistant can iterate against them or a human can fix them by hand.

This two-stage discipline is defense in depth in the precise sense that security architects use the phrase. The parser catches syntactic malformation; the validator catches semantic malformation; the two together catch the union of failure modes that either could catch on its own, plus a third category — cases that are syntactically admissible but semantically inconsistent — that neither stage could catch alone. The same artifact must pass both stages to proceed to generation. The conjunction is stronger than either stage alone, and the stages are independent enough that a defect in one is unlikely to be silently masked by the other.

The relationship of the grammar to the discipline of the field is worth stating directly because it is the deepest structural claim this chapter makes. What is admissible in the DSL is what the discipline has decided is a primitive. A new declaration type is added to the grammar when, and only when, the operational practice of the field has identified a pattern that recurs across applications and deserves first-class treatment. The grammar is the crystallization of disciplinary judgment, and its evolution is the formal record of what the field has come to consider primitive. A grammar that admits too much is a grammar that has not yet sorted what is essential from what is incidental. A grammar that admits too little is a grammar that has frozen its judgment prematurely. The right grammar is the grammar whose surface area matches the operational variety of the discipline, no more and no less. The framework-evolution discipline that Chapter 25 develops — primitives are added when applications stress the framework, never on speculation — is the structural defense against grammar bloat and grammar starvation.

The lineage that produced this approach to language design as constraint specification is long and deserves explicit acknowledgement. John Backus, in his 1959 description of what became BNF (Backus-Naur Form), gave the field its first precise formal mechanism for specifying programming language grammars. The contribution was not merely notational; it was the recognition that a grammar could be written down in a form independent of any particular implementation, that the grammar specified what was admissible in the language, and that the question of whether a given input conformed to the language could be reduced to the mechanical question of whether the input could be derived from the grammar's productions. The structural insight — that admissibility is a mechanical property of grammars — is the inheritance on which every subsequent discipline of language-based constraint rests. The Agicore DSL is, in this lineage, a grammar specified with full Backus discipline, whose conformance is mechanically verifiable by a parser whose structure mirrors the grammar's productions.

Niklaus Wirth's contribution to the lineage is structurally distinct and equally load-bearing. Wirth's 1976 *Algorithms + Data Structures = Programs* argued that the structure of a program follows from the structure of its data, and that the work of language design is largely the work of designing the data structures the language manipulates. Applied to DSL design, the Wirth insight reads: the work of designing the Agicore DSL is the work of designing the declaration types and their fields, because the parser, the validator, the generators, and the runtime all consume the same data structures derived from the grammar. A change to the grammar produces a change to the AST, which produces changes to every consumer. Conversely, a change to the runtime that requires a new field on a declaration requires that field to be added to the grammar, the parser, the validator, and the generators. The data-structure-driven discipline that Wirth advocated is exactly the discipline that keeps the Agicore compiler internally coherent as it evolves: the AST is the contract; everything else is a consumer of the contract.

Tony Hoare's contribution, from a different angle, completes the theoretical foundation. Hoare's 1969 axiomatic basis for computer programming introduced the discipline of preconditions and postconditions as formal proof obligations: a function's contract specifies what must be true before the function executes and what will be true after, and the function's correctness reduces to the proof that the postcondition follows from the precondition under the function's logic. Applied to the DSL constraint discipline, the Hoare insight reads: the parser's contract is that input that passes the parser is syntactically admissible; the validator's contract is that input that passes the validator is semantically coherent; the generator's contract is that artifacts emitted from a validator-passed AST are wire-correct; the two compilers' contract is that code that passes both is type-coherent end to end. Each stage has a precondition (the previous stage passed) and a postcondition (a stronger property holds). The pipeline as a whole is a correctness-by-construction discipline, in the precise sense Hoare made operational sixty years ago.

The minimum example below shows the simplest possible Agicore declaration, included here to make the constraint-by-grammar claim concrete. The grammar admits the declaration; the validator confirms its coherence; the compiler emits the deterministic runtime that the declaration specifies.

```agi
APP example {
  TITLE "Example"
  DATABASE example.db
}

ENTITY Student {
  name: string REQUIRED
  grade: string
  TIMESTAMPS
  CRUD full
}
```

This artifact — six lines of declaration — instructs the compiler to emit a complete Tauri application with a SQLite migration that creates a `students` table with `name` and `grade` columns plus timestamps, a Rust CRUD command set, TypeScript invoke wrappers, a Zustand store slice for student state, a list view and a form modal, and the Tauri configuration that wires the whole thing together. The grammar admitted the declaration because every keyword (`APP`, `ENTITY`, `TITLE`, `DATABASE`, `REQUIRED`, `TIMESTAMPS`, `CRUD`) is in the disciplinary vocabulary, every field type is admissible, and the nesting is legitimate. The validator confirmed coherence: the entity name is unique, the field types are recognized, the CRUD modifier is valid. An AI assistant authoring this declaration produces an output that either parses and validates or fails with a precise error. The trust is in the grammar; the AI's freedom is exercised within what the grammar admits.

The grammar's role as the precise location of the build-time / run-time boundary is now in view. Upstream of the grammar — at the AI authoring stage — the system is permissive: any AI, any prompt, any iteration is admissible, because the grammar is the mechanical gate that protects the downstream from authoring variance. Downstream of the grammar — at the compilation and runtime stages — the system is rigid: the AST is the contract, the generators are deterministic, the runtime is deterministic, and nothing about the authoring process can introduce variance into the execution. The grammar is the membrane through which the build-time freedom and the runtime discipline are kept in their respective domains. Without the grammar, the membrane has no specification, and the failures of the contemporary AI consensus described in Chapter 1 are the predictable consequence.

A practical observation about the grammar's role as crystallized discipline is worth making before this chapter closes. The Agicore grammar at the time of this writing admits fifty-eight declaration types across ten layers, from the application primitives that any working developer can author after an afternoon's reading through the semantic-infrastructure primitives that codify cross-organizational coordination patterns. Each of those declaration types was added when an application's authoring needs demonstrated that the pattern recurred and deserved promotion to a first-class primitive; none were added on speculation. The grammar's surface area is, in this sense, a measurement of the discipline's accumulated structure: it is the count of patterns the field has decided are primitive enough to deserve their own keyword. When a new pattern emerges that the existing primitives cannot express cleanly, the discipline's response is not to write application code that works around the grammar but to extend the grammar after the operational case has been made. This evolution discipline — the topic of Chapter 25 — is the structural defense against grammar bloat. It is also the structural defense against grammar starvation, because every operational pattern that recurs eventually surfaces as friction, and friction is the signal that triggers the grammar-extension cycle. The grammar grows by operational pressure, never by speculative addition, and the size of the grammar at any moment is the size of the discipline that has been operationally validated up to that moment.

The chapter that opens Part II treats the compilation pipeline as the central engineering artifact that this constraint discipline makes possible. With the grammar as the boundary established, the question becomes what the compiler does with the artifacts the boundary admits: how the AST is consumed, what generators emit which artifacts, how the two-compiler property closes the loop on wiring correctness, how protected files preserve hand-written implementation territory through regeneration. The discipline of the boundary is the topic of this chapter; the discipline of the pipeline downstream of the boundary is the topic of the part that follows.
