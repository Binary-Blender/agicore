# Chapter 22: HOC — The Andon Loop in Production

The Andon Loop, as Part IV described it, is a multi-stage mechanism with five distinct architectural commitments: a typed mutation policy that names what AI is allowed to modify and at what tier; a verifier that mechanically rejects out-of-scope proposals before any sandbox runs; a regression sandbox that replays the captured failure case plus recent production traffic; an optional NBVE shadow window that dual-executes the candidate against live traffic with an SPC-gated promotion decision; and a hash-chained ledger that records every transition with cryptographic continuity. The description was load-bearing. The defense was theoretical. What was missing, until HOC, was the demonstration that the entire mechanism survives contact with a real application — small enough to read in one sitting, real enough to run continuously against an actual deployment, and architecturally complete enough that every claim Part IV made shows up as code in the source file. The Home Operations Center is that demonstration. It is a 437-line `.agi` file that monitors a domestic network, classifies devices, refines firewall rules, and pulls andon cords when the deterministic expert system encounters conditions it cannot match. It was the first application in the project to declare a `MUTATION_POLICY` and run it end-to-end against a real human approving real mutations.

HOC's domain is small by design. A home network has perhaps fifty devices on a typical day; the classification problem has perhaps ten device types worth distinguishing; the latency budget for "this device just appeared, what is it" is generous; the consequences of misclassification are inconvenient rather than catastrophic. The smallness is the feature. The Andon Loop's first deployment had to be a system where every stage of the loop could be exercised regularly enough to surface bugs, but where the consequences of a bug were a smart bulb that turned the wrong color rather than a financial regulator threatening sanctions. The smallness also made HOC's source file readable end-to-end — every declaration visible, every mutation tier present, every reasoner concrete, every workflow inspectable in a single browser window. The architecture stood or fell on whether a single human, reading a single file, could verify that the Andon Loop's commitments were realized in code. They were. The verification was direct.

The application surface, stated in declaration counts, is this. Four `ENTITY` declarations cover the persistent model: `Device`, `ConnectionProbe`, `InternetOutage`, `AndonLightState`. Three `PACKET` declarations carry the typed message payloads — `ProbeResult`, `DeviceObservation`, `AndonStateChange`. Three `CHANNEL` declarations bus those packets through the system with `exactly_once` delivery semantics. Five `ACTION` declarations encode the deterministic operations: `ping_target`, `arp_scan`, `publish_andon_mqtt`, `send_notification`, `update_andon_state`. Two `MODULE` declarations — `internet_health` and `device_classifier` — group classification rules under shared activation logic, each declared with `EXPECTS_MATCH true` so that a no-match becomes an andon pull. Ten `RULE` declarations cover the classification logic — `classify_dns_failure`, `classify_complete_outage`, `classify_high_latency`, `classify_apple_phone`, `classify_smart_bulb_lifx`, and the rest — each tagged with `MUTATION_TIER 1` or `MUTATION_TIER 2` to declare what tier of mutation may modify it. Three `WORKFLOW` declarations — `probe_internet`, `scan_network`, `notify_andon` — orchestrate the operational steps with explicit `TIMEOUT` and `ANDON_ON` keywords. Two `REASONER` declarations — `andon_handler` and `weekly_kaizen` — provide the reactive and proactive entry points for AI authorship. Four `VIEW` declarations expose the operational surfaces — `Devices`, `RecentProbes`, `Outages`, `AndonLights`. Two `TEST` cases pin the integration paths the compiler cannot prove from declarations alone. And at the center of the file, the `MUTATION_POLICY` block that ties all of this to the Andon Loop machinery the framework provides.

The `MUTATION_POLICY` declaration deserves to be quoted in full because it is the load-bearing artifact of the application and because it shows every architectural commitment Part IV made realized in lines a practitioner can read.

```agi
MUTATION_POLICY hoc_classification_policy {
  TARGETS [internet_health, device_classifier]

  TIER 1 threshold_tuning {
    SCOPE              [RULES_modify]
    AUTO_DEPLOY        true
    REGRESSION_SUITE   24h_recent_workflows
  }

  TIER 2 new_classifier {
    SCOPE                [RULES_modify]
    AUTO_DEPLOY          false
    APPROVAL_AUTHORITY   chris
  }

  TIER 5 governance {
    SCOPE                [MUTATION_POLICY_modify]
    AUTO_DEPLOY          false
    APPROVAL_AUTHORITY   ORDERED [chris]
  }

  ANDON_RESPONDER       andon_handler
  IMPROVEMENT_REASONER  weekly_kaizen
  LEDGER                hoc_mutation_ledger
}
```

Three tiers, three policy postures. Each tier deserves field-by-field treatment, because the meaning of the policy is in the precise reading of each clause and in the difference between what each clause permits and what each clause forbids.

The T1 — threshold_tuning — tier reads as follows. The `SCOPE [RULES_modify]` clause permits exactly one kind of mutation: an edit to the body of an existing `RULE` targeted by the policy. The scope kind covers parameter changes within an already-declared rule — adjusting the latency threshold from `50ms` to `40ms` inside `classify_high_latency`, tightening a packet-loss percentage in `classify_dns_failure`, lowering a signal-strength cutoff in a wifi-quality rule. It does not cover adding a new rule (that would require `RULES_add`, not in the T1 SCOPE list). It does not cover modifying the rule's `MUTATION_TIER` tag (that would be `RULES_retier`, also not in scope). The constraint is mechanical: the verifier inspects every kind named in the claimed scope, looks up the minimum tier whose SCOPE list contains that kind, takes the maximum across all kinds, and rejects before the sandbox runs if the claimed tier is lower than the resolved tier. The `AUTO_DEPLOY true` clause means that once the regression suite passes, deployment is automatic — no review queue, no signoff, no notification. The `REGRESSION_SUITE 24h_recent_workflows` clause names the test corpus: the last twenty-four hours of recorded probe and observation packets, replayed through the sandboxed copy of the affected module with the proposed mutation applied. If any prior observation that the existing rules classified successfully now classifies differently, the sandbox rejects the mutation. The human is not in the loop for T1 not because review is undesirable but because the human cannot meaningfully evaluate whether forty milliseconds is a better threshold than fifty when the answer requires replaying twenty-four hours of probe data. The regression suite can. The architecture defers the question to the artifact that can answer it.

The T2 — new_classifier — tier reads differently. `SCOPE [RULES_modify]` permits the same kind of mutation in HOC's use, but the surrounding clauses change the posture. `AUTO_DEPLOY false` halts the pipeline after the sandbox passes; the mutation does not deploy on its own. `APPROVAL_AUTHORITY chris` names the single signer whose decision unblocks deployment. The mutation appears in the MutationConsole's Approvals tab with the proposal payload, the test evidence, the regression statistics, the diff against the current rule body, and the andon event that triggered the proposal. The practitioner reviews the full context and either signs (the mutation deploys and the ledger records the approval) or rejects (the mutation is recorded as rejected and the andon handler must propose differently next time). The user-in-the-loop semantics are explicit: the system does not deploy on a timeout, does not default-approve, does not nudge with escalating reminders. If the practitioner is away for a week, the proposal waits a week. The authority is genuine because the architecture does not silently route around its absence. T2 in HOC is reserved for proposals that materially change classification behavior — where the question is not "does this break the regression corpus" (the sandbox answers that) but "is this the kind of distinction the operator wants the system to make" (only the operator can answer that).

The T5 — governance — tier is the architecture's defense against its own subversion. `SCOPE [MUTATION_POLICY_modify]` permits exactly one kind of mutation: a change to the `MUTATION_POLICY` declaration itself — a new tier added, a SCOPE list expanded, an APPROVAL_AUTHORITY changed from a single signer to an ORDERED chain. `AUTO_DEPLOY false` is non-negotiable; no auto-deploy path exists at T5. `APPROVAL_AUTHORITY ORDERED [chris]` names an ordered approval chain with exactly one signer. The ORDERED form is what matters; the single-signer cardinality is incidental. An ORDERED chain enforces sequential approval where each signer sees every prior signer's signature and notes before signing, and any signer may veto with full prior-context awareness. In HOC the chain has one signer because HOC has one operator, but the code path is the same code path the Accelerando suite exercises with three signers (CEO, CTO, board chair) on regulated-domain T5 mutations. The forward-compatibility argument is precise: same declaration form, same generator, same runtime code, same ledger payload schema — only the cardinality of the chain differs. When HOC grows from a one-person deployment to a household-with-multiple-stakeholders deployment, the change is a single `ORDERED [chris, partner]` edit. No new code path is exercised. The mechanism is identical.

The `EXPECTS_MATCH true` contract on the two modules is the connection between the expert system and the Andon Loop. When a `DeviceObservation` packet arrives on the device channel, the `device_classifier` module evaluates its ten classification rules in priority order. If any rule matches, the device is tagged with its inferred type and the observation continues. If no rule matches — a device whose MAC vendor is unfamiliar, whose port profile is unrecognized, whose hostname does not parse — the module's `EXPECTS_MATCH true` contract triggers an andon pull. An `AndonSignal` is emitted; the deterministic execution path halts on this observation; the `andon_handler` REASONER is invoked. The handler reads the failure case, consults the mutation history (was a similar proposal already rejected?), and proposes a mutation — typically a new `RULE` matching the unknown device's observed fingerprint. The proposal then flows through the tier verifier, the regression sandbox, the optional shadow window, and — for T2 proposals — the MutationConsole, where the human reviews and signs. Every state transition lands on the `hoc_mutation_ledger` with a SHA-256 hash of the prior entry and the canonical event payload.

The module declaration that wires `EXPECTS_MATCH` to the policy is concise enough to read in full:

```agi
MODULE device_classifier {
  DESCRIPTION    "Classify devices observed on the home network"
  INPUT          DeviceObservation
  EXPECTS_MATCH  true
  MUTATION_POLICY hoc_classification_policy
  RULES [
    classify_apple_phone,
    classify_smart_bulb_lifx,
    classify_smart_bulb_hue,
    classify_chromecast,
    classify_printer_brother,
    classify_wifi_doorbell_ring,
    classify_thermostat_ecobee,
    classify_laptop_macbook,
    classify_router_eero,
    classify_unknown_iot
  ]
}
```

The two-line coupling — `EXPECTS_MATCH true` and `MUTATION_POLICY hoc_classification_policy` — is the entire bridge between the deterministic expert system and the AI-author cycle. The first line tells the runtime that no-match is a failure rather than a silently-tolerated condition. The second names the policy that governs what AI may propose in response. The worked sequence for a single andon-to-deploy cycle, exercised regularly during HOC's operation, runs as follows. An incoming probe produces a `DeviceObservation` packet — say, a device with MAC vendor `Espressif` (an ESP32 microcontroller) and an HTTP server on port 80 with a vendor string the existing rules do not recognize. The `device_classifier` module evaluates its ten rules; none match. The `EXPECTS_MATCH true` contract fires `pull_module_andon` with the observation payload, the module name, and the policy binding. An `AndonEvent` row is written; `AndonLightState` flips to red; the MQTT bridge publishes the change and the office bulb turns red. The `andon_handler` REASONER is invoked with the failure packet, the prior mutation history for similar failures, and the current rule set. The AI proposes a new `RULE` matching `mac_vendor == "Espressif"` AND `http_server_present == true`, tagged `esp32_iot_device`, with a claimed tier of 2 and a claimed scope of `[RULES_modify]`. The tier verifier finds `RULES_modify` in both T1 and T2 SCOPE lists, takes the minimum (T1) for that kind, takes the maximum across all kinds (still T1), and confirms that the claimed tier of 2 is at or above the resolved tier — pass. Because T2's `AUTO_DEPLOY` is `false`, the policy escalates the proposal to the sandbox. The sandbox spins up a copy of `device_classifier`, applies the new rule, replays the captured failure observation (now resolves), then replays the last twenty-four hours of `DeviceObservation` packets (none reclassify). Test evidence is recorded: one case resolved, twelve thousand eight hundred and forty-seven regression cases unchanged. The proposal transitions to `awaiting_approval`. The MutationConsole's Approvals tab shows the proposal; the office bulb turns yellow. The practitioner walks past, sees yellow, opens the console, reads the proposal — rule body, diff, test evidence, triggering observation — and signs. The signature is recorded in the approval_chain JSON. The proposal transitions to `deployed`. The rule is installed in the live module. The ledger records the deploy with a SHA-256 hash of the prior entry. `AndonLightState` clears. The bulb turns green. The next probe of the same device classifies successfully. The cycle is closed.

The `weekly_kaizen` IMPROVEMENT_REASONER is the proactive half of the architecture. It runs on a `SCHEDULE` of `weekly`, reads the prior seven days of `DeviceObservation` packets, and looks for efficiency candidates — rules that fire frequently with low diversity (suggesting the rule could be tightened), rules whose conditions overlap (suggesting consolidation), threshold values that no longer reflect the operational reality (suggesting tuning). It emits proposed mutations into the same channel the andon handler emits into; the proposals flow through the same gates. The architectural distinction between reactive and proactive is preserved by the schedule (`on_demand` versus `weekly`); the verification pipeline is identical from tier-verifier onward. This is the right architectural decomposition. Reactive responders close the loop on failure; improvement reasoners close the loop on optimization; both produce proposals that are the same kind of object and are gated by the same machinery.

The `weekly_kaizen` declaration in the source file is intentionally minimal:

```agi
REASONER weekly_kaizen {
  DESCRIPTION  "Weekly review of network behavior; propose rule refinements"
  INPUT        { CHANNEL device_observations  WINDOW "7d" }
  OUTPUT       { PACKET AndonStateChange }
  SCHEDULE     weekly
}
```

The four clauses do real work. `INPUT` names the channel and window — seven days of `DeviceObservation` packets retrieved from the channel's persisted history. `OUTPUT` declares the proposal payload type, which the runtime uses to validate the AI's response shape before submitting to the verifier. `SCHEDULE` is what `start_improvement_scheduler` polls; weekly resolves to a trigger that wakes the reasoner once every seven days. On each invocation the reasoner loads the seven-day observation corpus and computes, per rule, the fire count, the matched-device diversity, and the overlap rate with other rules. It looks for three patterns: rules that fire on hundreds of observations but classify them all as the same device (could be tightened); rules whose conditions overlap with others to the point that one is redundant (the older can be retired); threshold values that have drifted relative to the observed distribution (the threshold was set at the ninetieth percentile six months ago but the distribution has shifted). For each candidate it formulates a proposal — a tightened condition, a rule retirement, a threshold adjustment — with the claimed tier set to whichever tier permits the operation. The proposal flows through the same tier-verifier path; the same sandbox runs; the same MutationConsole receives anything that does not auto-deploy. The architecture is symmetric: there is no separate code path for proactive versus reactive proposals.

The MQTT bridge is the part that takes the metaphor of "pull the andon cord" and makes it physical. HOC publishes `AndonLightState` changes to a Home Assistant MQTT broker. Three smart bulbs in the practitioner's home network are subscribed to the topic; the bulb in the office, the bulb in the kitchen, and the bulb in the workshop literally turn red, yellow, or green as the system's andon state changes. The mapping from system state to bulb color is deterministic and exhaustive. Red means an unresolved andon pull — a device the classifier could not identify, an internet probe that failed in a way the classifiers do not recognize, a workflow step that timed out without a `COMPENSATING_ACTION`. Yellow means a pending mutation in a shadow window or awaiting approval in the MutationConsole — a T2 classifier rule the andon handler proposed and the sandbox accepted, waiting for the practitioner to look at it and sign; or a rule under NBVE shadow evaluation where the production output and the shadow output have not yet accumulated enough samples for the SPC gate to decide. Green means the system is operating within its declared expectations — no open andon pulls, no pending approvals, no active shadow windows.

What makes this realization of Visual Management architectural rather than aspirational is that the mapping is not a dashboard summary computed at display time; it is a function of the persistent state of the system. The `AndonLightState` entity has a single row per zone; the entity is updated as a side effect of every transition in the andon and mutation pipelines; the MQTT publication is a deterministic action triggered by the update. The bulb color is a projection of the database state, not an interpretation. A practitioner who walks into the office and sees red knows, with the same confidence they would have inspecting the ledger directly, that an unresolved andon exists. The CSE-textbook chapter on Visual Management — Toyota's discipline of making operational state physically visible to the people who can act on it — finds its Agicore realization in three smart bulbs the practitioner sees the moment they walk into a room. The discipline Toyota required of its assembly-line workers ("when you see red, stop and resolve before resuming") is enforceable in HOC because the red has the same meaning as the database row, the database row the same meaning as the ledger entry, and the ledger entry the meaning Part IV declared. This is not a metaphorical andon. It is an andon.

The `@phase11` markers throughout HOC's source deserve their own treatment because they make operational pressure visible as a property of the source file. The `WORKFLOW` declarations in HOC use keywords like `ANDON_ON`, `ROLLBACK_BOUNDARY`, `COMPENSATING_ACTION`, and `TIMEOUT` that the Agicore v1.0 parser does not yet fully support as first-class keywords. They appear in the source as `@phase11 ANDON_ON internet_probe_failed`, `@phase11 TIMEOUT 5s`, `@phase11 ROLLBACK_BOUNDARY external`. The `@phase11` prefix marks them as Phase 11 DSL extensions — keywords the framework's roadmap has scheduled but has not yet implemented.

Each marker corresponds to a gap HOC encountered in practice and documented rather than worked around silently. `@phase11 ANDON_ON internet_probe_failed` records that `probe_internet` needs a way to declare which named failure conditions pull an andon at the workflow level, distinct from rule-level no-match andons; the keyword is desired as a first-class workflow attribute so the parser can validate the named condition against the workflow's declared error variants. `@phase11 ROLLBACK_BOUNDARY external` records that `notify_andon` includes a step (publishing to an external MQTT broker) whose effect cannot be transactionally undone if a later step fails; the boundary keyword tells the runtime that compensating actions are required across this boundary rather than naïve rollback. `@phase11 COMPENSATING_ACTION send_andon_cleared` records the specific compensating action that should fire when an external-boundary-crossing step is undone — publishing a "cleared" message after a "pulled" message was sent. `@phase11 TIMEOUT 5s` records that `ping_target` needs a declared maximum duration, after which the workflow runner treats the step as failed and, by virtue of the surrounding `ANDON_ON`, pulls an andon.

None of these are workarounds. They are forward-declarations of features the framework owes the application. The `@phase11` markers are operational-pressure feedback made into source comments — the application telling the framework, in the framework's own file format, what the framework still owes it. Phase 11 of Agicore's roadmap is driven directly by what HOC's source file says HOC needs: the prioritization is mechanical, the requirements are concrete, and the test cases are the existing HOC workflows that will be re-parsed with the new keywords elevated to first-class status the moment the implementation lands. This is what it means for an architecture to evolve through operational pressure rather than speculative roadmap planning, and Chapter 25 will treat the discipline of that evolution in full.

What HOC proves, then, is the set of claims Part IV defended, now realized as a running system. It proves that the tier verifier works mechanically: a proposal whose claimed scope exceeds the tier it claims is rejected before the sandbox runs. The proof is not theoretical. The author of this textbook has personally watched the verifier reject a proposal where the andon handler, in a moment of generative enthusiasm, attempted to propose both a new `RULE` and a modification to a `MODULE`'s `EXPECTS_MATCH` setting in the same packet. The first was T2; the second was T4. The verifier resolved the required tier as 4, compared it to the claimed tier of 2, returned `tier_rejected`, and the sandbox never ran. The MutationConsole logged the rejection with full context. The hash-chained ledger recorded the rejection alongside every other state transition. The system continued operating. The andon handler, on the next pull, proposed a strictly-T2 mutation, the verifier accepted it, the sandbox passed, the MutationConsole queued it for approval, the practitioner reviewed and signed, the rule deployed. Every step happened. Every step was logged. The verifier, the sandbox, the console, and the ledger did exactly what Part IV said they would do.

HOC proves the sandbox works against captured production traffic. The `REGRESSION_SUITE 24h_recent_workflows` clause on T1 triggers, before any T1 mutation deploys, a replay of the last twenty-four hours of recorded probe and observation packets through the sandboxed copy of the affected module. If the mutation breaks any prior observation that the existing rules classified successfully, the sandbox rejects the mutation and the andon handler must propose differently. The HOC deployment has accumulated, at the time of writing, a regression corpus large enough that subtle threshold changes can be evaluated against meaningful traffic volume. The corpus grows automatically as the system runs. The IMPROVEMENT_REASONER, on its weekly cycle, has the same regression corpus available to evaluate its proposed consolidations. This is what Chapter 18's regression-suite-resolver looks like in operation.

HOC proves the ledger's hash chain is verifiable. A standing background task — `verify_ledger_integrity` — runs nightly across the `hoc_mutation_ledger`. Every entry's `self_hash` is recomputed from the prior `prev_hash` joined with the canonical event payload (ledger_name, sequence number, proposal id, policy name, event type, actor, payload JSON, recorded_at timestamp, all separated by the unit separator and hashed under SHA-256). The recomputed hash is compared to the stored value. The chain has held since the deployment began. The optional file-system mirror — `AGICORE_LEDGER_SINK_PATH` — writes a `.jsonl` per ledger name; HOC's ledger is mirrored to a directory under the user's profile, with one entry per line, per-write `fsync`, suitable for off-DB archival or for shipping to an external auditor if HOC ever scaled to a deployment where an external auditor cared. This is what Chapter 19's cryptographic audit trail looks like in operation.

HOC proves the approval chain enforces what it declares. T2 mutations sit in the MutationConsole until the practitioner signs them; the system does not deploy them on a timeout or default-approve them on any condition; if the practitioner is away for a week, the proposal waits a week. The ORDERED chain on T5 — even with one signer — uses the same code path as the production-grade ORDERED chains the Accelerando suite uses with three signers; the test of that code path is being exercised every time HOC's ledger records a T5 transition, even though the chain is short. The architecture is the same architecture. The deployment is at small scale. The mechanism is identical.

The principle HOC validates is the principle Chapter 16 stated as the architectural inversion. AI lives at the edit boundary. The deterministic expert system runs at runtime. The MUTATION_POLICY is the typed contract that names what AI may modify and what authority is required. The verifier is the mechanical gate that enforces the contract. The sandbox is the regression evidence. The ledger is the cryptographic record. The MutationConsole is the surface where humans exercise their authority over consequential changes. Smart bulbs are the surface where humans see the system's operational state without consulting a dashboard. The Toyota Production System's andon — the cord that any worker may pull, the light that any worker may see, the kaizen cycle that any improvement may flow through — has been translated into a system of declarations, gates, and signatures, and the translation runs on a single laptop, monitoring a single household network, with one human in the approval chain. The smallness is the proof of the architecture, not its limitation. The same mechanism scales. The next chapter shows it scaling to eighteen interoperating applications covering an entire enterprise.
