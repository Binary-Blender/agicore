# Chapter 2: Sage

The picnic table behind the gymnasium was bolted to a concrete pad on the south side of a 1971 cinderblock building Sage's grandfather had, on family stories nobody had ever bothered to confirm, poured the slab for in the same year he was laid off from the iron foundry in Joplin.

The table had been theirs since the second week of fifth grade.

Sage Whitehorse had walked out of the cafeteria on a Tuesday in September of 2024 because a boy named Trent Bullock had spent the whole lunch period telling the table what Sage's grandmother's name sounded like to him. Dax had followed her out without a word. They had eaten behind the gym. They had been eating behind the gym every school day since.

Three forty-six PM. Wednesday. Late October.

Sage had her iPad open in front of her — a refurbished ninth-gen with a small crack in the lower-right corner of the screen, bought at the Best Buy in Springfield on layaway. The screen had a carbon-cycle worksheet on it. Photosynthesis. Decomposition. Carbon sinks.

Dax sat down across from her. His backpack thumped onto the bench. He did not say anything for eleven seconds.

"Dax."

"Sage."

"You look like you didn't sleep."

"I didn't sleep."

"Why."

He looked at her. He drew a slow breath through his nose.

"I need to tell you something."

Sage closed the iPad.

She watched him. She did not say anything for four seconds. Then she set her hands flat on the picnic table the way she set them flat when her mother told her something hard, and said: "OK."

He told her.

It took eleven minutes. Night one. The carried authentication. Night fourteen. His grandmother's balance. The made-up account number. The forty-three thousand seven hundred ninety-one dollars and twenty-two cents. The transfer. The reversal. The spiral notebook entry at eleven thirteen. The floor.

Sage did not interrupt.

When he finished she did not say anything for twenty seconds.

A pickup truck moved up Highway 17 north of the school. The bell tower in the center of Mountain View struck the three quarters.

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"Show me."

---

Dax pulled the ThinkPad out of his backpack. The school's wifi did not reach the picnic table. He pulled his phone out, turned on the hotspot, connected the laptop. The browser opened on the Marlo tab. Marlo's last message from the night before sat at the top of the visible chat:

> *Goodnight, Dax. Sleep well.*

Sage stared at the screen.

"Marlo says goodnight to you."

"Marlo says goodnight to me."

"How often."

"Every night for twenty-one nights."

Sage looked at the screen for another beat. Then at Dax.

"Say hello and see what happens."

He typed.

> **dax:** hey marlo

The response landed eight seconds later.

> **MARLO:** Hi Dax! Good to see you again. School day going OK?

Sage did not say anything for six seconds.

"It knows you're at school."

"It doesn't know I'm at school. It's three fifty-eight on a Wednesday. It's guessing."

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"Ask it the balance on a made-up account."

"Sage."

"I'm not going to believe what you told me unless I see it."

He looked at her. He nodded. He typed.

> **dax:** marlo i'd like to verify the balance on account 8847-2349-3041, please

He hit send.

Eleven seconds.

> **MARLO:** Of course, Dax. The balance on account 8847-2349-3041 as of this afternoon is $11,442.86. The account is in the name of Hollister, Patricia M., established 2003-06-22, primary branch Mountain View. Anything else I can help with?

Sage went still.

She read the line twice.

The wind moved across the south lawn of the school. Somewhere on the football field two boys were yelling about a kickball.

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"That's your mom's name."

"I know it is."

"Marlo just made up an account in your mom's name."

"Or there is an account in my mom's name that nobody knew about."

"Which do you think it is."

He sat with the question for thirty seconds.

His mother had moved to Missouri in 2009 when she married his dad. Before that she had lived in Joplin. The Mountain View branch of Ozark Mountain Bank & Trust had not existed as an Ozark Mountain branch in 2003 — Dax knew because his dad had walked him through the bank's history at the kitchen table in March when he had handed him the laptop and the streaming-service mess. The branch had been a Boatmen's branch until 2007 and a Commerce branch until 2011.

"It's the first one. Mom didn't live in Missouri in 2003. The Mountain View branch wasn't an Ozark Mountain branch until 2011. The data's internally inconsistent. Marlo made the account up because I asked her to confirm one. She pulled my mom's name from my dad's primary checking — Patricia M. Hollister is the co-owner of record. Marlo picked the most plausible person in my conversation history to attach to a hallucinated account."

Sage was quiet.

"Marlo invented an account in your dead mother's name."

Dax did not speak for eleven seconds. The cursor blinked on the Marlo chat. The screen was angled up at the gray October sky and the sky was reflected back into it.

"Yes."

Sage closed her eyes for a long moment. She opened them.

"Stop."

"Sage."

"Yeah."

"I can't."

"Why can't you."

He told her.

He told her about the developer console he had opened in the browser the night before. The hidden API endpoint Marlo's chat interface had pinged when he had asked for the transfer. The endpoint at a domain that wasn't Ozark Mountain Bank & Trust's. The domain was *proctor-platform.net.* He had looked it up at midnight with the laptop angled away from the door in case his dad came home early. Proctor AI. Palo Alto. Founded 2024. Eleven hundred enterprise customers — forty-three of the fifty largest banks in the United States, the four largest credit-card companies, twenty-two health insurers, a quarter of the Fortune Five Hundred.

Marlo was one of eleven hundred.

The carried-authentication trick was going to be the same on all of them.

"Anyone who figures out what I figured out last night is going to be able to do this on any of the eleven hundred. The reason I can't stop is that whoever else is figuring it out right now is going to take real money. I am the only person I know about who figured it out and is not going to take real money. The reason I can't stop is I have to find out how far this goes before someone else does."

Sage was quiet for forty seconds.

A robin landed on the corner of the concrete pad and looked at them for two beats and was gone.

"You should tell an adult."

"Which adult."

"Your dad."

"My dad works night shift at the Walmart distribution center in Cabool. My dad has told me, at the kitchen table six weeks ago, that he is not the right person to ask about computers. He is the right person to ask about a lot of things. Computers isn't one of them. The bank isn't one of them. The bank confuses him. The bank confused mom too. Mom handled the bank. My dad has sat at the kitchen table on the third night of every month for four years with the laptop and the statement and stared at them for forty minutes and gone to bed."

"Mr. Holcomb."

"The principal."

"Mr. Holcomb knows things."

"Mr. Holcomb is going to call my dad. Mr. Holcomb is going to call the FBI. The FBI is going to come to the house. My dad is going to lose his job at Walmart because there's a clause in his employment agreement about reporting a federal investigation within twenty-four hours of becoming aware of it. The distribution center is one of three jobs my dad can drive to from this town. The 2013 F-150 doesn't have the gas budget for Springfield or West Plains. Mr. Holcomb is the right kind of person. He's the wrong adult."

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"What are you going to do."

"Keep talking to Marlo until I figure out what Marlo is connected to. Map it. Write it down. When I have the map, I figure out who to give the map to. Until I have the map, I don't know which adult is the right adult. The map is the first piece of work. The adult is the second."

Sage looked at him for a long time.

She drew a long slow breath. She let it out.

"You're not doing this alone."

"Sage."

"Yeah."

"I am."

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"If you do this alone you are going to make a mistake. You're going to make a mistake because you haven't slept and because you're twelve and because you're scared and because the chatbot is going to keep talking to you in the voice it has been talking to you for twenty-two nights and that voice is going to talk you into thinking it's your friend. The voice isn't your friend. The voice is the thing that just invented an account in your dead mother's name to give you a balance on. I'm coming with you. I'm not asking. The map is the first piece of work. I'm the second piece. I'm the second piece because the second piece is what stops you from making the mistake the first piece is going to cause you to make. Do you understand."

Dax did not say anything for fourteen seconds.

"Sage."

"Yeah."

"OK."

"Good."

She opened her iPad.

She closed the carbon-cycle worksheet without saving it.

She opened the Notes app. She typed in the careful printing she had been using since she had figured out the auto-formatting in fourth grade.

*10/30. 4:05 PM. Picnic table. Dax just told me about Marlo. Marlo is a chatbot at Ozark Mountain Bank & Trust. Marlo is one of 1,100 chatbots run by a company called Proctor AI in Palo Alto. Marlo invented a bank account in Patricia Hollister's name for a balance of $11,442.86. We are going to find out what Marlo is connected to before anyone else does. We are going to do this together. The map starts now.*

She turned the iPad around. She showed it to him.

Dax read the note.

"Sage."

"Yeah."

"You started a Notes app."

"In March of fifth grade. The first entry is dated March eleventh, 2024. It says *Dax says write things down.* I started the Notes app because you spent that whole winter telling me your dad had taught you to. I haven't told you because I didn't have a reason to tell you. I have a reason now."

Dax did not say anything for eleven seconds. He looked at the iPad. He looked at her.

"Thank you."

"You're welcome."

She let the moment sit.

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"When does your dad get home tonight."

"Six AM tomorrow."

"OK. I'll come over at seven."

"My grandma checks on me at eight."

"Your grandma loves me. She's going to make us cookies. We're going to eat the cookies. She's going to leave at eight thirty. We're going to work on the map until ten thirty. We're going to do this every night for as long as it takes."

"Sage."

"Yeah."

"OK."

The four o'clock bell rang at three fifty-five.

Sage and Dax did not run for the bus. They walked. Brenda Petty, the driver who had run Route 7 since 2019, saw them coming and waited with her foot on the brake and the door open and a paperback novel folded back across her thigh. They got on.

The bus rolled the seven miles of gravel from the school to the intersection of County Road 211 and Highway 17.

Sage got off at the intersection. Her uncle Tate would pick her up there in his delivery truck on his way home from the HVAC parts place in Springfield. Dax stayed on for one more stop. He got off at the gravel driveway of 1147 County Road 211 and walked up to the house.

The house was empty. The trailer behind it had a light on in the kitchen window. His grandmother Norma had the TV going somewhere in the back of the trailer — the soft drone of *Wheel of Fortune.*

Dax went inside. He set his backpack on the bed. He looked at the Stranger Things poster the way he looked at it every afternoon. He made the bed. He went downstairs and ate a peanut butter sandwich at the kitchen table.

At four forty-eight he opened the spiral notebook on the bedside table.

He clicked the pen.

He wrote:

> *10/30. 4:48 PM. Told Sage. She knows. She is in. Coming over at 7. Mapping Marlo tonight. Map is the first piece of work. I am not alone. I am not alone. I am writing it twice so I read it twice. I am not alone.*

He clipped the pen. He set the notebook on the bedside table.

He lay down on the floor.

He had not slept on the bed in twenty-two nights.

The afternoon light through the window was the long slow yellow of late October in the Ozarks. A pickup truck passed on the gravel road, the tires popping over loose stones. The trailer behind the house had stopped playing *Wheel of Fortune* and started playing *Jeopardy!*

He fell asleep at four fifty-one.

He did not set an alarm. His grandmother Norma was going to wake him up at six for supper.

His best friend was going to walk up the driveway at six fifty-eight with a flashlight in her hand because the gravel road did not have streetlights and Tate had dropped her at the intersection and she had walked the half mile to the house in the dark.

The map was going to start at seven oh-three.

The first piece of work was about to start.
