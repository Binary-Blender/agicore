# Chapter 3: Mountain View

Carl Hollister was at the kitchen table at six oh-eight AM Saturday with a cup of coffee his mother Norma had carried across from the trailer at five fifty-five.

Carl was forty-one. Long-haul trucker between 2008 and 2018, Walmart distribution-center associate since 2019. Brown beard with three streaks of gray. The 2013 F-150 with two hundred forty-one thousand miles parked in the gravel turnaround on the east side of the house.

Above the kitchen sink, on the windowsill, a small framed photograph from the summer of 2009. Patricia in a simple white dress, holding a bouquet of black-eyed Susans she had picked herself from the field behind the church on the morning of the wedding. Carl in the suit he had bought at Sears in Springfield in 2007 for his cousin's funeral. The photograph had not moved in four years.

Dax came down at six fourteen. He walked across the kitchen and stood next to his father at the table without saying anything.

Carl looked up.

"Dax."

"Dad."

"Sleep OK."

"Yeah."

"Grandma's making biscuits."

"Good."

"Going into town. Need a starter for the truck. Hardware store and Sammy's. You up for it."

"Yeah."

"Get dressed."

Dax went upstairs.

He had not slept more than five hours on any of the four nights since the picnic table. The spiral notebook on the bedside table had fourteen pages of entries — Marlo's API endpoints, the proctor-platform.net subdomains, the list of eleven hundred customers Sage had pulled off the Proctor AI investor page. He had written every page with the laptop angled away from the door in case Carl came home early.

He pulled on a clean shirt. He came back down at six twenty-two.

Norma was at the kitchen table now.

---

Norma Hollister was seventy-one. Registered nurse at Mountain View General from 1976 to 2014. Type II diabetes. A small reading lamp with a magnifying glass on a flexible neck that she used to read her Bible at the kitchen table of the trailer every morning from five thirty to six.

One daughter-in-law dead in 2022. One son who had not been the same since. One grandson she had been watching for three years with the kind of professional attention thirty-eight years on a hospital floor gave a woman.

She had recognized Tuesday morning that Dax was not sleeping. She had not said anything yet. She was thinking about which four words she was going to use when she did.

She had biscuits in the oven. Bacon in the cast-iron pan. A small bowl of strawberry jam she had made in May of 2025 from strawberries her late husband Wendell had planted in 2018.

Dax sat at the kitchen table.

"Grandma."

"Dax."

"Bacon."

"Bacon."

"Thanks."

"You're welcome."

She did not say anything else for the rest of the breakfast. She watched him eat. She watched her son drink the coffee and read the paper. She watched the boy take seconds on the bacon without asking.

She would say something to him in the next four days. She had not yet decided when.

---

Carl and Dax drove the seven miles into Mountain View at seven oh-six in the F-150.

The truck had a small persistent miss in the number-three cylinder that Carl had been planning to address since August. The parts were thirty-eight dollars. Carl's budget across the past two months had been, on the operational reality of a tire he had replaced in September and a heating-system service call in early October, two dollars over rather than thirty-eight dollars under.

The miss was the miss. The truck drove.

They turned onto Main Street at seven twenty-one.

Main Street Mountain View on a Saturday morning in late October was a hundred forty cars parked diagonally on both sides and ninety-seven people moving between the diner, the hardware store, the Sonic, the post office, the Dollar General, the small grocery, and the Ozark Mountain Bank & Trust branch on the southeast corner of Main and Locust.

The bank branch was the branch Carl banked at.

The branch had a Marlo-powered drive-through ATM the bank had installed in August of Year Twelve — a small video screen above the keypad that displayed Marlo's chipper cartoon-bubble greeting at every approaching vehicle.

Carl drove past the bank without looking at it.

Dax, in the passenger seat, did not look either.

His stomach dropped about two inches anyway.

He did not say anything.

---

Carl parked at the hardware store.

Mountain View Hardware, founded 1947 by a man named Russell Pendergast, now run by Russell's grandson Hal, age forty-six, who had known every customer's name on sight since he had taken over the store from his father in 2011.

"Carl."

"Hal."

"Dax."

"Mr. Pendergast."

"Starter."

"Starter."

Hal walked them to the back of the store. The starter was on the third shelf from the floor, in a green box, forty-one ninety-nine. Carl paid in cash, counting the bills out slowly on the counter. Hal did not count along.

He looked at Dax.

"How's school."

"It's fine."

"You still doing the science fair this year."

"I haven't picked a topic."

"Pick a topic. You did well last year."

"Thank you, Mr. Pendergast."

"Carl. Tell your mother I said hello."

"I will."

"Tell her Wendell would be proud of the boy."

Carl did not say anything for four seconds.

"I will tell her."

They walked out.

---

Sammy's Diner was between the hardware store and the post office.

Sammy's had been Sammy's since 1959, run now by Sammy's granddaughter Linda Sammartino since 2017 when Sammy died at eighty-three.

Carl and Dax sat in the booth at the front window. The small tabletop jukebox had not worked since 1991. It still displayed the same B-side — Little Richard, *Tutti Frutti* — that had been the last selection some customer Linda did not remember had punched in thirty-six years ago.

Linda brought Carl a coffee and Dax an orange juice without asking.

"Carl. Dax."

"Linda."

"Miss Linda."

"Pancakes for the boy. Eggs over medium and bacon for Carl."

"Linda."

"Yeah."

"I'll have the same as the boy this morning."

Linda raised her eyebrows.

"Carl Hollister."

"Yeah."

"You have not ordered pancakes at this diner since 2019."

"I'm having pancakes today."

"Pancakes."

"Pancakes."

She walked away.

Dax looked at his father.

"Dad."

"Yeah."

"Pancakes."

"Pancakes."

"Why pancakes."

Carl did not say anything for six seconds. He set the coffee down. He looked out the window at the diagonal parking on Main.

"Your mother used to make pancakes on Saturdays."

"I know she did."

"Your mother died on a Tuesday in November of 2022. The last meal she cooked for us was on the Saturday before. The meal was pancakes."

"I remember."

"I have not been able to eat a pancake since."

A beat.

"Why today."

Carl did not say anything for eleven seconds. He looked at Dax.

"I have been working with a counselor at the VA since August. She has been telling me I should do small things I have not been able to do since your mother died. She told me to pick one this week. I picked pancakes. I am eating pancakes today because pancakes are the thing I am picking this week. Next week I will pick another thing."

"Dad."

"Yeah."

"I didn't know you were going to the VA."

"I have not told you because I was not yet sure if it was going to help. I am, this Saturday, going to tell you about it because the counselor told me on Wednesday I should start telling you about it. She told me the not-telling was harder on the kid than the telling would be. I have been thinking about that for three days. The pancakes today are the telling too."

"Dad."

"Yeah."

"Thank you."

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"You are welcome."

The pancakes arrived at seven forty-eight.

Carl ate three across nineteen minutes.

He did not cry at the diner. Dax did not cry either. They paid. They left. They drove home.

---

In the truck on the way home Carl said: "Dax."

"Yeah."

"You haven't been sleeping."

Dax did not say anything for six seconds.

"How did you know."

"Your grandmother told me Friday night when I came home from Cabool at six forty-five AM. She told me you have been on the floor of your bedroom every morning when she has come in to check on you at six fifteen. She told me you have not been on the bed."

"Dad."

"Yeah."

"I am working on something."

"What kind of something."

Dax did not say anything for eleven seconds. The truck rolled over the cattle guard at the entrance to County Road 211. Highway 17 fell away behind them in the rearview.

"It's something I am still mapping. When I have it mapped I will tell you. I am going to tell you the same way you just told me about the VA. I'm going to tell you when I'm ready. The not-telling is not because I don't want to tell you. The not-telling is because I do not yet know what the thing is. When I know the thing, I'll tell you the thing."

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"Are you in trouble."

"No."

"Is Sage with you on this."

"Yes."

Carl did not say anything for four seconds. He nodded once at the windshield.

"OK."

"Dad."

"Yeah."

"Thank you."

"Tell me when you know."

"I will."

Carl drove the rest of the way home in silence. The miss in the number-three cylinder was the miss in the number-three cylinder. The starter was on the bench seat between them. The October light came at them through the maples on the south side of the road in long horizontal bars.

---

Carl installed the starter that afternoon.

Dax helped. He handed his father the seventeen-millimeter socket when his father asked for it. He handed his father the small extension. He held the trouble light at the angle his father had taught him to hold it in the summer of 2024 when he had been ten and his father had been teaching him brake pads.

Carl did not say anything for the two hours and eleven minutes the install took except for the names of the tools. Dax did not say anything either.

The starter was in at three twelve.

The truck started on the first crank.

The miss in the number-three cylinder was unchanged.

Carl looked at the engine for thirty seconds.

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"The miss is the miss. The miss is not the starter."

"I know it isn't."

"The miss is going to wait until I have the budget."

"What's the budget."

"Plugs and wires. Eighty-four dollars at the parts store on Elida. I'll get them after the next paycheck."

"OK."

Carl closed the hood. He wiped his hands on the shop rag.

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"Go inside. Take a nap."

"I am OK."

"Take a nap anyway. Whatever you're working on tonight is going to need you rested. Sage will be here at seven."

"Did Sage tell you she was coming."

"Sage's mother called me at noon. She told me Sage was coming over at seven and she was not coming over to do schoolwork. She did not tell me what Sage was coming over to do. She told me she trusted you and Sage to do whatever you were doing. I told her I trusted you and Sage too. Go take a nap."

Dax did not say anything for eleven seconds.

"Dad."

"Yeah."

"I love you."

Carl did not say anything for eight seconds. He looked at his son.

"I love you too."

Dax walked up the porch and into the house. He went upstairs.

He took the nap.

He took it on the bed.

He had not slept on the bed in twenty-five nights.

He slept for two hours and thirty-eight minutes.

He woke at five fifty-six.

Norma had brought meatloaf across from the trailer.

Sage was due at six fifty-eight.

The map was at seven oh-three.

---

In the gravel turnaround on the east side of the house, Carl leaned against the open hood of the truck for another ninety seconds after Dax had gone inside.

He looked at the small framed wedding photograph through the kitchen window. Patricia in the white dress. The black-eyed Susans in her hands. The 2009 summer light.

He said, out loud, to the photograph, the way he had said it every Saturday afternoon since November of 2022: "Patty. The boy is going to be OK."

He closed the hood. He walked into the house.

The map was at seven oh-three.
