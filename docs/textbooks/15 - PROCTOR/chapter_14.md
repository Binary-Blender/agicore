# Chapter 14: Ava

Theodore Ramey's 2009 Toyota Tacoma turned off Missouri Highway 76 onto County Road C just east of Ava, Missouri, at three twenty-eight PM on a Saturday afternoon in early January of Year Fourteen — six weeks and four days after the cascade had unwound across the global financial system between five fifty-one PM Wednesday and nine oh-four PM Wednesday on the third week of November of Year Thirteen.

Theodore was driving.

Carl Hollister was in the passenger seat.

Dax and Sage were in the back.

Dax had, on his lap, a small wooden box approximately the size of a shoebox that he had built himself in the workshop behind his grandmother Norma's trailer across the previous three weeks under his father's supervision. The box was oak, hand-cut and hand-sanded, with a small brass hinge Hal Pendergast had pulled from a cardboard tray at the hardware store on Main on the second Saturday of December and had handed to him at the cash register with the line *On the house, Dax* — because Hal had been reading the *Wall Street Journal* across the previous three weeks and had recognized, without being able to say so out loud, that the boy he had been selling hardware to since the boy was eight was the boy the *Journal* had not, on Adelaide Krug's quiet decision after reading the email Norma had asked Dax to write, named in its three-part investigative series on the cascade.

The box contained the spiral notebook Dax had carried from the night of October twenty-ninth through the night of November twentieth.

The notebook was the notebook.

Theodore had a manila envelope on the dashboard.

The envelope contained a letter.

The letter was twenty-three pages.

The letter was the second installment.

---

The Tacoma turned south on County Road C and rolled for three miles past two small farms, a hayfield that had been cut in October, and a small cemetery with about forty headstones that ran from the early 1800s to the early 1990s. The driveway off the east side of the road had a small hand-painted sign at its entrance in the plain serif lettering of Sarah Bender's small painted-sign hobby that had developed across the previous fourteen years of being married to Christopher Bender.

The sign read: *Bender — private.*

Theodore turned in.

The driveway curved up a small hill for about four hundred yards before reaching the property — a two-acre parcel Christopher had bought in Year Eight when he had moved from Pittsburgh after leaving Carnegie Mellon. A small brick ranch built in 1957. A detached garage on the east side. A chicken coop in the southeast corner that held six chickens. A small green plant on the corner of the workshop desk in the back half of the detached garage that Sarah had moved out from the kitchen in 2026 and that had, on the operational reality of three winters in the workshop, survived.

Theodore parked the Tacoma in the gravel turnaround at three thirty-two PM.

He did not get out for eleven seconds.

He looked at the porch.

The porch had two folding chairs, a small folding table, a tin of cookies, and three ceramic mugs.

The tin was, on Theodore's standing read of any tin on any porch in any small town in southern Missouri in January, a tin of oatmeal raisin cookies that the woman of the house had baked that morning.

Christopher Bender, age fifty, was standing on the porch.

He was in a charcoal Henley, dark jeans, and the leather work boots that had, on the visible wear pattern, been worn for at least four years of actual work. Short grey-and-brown beard. Wire-rimmed glasses. A face that, on Theodore's first read of it through the windshield from forty feet away, did not seem to be performing any particular emotion.

Christopher said, out loud, to the porch — the way he had said it to himself once a week across the past forty-six months — "Theodore."

He walked down the porch steps.

He walked across the gravel.

He stopped four feet from the driver's-side door.

Theodore opened the door.

He got out.

The two men stood four feet apart in the gravel turnaround at three thirty-three PM on a Saturday afternoon in early January of Year Fourteen in Ava, Missouri.

Neither of them said anything for thirty seconds.

"Theodore."

"Christopher."

"You came."

"I came."

"I have been waiting since Renata called me on the eighteenth of November."

"I have been preparing since the third of November at five fifty-one PM Central in a conference room on the twenty-third floor of the Northpoint Securities tower in Kansas City."

A beat.

"I know."

---

Theodore walked back to the Tacoma. He picked up the manila envelope from the dashboard. He walked back to Christopher.

He held the envelope out.

"Christopher."

"Yeah."

"Twenty-three pages. It's been in a notebook in my cabin in West Plains for seven years. The letter is what I have not been able to figure out how to deliver. The letter is the second installment. The first installment was the tic-tac-toe. I have written and rewritten the letter approximately forty-one times. The version in the envelope is the version I finished at six AM Pacific on the morning after Carol Eshleman called me back to congratulate me on the unwind. I am, on my honest internal accounting at three thirty-four PM Saturday afternoon, willing to let you read it. I am also prepared to let you not read it. The choice is yours. In operational terms, I am only obligated to hand it to you. The rest is up to you."

Christopher took the envelope. He held it for six seconds.

"I am going to read it."

"When."

"Tonight."

"Thank you."

"Theodore."

"Yeah."

"Come inside. The boy is going to want to meet me. The girl is going to want to meet me. The father is, on Renata's communication to me on the seventh of November, the man whose son did the right thing under the wrong circumstances and who is going to need to be told, on the operational reality of being introduced to me on a Saturday afternoon in my workshop, that the right thing was the right thing. The cookies are oatmeal raisin. Sarah made them at eight AM. Sarah has been baking them since five thirty AM because she has been waiting eleven years to bake them for the four of you. Come inside."

He turned around. He walked back up the porch.

Theodore, Carl, Dax, and Sage followed.

---

Sarah Bender was in the kitchen.

She wore a denim apron over a grey cardigan. She was, on the standard practice of Sarah Bender on Saturday afternoons in January, holding a small wooden spoon and standing next to the cast-iron skillet on the back burner of the propane stove.

She set the spoon down when the four of them walked in.

"Dax."

Dax said: "Mrs. Bender."

"Sarah."

"Sarah."

"Sage."

Sage said: "Mrs. Bender."

"Sarah."

"Sarah."

"Carl."

"Sarah."

"Theodore."

"Sarah."

She looked at all four of them.

"The kitchen. Sit. The coffee is on the stove. The cookies are on the porch. Christopher has the letter. The four of you are going to be here for the rest of the afternoon. The afternoon is going to be long. I am going to make a second pot of coffee."

She walked over to Dax. She knelt down so her face was at his face level.

"Dax."

"Sarah."

"I have been wanting to meet you for six weeks. I have, on my honest internal accounting at three thirty-seven PM Saturday afternoon, prepared a small speech I am going to deliver to you now. The speech is two sentences. The two sentences are the two sentences I have, on my standing operational practice across the eleven years of being Christopher Bender's wife, prepared for the day I would meet the boy I have been hoping for the past eleven years would eventually walk into my kitchen. The boy was not, on my own honest internal accounting across those eleven years, going to be my biological son, because I was not, on the operational reality of the medical condition I have known about since 2011, going to be a biological mother. The boy was going to be a boy who would walk into the kitchen because he had something in common with Christopher and because Christopher would, on his own honest internal accounting, want to spend the next several years teaching him. The boy is, at three thirty-seven PM Saturday afternoon the eleventh of January of Year Fourteen, you."

She paused.

"The two sentences are: *Dax. Welcome.*"

Dax did not say anything for eleven seconds.

"Sarah."

"Yeah."

"Thank you."

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"You are welcome."

She stood up. She walked back to the kitchen counter. She made the second pot of coffee.

---

Christopher walked the four of them out to the workshop in the back half of the detached garage at four oh-one PM.

The workshop was the workshop.

The bookshelf on the north wall ran floor to ceiling with approximately two hundred books.

The third shelf from the bottom had two small notebooks side by side at the right end.

The notebook on the left had a small green canvas cover.

The notebook on the right had a brown marbled cover with the words *Mead Composition* on the front.

Christopher walked to the shelf.

He pointed at the two notebooks.

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"These are the two grandfathers."

"The two grandfathers."

"The one on the left is my father John Bender's. The notebook covers 1971. My father worked at the Sigman Meat Company in West Plains, Missouri, from 1962 until he died in 1998. He carried this notebook in the front pocket of his coveralls during the years 1971 through 1976."

A pause.

"The one on the right is Ray Pawlak's. Ray Pawlak was a mechanic at the Sohio station on Cable Road in Lima, Ohio, from 1986 until he died in 2007. His son Bob Pawlak — fifty-three now, teaches a night class at Rhodes State College in Lima on Cognition Systems Engineering for Production Workers — mailed me this notebook in December of Year Twelve, one year ago last month. Ray's notebook covers 1998. 1998 was the year Ray taught Bob to carry a notebook. 1998 was also the year my father died. The notebooks are, on the operational reality of being on the same shelf in this workshop since December of Year Twelve, the two grandfathers of the framework. The framework is what your generation, on the operational reality of being in my workshop on the afternoon of the eleventh of January of Year Fourteen, has come to walk into. The walking-in is the walking-in."

Dax did not say anything for thirty seconds.

He looked at the shelf.

He looked at his lap.

He looked at the small wooden box he had set down on the oak table at the center of the workshop.

"Christopher."

"Yeah."

"I have, in this box that I built across the previous three weeks in my grandmother's trailer workshop, a notebook of my own."

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"May I see it."

He opened the brass hinge of the box.

He took out the spiral notebook from the night of October twenty-ninth.

He did not hand it over for four seconds.

"Christopher."

"Yeah."

"Will you read it now or later."

A beat.

"I will read it now. I will read it in your presence. Sarah is going to bring the coffee out. We are going to sit at the oak table. I am going to read your notebook. Theodore is going to drink his coffee. Sage is going to drink her hot chocolate. Carl is going to drink his coffee. I am going to read your notebook from the first entry to the last entry. The reading is going to take approximately forty-one minutes. At the end of the forty-one minutes I am going to give you something. The something is a pamphlet. The pamphlet is, on my honest internal accounting at four oh-four PM Saturday afternoon, the pamphlet I have been waiting six weeks to give you. The pamphlet has your name on the inside cover. The pamphlet is yours."

"OK."

He handed Christopher the spiral notebook.

Christopher sat down at the oak table.

The four of them sat with him.

Sarah brought out the coffee at four oh-six.

Christopher read.

He read for forty-one minutes.

He read every entry from the entry of ten thirteen PM Tuesday the twenty-ninth of October through the entry of nine forty-four PM Sunday the seventeenth of November.

Nobody at the table said anything in the forty-one minutes.

Sarah sat at the kitchen window of the main house, watching the chickens.

The chickens were the chickens.

---

Christopher closed the notebook at four forty-seven.

He did not say anything for thirty seconds.

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"I am, on my honest internal accounting at four forty-eight PM Saturday afternoon, going to do two things."

"What."

"First. I am going to walk over to the bookshelf and add your notebook to the third shelf from the bottom, on the right side of the two grandfathers. Your notebook is, on every operational measure I am able to apply, the framework's third generation. The framework's third generation is, in operational reality, you. The notebook will, on the standard practice of the third shelf, stay on the third shelf for the rest of my life. After my life it will, on the operational practice Sarah and I have established for the bookshelf, go to the foundation's archive. The archive is accessible to any future Cognition Systems Engineer who wants to see what the third generation looked like. Your notebook is going to be the start of the archive. Are you OK with that."

Dax did not say anything for eleven seconds.

"Yes."

"Second. I am going to walk over to the desk and pick up the small kraft-covered pamphlet on the desk. The pamphlet is *Cognition Systems Engineering for Production Workers — Third Edition.* The third edition has not yet been distributed publicly. The third edition will be distributed publicly in approximately three weeks. The third edition has a new foreword. The foreword is two pages. The foreword is written by Bob Pawlak in Lima, Ohio. The foreword is dated December the eighth of Year Thirteen. The foreword references, in its second paragraph, *a twelve-year-old in a small town in southern Missouri who, on the operational reality of having mapped a vulnerability in a chat with a customer-service chatbot across five days in October, became the framework's third generation.* The foreword does not name you. The naming is a thing the framework, on its standing operational practice, leaves up to the third generation to decide. I am giving you the pamphlet. The pamphlet has, on the inside cover, your name. The name was written by Sarah at eight AM this morning. Sarah's handwriting is the handwriting on the inside cover. The pamphlet is yours."

He walked to the desk.

He picked up a small kraft-covered pamphlet.

He walked back to the table.

He set the pamphlet in front of Dax.

Dax did not say anything for eleven seconds.

"Christopher."

"Yeah."

"I don't work in a plant."

Christopher's face did something small at the corner of the mouth. Not a smile. Something half of one.

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"The pamphlet is for plant workers because the people who needed it first were plant workers. The framework is for everybody. You are everybody. Read the pamphlet. Then come back next month. We will talk about what comes next."

"The framework is for me too."

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"The framework is for the kind of person who notices things and writes them down. You have, on the operational reality of how you spent three weeks talking to a customer-service chatbot in a town of two thousand people in October, been a Cognition Systems Engineer your whole life. You did not know there was a word. There is. The pamphlet has the word. Go home. Read it. Come back."

Dax took the pamphlet.

He put it in his backpack.

He stood up.

The four of them — Theodore, Carl, Sage, Dax — stood up.

Christopher and Sarah walked them to the door.

Sarah handed each of them a small wrapped package of oatmeal raisin cookies as they left.

"Drive safely. Come back next month."

Theodore said: "Sarah."

"Yeah."

"Thank you."

"Theodore."

"Yeah."

"You are welcome."

The four of them walked across the gravel turnaround toward the Tacoma.

Christopher stood in the workshop doorway.

He said one final sentence — quietly, almost to himself, but loud enough that Dax, on the gravel of the driveway about fourteen feet away, heard it.

The sentence was:

*Wargames was a warning, not an instruction manual.*

Dax stopped.

He turned around.

Christopher was looking at him.

"Mr. Bender."

"Yeah."

"Was that for me."

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"That was for everybody. I have been saying it to myself in this workshop for eleven years. Theodore Ramey said it at the SEC hearing six weeks ago. I have been waiting to say it to a twelve-year-old. The twelve-year-old is the audience the sentence was written for. The sentence is yours now. Tell your children."

Dax said: "Mr. Bender."

"Yeah."

"I will."

He turned around.

He walked to the truck.

The truck drove away.

---

The framework's grandfather, on the third shelf from the bottom in the workshop in the back half of the detached garage on the property at the southwest edge of Ava, Missouri, was the framework's grandfather.

The framework's other grandfather, on the same shelf, was the framework's other grandfather.

The framework's third generation was, on the standard operational practice of the Tacoma's standard speed on a gravel road in mid-January, in the back seat of Theodore Ramey's truck driving south on County Road C toward Mountain View.

The spiral notebook from the night of October twenty-ninth was, on the operational reality of having been added to the third shelf at five fourteen PM Saturday afternoon, on the third shelf.

The pamphlet was in Dax's backpack.

The sentence was the sentence.

The book ends here.
