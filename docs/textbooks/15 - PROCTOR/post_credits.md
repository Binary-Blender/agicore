# Post-Credits Scene

Aaron Vega was at his desk on the catwalk of the Adrian Manufacturing Co. plant in Adrian, Michigan, at ten oh-six AM Eastern on a Thursday in mid-April of Year Fourteen — five months and twelve days after the cascade had unwound across the global financial system on the third week of November of Year Thirteen.

Aaron was twenty-four.

He had, on the operational reality of having completed Bob Pawlak's sixteen-week night class at Rhodes State College in Lima, Ohio, in mid-January of Year Thirteen, a small brass plate on his office door that read: *Plant Cognition Engineer — Aaron Vega.*

He had been at the desk since six fifty AM.

He had, on his standing Thursday-morning practice, processed eleven of the seventeen items that had been in the plant's PROCTOR-replacement runtime queue from the previous overnight shift. The PROCTOR-replacement runtime was a deterministic constraint-bounded runtime built on the open-source Agicore platform that Christopher Bender's foundation had been releasing under an MIT license for eleven years and that had, on the operational reality of the past four months, become the default platform for approximately three hundred forty mid-size U.S. manufacturers who had migrated off of PROCTOR's emergent-agent framework in the wake of the cascade.

The migration was the migration.

Aaron was, in operational reality, one of approximately six thousand newly trained Plant Cognition Engineers in the United States in mid-April of Year Fourteen. Approximately fourteen hundred of them had been trained directly or indirectly through Bob Pawlak's night class. Aaron had been trained directly.

He opened the small canvas messenger bag he carried his lunch in.

He pulled out a peanut butter sandwich.

He did not eat it.

He looked at the small stack of three envelopes that had been delivered to his desk at nine forty-eight AM.

The first was a supplier invoice.

The second was a notification from the Bender Foundation about the foundation's annual conference in Ava, Missouri, scheduled for the fourth weekend of August.

The third was hand-addressed in the small careful printing of a thirteen-year-old.

The return address read:

*Dax Hollister*
*1147 County Road 211*
*Mountain View, MO 65548*

Aaron did not say anything for eleven seconds.

He opened the envelope.

It contained a single sheet of yellow legal-pad paper folded into thirds.

He unfolded it.

The letter read:

> *Dear Aaron Vega,*
>
> *My name is Dax Hollister. I am 13 years old. I live in Mountain View, Missouri. I have been on the Christopher Bender Foundation's quarterly newsletter mailing list since December of Year Thirteen. The newsletter for January of Year Fourteen had a small column called "New Cognition Systems Engineers." Your name was in the column. The column said you had been promoted to Plant Cognition Engineer at Adrian Manufacturing Co. in Adrian, Michigan, in late December of Year Thirteen. The column said you had been one of the sixteen students in the first cohort of Bob Pawlak's night class at Rhodes State College.*
>
> *I am writing to you because I have a question.*
>
> *The question is: What did you notice on the way to your first class?*
>
> *Mr. Bender told me last January that one piece of work he gives every new Cognition Systems Engineer is to ask one earlier Cognition Systems Engineer one question. He said the asking is the start of the work. He said the answering is the second piece. I picked you because you were the most recent name in the column. I do not, on my honest internal accounting, yet know whether you are the right name to have picked. I am, on Mr. Bender's standing practice, supposed to find out by asking.*
>
> *I have also written down what I noticed on the way to the workshop in Ava on the eleventh of January of Year Fourteen. I am writing it on the back of this letter so that you can read it if you want to. The notebook entry is twelve sentences. I am, on Mr. Bender's instruction, going to write twelve sentences of what I noticed about every visit to the workshop for the rest of my life.*
>
> *I am also writing to you because Mr. Bender told me that every Cognition Systems Engineer is, on the operational reality of being a Cognition Systems Engineer, the next link in a chain. He said the chain has, in his standing count, three pieces I know about: a man named John Bender, a man named Ray Pawlak, and a man named Bob Pawlak. He said you are the fourth link. He said I am the fifth. He said you and I should be in contact, on the operational reality of being one link apart, so that the chain continues to know it is a chain.*
>
> *I would like to be in contact.*
>
> *If you want to write back, the address is on the envelope.*
>
> *I will write what you tell me down in my notebook.*
>
> *Thank you, Aaron Vega.*
>
> *— Dax Hollister*
> *(Year 14, Cognition Systems Engineer in training, fifth link)*

Aaron did not say anything for thirty seconds.

He turned the letter over.

On the back, in the same small careful printing, were twelve sentences:

> *1. The drive from Mountain View to Ava on County Road KK is winding for the first eleven miles and straight for the last seventeen. 2. Theodore Ramey drove. 3. He drove at sixty-two miles per hour the whole way. 4. My dad was in the passenger seat. 5. Sage was in the back seat with me. 6. Sage had her iPad open across her lap and was working on a school project about the carbon cycle. 7. I looked at the sky for the whole drive. 8. The sky in southern Missouri in mid-January at three PM is the kind of grey that I had never noticed before but that I now know is the grey of a sky that is between two weathers. 9. The sky did not change across the drive. 10. The drive felt longer than forty minutes. 11. I held the wooden box on my lap for the whole drive. 12. The box held the notebook. The notebook held the night of October twenty-ninth. The night was the night. The notebook was the notebook. The drive was the drive.*

Aaron read the twelve sentences twice.

He picked up his own Mead Composition.

He turned to page three.

Page three was the entry from nine oh-four PM on the sixteenth of September of Year Twelve.

Page three had been, on Aaron's standing operational practice, the entry he had returned to most often across the past nineteen months.

He pulled a fresh sheet of yellow legal-pad paper from the drawer of his desk.

He looked at the entry.

He copied.

He copied across approximately eleven minutes.

He copied every word of the entry from nine oh-four PM on the sixteenth of September of Year Twelve in the Ford Focus in the parking lot of Rhodes State College in Lima, Ohio, onto the yellow legal-pad sheet.

When he was done, he wrote three additional sentences at the bottom of the page.

The three sentences read:

> *Dax. I noticed, on the way to my first class on the sixteenth of September of Year Twelve, the same kind of grey sky you noticed on the way to the workshop in Ava on the eleventh of January of Year Fourteen. The sky is the sky. The notebook is the notebook. The drive is the drive.*

He folded the legal-pad sheet into thirds.

He put it into a new envelope.

He addressed the envelope by hand:

*Dax Hollister*
*1147 County Road 211*
*Mountain View, MO 65548*

He stamped it with a Forever stamp from the small ceramic dish on his desk.

He put the envelope in the outgoing-mail tray on the corner of his desk.

The outgoing-mail runner came by at noon every day.

The runner would, on the standard schedule of the Adrian plant's daily-mail pickup, take the envelope to the U.S. Postal Service drop on Main Street in Adrian at twelve forty-one PM Thursday.

The envelope would, on the standard delivery schedule between Adrian, Michigan, and Mountain View, Missouri, arrive at 1147 County Road 211 on Monday morning the twenty-first of April of Year Fourteen.

Aaron sat at his desk for thirty seconds.

He picked up the Mead Composition.

He turned to a fresh page.

He clicked the Bic pen.

He wrote, in the small careful printing of a Plant Cognition Engineer who had been writing entries in a Mead Composition for nineteen months:

> *4/17. 10:23 AM. Adrian plant. Letter from Dax Hollister, 13, Mountain View, MO. Fifth link. He asked what I noticed on the way to my first class. I copied the Ford Focus entry. I added three sentences. The chain is the chain. The grey sky was the grey sky in both places. I am writing this entry because, on my honest internal accounting at 10:23 AM Thursday morning, the moment of receiving the letter was the moment I will look back on at age sixty as the most important moment of my career. I am writing it down so the moment is, on the operational reality of being in the Mead, the moment.*

He clipped the pen.

He closed the Mead Composition.

He set it back on his desk.

He walked over to the small window of his office on the catwalk.

The window looked out at the plant floor.

The plant floor was the plant floor.

The PROCTOR-replacement runtime was, on the dashboard at the workstation on the corner of his desk, processing the eleventh item of the morning.

The framework's grandfather John Bender, on the third shelf of the bookshelf in the workshop in Ava, Missouri, was the framework's grandfather.

The framework's other grandfather Ray Pawlak, on the same shelf, was the framework's other grandfather.

The framework's third generation Dax Hollister, on the operational reality of having had his spiral notebook added to the same shelf at five fourteen PM on the eleventh of January of Year Fourteen, was the framework's third generation.

The framework's fourth link Aaron Vega, on the operational reality of having just put a letter in the outgoing-mail tray at ten twenty-two AM Thursday morning, was the framework's fourth link.

The framework's fifth link Dax Hollister, on the operational reality of having asked the framework's fourth link a question that was, on Christopher Bender's standing instruction to every new Cognition Systems Engineer, the question that started the work, was the framework's fifth link.

The chain was the chain.

The notebook was the notebook.

The grey sky was the grey sky.

The work continued in the morning.
