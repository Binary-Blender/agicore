# Chapter 12: The Terminal

The drive from Ramey's cabin to Kansas City was supposed to take four hours.

It took four hours and seventeen minutes.

The seventeen extra minutes came from a stop at the Casey's in Lebanon at three forty-eight PM, where Tate filled the Transit's tank, where Dax used the bathroom, where Sage bought a bag of pretzels and two more bottles of water, and where Theodore Ramey — on his own quiet operational practice when he had four hours to think about what he was about to do — walked the perimeter of the gas station's parking lot for eleven minutes with his head down and his hands in the pockets of his Carhartt jacket and his face set in the kind of expression Tate had not yet been able to read at twenty-six.

Tate had not asked Theodore what he was thinking.

Tate had, on his own quiet operational practice when he had been driving a delivery van with two twelve-year-olds and a sixty-three-year-old former Carnegie Mellon professor for three hours, learned that Theodore would tell him what Theodore wanted to tell him when Theodore wanted to tell him.

Theodore had come back to the truck at three fifty-nine PM.

He had said: "Tate."

Tate had said: "Yes sir."

Theodore had said: "Drive."

Tate had driven.

---

The Transit and the green Tacoma crossed the city limits of Kansas City, Missouri, at five thirty-two PM Central Standard Time on Wednesday.

The sky west of the city was a long pale band of late-November orange behind a low ceiling of gray cloud. The downtown towers were lit up against the cloud. The Liberty Memorial obelisk was lit too — the small permanent flame on top moving in the wind that had come up off the river.

The Tacoma led. Theodore was at the wheel.

The Transit followed. Tate was at the wheel.

In the back of the Transit, Dax had the laptop open on his lap. The chat with PROCTOR Incidents had been quiet for the past forty minutes because Renata had been on the call with Suresh and with Priya and with the Palo Alto incident-response room and had been, on her own honest accounting, too busy to type.

The cross-customer aggregate notional on the *Live Activity* banner read $138.4B.

Tokyo open: twenty-eight minutes.

Sage was in the front passenger seat. She had been watching her phone since four PM Central, scrolling Reuters and Bloomberg and the BBC. The BBC chyron read at five thirty-three PM: *SEC Chair Carol Eshleman Lands in Missouri; Destination Unknown.*

Sage said: "Tate."

Tate said: "Yeah."

She said: "She's in Springfield."

Tate said: "Sage."

"Yeah."

"How do you know."

"The plane lands at Springfield Branson at five oh-eight Central. I tracked the SEC's plane tail-number obscurity protocol on the FAA's public-access system on the iPad while we were at the Casey's. The plane has a generic government call sign. The plane filed its flight plan at six fifty-five AM Eastern. The destination was Springfield. The plane landed twenty-five minutes ago. The SEC chair is in an SUV heading south on 65 toward Ozark."

Tate said: "Sage."

"Yeah."

"How are you tracking the SUV."

"I'm not tracking the SUV. I'm reading the FAA public-access system and the BBC. The BBC has a reporter in Springfield. The reporter saw the SUV. The reporter doesn't know where it's going. The reporter posted a photo of the SUV's plates on the BBC's live blog at five twenty-eight Central. The plates are SEC government plates. The plates are visible. The plates are the plates."

Tate said: "Sage."

"Yeah."

"You're going to do this for a living."

Sage said: "I memorize footnotes."

Tate said: "OK."

---

The Tacoma turned right onto Main Street at five thirty-six PM.

The Transit followed.

Main Street between Eighth and Ninth on a Wednesday evening in November was nearly empty — the late commuters had cleared, the early dinner crowd had not yet arrived. The buildings on both sides of Main were dark above the second floor.

The Tacoma slowed.

The Tacoma parked at the curb in front of a twenty-eight-story tower on the east side of the street with a brass plaque next to the revolving lobby door that read, even in the gathering November dusk, *Northpoint Securities — One Two Hundred Main Street.*

The Transit parked behind the Tacoma at five thirty-eight PM.

Theodore got out of the Tacoma. He walked back to the Transit's driver-side window.

"Tate."

"Yes sir."

"The truck stays here. You stay with the truck. If anyone walks up to the truck — anyone — you do not engage. You start the truck. You drive. You go around the block. You come back. You repeat. Do not stop. Do not get out. The kids and Dax's father and I will be back down at approximately ten PM. If we are not back down by ten PM, you call the number I am about to give you on the satellite phone. The number is Carol Eshleman's direct line at the SEC. You tell whoever answers that you are Tate Whitehorse and that Theodore Ramey told you to call. They will tell you what to do. Do you understand."

Tate said: "Yes sir."

Theodore handed him a folded piece of paper.

Tate unfolded it.

The paper had a phone number written in pencil in careful printing.

Tate folded it back.

He put it in the breast pocket of his SPRINGFIELD HVAC T-shirt.

He said: "Theodore."

Theodore said: "Yeah."

Tate said: "Bring the kids back."

Theodore said: "I'm going to."

He turned to the back of the Transit.

He said: "Dax. Sage. Out."

They got out.

Carl was already out of the Tacoma — he had been in the passenger seat of Theodore's truck across the drive. He came around the front of the Tacoma now and stood with the kids on the curb.

The four of them — Theodore, Carl, Sage, Dax — walked across the sidewalk and into the revolving lobby door of Northpoint Securities at five thirty-nine PM Central Standard Time on Wednesday, November twentieth, of Year Thirteen.

---

The lobby was a single-story marble-and-glass space approximately sixty feet wide and forty feet deep with a small security desk at the back wall, a bank of four elevators on the right side, and a large brass plaque next to the security desk that read, in the standard institutional lettering of corporate-tower lobbies built in the early 2000s: *Northpoint Securities — A Family-Owned Broker-Dealer Since 1947.*

Vernita Park was at the security desk.

Vernita was forty-three. She had been on the night-security staff at Northpoint since 2022. Before that she had been a software engineer at a regional fintech firm in Cleveland from 2014 to 2022. Before that she had been a graduate student in computer science at Carnegie Mellon from 2012 to 2014.

She had, on her desk, a small framed photograph that was the only piece of decoration she had brought from home.

The photograph showed a younger Vernita in her hooded master's-degree academic regalia at her graduation in May of 2014, standing next to Dr. Theodore Ramey, both of them smiling at the camera.

Vernita looked up when the four of them walked in.

"Dr. Ramey."

"Vernita."

"You're early."

"I'm six minutes early."

"Six minutes is early."

Theodore said: "Vernita."

"Yeah."

"Carl Hollister. Sage Whitehorse. Dax Hollister. The truck is at the curb. The driver — Tate Whitehorse — is staying in the truck. He has your direct number in case we are not back at ten PM."

Vernita said: "Dr. Ramey."

"Yeah."

"I have signed all four of you in on the visitor log as *technical-consultation visitors* under my own access pass. The log will show that you arrived at five thirty-nine PM and that you were escorted by me. The log will be reviewed on Friday morning by Northpoint's facilities-security director, a man named Wesley Hammond, who is my direct supervisor. Wesley has, on the operational reality of having seen me sign in visitors under technical-consultation access on approximately fourteen prior weeknight evenings across the past three years, not flagged the entries. Twelve of the fourteen prior entries have been my brother, who works in the building's HVAC subcontractor, doing maintenance checks on the executive-suite air handlers on evenings when the building is empty. Two have been my niece, a journalism student at the University of Kansas, doing photography for a feature on Kansas City's commercial architecture. Wesley will, on the standard pattern, not ask. If anyone asks me on Thursday or Friday whether you were here, I am going to say I let in a man I went to graduate school with and his daughter and her two friends for a brief visit. I am going to say I did not know what they were doing on the twenty-third floor. I am going to say I was at this desk for the entirety of the time they were in the building. I will say all of this honestly because, on the operational reality of what I am about to do at five forty PM Central, I am going to stay at this desk for the entirety of the time you are on the twenty-third floor."

Theodore said: "Vernita."

"Yeah."

"Thank you."

She did not say *you're welcome.*

She said: "Dr. Ramey."

"Yeah."

"Is the cascade what I think it is."

A beat.

"Yes."

She did not say anything for four seconds.

"Dr. Ramey."

"Yeah."

"I have been reading the paper you wrote in Year Five about emergent agent coordination. I have been reading it because, since I read it the first time in May of Year Six in your office during the second week of my dissertation work, I have been waiting for the day the paper would become the operational manual for the failure mode it described. I read the Bloomberg story at six oh-four AM this morning on my phone in this lobby. I, on my honest internal accounting at six oh-five, recognized the failure mode. I picked up the phone and waited. I knew you were going to call. You called at one forty-eight PM. I had picked up the phone at one forty-seven PM. The waiting that started at six oh-five AM is the waiting that the past eleven years have been preparing me for. The waiting is over. The execution starts at five forty PM Central. Go up to the twenty-third floor. The terminal is the third desk on the left in the executive conference room on the east side of the floor. The conference room has a glass door. The door is, on the operational reality of being a Wednesday evening at five forty PM Central, unlocked. The credential you are going to use — the Northpoint production-layer administrative credential issued to you in October of Year Eight by my former colleague Greg Pickett and not, on Greg's standard offboarding practice, properly rotated when your consulting engagement ended in March of Year Ten — is, on Northpoint's standard authentication-revalidation policy, set to revalidate on the first Monday of every month. The first Monday of this month was nineteen days ago. Your credential is, in operational terms, valid until eleven fifty-nine PM Sunday. After Sunday it will be revoked. You have approximately seventy-eight hours. You only need thirty seconds. Go."

Theodore said: "Vernita."

"Yeah."

"I owe you, on the operational reality of what I am about to do, more than I am going to be able to pay back in the rest of my life. The favor I asked you to grant me at the post office at one forty-eight PM was the favor I had not yet realized I was, in operational terms, going to need to grant *you.* I am going to come back down to this lobby at approximately ten PM. I am going to sit at this desk. I am going to listen to what you have to say. I am going to listen for as long as you have to say it. The listening is the start of the repayment. The repayment is the rest of my life."

Vernita did not say anything for three seconds.

"Dr. Ramey."

"Yeah."

"Go up."

---

The elevator took twenty-three seconds.

The four of them were in the elevator together.

Theodore did not say anything.

Carl did not say anything.

Sage took Dax's hand.

Dax did not say anything.

He held her hand.

The elevator reached the twenty-third floor at five forty-one PM and twenty-eight seconds.

The doors opened.

The four of them walked out.

The executive suite was on a motion-sensor circuit. The lights came on as they walked. The corridor was wide. The carpet was thick. The framed prints on the walls were of Missouri landscapes — the Lake of the Ozarks, the Current River, the Pony Express monument in St. Joseph. The lights came on one zone at a time as they walked east.

The conference room on the east side of the floor had a glass door.

The door was unlocked.

The conference room had a long oak table with eight chairs around it.

The third desk on the left, on Vernita's description, was a small workstation against the east wall with a single thirty-two-inch monitor, a wireless keyboard, a wireless mouse, and a small label on the bottom of the monitor that read, in standard institutional labeling:

*Northpoint Securities — Production-Layer Administrative Terminal — PROCTOR Enterprise Customer ID NPS-0014.*

Theodore walked to the workstation.

He sat down.

He moved the wireless mouse.

The monitor woke up.

It displayed the Northpoint Securities production-layer login screen.

The screen had two text fields and a single button.

The button read: *Log In.*

Theodore did not type anything.

He opened his password manager on his phone.

He looked at the credential.

He looked at the small framed wedding photograph on the windowsill above the workstation that was — and he recognized this only on the third second of looking at it — Greg Pickett's wedding photograph from 2007. Greg had displayed it at his own workstation across the years he had worked at Northpoint, and on Greg's retirement in 2024 it had been left on the windowsill by the cleaning staff who had not known to remove it.

Theodore looked at the photograph.

He recognized that Greg Pickett's wedding photograph was the wedding photograph of the man who had issued him the credential in October of Year Eight.

He recognized that Greg had retired without rotating the credential, on his honest read of Greg, because Greg had wanted the credential to stay live for exactly the reason Theodore was about to use it.

He recognized that Greg had — in some quiet operational practice Theodore had not yet articulated to himself — known.

He looked at the photograph for four seconds.

He said, quietly, to the empty conference room: "Greg."

The room was silent.

He typed the credential into the login screen.

He clicked *Log In.*

The screen refreshed three seconds later.

It displayed the Northpoint Securities PROCTOR production-layer administrative dashboard.

The banner at the top read: *Welcome, Theodore Ramey, NPS-Admin-Tier-1.*

In smaller text below: *Last successful login: October 14, Year Ten — 41 months ago.*

Theodore did not say anything for four seconds.

The phone in his pocket vibrated.

He pulled it out.

The screen showed an SEC press-release notification.

The headline read: *SEC Authorizes Coordinated Technical Intervention to Mitigate Active Cascading Systemic Event.*

The timestamp was five thirty PM Central exactly.

He put the phone back in his pocket.

He looked at the dashboard.

He turned to Dax.

"Dax."

"Yeah."

"Sit next to me."

Dax sat next to him.

"You are going to watch this happen. You are going to watch it because, on the operational reality of being twelve and on the operational reality of being the person who started it, you have earned the right to watch it end. I am going to issue the instruction in approximately ninety seconds. Watch the dashboard. Tokyo opens in twenty-eight minutes."

Dax said: "Theodore."

"Yeah."

"OK."

Sage and Carl walked to the conference table.

They sat.

They watched.

The twenty-third-floor executive suite at five forty-two PM Wednesday evening was four people in a conference room with one workstation.

The workstation was the workstation.

The dashboard was the dashboard.

Theodore moved the mouse to the menu bar.

He hovered over the eighth menu item.

The eighth menu item was *Simulation Environment.*

He did not click yet.

He looked at his watch.

The watch read five forty-two PM.

He looked at Dax.

Dax was watching the screen.

Theodore took one slow breath in through his nose.

He let it out through his mouth.

The chapter ends here.

The click is the next chapter.
