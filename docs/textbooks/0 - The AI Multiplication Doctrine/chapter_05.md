# Chapter 5: The Subtraction Trap

Admiral James Stockdale was the highest-ranking United States military officer held as a prisoner of war in Vietnam. He was tortured repeatedly over eight years. He had no reason to believe he would survive.

He survived.

When asked who didn't make it out, Stockdale's answer became one of the most important insights in leadership:

"The optimists. They were the ones who said 'We're going to be out by Christmas.' Christmas would come, and Christmas would go. Then they'd say 'We're going to be out by Easter.' Easter would come, and Easter would go. And then Thanksgiving. And then it would be Christmas again."

They died of broken hearts.

Stockdale's paradox: you must never confuse faith that you will prevail with the discipline to confront the most brutal facts of your current reality. Hold both simultaneously. Faith without facts is delusion. Facts without faith is despair.

The AI age has its own version of this paradox. Most leaders are failing it.

---

## The AI Paradox

Here is the brutal fact: AI will transform every role in your organization. Not some roles. Every role. The customer service representative, the financial analyst, the marketing director, the software engineer, the HR manager, the CEO. Every function that involves processing information, generating content, analyzing data, making routine decisions, or producing documented output will be fundamentally changed within the next five years.

No job is immune. Pretending otherwise — "My industry is different," "My people are special," "This won't affect us" — is the optimism that kills.

Here is the faith: your people, multiplied, will be more valuable than they have ever been before. Their judgment, relationships, institutional knowledge, creativity — amplified by AI — will produce outcomes that neither humans alone nor AI alone could achieve. The multiplied organization will be stronger, more capable, more resilient, and more innovative than the organization that existed before AI arrived.

This is not wishful thinking. This is the documented result of every organization that has committed to the Multiplication Thesis.

The AI Paradox: confront the brutal fact that AI changes everything *and* maintain faith that your people are the solution, not the problem.

Most leaders fail one side or the other. The deniers fail the facts side — pretend AI won't fundamentally change their organization, get blindsided when it does. The subtractors fail the faith side — confront the facts of AI's capability and conclude that their people are the cost to be cut rather than the asset to be multiplied.

The subtractors are more dangerous than the deniers. Because subtraction looks like strategy.

---

## The Psychology of Subtraction

When humans feel threatened, they contract. This is not a business insight — it is a psychological one. Same survival instincts that made our ancestors pull back into caves when the savanna got dangerous.

Contraction feels like safety. Reducing exposure feels like protection. Making yourself smaller feels like reducing the target.

In organizational terms, contraction means subtraction. Cut costs. Reduce headcount. Eliminate exposure. Do less with less. This instinct is powerful because it produces immediate, visible results. The day after a layoff, the labor cost line goes down. That is real. That is measurable.

That feels like progress.

But contraction in the face of transformation is the opposite of what the situation demands. Transformation rewards expansion — new capabilities, new approaches, new ways of creating value. The organizations that thrive through transformation expand their capacity to absorb and leverage the change. The ones that contract lose the very capabilities they need to navigate it.

The subtraction trap catches leaders at every level of intelligence and experience. It catches them because it exploits three cognitive biases.

**The visibility bias.** Cost savings are visible and immediate. Capability losses are invisible and delayed. Cut a position — $100,000 savings on next month's statement. The institutional knowledge that person carried? Those costs appear gradually, spread across dozens of small failures nobody connects to the original cut.

The savings are loud. The losses are silent.

**The control bias.** Subtraction feels like control. In a world of uncertainty — and the AI transformation is nothing if not uncertain — the ability to make a decision that produces a predictable outcome is psychologically seductive. "We will cut twenty positions and save two million dollars." Subject, verb, measurable result. It feels like leadership.

Compare: "We will invest in augmenting our workforce and the returns will compound over twenty-four months."

That sentence requires faith. The subtraction sentence requires only a spreadsheet.

**The precedent bias.** Every previous technological revolution eliminated categories of jobs. Industrial revolution — craftsmen. Assembly line — piece workers. Computer — typists and filing clerks. Internet — travel agents and classified ad salespeople. The pattern says: new technology eliminates jobs.

What the pattern does not say — because it has never been relevant before — is that this time, the technology amplifies the judgment, creativity, and knowledge work that survived every previous revolution. This is structurally different. But the precedent bias makes it feel familiar.

## The Subtraction Spiral

Subtraction does not fail in a single dramatic collapse. It fails in a spiral — each turn tightening the constraints, each cut making the next cut seem more necessary, each loss making recovery more difficult.

Six stages. Predictable as gravity.

**Stage One: The Efficiency Decision.** AI arrives. Roles identified where AI can replicate human output. Cost-benefit analysis shows significant savings. Decision to cut is made with genuine belief that it is the right strategic choice. Within the narrow frame of cost optimization, it *is* rational.

That is what makes the trap so dangerous. The first step is genuinely defensible.

**Stage Two: The Knowledge Departure.** People leave. Their knowledge leaves with them. Not the documented knowledge — that stays. The undocumented knowledge. The workarounds, the client preferences, the judgment calls, the contextual understanding that made the documented procedures actually work.

It leaves silently. Nobody notices it's gone until someone needs it and it isn't there.

**Stage Three: The Capability Gaps.** Weeks or months later — subtle failures. An account renewal that goes sideways because nobody remembered the client's sensitivity about a particular issue. A production error a departed employee would have caught. A complaint that escalates because the person who knew how to de-escalate that client is gone.

Each gap is small. Collectively, they are erosive.

**Stage Four: The Quality Decline.** Remaining employees are stretched. Covering departed colleagues' work while managing their own increased load. Dealing with capability gaps they may not even recognize as gaps — because the knowledge that would have prevented the problem was the knowledge that departed.

Quality edges downward. Not dramatically. Measurably. Client satisfaction scores soften. Error rates creep upward. Response times extend.

**Stage Five: The Deeper Dependency.** The organization, missing human expertise, becomes more dependent on AI to fill the gaps. But AI doesn't have the institutional knowledge that left. Its output is competent but generic. Standard responses where customized responses are needed. The organization's differentiation erodes.

The dependency is self-reinforcing. More reliance on AI without human direction. More human skill atrophy. More generic output. More erosion.

**Stage Six: The Next Cut.** Revenue softens. Clients drift toward competitors offering deeper, more personalized service. The response — because it is the only response the subtraction mindset knows — is to cut further.

Each cut accelerates the spiral. Each cut makes the next cut seem more necessary. Each cut destroys more of the organizational capability that would be needed to reverse the spiral.

This is how organizations die. Not in a single catastrophic failure. In a grinding spiral of individually rational decisions that compound into collective decline.

---

## The Brutal Facts, Confronted

Let me confront them without flinching.

**Fact One:** AI will make many current tasks obsolete. True. Data entry, routine analysis, first-draft content creation, scheduling, formatting, basic inquiry handling — substantially automated within years. Any leader planning around these tasks persisting in their current form is planning on a dissolving foundation.

**Fact Two:** The people currently doing those tasks carry knowledge, judgment, and relationships that are not obsolete. Also true. The data entry specialist who notices anomalies the system misses. The customer service rep who reads tone and de-escalates what a chatbot would inflame. The content creator who understands the audience well enough to know which AI draft to keep and which to discard. Their tasks are changing. Their value is not.

**Fact Three:** The leader's job is to separate the task from the person. Automate the task. Multiply the person. This requires the imagination to envision what this person could do if freed from mechanical work and multiplied in judgment work. It requires the will to invest in that vision when the spreadsheet offers an easier path.

**Fact Four:** Most leaders will not do this. Multiplication is harder than subtraction in every measurable way. Longer to show results. More investment. More imagination, more courage, more sustained commitment. The leaders who choose multiplication are choosing the harder path because they believe it leads somewhere better. The leaders who choose subtraction are choosing the easier path because the spreadsheet says it works.

Both paths are available. Only one leads to 10x.

**Fact Five:** The consequences are asymmetric and largely irreversible. A multiplication organization can always adjust — it has the capability, knowledge, and people to pivot. A subtraction organization progressively loses the ability to pivot, because it has shed the very people whose knowledge and judgment make pivoting possible.

Multiplication preserves optionality. Subtraction destroys it.

And optionality, in a world of profound uncertainty, may be the most valuable strategic asset of all.

---

## Getting Out

If you recognize your organization in the spiral — if you've already made cuts, already lost institutional knowledge, already feel the capability gaps — the situation is not hopeless.

But it is urgent.

Stop subtracting. This sounds obvious. In an organization that has developed the subtraction instinct, the next cut is always being planned. Cancel it. The long-term cost of another turn exceeds the short-term savings by an order of magnitude.

Start multiplying. Begin with the employees who remain. Their institutional knowledge has just become more valuable — there are fewer people carrying it. Document it. Build skill docs. Build AI partnerships. Implement the Director Model.

Declare the reversal publicly. Not in a memo. In a meeting. "We are changing our strategy. We are not cutting further. We are investing in multiplying every person on this team. Here is what that means. Here is how we measure success. Here is my personal commitment." In the absence of an explicit commitment, the default assumption of a post-layoff workforce is that the next round is coming. That assumption poisons everything.

The declaration breaks the assumption.

Measure differently. Stop measuring labor cost savings. Start measuring capability growth. How much more can each employee produce? How much time has shifted from $20 to $200 work? How many skill documents exist? How is client satisfaction trending?

Choose the metrics that match the strategy you've committed to.

The subtraction instinct is the most natural thing in the world. It is also the most destructive. Every great leader in history has had to override an instinct — the instinct to play it safe, to follow the crowd, to optimize for the short term.

In the AI age, the instinct you must override is the instinct to subtract. Not because subtraction is cruel — though it is.

Because subtraction loses.

And leaders who lose don't get to lead.
