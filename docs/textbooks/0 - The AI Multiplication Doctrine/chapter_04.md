# Chapter 4: First Who, Then Multiply

There is a principle in leadership that most people get backward.

The conventional wisdom: define the vision, set the strategy, then find the people to execute it. Strategy first, people second.

The great organizations do the opposite. They start with the people. Get the right people in place. *Then* figure out where to go. Because the right people — people with character, judgment, and the capacity to adapt — will find the right strategy. The wrong people, no matter how brilliant the strategy, will fail to execute it.

People first. Strategy second. This has always been true.

The AI age has expanded it in ways nobody anticipated.

---

## The AI-Age Update

Most leaders, when they encounter AI as a strategic force, start with the technology. They evaluate tools. Assess capabilities. Benchmark vendors. "What can AI do for our organization?"

This is starting with the "what." It is the organizational equivalent of buying a bus and then trying to figure out who should ride it.

The right sequence — the one that produces 10x organizations — is different.

First, understand your people. Not their job descriptions — those are artifacts of the pre-multiplication era. Their capabilities. Their knowledge. Their judgment. What do they know that nobody else knows? What decisions do they make that nobody else could make as well? What relationships do they hold? What institutional memory do they carry?

Second, multiply each person. Build AI systems tailored to their specific work — not generic tools, but specialized partnerships that amplify their particular expertise. A skill document that captures their knowledge. An AI partnership that extends their production capacity. A Director Model that elevates their role from executing to directing.

Third — and only third — reimagine what your multiplied workforce can achieve.

The strategic possibilities of a multiplied workforce are categorically different from an unmultiplied one. You cannot see the destination until you've multiplied the drivers.

First who. Then multiply. Then what.

---

## The Underleveraged Asset

There is an asset in your organization that does not appear on your balance sheet.

It is the accumulated knowledge, judgment, and institutional memory of your existing workforce. The senior account manager who has navigated twelve contract renewals with your most difficult client and knows exactly which concerns to address in which order. The operations lead who has kept the supply chain running through three crises by making decisions that no manual describes. The product designer who can look at a prototype and tell you in ten seconds which elements will confuse users — not because of data, but because of fifteen years of watching users interact with interfaces.

This knowledge is invisible. It lives in people's heads, in their instincts, in the accumulated weight of thousands of decisions made under real conditions with real consequences. It does not appear in any report. No system tracks it. In the language of this book — it is a vast reservoir of $200 work. Irreducible human judgment that no AI model can replicate because no AI model has access to the context from which that judgment was formed.

Here is the strategic tragedy: most organizations are sitting on this gold mine while discussing whether to fill it in and build a parking lot.

The Replacement Thesis looks at the tasks — the visible, measurable, $20 outputs — and concludes AI can produce the same outputs faster and cheaper. Correct about the tasks. Catastrophically wrong about the people. Because the people are not their tasks. The people are the accumulated knowledge that makes the tasks produce good outcomes instead of merely completed ones.

Replace a person — lose the tasks and the knowledge.

Multiply a person — keep the knowledge and amplify its reach.

---

## The Right People for a Multiplied Organization

If "First Who" still matters — and it does, now more than ever — the question becomes: who are the right people for a 10x organization?

The answer is not "people who are good with AI."

That is a learnable skill. Any reasonably intelligent person can become proficient with AI tools in weeks. Selecting for AI proficiency is like selecting for typing speed. It correlates with nothing important.

The right people have four characteristics. None of them are technical.

**Deep domain expertise.** Multiplication amplifies what you already know. An employee with deep expertise in your industry, your clients, your market, your craft — their knowledge, externalized through skill docs and amplified through AI partnerships, becomes a force multiplier for the entire organization. An employee without domain expertise has nothing to multiply. AI will make them faster at being generic. That is not a competitive advantage.

**Irreplaceable judgment.** Some decisions cannot be algorithmized. The ability to weigh competing priorities. To read a client's unstated concerns. To know when the data is misleading. To trust an instinct that contradicts the spreadsheet. People with this judgment are the most valuable assets in a multiplied organization — because their judgment scales with AI. An employee whose judgment you trust at 1x is an employee whose judgment you can deploy at 10x.

**Growth mindset toward amplification.** Not everyone will embrace multiplication. Some will see it as threatening. Others as pointless. Others as too much effort. The right people see augmentation as an opportunity — they are curious about what they could become if their production capacity were multiplied by ten. This is not a fixed trait. It can be cultivated through leadership, culture, and evidence. But it must be present — or developable — for multiplication to take hold.

**Institutional knowledge that would be catastrophic to lose.** Every organization has people who carry knowledge that exists nowhere else. If they leave, the organization doesn't just lose a headcount. It loses a capability that may take years to rebuild — if it can be rebuilt at all. These people are multiplication priorities not because they are the most technically proficient, but because their knowledge is the most valuable and the most vulnerable.

## The Wrong People Off the Bus — Reframed

Good leadership has always been honest about removing people who don't belong. Not cruel — but honest. Getting the wrong people out with dignity and speed.

The AI age adds a critical distinction that previous eras never required: the difference between people whose *tasks* are being automated and people who *refuse to be multiplied*.

These are not the same thing.

An employee whose tasks are being automated is not the wrong person. They are a person whose role needs to be transformed. The customer service rep whose ticket-handling work is now handled by AI is not obsolete — they are freed. Freed to apply their judgment to complex cases. Their pattern recognition to process improvement. Their relationship skills to client retention. The task is gone. The person's value is not.

The leader's job is to see the person, not just the task.

An employee who refuses to be multiplied — who insists on doing everything the old way, who dismisses AI as a fad, who actively resists documentation and augmentation — is a different matter. And even here, the distinction matters.

The reluctant-but-capable employee is not the wrong person. They are scared. Their identity is built on "I do this work" — and when the work changes, the identity feels threatened. This person needs leadership, not removal. They need to see multiplication demonstrated. To watch a colleague go from overwhelmed to elevated. They need time, safety, and evidence. Give them all three, and most will come around.

The actively resistant employee — the one who has seen the evidence, received the support, and still chooses to be a ceiling on the organization's potential — is the wrong person for a 10x organization. Not because they lack talent. Because they have decided the transformation is something to fight rather than embrace. This decision, sustained over time and in the face of evidence, makes them a drag on every team, every initiative, every colleague trying to grow.

Addressing this requires leadership honesty. Clear expectations. Fair timelines. Genuine support. And — if the resistance persists — the willingness to make the hard call.

The distinction matters because the easy path is to conflate the two — to label every slow adopter as a resistor and remove them. This is the Replacement Thesis in disguise. It will cost you the very institutional knowledge and judgment you need to make multiplication work.

## The Multiplication Audit

Before buying any AI tool. Before evaluating any vendor. Before launching any "AI transformation initiative."

Audit your people.

Not a performance audit. A multiplication audit. The question is not "Who is performing well?" — it is "Whose expertise would be most valuable if multiplied?"

For every person in your organization, assess three things.

**Knowledge depth.** How much does this person know that exists nowhere else? Client histories, process expertise, market intuition, technical knowledge accumulated over years. The deeper the knowledge, the higher the multiplication priority — because deep knowledge, once externalized and amplified, becomes organizational leverage of extraordinary power.

**Judgment quality.** How much do you trust this person's decisions? Not their compliance. Not their efficiency. Their judgment. When they say "I think we should go this direction" — how often are they right? People with strong judgment are multiplication gold. AI can extend their judgment across more decisions, more situations, more domains than they could ever reach alone.

**Vulnerability to departure.** How much would it hurt if this person left tomorrow? Not their task output — their knowledge. Their relationships. Their judgment. The more it would hurt, the more urgent the multiplication. Every day their expertise remains undocumented is a day the organization is exposed to catastrophic loss.

Map your people on these three dimensions. The highest priorities — deep knowledge, strong judgment, high departure vulnerability — are where multiplication starts. Multiply them first. Capture their knowledge first. Build their AI partnerships first.

Then expand outward.

---

## The New "What"

Here is what happens when you get the sequence right.

You start with your people. Understand their knowledge, their judgment, their institutional value. Multiply each person — skill docs that capture their expertise, AI partnerships that extend their capacity, the Director Model that elevates their role. And then you look up and realize the organization you lead is capable of things you never imagined.

The customer service team that used to handle complaints now runs a proactive client intelligence operation. The engineering team that used to maintain the product now innovates continuously. The sales team that used to cover a territory now covers a market. The operations team that used to keep things running now optimizes for outcomes you didn't know were possible.

This is why the "what" comes last. You cannot see the "what" of a multiplied organization from the vantage point of an unmultiplied one. The possibilities are not incremental.

They are categorical.

The principle hasn't changed. People first, strategy second — always. But the "strategy second" has expanded beyond anything previous leadership thinking imagined. Because when you multiply the right people, the strategic possibilities become extraordinary.

Not incrementally better. Categorically different.

That's the 10x leap. And it starts — as it always has — with who's on the bus.
