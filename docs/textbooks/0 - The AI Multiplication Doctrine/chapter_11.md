# Chapter 11: Optimize — Lead When Everyone Is 10x

This is the chapter nobody else is writing.

Hundreds of books about implementing AI. Dozens about managing the transition. A handful about organizational changes AI demands. Almost nobody writing about what happens *after* the multiplication succeeds.

This is a problem. The hardest leadership challenges are not in the multiplication itself — they are in what comes after. When your workforce is 10x, old management breaks. Old strategies become irrelevant. Old org charts become cages. Old definitions of success become ceilings.

The leader who multiplies their workforce and then manages the multiplied organization with pre-multiplication methods will fail — not because the multiplication was wrong. Because they didn't prepare for its consequences.

The Optimize pillar of TAO is about leading the organization multiplication creates. The problems of abundance — a category most leaders have never encountered.

---

## The 10x Problem

Before multiplication, the bottleneck is production. Can we produce enough? Serve enough clients? Create enough content? Analyze enough data? The answer is usually no. Management is the art of allocating scarce production capacity across competing demands.

After multiplication, the bottleneck shifts.

Production capacity is abundant — sometimes embarrassingly abundant. A team that strained to produce ten reports per month now produces a hundred. A department that struggled with response times now responds in minutes.

The new bottleneck is not "Can we produce enough?"

The new bottleneck is "What is worth producing?"

Fundamentally different leadership challenge. The skills that make you a great manager of scarce capacity — prioritization, resource allocation, efficiency optimization — are not the skills that make you a great leader of abundant capacity.

Managing scarcity is about saying no to the right things. Leading abundance is about saying yes to the right things.

The scarcity manager asks: "Given limited capacity, which of these ten projects should we pursue?" Small solution space. Choose from what's in front of you.

The abundance leader asks: "Given unlimited capacity, which of these hundred possible projects would create the most compounding value?" Enormous solution space. Could do anything. The challenge is figuring out what to do.

That is the 10x problem. So much production capacity that the constraint shifts from doing to deciding what's worth doing.

---

## The Paradox of Abundant Production

When everyone produces at 10x, production is no longer the differentiator.

Two consulting firms. Before multiplication — both produce twenty client reports monthly, comparable quality. After multiplication — both produce two hundred. Production is no longer scarce. It is abundant.

So what differentiates them?

**Quality differentiates them.** When production is abundant, higher standards win. You can produce many options and ship only the best. The organization with lower standards ships everything and lets mediocrity accumulate.

**Taste differentiates them.** The ability to look at ten options and know which one is right — purely human capability. AI produces options. Humans select. The organization with better taste ships better work.

**Strategy differentiates them.** When capacity is unlimited, the question shifts from "What can we produce?" to "What should we produce?" Better strategic judgment directs abundant capacity toward higher-value problems.

The paradox: multiplication makes production easy and differentiation hard. The organizations that win in the 10x era are not the ones that produce the most. They are the ones that produce the *right things*.

Knowing what the right things are is a purely human judgment that no amount of AI can automate.

## Strategic Reframing

The most common mistake after successful multiplication: letting 10x employees do the same work faster.

The customer service team handles four hundred tickets instead of forty. The marketing team produces fifty content pieces instead of five. The engineering team ships ten features per sprint instead of one.

Spectacular numbers. Strategic failure.

Doing the same work ten times faster is a linear improvement applied to a compounding opportunity.

The 10x organization does not do the same work faster. It does categorically different work.

The customer service team handles tickets *and* runs a proactive client intelligence operation — identifying patterns, flagging emerging issues before clients report them, feeding insights to product development. The tickets are handled. The real value is in the intelligence layer that freed capacity made possible.

The marketing team produces content *and* runs continuous audience research, tests content variants at scale, develops new formats, builds data-driven strategy. The content is produced. The real value is in the strategic capability.

The engineering team ships features *and* dedicates a third of capacity to technical debt, exploratory prototyping, and user research. The features ship. The real value is in the quality and innovation velocity.

In each case — a portion handles the original workload at higher quality and lower stress. The remainder redeploys toward higher-value activities impossible under scarcity. This split is the leader's most important decision in the Optimize phase.

It determines whether multiplication produces linear gains — same work, faster — or compounding gains — new capabilities funded by freed capacity.

## The Innovation Pipeline

When production is handled, freed capacity should flow into innovation. Not because innovation is trendy — because innovation is how multiplied organizations stay multiplied.

Three components.

**Hallucination Harvesting.** This concept takes AI's most criticized failure mode and turns it into an organizational asset. When AI produces incorrect, off-target, or unexpected output, most organizations discard it. The 10x organization examines it.

Every AI "failure" reveals something. An unmet need. An unconsidered angle. A gap in the skill doc. An opportunity nobody articulated.

At the organizational level: systematically collect and analyze AI failures across teams. What patterns emerge? What do failures reveal about what customers need, what the market is missing, what the organization doesn't know? Monthly review across teams turns noise into signal. The failure becomes free market research.

I built this practice into TAO because I noticed something during hundreds of implementations: the most interesting ideas consistently came from examining what went wrong, not what went right. The system's mistakes are insights about what the specification missed.

**Innovation Sandboxes.** Multiplied employees with freed capacity need structured environments for experimentation. A sandbox is a protected space — portion of time, set of resources, clear permission — for ideas emerging from multiplied work.

Rules: the idea must be documented. The experiment must have a measurable hypothesis. Results must be shared. Within those rules — freedom. Production pressure is handled by AI. The sandbox time is for the human to be human. Curious. Creative. Exploratory.

**The Idea Capture System.** When fifty employees are each 10x productive, the volume of ideas exceeds what any suggestion box or quarterly review can handle. The 10x organization needs a lightweight, continuous system for capturing insights — a channel where employees share observations, patterns noticed, improvements prototyped. Reviewed weekly by a team empowered to fast-track the best ones.

The innovation pipeline is not separate from multiplication. It is multiplication's most valuable output.

## When Everyone Becomes a Builder

There is a consequence of multiplication that most leaders have not thought through. It is the most disruptive and the most exciting thing in this book.

When you multiply your workforce, you do not just make them faster at their existing jobs. You expand the categories of work they can do.

Here is what I mean. A payroll specialist — let's call her Janet — gets multiplied. She has skill docs, an AI partnership, the Director Model. Her payroll work that used to take forty hours a week now takes twelve. She has twenty-eight hours of freed capacity.

The obvious assumption: Janet does more payroll. Or takes on adjacent payroll work. Or mentors juniors. These are fine. They are also a massive underestimation of what has happened.

What has actually happened is that Janet now has access to capabilities that were previously reserved for specialists, consultants, and senior leadership:

**Research capability.** Janet notices a pattern in payroll data — a cluster of clients in a specific industry with unusual compensation structures. Before multiplication, she would have noted it and moved on. Now she directs AI to research that industry. In four hours — not four weeks — she has a competitive landscape analysis, a gap assessment, and a validated hypothesis about an underserved market segment.

**Specification capability.** Janet's hypothesis is compelling. She uses AI to develop it further — a concept brief, then requirements, then a technical specification for a product that would serve this market. She moves through a four-level specification stack — concept, requirements, technical spec, implementation plan — that would have traditionally required a product manager and an architect working together for months.

**Prototyping capability.** Janet builds a working prototype. Not because she learned to code. Because AI writes code and Janet directs AI. She specifies what the product should do. AI builds it. She tests it. She iterates. In two weeks, she has a functional demo.

Janet in payroll just did the work of a market researcher, a product manager, a software architect, and a developer. Not because she became four people. Because AI multiplication expanded her capabilities beyond her job description — and she had the freed capacity to explore.

This is happening right now, in every organization that has multiplied its workforce. The question is not whether it will happen. The question is whether your organization is ready for it.

## The Internal Entrepreneur Problem

Janet has a decision to make.

She discovered a market opportunity. She validated it. She built a prototype. She has the skills, the AI partnerships, and — now — the confidence that comes from having done something extraordinary.

She can take this to her leadership and say: "I found something. Here's the research. Here's the prototype. I think we should pursue this."

Or she can take it home. Quit her job. Launch a one-person startup with zero overhead and a working prototype. Become the competitor you just read about in Chapter 16.

The difference between these two outcomes is not Janet's character. It is your organization's environment.

If Janet's organization has no mechanism for capturing ideas that come from outside someone's job description — if there's no path from "payroll specialist with a great idea" to "leader of a new initiative" — Janet will leave. Not because she's disloyal. Because she's rational. The organization offered her no path, so she created her own.

If Janet's organization has the right environment, she stays — and the organization gains a new market, a new product, and a proven innovator. The organization captures the value of the very multiplication it invested in.

Here is what that environment requires:

**Permission to explore beyond your role.** Not informal permission — structural permission. Time allocated for sandbox exploration. An explicit statement from leadership: "If you discover something while multiplied, we want to hear about it. You will not be punished for working outside your job description. You will be rewarded."

**A path from idea to initiative.** When Janet brings her research and prototype to leadership, there must be a defined path — not a suggestion box that disappears into a void. A review process. A decision timeline. A commitment to respond with "yes, let's explore this" or "not now, but here's why" or "this is promising, let's assign resources." The path must be credible and fast. Weeks, not quarters.

**Ownership for the discoverer.** If the organization pursues Janet's idea, Janet should lead it — or at minimum be a central participant with meaningful ownership. Not "thank you, we'll take it from here." If the person who discovered the opportunity is sidelined from executing it, you have sent a message to every other employee in the organization: your ideas will be extracted, not rewarded. Nobody will bring you the next one.

**Ascending role, not just ascending challenge.** Janet should not remain a payroll specialist who happens to lead a side project. If her discovery is real, her role should change. Director of the new initiative. Lead of the new product line. Whatever title matches the scope of what she's building. The Director Model applies here — Janet was already a Director of Payroll. Now she is a Director of something bigger.

**Equity in the outcome.** If Janet's discovery generates revenue, Janet should share in it. Revenue participation. Bonus structure tied to the initiative's success. Maybe equity if the initiative spins out. The alignment must be real — not a performance bonus that the organization decides after the fact, but a predetermined share that Janet negotiated when she proposed the initiative.

This is the difference between an organization that multiplies employees and an organization that multiplies employees *and captures the compound value of that multiplication.* The first organization creates 10x producers. The second creates 10x producers who are also 10x innovators — and who stay because the organization gave them a better path than starting alone.

The internal entrepreneur is not a problem to manage. It is the highest-value output of the multiplication flywheel. Every Janet is a potential new revenue stream, a potential new market, a potential competitive advantage that no competitor can replicate — because the insight came from your institutional knowledge, your client data, your organizational context.

Lose Janet, and you've funded a competitor's R&D. Keep Janet, and you've built an innovation engine that compounds.

The choice is the environment you create.

## The Watch-Diagnose-Encode Cycle

There is a practice from the Toyota Production System that most leadership books ignore because it sounds operational. It is the most powerful strategic practice available to a 10x organization.

Watch. Diagnose. Encode.

**Watch:** Observe the production systems across your organization. Not the outputs — the patterns. Which teams' AI partnerships produce consistently high-quality output? Which teams struggle? Where are the recurring quality failures? Where do the bottlenecks form? What are the defect patterns?

This is visual management applied to leadership. The CEO who can see production system health across the organization — quality gate pass rates, edit distances, throughput trends — sees problems before they compound and opportunities before they expire.

**Diagnose:** When you see a pattern — good or bad — diagnose the root cause. A team consistently producing excellent output has something in their production system worth understanding. What is it? A better skill doc? A more effective quality gate? A workflow innovation? Find it.

A team struggling with quality has a systemic issue. Not a people issue — a system issue. The skill doc may be incomplete. The quality gate may be too loose. The workflow may have a bottleneck. Find the root cause. Not the symptom.

Ohno had a practice called "the five whys." When a defect occurred, he asked why five times. Not once — five times. Because the first "why" gives you the symptom. The fifth gives you the root cause. Apply this to your information production systems. The team's output quality dropped. Why? The AI output needed more editing. Why? The skill doc didn't cover the new client requirements. Why? The skill doc hasn't been updated in six weeks. Why? The team wasn't doing weekly refinements. Why? Nobody made refinement part of the workflow.

Root cause: missing process step. Fix: encode weekly refinement into the standard workflow. The defect cannot recur.

**Encode:** When you find something that works — a better process, a better approach, a better standard — encode it. Not in one person's head. In the system. Update the skill doc. Revise the workflow template. Adjust the quality criteria. Make the improvement structural so every team benefits.

This is the difference between personal improvement and organizational improvement. Personal improvement dies when the person goes on vacation. Organizational improvement lives in the standard — permanently, compounding, available to everyone.

One percent improvement per week. Sixty-seven percent per year. But only if it's encoded.

The Watch-Diagnose-Encode cycle, practiced monthly at the leadership level and weekly at the team level, turns the 10x organization into a learning organization. Not in the vague, aspirational sense. In the manufacturing sense — a system that gets measurably better with every cycle, because every improvement is encoded in the standard and the standard is the floor that continuously rises.

## Cultivating Taste

When AI handles production, human taste becomes the ultimate differentiator.

Taste is not aesthetic preference. It is the ability to look at a set of options and know which one is right — not by analyzing data, but by integrating experience, judgment, context, values, and an internalized sense of quality that cannot be articulated as a rule.

The editor who reads a draft and knows which sentence to cut. The designer who looks at three prototypes and knows which one the market wants. The consultant who reviews an analysis and knows which insight will resonate with this specific client. The leader who hears ten proposals and knows which one will compound.

AI produces options. Many options, quickly, cheaply, in infinite variation. It cannot select among them with taste — because taste requires holistic, values-laden, contextually sensitive judgment. Years of human experience. Cannot be reduced to training data.

In a world of abundant production, organizations with the best taste produce the best work. Selecting from abundance — the best among hundreds — rather than shipping from necessity.

Cultivating taste is the leader's responsibility.

**Exposure to excellence.** Teams regularly review excellent work — competitors, adjacent industries, historical examples. Not to copy. To calibrate. The more excellence you absorb, the more sensitive your quality detector becomes.

**Permission to reject.** In scarcity, rejection is costly — no time to redo. In abundance, rejection is cheap — new version in minutes. Give explicit permission to reject work that doesn't meet the standard. Make rejection a sign of quality consciousness, not inefficiency.

**Time to think.** Taste requires reflection. Cannot be exercised at speed. Protect thinking time — time the team is not producing, reviewing, or meeting. Just thinking about quality. Considering options. Developing judgment.

Feels unproductive.

It is the most productive time in the organization.

## The CEO's Own Multiplication

This book has focused on multiplying your workforce. But there is a question most leaders haven't asked yet.

What about you?

The CEO is not exempt from multiplication. You are not just the person who directs the multiplied organization — you are a person whose own capability can be multiplied by AI in ways that were impossible two years ago.

I want to give you one example. Not because it is the only one — but because it illustrates the kind of strategic thinking that separates the 10x leader from every other leader trying to navigate this transformation.

**Activity Mining.**

Your organization generates millions of activity records every day. Who did what, in what order, how long it took, what failed, what succeeded. These records exist in audit trails, compliance logs, ERP systems, CRM systems, financial systems. They are created for regulatory compliance and then ignored for 360 days of the year.

They are the most valuable data asset in your organization. And nobody is reading them.

Activity mining is the systematic application of AI reasoning to these records — producing organizational intelligence that was previously accessible only through $500,000 consulting engagements. Or not accessible at all.

The distinction from data mining is critical. Data mining answers "what happened?" — revenue up 8%, churn at 12%, costs down 3%. Dashboards. Outcomes. Results.

Activity mining answers "how is everyone actually working?" — and more importantly, "why are they winning or losing?"

Five questions applied to your activity streams:

**Where does work stall?** Not where managers think it stalls — where the data shows it stalls. The 36-hour gap between Team A finishing and Team B starting that accounts for 60% of total process time. Invisible in traditional dashboards. Obvious in activity sequences.

**What is being done manually that shouldn't be?** The same person performing the same five steps every Monday. Repeated sequences — automation candidates with complete specification already embedded in the pattern. Steps, frequency, time savings. The system tells you exactly what to automate and how much it will save.

**What patterns precede failures?** Look backward from every failure. What happened in the hours and days before? Recognizable patterns emerge — precursor sequences that appear before problems manifest. Risk management shifts from retrospective to predictive.

**Where do the best performers diverge?** Nine people follow the expected sequence. One person follows a different path — different order, different tools, different timing. That person has 0.5% error rate and 2x throughput. Activity mining reveals exactly what they do differently. Often they cannot articulate it themselves. The data can.

**What is the organization learning?** Examine patterns over months and quarters. Are processes getting faster? Error rates declining? New tools adopted? This reveals the organizational learning curve — usually invisible. You can now see whether your organization is improving, plateauing, or degrading. In real time.

Here is what makes this a CEO tool, not an IT tool. The intelligence comes in three scopes.

Global scope — for you. Enterprise-wide patterns, cross-departmental trends, strategic bottlenecks. "Cross-departmental handoff times increased 23% over two quarters, correlating with operations headcount reduction. Estimated impact: 4% increase in order cancellations."

Team scope — for managers. Department patterns, process bottlenecks, efficiency opportunities. Aggregated and anonymous. "This step takes 36 hours on average" — not "these three people are slow."

Individual scope — for each employee. Personal work patterns, skill development, personal efficiency. Visible *only to the individual themselves*. A personal coach that is a mirror, not a window.

This trust architecture is non-negotiable. The moment managers can see individual activity patterns, employees optimize for appearance instead of actual work. The data becomes corrupted. The system becomes surveillance. And surveillance destroys the Culture of Multiplication faster than anything else.

A replacement-minded CEO would use activity mining to identify who to cut. A Level 10x Leader uses it as a diagnostic tool — finding bottlenecks to dissolve, automation candidates to implement, performance patterns to spread, and organizational learning trends to accelerate.

Same data. Same technology. Different lens. Radically different outcomes.

This is what I mean by the CEO's own multiplication. Not producing more memos or analyzing more spreadsheets. Using AI to see the organization in ways that were structurally impossible before — and making strategic decisions informed by intelligence that would have required a McKinsey engagement and six months of analysis.

You can have it weekly. For near-zero cost. Because the activity data already exists in your systems.

You just need to start reading it.

## The Leadership Shift

The Optimize phase requires a fundamental shift in what the leader does.

Pre-multiplication: the leader's primary function is allocation. Distributing scarce resources — people, time, money, attention — across competing demands. This is management. Valuable, necessary, and — with multiplication — increasingly insufficient.

Post-multiplication: the leader's primary function is direction. Determining what the organization's abundant capacity should be directed toward. Strategy in its purest form — not "how do we do what we're doing more efficiently" but "given that we can do almost anything, what should we do?"

This shift is disorienting. Leaders who built careers on allocation — prioritization, resource optimization, efficiency management — find those skills necessary but no longer sufficient. The 10x organization needs allocation for routine work. Its competitive advantage lives in direction.

The leader who masters this shift becomes something new. Not a manager of scarcity. A curator of abundance. They don't tell the organization what it can't do. They tell it what it should do.

That "should" is the most consequential judgment in the organization.

The Optimize phase is where the 10x organization separates permanently from the merely augmented organization. Anyone can multiply production. The leaders who build for the ages know what to do with abundant production — who shift from "do more" to "do what matters."

That's not a technology problem. That's a leadership problem.

And it's the most important one you'll ever solve.
