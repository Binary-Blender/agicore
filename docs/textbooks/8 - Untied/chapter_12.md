# Chapter 12: The Other Three

Priya Ramaswamy made her coffee at ten thirty in the morning. The kitchen in the Northside walk-up was small and south-facing and warm in April, and she had been working since seven and had earned the late breakfast.

She poured the coffee. She sat down at the kitchen table. She opened her laptop.

The HIPAARail dashboard came up the way it came up every Wednesday morning, with the monthly numbers in the top-left tile.

She read them.

One hundred sixty-seven paying clinics. Average monthly subscription, eight hundred and ninety dollars across the three tiers — five-ninety for the small clinics, eight-ninety for mid-size, fourteen-ninety for the large ones. Monthly recurring revenue: one hundred forty-eight thousand dollars. Annual run rate: one point seven-eight million.

Operating costs for March: four thousand eight hundred and twelve dollars. AWS bill, accounting fees, the legal retainer she paid the small healthcare-compliance firm in Columbus, the dev tools, the domain, the email. That was the whole stack.

Take-home, after taxes set aside: she had been averaging a hundred and seventeen thousand a month. Roughly one point four million a year, after tax.

She closed the dashboard.

She drank her coffee.

Brisket was asleep on the couch in the living room, in the patch of sun that moved across the cushion from ten to noon every clear day. He was twelve now, and slower than he had been three years ago, and he slept through most of the morning unless food was being prepared.

Priya was wearing flannel pajama pants and a long-sleeve T-shirt that had a coffee stain on the cuff. She had not been planning to leave the house until the noon walk. She had not put on a bra.

She opened her email. Forty-three new messages overnight, mostly automated. Three customer-support tickets, all routine. One renewal confirmation from a clinic in Tucson — they had upgraded from the small tier to the mid tier. She processed each one with the toolkit's drafting layer, reviewed the responses, sent them, closed the laptop.

The whole pass took her fifty-one minutes.

---

She had been asked, four times in the last year, whether she was going to hire someone.

The first ask had come from her old project lead at Hartwell, Gerald Hsu, who had stayed at Hartwell through the slow collapse and was now at a smaller firm in Worcester. He had called her in June. He had asked, casually, whether she would consider bringing him on as a co-founder. She had said no. She had said it kindly. He had understood.

The second ask had come from a venture capital firm in Cincinnati that had found her through her landing page. They had wanted to give her two million dollars and a hiring plan. She had said no. She had not said it kindly. She had been annoyed.

The third ask had come from her sister, who lived in Pittsburgh and worried, in the way that older sisters worry, that Priya was working too much alone. Priya had explained the numbers. Her sister had said, *but who are you going to talk to all day?* Priya had said, *the customers.* Her sister had not been satisfied with the answer.

The fourth ask had come from her mother, two months ago, over a phone call on a Sunday evening. Her mother had asked whether she was lonely. Priya had said no. Her mother had asked whether she was sure. Priya had said yes. Her mother had said, *you have always been the kind of person who needed a long leash. I am glad you have one now.*

Priya had cried for about thirty seconds after that call. Not sadly. The other kind.

---

She put on a coat at noon.

Brisket heard the coat and got up off the couch and waited by the door with the slow patience of an old dog. She clipped on his leash.

They walked the Mill Creek Greenway, the way they did every weekday at noon. The route was a mile and a half, mostly flat, along the creek under the budding sycamores. In April the path was full of bicyclists in the warmer afternoons and joggers in the cooler mornings. At noon it was quiet.

Priya walked with her hands in her coat pockets. She did not bring her phone on these walks. She had stopped, six months ago, bringing her phone. The walks were the part of the day she protected.

She thought, as she walked, about a sentence she had been formulating for a few weeks.

*I am not lonely. I am alone, and I am not lonely. There is a difference. The difference is that I chose this and I keep choosing it every day, and I have not had a day in two years where I wanted the choice to be different.*

She turned the sentence over in her head.

She thought it was true. She thought it would stay true.

She thought, further down the path, near the bend where the creek widened into the small pond with the ducks, a second thing:

*I am the version of me I was building toward for thirty years. I just didn't know it was going to be alone.*

She did not say either sentence out loud. There was nobody on the path to say them to.

Brisket stopped to investigate something at the base of a sycamore. Priya waited. He sniffed for a long time. He moved on. They kept walking.

They got back to the walk-up at one o'clock. Priya hung up the coat. She made a bowl of lentil soup and a piece of toast. She ate at the kitchen table with the laptop closed.

At three she sat back down to code.

She worked until six. She built a feature she had been thinking about for two weeks — a small reporting module that her largest customer had been requesting for a month. She tested it. She deployed it. She emailed the customer a one-paragraph note.

The customer wrote back at six-fifteen with three exclamation points.

Priya closed the laptop.

The April light through the west-facing window was the long slanted gold that she liked best. Brisket had come back to the patch on the couch. The walk-up was warm and quiet and hers.

---

Ben Kowalczyk sat at his desk at the Three Rivers Studio loft on a Thursday morning in April and watched the light hit the loading-dock wall across the alley.

The light was the reason he had taken this office. Eli had wanted a place on Hennepin, closer to the trains, with better lunch options. Marcia had been agnostic. Ben had walked into the loft on a Tuesday morning two years ago and seen the way the eastern light came through the high windows and crossed the loading-dock wall in a slow yellow band, and he had told the leasing agent, *we'll take it.*

He had been right about the light.

The firm was five people now. Ben, Eli Choudhry, Marcia Reuter — the three founding partners. Plus Tessa Brand, a design contractor at sixty percent, who came in Mondays and Wednesdays and worked the rest of her week from home. Plus Yuki Okada, a junior backend developer they had hired full-time eight months ago.

They had turned down a sixth hire last month. They had interviewed her twice. She had been good. They had said no. They did not want to be a firm. They wanted to be three to five people, max, for as long as they could sustain it.

The firm was profitable. Each of the three founding partners was paying themselves two-sixty base plus roughly forty-five thousand in profit distributions. They worked four days a week. Friday was everyone's day off, by collective decision, including Yuki and Tessa.

Eli was at his standing desk on the far side of the loft. He had been working on a side project on Fridays — a small open-source library for a niche cryptographic use case that he had wanted to write for years. Ben had been helping him with the architecture. They worked on it together on Friday mornings sometimes, at Eli's apartment, with coffee. It was not work. It was the thing they did instead of work.

Marcia was in the small conference room on a customer call. Ben could see her through the glass, leaning back in her chair, gesturing with one hand. She was taking her younger son Aaron to Iceland in June, for two weeks. She had been planning it since January. The firm would cover. They had built in coverage for exactly this kind of thing.

Ben's phone rang.

He picked up.

"Maya."

"Ben."

"How's your morning."

"Quiet. Listen — I have a referral for you."

"Tell me."

"A Wisconsin manufacturer. Family-owned, second generation, doing about eighteen million in revenue. They need an integration platform between their ERP and the new fulfillment system they're standing up. It's a six-month engagement, maybe eight. They want to retain after. Too small for us to staff well. Exactly your size."

"They asked for you specifically."

"They asked for whoever I trusted. I trust you."

Ben laughed.

"Thanks, Maya."

"Thank *you*," she said. "If I tried to keep them on our roster I would lose them inside a year. They want a small team. They want continuity. They want exactly what you do."

"Send me the contact."

"Already in your inbox."

"You're a menace."

"I'm an ecosystem."

They both laughed.

"Talk soon," Maya said.

"Talk soon."

Ben hung up.

He sat for a second looking at the phone in his hand.

He thought: *we are not competing. We are an ecosystem.*

He had thought this before. He had thought it the first time Maya had sent them a referral, eighteen months ago. He had thought it again at the Three Rivers anniversary dinner last spring, when he had stood up to give the toast and had ended up, by accident, raising a glass to Stillwater. He thought it now, holding the phone, looking at the loading-dock wall across the alley.

He opened his inbox. The Maya referral was there. He read the contact details. He drafted a brief intro email to the Wisconsin manufacturer — friendly, specific, with two sentences about Three Rivers's approach and one sentence about the next available start date. He ran it through the toolkit's tone-check layer the way they did with all customer correspondence. He sent it.

He looked up.

Eli was at his standing desk, headphones on, drawing on a tablet. Marcia was still on her call, now laughing about something. Yuki was at his desk in the back row, working through a bug, his face close to the monitor. Tessa's desk was empty — Thursday, not her day — but her second monitor was on, with a Spotify playlist she had left running. The wall above her desk had a postcard from her sister in Marseille pinned to a corkboard.

Ben thought: *this is a place. This is not a job. This is a place where I work with people I trust.*

He thought: *there are other shapes this could have taken.*

He thought: *this is the shape that worked.*

He got back to the morning's work.

Aliyah Hassan led her team stand-up at nine-fifteen on a Wednesday morning at Brunswick Capital.

The room was on the twenty-second floor of the IDS Tower in downtown Minneapolis. The window faced east, toward the river. The conference table was glass. There were three chairs around it because the team was three people.

Maya Nguyen, thirty-one, lead developer, sat to Aliyah's left with a coffee. Henry Stenson, twenty-eight, the junior, sat across from Aliyah with a notebook. Aliyah sat at the head, with her laptop open and the sprint board on the screen.

"Top three for the day," Aliyah said. "Henry, go."

"I'm finishing the validator on the compliance-flag pipeline. Should land before lunch. Then I'm picking up the test coverage for the new rule-loader."

"Good. Maya."

"I'm pairing with you on the audit-log refactor at ten. Then I have the architecture review with Don at two."

"Good. I'm with Maya at ten through twelve on the refactor. Lunch with Renata at twelve-thirty. Architecture review with you and Don at two through three. Then I'm head-down on the rule-loader spec from three until I leave at five."

She closed the laptop.

"Anybody blocked."

"Nothing," Maya said.

"Nothing," Henry said.

"Good. Talk at ten."

They scattered. Aliyah picked up her coffee and walked back to her desk.

She liked the rhythm of it. She had not expected to like it. When she had taken the job at Brunswick Capital, fourteen months ago, she had braced herself for the salaried numbness she remembered from her two years at Hartwell — the long meetings, the political fog, the sense of being a small unimportant gear in a large indifferent machine. Brunswick had not been like that. Brunswick had been small enough that the work mattered visibly. The compliance group was eleven people. The AI tooling team was three. The CTO knew Aliyah's name and used it.

The pace was humane. She worked thirty-two hours a week. She took Fridays. Her salary was a hundred and forty-five thousand, with a fifteen-percent bonus that had hit at full last December. Her health insurance covered her boyfriend's dental.

She coded from ten until twelve with Maya, refactoring the audit log. It was tight focused work. They closed the refactor at eleven-fifty. Maya sent the PR up. Aliyah grabbed her coat.

---

Renata Diaz was already at the Vietnamese place on Lake Street when Aliyah got there.

They had been doing this lunch monthly for nine months. They had been introduced through a Twin Cities tech-women dinner that someone at the U had organized. Renata had been the kind of senior woman that Aliyah had wanted to know — not because Aliyah wanted anything from her, but because Renata was good and warm and ran her function with a clarity Aliyah admired.

They ordered. They caught up on the small things — Renata's daughter starting kindergarten, Aliyah's boyfriend Sam getting promoted at 3M, the weather, the river.

Then Renata, at the third refill of the tea, said it again.

"I want to make the offer one more time, Aliyah. The Senior Partner track at Stillwater. We would bring you in as an Associate with a clear path to partner inside four years. Senior Partner inside seven. The compensation is real. The work would suit you. You would be on the same team as a lot of people you would learn from."

Aliyah smiled.

"Renata."

"I know."

"You have asked me three times."

"I have asked you three times."

"And every time I have said the same thing."

"You have."

"I am happy here. I mean it. The work suits me. The pace suits me. My team is good. My boss is good. I can run for an hour after work and have dinner with Sam and not think about my job until nine in the morning. That is the life I want right now."

Renata nodded.

"I know," she said. "I know you mean it. I keep asking because I want you to know the offer is real. The offer stands. Forever. Whenever you want it."

Aliyah looked at her for a second.

"That's why I love that you keep asking. You're not trying to convert me. You're just keeping the door open."

"That's exactly what I'm doing."

"I appreciate it."

"I know."

The check came. Renata paid. They walked out onto Lake Street into the April sun.

---

Aliyah ran along the Mississippi at six o'clock.

She ran the West River Parkway from the Lake Street bridge down past the Stone Arch and back. Four miles. The river was high with spring runoff and the gulls were back. The light over the downtown skyline was the slow gold of the long April evenings.

She thought, as she ran, about Renata's offer. She had thought about it every time Renata had made it. She always landed in the same place.

*I tried the thing. The thing was not for me. The thing being not for me does not mean the thing was wrong. It means I needed to try the thing to know.*

She had earned this. The thirty-two-hour weeks. The boyfriend who was kind and steady. The apartment with the south-facing window. The team that liked her. The boss who respected her. The work that was real and useful and bounded.

She was twenty-eight years old. She had tried solo at twenty-six. She had been honest with herself about it at twenty-seven. She had landed somewhere that fit at twenty-eight.

She ran past the Stone Arch Bridge. The water was loud over the falls.

She turned around at the milepost and ran home.

---

The three of them — Priya in Cincinnati, Ben in Minneapolis, Aliyah at Brunswick Capital — would never meet. They would not know one another's names. They would not know that they had each, in their own way, helped define the shape of an industry that had reshaped itself in three years. Each of them was simply doing the work that suited them. That, in the end, was the only thing the new equilibrium required: that the work suit the person. The rest was arithmetic.
