# Chapter 13: The Equilibrium

Maya Holm-Russo sat at her desk on a Wednesday morning in May and read her monthly numbers.

The numbers were on a single sheet of paper. She had asked Aaron to print it for her every month for the last decade. Aaron had stopped asking why. She liked paper. She liked the way numbers settled differently in her head when she was holding them.

She read down the column.

One hundred thirty-two people at Stillwater. Down from one hundred forty-five at the start of all this. Stable, for the first time in three years.

Revenue: thirty-one point four million in Year 3. Up four percent on the Year 1 baseline of thirty point two. Projected thirty-two million in Year 4.

Profit margins: eleven percent. Down from the sixteen they had run before the Senior Partner tier had been stood up. Widening monthly. She had told Lena Schultz they would be back to fourteen by the end of Year 4. She believed it.

The Senior Partner tier: nineteen people. Five of them returnees from solo work. Damon Wright was one of those five. Tomás Reyes, who had joined in February, was another.

Engineering turnover in the trailing twelve months: six percent. The industry average, according to the most recent report from the consultancy that tracked these things, was twenty-three.

Client retention: ninety-six percent.

She read the sheet a second time. She put it in the top drawer of her desk where she kept the printed numbers for the year. There were five months of them in there now. She closed the drawer.

She thought, briefly, about what each of those numbers had cost.

The hundred and thirty-two people had cost her thirteen difficult exits over two and a half years — some by choice, hers and theirs, and some by mistake. The four percent revenue growth had cost more than four percent of her sleep. The eleven percent margin had cost her sixteen, in the short term, and she had not yet seen the long-term curve bend back the way Lena had promised it would, though it was bending. The Senior Partner tier had cost roughly two million dollars in incremental compensation in Year 3 alone. The six percent turnover had cost the firm a year of operational mess she had not wanted but had not avoided. The ninety-six percent client retention had cost her, personally, about forty more dinners away from Carlo than she would have liked.

These were the trades.

She had made them. She had made them slowly, and not always cleanly, and the firm had survived them. She had not been sure, in March of Year 2, that the firm would survive them. She had not been sure in November of Year 2, either. She was sure now.

She got up from the desk.

---

She walked.

The third-floor open workspace was busy on a Wednesday morning. The light through the east windows was the same light it had been when she had opened the office in 2014. The same brick. The same warehouse beams. The eighth-floor conference room had been converted, in February, to the Senior Partner Circle meeting room — long table, nineteen chairs, a coffee setup, a whiteboard the size of a billboard. Otherwise the building was the building.

She walked past Damon Wright's desk.

Damon was standing at a small whiteboard on wheels with Jessica Kao. They were drawing something — boxes and arrows, the architecture of a thing Maya did not need to follow. Damon was holding the marker and gesturing with his other hand. Jessica was nodding and writing in her notebook. They both had coffees on Damon's desk.

Damon saw Maya. He nodded. He smiled.

Maya nodded back. She kept walking.

She walked past Tomás Reyes's desk. Tomás was on a phone call, in Spanish, with his back to the workspace. He was leaning back in his chair with one foot on the desk drawer. He looked comfortable. He looked like someone who had stopped trying to do the wrong thing and had finally come home to the right thing. He had told Maya, in their first conversation in February, that he had been trying to be a firm of one for eighteen months and that the loneliness had nearly killed him. He had said it plainly. Maya had hired him that week.

She walked past Renata Diaz's desk.

Renata waved.

Maya walked past Aaron Park's office. Aaron was on a call, through the glass, with what looked like a client. He saw her and gave her a small thumbs-up. She gave him a small thumbs-up back.

She kept walking.

She walked to the far side of the loft, where the windows faced the Mississippi.

She stopped at the window.

The May light was clear. The river was high with spring runoff and the surface was textured with the long slow swirls of the current pulling against the bridge piers downriver. The sky was the pale even blue of the upper Midwest in late spring. A barge was working its way upstream, slow and patient, pushing against the current.

She stood at the window.

---

She thought about the October when this had started.

She thought about Ben Kowalczyk at Carlo's restaurant on the Monday of the second week of October, telling her four months early. She remembered the exact moment — Ben across the table from her with the soup he had not eaten, his hands folded on the table, telling her in his quiet straightforward Ben way that he and Eli and Marcia were leaving in February. She remembered walking back to the office afterward and calling Aaron from the sidewalk and saying *this is the leading edge.* She remembered Aaron saying *I've been waiting for you to say that.*

She thought about Karen Voss.

Karen had had lunch with her four times in the eighteen months since Karen had left Hartwell. The first lunch had been awkward. Karen had been carrying a kind of professional grief that Maya had recognized but had not been able to touch. The second lunch had been better. By the third lunch they had been talking like colleagues. At the fourth lunch, in March, Karen had given Maya the spreadsheet she had kept — names, dates, reasons — and had said, *I'm not going to need this anymore. I thought you might want to see what it looked like from the inside.* Maya had read it on the plane home. It had taken her three readings to get through it without setting it down.

She thought about Damon's text from the kitchen table at midnight. *Can we have coffee.* Three words. She had known what they meant before she read them.

She thought about the board meeting. Lena Schultz across the table, looking at the package Maya had built and saying *Maya, this is the most expensive thing you have ever done and it is also the only thing I think can possibly work. Do it.* Lena had been right on both counts.

She thought about the all-hands in March of Year 2. The room quiet after she said *what I want you to know is that I see what is happening.* The applause that was not loud but was specific. The two people who had come up to her afterward and said *thank you* without saying anything else.

She thought about Cliff Mendelson, the insurance-carrier CIO, who had nearly defected to a solo developer in Year 2 and had decided not to. She thought about the long conversation in his office in St. Paul, where he had told her, finally, what it was that had made him stay. *I don't want to bet my career on one person,* he had said. *I want to bet my career on a team that is going to show up the same way every week for the next ten years. You can sell me that. He can't.* She had taken the sentence home with her that night. She had built the partner-tier pitch around it.

She thought about the people she had not been able to keep. She thought about Tess Lin, who had left in Year 2 to start a small firm in Cambridge and had told Maya, in a kind email three months later, that the new firm was good but that she missed Stillwater. Tess had not come back. Tess had built something she liked and had decided not to undo it. Maya respected that. Maya also wished she had moved faster in Year 1.

She thought about the dozen mistakes. The compensation gaps she had not caught. The two associates she had promoted too fast. The senior architect she should have made a Senior Partner in November and had not, who had left in January for a competitor. She thought about each of them by name. She thought she would think about each of them by name for the rest of her career. That was the cost of doing the work in real time, with real people, while the ground shifted.

She thought: *I have not figured this out alone. I have not figured this out perfectly. I have made mistakes. I have lost good people who should not have left. I have retained some people who probably should have gone.*

She thought: *but the firm is on a footing I can stand on.*

She thought: *the labor market I operate in is different than it was three years ago. The economics are different. The competitive landscape is different. What has not been different — what has gotten clearer, in fact — is that good people want to do good work alongside other good people. That some configurations of "doing good work alongside other good people" involve being in a firm, and some don't. And that the firms that are healthy in this new world are the ones that make it actually attractive to be part of one.*

She stood at the window a moment longer.

The barge had moved a hundred yards upstream. She watched it work. The pilot was steady. The river was patient with him. He was patient with the river.

She thought, watching the barge, about the long arc of the last three years.

She thought it had been less dramatic than it had felt at the time. There had been no single crisis. There had been no quarter where the firm had nearly failed. There had been no all-hands where she had cried, no board meeting where Lena had pulled her aside and told her she might lose the support of the room. The thing that had happened had happened slowly. Two and a half years was a long time. The people who had left had left over months. The people who had returned had returned over months. The Senior Partner tier had been built brick by brick — first the compensation model, then the legal structure, then the equity-vesting framework, then the partner-circle governance, then the slow accumulation of the nineteen people who now sat in it.

None of it had felt heroic from the inside.

She thought: *that is probably what the work looks like when it is the right work. It looks like a barge on a river. It looks like patience.*

She turned away from the window and walked back to her desk.

---

She got to Carlo's restaurant at six-thirty.

Wednesday nights were slow. The dinner rush at Carlo's was Thursday through Saturday. On Wednesdays Carlo cooked for the two regulars in the corner booth — a retired couple from the neighborhood who came in every Wednesday for the gnocchi — and for whoever wandered in off Hennepin, which was usually nobody before seven.

Maya took her usual stool at the bar.

Carlo was behind the line. He saw her come in. He nodded. He did not say anything yet. He was finishing the gnocchi for the corner booth.

The dining room was warm. The lights were low. The kitchen smelled of butter and sage. A jazz record was on, low — Bill Evans, the *Sunday at the Village Vanguard* sessions Carlo had been playing on Wednesday evenings since 2011.

Maya watched him work.

He plated the gnocchi. He garnished it with sage and a few twists of pepper. He set the two plates on the pass and called *behind* to the server, a young woman named Anna who had been with Carlo since high school. Anna picked up the plates and took them to the corner booth. The retired couple thanked her.

Carlo wiped his hands on the side towel. He came around the line. He set a glass of red wine in front of Maya without asking. He did not ask because he never asked.

He leaned his elbows on the bar.

"How was the day," he said.

"It was a good day."

"Tell me."

She told him about the morning walk. She told him about the numbers. She told him about Damon and Jessica at the whiteboard. She told him about Tomás Reyes on the phone in Spanish with his foot on the desk drawer. She told him about the barge on the river.

Carlo listened. He did not interrupt. He sometimes nodded.

When she was done he refilled her wine.

He said: "Maya. Tell me about Year 4."

She thought for a moment.

"Year 4 is going to be quieter than the last two years," she said.

"Tell me how."

"The Senior Partner tier is mature. The compensation model has settled. Renata has the people function running cleaner than it has ever run. Aaron has the operations side dialed. The big strategic decisions for the year are about which engagements to take and which to decline. That is a good kind of decision to be making. That is the kind of decision a firm should be making when it is running well."

"What else."

"I am going to take a week off in July. I have not taken a full week off since 2019."

Carlo looked at her over the bar.

"You told me that last year."

"I know."

"And the year before."

"I know."

He smiled.

"I will believe it when you are on the plane."

"That's fair."

"Where are we going."

"I was thinking Italy. I was thinking your cousin's village. I was thinking we spend a week eating tomatoes and not answering email."

"I would like that."

"Me too."

He nodded.

"What else about Year 4."

She thought.

"I want to bring two more Senior Partners in. Quiet hires. Returnees. Aaron has two names. Renata has one. I want to talk to all three before September."

"You will."

"I will."

"And Damon."

"Damon is going to lead the biggest engagement we have ever taken on. We won the insurance retainer last month. Five years. Seven point eight million a year. Damon and Jessica are going to architect it. Damon does not know yet that it is going to be his. I am telling him on Monday."

"He will say yes."

"He will say yes."

She drank her wine.

"Carlo."

"Yes."

"Quieter is good."

"Quieter is good."

He came around the bar and kissed the top of her head and went back to the line. Anna brought her a small plate of olives without being asked. The Bill Evans record turned over to the second side. The retired couple in the corner booth finished their gnocchi and asked for the check.

Maya sat at the bar with her wine and watched her husband cook.

---

By May of Year 3 the industry had reached a kind of steady state. The big consultancies that had not adapted had thinned. Some had been sold for parts. Some had gotten smaller and quieter and were now boutiques. The mid-size consultancies that had adapted — Stillwater Software among them — were thriving. The thousands of solo founders who had left were now an ecosystem in themselves. Some of them, like Priya Ramaswamy, stayed solo and built quiet kingdoms. Some, like Ben Kowalczyk, found small partnerships and built tight, profitable studios. Some, like Damon Wright, came back to firms they trusted, on terms that respected what they had learned alone. Some, like Aliyah Hassan, took salaried roles at companies that needed exactly their kind of mind. None of these outcomes was the right outcome. All of them were right outcomes for the people who chose them.

Maya Holm-Russo, in the spring of Year 3, walking through her own office on a Wednesday morning, did not believe that she had solved any large industry problem. She believed that she had figured out how to run her firm honestly inside an industry that had changed. That was enough.

The firms that did not figure that out are not mentioned in this book by name. They are mentioned in industry retrospectives now and then. They are mentioned less often each year.

Nobody remembers them.
