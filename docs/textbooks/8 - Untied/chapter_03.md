# Chapter 3: The Attic in Cincinnati

Priya Ramaswamy woke at seven on the second Tuesday of November to the sound of her dog Brisket sighing on the rug at the foot of the bed.

Brisket was a forty-pound mutt of indeterminate parentage, mostly brown, with one white sock on his back left foot and a tail that curled over his back when he was happy. She had adopted him from a Northside rescue eighteen months ago. He had been called Biscuit at the shelter. She had renamed him Brisket on the drive home because she had been hungry and because she liked the sound of it better and because he had not seemed to mind either way.

He was sighing now in the way that meant *I am awake, and you should be also.*

She turned over and looked at the clock. Seven-oh-three. She put her feet on the cold floor. She picked up the sweatshirt she had left on the chair and pulled it on over her T-shirt and walked into the kitchen.

The apartment was a third-floor walk-up in a converted Victorian on a quiet street in Northside Cincinnati. The bottom two floors belonged to a retired English professor named Mrs. Chen who lived alone, played the piano in the evenings, and had not asked Priya a personal question in eighteen months. The top floor was Priya's. It had three rooms — the bedroom under the eaves, the kitchen with the gas stove and the window over the alley, and the attic-office at the front of the house with the sloped ceiling and the one big west-facing window that looked out over the tops of the maples on Hamilton Avenue.

She had moved into the apartment two years ago, when she had still been at Hartwell Digital and the rent had been the kind of luxury her Hartwell salary could absorb without thinking about it. She still had eight months left on the lease. She had decided two weeks ago, on an afternoon when the prospect math had started to come into focus, that she was going to renew it.

The kitchen smelled of coffee and dog. She put the kettle on. She ground beans for the pour-over. She filled Brisket's bowl with kibble and topped it with the spoonful of pumpkin he expected. He ate it standing up, with the methodical patience of an animal who had once been very hungry and now was not.

She took her mug to the front window and looked at the street. There was frost on the cars. A school bus went past. A man in a long coat walked a dachshund on the opposite sidewalk.

It was the second Tuesday in November. It was her twenty-third day as the founder and sole employee of HIPAARail, Inc., a Delaware C-corp she had filed for online two weeks before her last day at Hartwell. She had quit Hartwell on Friday, October thirty-first. Today was day twenty-three. She had been counting.

She drank her coffee at the window.

---

At seven thirty she put Brisket on his leash and took him out the side door and down the wooden stairs and out into the cold morning of Northside. The neighborhood was old streetcar suburb, brick storefronts on the avenue, narrow houses on the side streets, a coffee shop that had been there for fifteen years and a brewery that had been there for five. She walked Brisket in a loop — down to the avenue, past the coffee shop where she nodded at the barista through the window, past the bakery, past the closed front of the brewery, up to the small park where the Friends of Northside had planted oaks, around the park twice while Brisket sniffed every tree, then back along Chase Avenue to the house.

Forty-five minutes. The same loop she did every morning.

She had spent the last fourteen days of her four years at Hartwell training her replacements and writing transition documentation that, she suspected, no one would read after her last day. She had said her goodbyes on Friday. She had taken her plants and her two framed photographs and her favorite pen and her keyboard out in a banker's box and she had ridden the elevator down to the lobby of the Hartwell Building one last time and she had taken an Uber to South Station and she had ridden the train to her connection and she had flown home to Cincinnati on a Saturday morning flight that had landed at eleven.

She had not been back to Boston since.

She did not miss it.

She wanted to be careful about that, because she had read enough about founder psychology to know that the first three weeks of solo work could feel either like a vacation or like a free fall and that the difference between the two could be deceptive. She had been guarding against the vacation feeling. She wanted the work to start before she let herself enjoy any of it.

But she was — she had to admit — enjoying the work.

---

At nine she was at her desk in the attic-office with her second cup of coffee.

The desk was a four-foot piece of butcher block on two filing cabinets that she had assembled herself the second weekend after the move. The monitor was a thirty-two-inch ultrawide she had bought used from an architect downsizing. The chair was an ergonomic she had paid more for than she had ever paid for a piece of furniture, and which had been the best decision of her life. Behind the monitor the west-facing window framed the bare November tops of three maples and the slate roof of the house across the street.

Brisket lay on the small rug under the desk, with his chin on her left foot.

She had a list of three things to do today.

One: finish the customer-onboarding flow for the clinic in Lexington that had signed an LOI the previous Friday and was scheduled to go live December first.

Two: prepare for the two prospect calls she had scheduled for one-thirty and three.

Three: review the proposal draft she had started yesterday for a clinic in Dayton, which she planned to send by end of day.

She opened her laptop.

She opened NovaSyn Chat Lite in the second monitor's right pane — the lightweight desktop version of the same orchestrator she had used every day for the last two years at Hartwell, the one Hartwell had licensed for its engineers and the one Hartwell had assumed, when she had requested an export of her personal prompt library on her last day, that she was taking for nostalgic reasons. She had taken it because the prompt library was hers. She had built it. The firm had paid her salary while she had built it, but she had built it on her own time at her own kitchen table, the way Ben Kowalczyk would later be described as having built his spreadsheet model, except that her artifact had been a library and his had been a forecast.

She opened the prompt library now. She loaded the customer-onboarding template. She loaded the proposal template. She loaded the discovery-call notes template. She loaded the follow-up-email template.

She put her hands on the keyboard.

She started.

---

By twelve thirty she had done it.

The onboarding flow for the Lexington clinic was finished. She had built the user accounts. She had configured the document repository. She had run a test of the audit-log export. She had drafted the welcome email. She had drafted the kickoff-meeting agenda. She had built a sample compliance report for the clinic's first month so they would see what the artifact looked like when it came out of the system. She had recorded a five-minute screen-share walkthrough.

The work would have taken her two and a half days at Hartwell. She had done it in three and a half hours.

The reason was not that she was working harder. The reason was that there was no overhead. There was no project manager to brief. There was no slide deck to assemble. There was no biweekly status meeting. There was no Slack thread with twelve people on it. There was no client-services partner to update. There was no internal review. There was no compliance-of-the-compliance-work, which had been a thing at Hartwell that she had been trying for two years to explain to people who had never worked at a firm. There was just her, her tools, the client's requirements, and the work.

She closed the laptop. She stretched. Brisket got up, stretched in mirror, and walked out of the office.

She followed him to the kitchen.

She made a sandwich — turkey, sharp cheddar, mustard, on bread from the bakery on the avenue. She ate it standing at the kitchen counter and watched the alley out the back window. A neighbor was hanging laundry on a line strung between two porches. It was forty-one degrees out. The laundry would freeze before it dried. The neighbor would bring it in stiff and she would not mind. The neighbor was an old woman from somewhere in the Balkans who had been doing this since before Priya had been born and who, Priya assumed, was not going to be told by anyone about the inefficiencies of her drying method.

Priya thought, eating her sandwich, *I do not need anyone to tell me how to do this either.*

She was thirty-one. She had been the kind of student who got the second-highest grade on the test by half a point and then thought about the half point for a week. She had been the kind of engineer at Hartwell who reviewed her own pull requests at midnight before she submitted them so she would not be embarrassed in the morning. She had been the kind of person who, when she was given the chance to lead a sub-team of three junior engineers, had said no, because she had not yet been confident that she could carry the work and the management at the same time. She had been told, in her last performance review at Hartwell, that her ceiling at the firm was high but that to reach it she would need to do more of what she was bad at and less of what she was good at, which was a sentence that had stayed with her for nine months.

She had been thinking about it again, on a Thursday in April, at her own kitchen table at eleven at night, with a glass of red wine and her laptop and the early prototype of HIPAARail on her screen. She had thought: *what if the work I am good at is the work, and the work I am bad at is the firm.*

That had been the night she had really started.

---

At one she sat back at her desk.

The first prospect call was at one thirty. The clinic was a five-provider primary-care practice in Crestview, Kentucky, about twenty miles south of Cincinnati. She had identified them eight months ago from a list of HIPAA enforcement actions that the Department of Health and Human Services published quarterly. The clinic had not been a target of any enforcement action. They had been adjacent to one — they shared an electronic-health-records vendor with a clinic in Louisville that had been fined three hundred and eighty thousand dollars for a breach. Priya had read every public record she could find on the Louisville case. She had built a one-page brief on the kinds of compliance failures that had led to the fine. She had identified four other clinics in the region that used the same EHR vendor. The Crestview clinic was one of them.

She had cold-emailed the clinic's office manager in late August, while she had still been at Hartwell. The email had not pitched anything. It had said: *I am a healthcare-compliance engineer based in Cincinnati. I read the Louisville HHS settlement last week and I noticed that your clinic uses the same EHR vendor. I built a checklist of the four configuration changes that would have prevented the Louisville breach. Would you like me to send it to you? No charge.*

The office manager had replied in eleven minutes. The checklist had been sent. Three days later the office manager had asked Priya if there was someone at her firm who consulted on this kind of work. Priya had said: *I'm planning to leave my firm and start my own consulting practice in the fall. Would you be open to a call once I'm independent?* The office manager had said yes.

Today was the call.

Priya prepared for it the same way she prepared for every prospect call. She loaded her discovery-call notes template. She pulled up the clinic's website, their state licensure record, their published quality measures. She loaded a brief summary of the EHR vendor's recent security advisories. She drafted a one-page agenda. She closed her eyes and ran the call in her head for two minutes. Then she opened her eyes and put on her headset.

The call went well.

The office manager, a woman named Doreen Yost, had her practice's medical director on the line and the practice's outsourced IT contractor as well. They had questions. Priya answered them. They had concerns. She acknowledged them. They had two specific configuration questions about their EHR. She did not know the answer to one of them. She said: *I'd want to confirm this with the vendor's documentation before I give you a recommendation. I can have an answer back to you by end of day tomorrow.* Doreen Yost said that was fine.

The call ran forty-three minutes. At the end of it, Doreen Yost said she would like to see a proposal. Priya said she would have one over by Friday. Doreen Yost said that would be wonderful.

Priya hung up.

She wrote down three things in her notebook.

One: *forty-three minutes felt right.*

Two: *the IT contractor was the one who needed to be persuaded. Future calls — make sure his counterpart is in the room.*

Three: *I need to know the EHR vendor's security advisories cold. Build a reference doc by Friday.*

She closed the notebook.

---

The second call, at three, was a clinic in Dayton.

She had been working their proposal for a week. The clinic was a four-provider OB-GYN practice. The office manager — a man named Reggie Halverson, sixties, ex-Army, methodical to the point of slowness — had been on three prior calls with Priya in the last month. The first had been a discovery call. The second had been a follow-up. The third had been a technical walk-through of the HIPAARail dashboard, which Priya had shared via screen.

This call was supposed to be the close.

Priya had been thinking about it since breakfast.

She had drafted the proposal in NovaSyn the night before. She had not let the model write the proposal. She had let the model draft three opening paragraphs and she had read all three and she had thrown out two and rewritten the third by hand. She had let the model suggest the pricing structure and she had ignored the suggestion and used her own. She had let the model draft the timeline and she had used the model's draft as a starting point and edited it down by half. The way she worked with the model was the way she had worked with the senior architects at Hartwell when she had been a junior — she brought her own draft, she asked for opinions, she took the parts that improved her thinking, and she made the final call herself.

She had her own view first. Then the tool. Then her decision.

She loaded the proposal in the second monitor. She loaded her notes from the prior three calls in the right pane. She opened the dial-in.

Reggie Halverson came on the line.

"Ms. Ramaswamy."

"Mr. Halverson. Good afternoon."

"I have looked at the materials you sent."

"I appreciate that."

"I have a couple of questions."

"Please."

He asked his questions. They were good questions. They were the questions of a man who had spent his career making sure the supply requisitions matched what had been delivered. Priya answered each one. On the second one she had to think for a moment. On the third one she said: *Mr. Halverson, I want to be clear that the system will flag this kind of policy violation but it will not stop it. The decision to act on the flag is going to remain with your practice. I do not want to oversell what the tool does.*

There was a small pause.

"I appreciate you saying that."

"Yes, sir."

"What's the price."

"Eight hundred and ninety a month, billed annually. Twelve-month minimum on the first contract. After twelve months it goes month to month. No setup fee. The first thirty days are at no charge — I want to be sure the system is doing for you what you think it's going to do."

"That sounds right."

"Would you like to proceed."

He was quiet for a moment.

"Yes."

Priya did not say anything for a beat. She let the word sit.

"Thank you, Mr. Halverson. I will send the agreement over by six tonight. If you can have it signed back to me by Friday morning, I can have the system stood up for your practice by November twentieth."

"That works."

"Thank you."

"Welcome aboard, Ms. Ramaswamy."

She held the phone for a second after he hung up.

Then she set it down on the desk.

Then she laughed.

She laughed out loud, alone, in her attic-office, for thirty seconds. Brisket lifted his head from the rug and looked at her with the puzzled expression he reserved for human behavior he had not been trained for. She kept laughing. She put her face in her hands and laughed into them.

When she stopped she was crying a little. She wiped her eyes with the back of her wrist.

She picked up her phone and called her mother in Pittsburgh.

"Mom."

"Priya."

"I got my first customer."

There was a small silence.

"Tell me about them."

She told her mother about Reggie Halverson and his Army manner and his careful questions and the eight hundred and ninety a month. Her mother, who was a retired hospital administrator and who had spent thirty years explaining to surgeons why the budget would not allow what the surgeons wanted, said: *eight hundred and ninety is a good number. Multiply it. Tell me where it goes.*

"Mom."

"I know. I know."

"Forty-five clinics gets me to four hundred thousand a year."

"And how many clinics are there in the country."

"Two hundred and twenty thousand."

"Priya."

"I know."

"All right."

"Mom."

"Yes."

"I have to go finish the agreement."

"Go."

She hung up.

---

She wrote the agreement in forty minutes. She used a template she had built from three legal-services subscriptions and one paid review from a healthcare-specialist attorney in Cincinnati who had charged her four hundred dollars to look at it. She had the agreement reviewed against the model. She corrected three small things. She sent it to Reggie Halverson at five forty-six.

She closed her laptop at six.

She made dinner — pasta with butter and parmesan and a soft-boiled egg, which was what she made when she was happy. She ate it at the kitchen table with a glass of the cheap red wine she kept in the cabinet. She fed Brisket. She let him out one more time. She washed her plate.

By eight she was in the small armchair by the front window with a paperback novel and a wool blanket over her lap.

It had started to snow.

The flakes were small and slow and lit from below by the streetlight at the corner. Brisket was asleep on the couch under the window, with his tail curled over his nose. The radiator was knocking the way it did. The book was a novel set on a cattle ranch in Wyoming in the nineteen-twenties, by a writer she had not read before, and the first chapter was about a man teaching his son to repair a fence in the snow.

She read for forty pages.

She thought, halfway through, about the day. The Lexington clinic. The Crestview call. The Dayton close. The eight hundred and ninety dollars a month, which would arrive in her checking account starting in December, and which would arrive again in January, and which would not stop arriving unless Reggie Halverson decided to cancel, which she did not believe he would. She thought about the seventeen prospects on her list. She thought about the model she had built that said that if she closed nine of them in the first six months her business would be self-sustaining. She thought about the model she had built that said that if she closed thirty-five in the first eighteen months she would never need a job again.

She thought about Brisket, on the couch.

She thought, with a clarity that surprised her, *I am alone, and I am not lonely. There is a difference.*

She did not yet know whether the difference would hold up for a year, or for five years, or for ten. She did not yet know whether she would, somewhere along the way, find that the work was no longer the work and that the loneliness was no longer not loneliness. She suspected she might. She also suspected that she would deal with that, if it came, when it came.

For tonight there was the snow. There was the book. There was the dog. There was the eight hundred and ninety dollars a month, signed in a Dayton office park by a man she had earned the trust of with a checklist and three honest calls and one acknowledgement that her product could not do something.

She read another fifteen pages.

She closed the book.

She turned off the lamp.

She sat in the dark and watched the snow.

---

Priya Ramaswamy, in the third week of November in the first year of the rest of her career, had no idea what she had become part of. The other thousands of people doing the same thing across the country had no idea either. Each of them, individually, simply could.

The industry around them was changing. The firms they had left were beginning to notice or beginning not to notice. The clients they were taking on were finding, for the first time in their working lives, that they could buy enterprise-grade software from a single person who answered her own email and who knew their business by name.

None of that was visible from the attic-office at the top of the converted Victorian on Hamilton Avenue.

What was visible was the snow, and the dog, and the laptop closed on the desk in the next room, and the small lamp Mrs. Chen had turned on in her parlor on the floor below.

The city was quiet.

The first customer had said yes.

Priya Ramaswamy, who had never wanted to be a founder when she was a child and had never wanted to be one in college and had never wanted to be one at Hartwell, sat in the dark in her front room and watched the snow come down on Cincinnati and was, by every measure she could think of and by some she had not yet thought of, content.
