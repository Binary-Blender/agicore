# Chapter 5: Karen's Memo

Karen Voss arrived at the Hartwell Building at six forty-five on a Tuesday morning in February. The lobby was nearly empty. The security desk man, Roland, nodded at her as he had nodded at her every morning for eight years. She nodded back. She rode the elevator to the twenty-third floor — one floor below Davis Hartwell's — and unlocked her office and turned on the lamp on her desk and not the overhead lights, because she did the careful work in lamp-light and she had careful work to do today.

She set her coat on the hook. She set her bag on the chair. She set a paper cup of coffee from the lobby kiosk on the coaster at the corner of the desk.

Then she opened the right-hand drawer and took out the memo.

It was five pages. It was bound with a single brass fastener at the top. The cover page read, in plain Times Roman, *The Talent Migration Problem and What We Should Do About It — K. Voss to D. Hartwell — Confidential — February.*

She had finished the first draft three weeks ago.

She had revised it eleven times.

She had let it sit on her desk for the last fourteen days, in that same drawer, while she did her ordinary work and waited for the sentence at the bottom of page five to either change or settle. It had settled. The sentence read: *we have, at most, two quarters to begin acting on this. After that the curve will be too steep.*

She picked up the memo and laid it flat on her blotter and read it through one more time.

---

She had built the underlying spreadsheet privately, on her personal laptop, at her kitchen table on Sunday afternoons, over the previous four months. Sixty-three rows. Each row was a name. Each name was a person who had left Hartwell Digital in the last fourteen months.

Columns: hire date, departure date, title, tenure, last salary, reason given on exit interview, reason Karen actually believed.

Sixty-three names.

Forty-one of them at the senior-engineer level or above.

Twenty-eight of them — Karen had checked the LinkedIn profiles, the announcements, the small-business filings in Massachusetts and New York and three other states — had started their own firms or joined firms of one to five people.

Eleven had taken in-house roles at clients Hartwell had served.

Six had gone to other consultancies.

The rest had retired, taken family leave, or — in two cases — taken roles at firms Karen had never heard of which she could not, even with a careful search, identify as anything but solo operations behind a small LLC.

Karen had built a column for what she called the *replacement math.* It was the column that had taken the longest. For each senior departure, she had estimated what Hartwell would need to charge a client per hour to replace that person's work at the firm's standard margin. Two hundred dollars per hour of internal engineer cost, plus overhead allocation, plus partner margin, plus risk premium. The number came out to roughly four hundred and twenty dollars per hour. Then she had estimated what the departed engineer would charge that same client, billing directly, with AI augmentation, working from home. Three hundred dollars per hour. Sometimes two-seventy-five.

The departed engineer was forty percent cheaper than Hartwell could match. Without losing money.

She had run the math three different ways. She had run it conservative and generous. She had run it with optimistic assumptions about Hartwell's overhead and with pessimistic ones. The forty-percent gap was the gap. It did not move.

The hourly billable model, she had written on page three, *is not under pressure. It is structurally compromised. The compromise is not visible from inside the model because the model can still close engagements. It is visible only when one looks at what former employees are now charging clients we would have charged twice as much.*

She had circled the word *structurally* in pencil, three weeks ago, and not erased the circle.

---

She drank her coffee. She read pages one through three again. She turned to page four — the proposal pages.

The proposal had four parts.

ONE — restructure compensation. Move from cash-heavy salary to a partnership-style mix: base salary at the seventieth percentile of market, supplemented by a profit-participation pool that paid out quarterly to all employees above a tenure threshold. Top earners at the firm would, in a good year, take home thirty to forty percent more than they currently took home. In a bad year they would take home less. The shape of the income would look like a partner's income, not an employee's.

TWO — create a principal-partner tier. Senior engineers and architects at five-plus years of tenure who chose to opt into this tier would receive equity-style compensation, a seat on a partnership council that met quarterly with Davis, and the authority to select their own engagements within a quota. They would not be billable-hour-managed. They would be revenue-share-managed.

THREE — pilot a four-day workweek for engineering. The firm would commit to a six-month pilot in one office — Karen had suggested Cambridge — with the explicit understanding that if utilization held at eighty percent of the five-day baseline, the pilot would expand firm-wide. The pilot would be announced publicly as an investment in retention.

FOUR — shift from hourly billing to retainer-and-outcome pricing. Hartwell would propose to its top twenty clients a transition over twelve months to multi-year retainer relationships with embedded teams. Pricing would be predictable. The firm would no longer be in the position of needing to defend its rate against a solo engineer's rate. The firm would be selling something a solo engineer could not sell: continuity, depth of team, embedded presence, the safety of a roster.

Karen had ended the memo with one paragraph that she had rewritten the most times.

*None of these moves is sufficient on its own. The combination of all four, taken seriously, would buy Hartwell Digital approximately five years to develop a more complete strategic response. Less than all four will, in my judgment, not be enough. Doing none of these things is, in my judgment, the same as choosing the firm's eventual contraction.*

She read the paragraph one more time.

She closed the memo.

She stood up. She put on her suit jacket. She picked up the memo. She walked to the elevator. She rode up one floor.

---

Davis Hartwell's assistant Maddie greeted her at the door with a small smile.

"He's expecting you. Go on in."

Karen went in.

Davis was at his desk. He was wearing a charcoal suit and a tie she had seen him in three times this month. He stood up to shake her hand, as he always did, and then sat back down and gestured to the chair across from him.

"Karen. The memo."

"The memo."

She set it on the desk in front of him. She sat down.

"Take your time," she said. "I'll wait."

"All right."

He opened it.

She watched him read.

He read carefully. She had not been sure if he would. He had a way, with documents, of sometimes skimming the executive summary and asking the writer to walk him through the rest. This morning he did not do that. He turned to page two and read all of it. He turned to page three and read all of it. He paused at the replacement-math table for what Karen counted as forty seconds. He turned the page.

He read page four — the proposal.

He read page five — the closing paragraph.

He closed the memo.

He set it down on his blotter.

He folded his hands on top of it.

"Karen," he said.

"Davis."

"This is good work."

"Thank you."

"I want to think about it. I want to take it to Geoffrey and the board next week. I'd like to give them a chance to weigh in before I respond formally."

"That's reasonable."

"Will you be okay with that timing."

"Yes."

"All right. Thank you for this."

"Thank you for reading it."

She stood up.

She did not say what she wanted to say, which was: *Davis. Read the closing paragraph again before you take it to Geoffrey. Read it as if your father had written it.* She did not say it because she had been at the firm long enough to know that the closing paragraph was already the most direct thing she could have written, and that saying it more directly to Davis's face would not change what Davis was going to do.

She walked out of the office.

Maddie smiled at her on the way past.

Karen rode the elevator down one floor.

She went into her office.

She closed the door.

She sat at her desk for ten minutes without doing anything.

Then she opened the spreadsheet on her laptop and added two new rows — two senior engineers who had given notice the previous Friday — and she went back to work.

---

The board met the following Wednesday morning.

Karen was not in the room. She had not expected to be. Hartwell Digital's board had five members and Karen, as COO, attended quarterly reviews but not strategic-deliberation sessions. The five members were Davis Hartwell, Geoffrey Stansfield, a retired managing director from a Boston law firm named Margaret Ouellette, a Connecticut family-office principal named Theo Bishop, and an emeritus member — a man named Walter Kessel who had been on the board since 2001 and who attended sporadically. Walter was present this morning, Karen learned later. Walter was eighty-three.

Davis presented the memo. He walked the board through Karen's findings. He walked them through the replacement math. He walked them through the four proposals.

The board reviewed the memo for forty-five minutes.

Karen reconstructed the meeting afterward, partly from what Davis told her and partly from what Margaret Ouellette — who had become, over the years, a quiet friend — told her over lunch the following Friday.

Geoffrey Stansfield had spoken first.

Geoffrey had been on the board since 1998. He had been Edward Hartwell's college roommate. He was seventy-one. He had chaired the board for nineteen years. He was, by every measure that mattered to him and to the rest of the room, a serious man, and what he said in the meeting was a serious man's assessment.

Geoffrey said, in substance: *We have run this firm on the billable-hour model since 1998. The model has worked. The model has survived offshoring. The model has survived the cloud. The model has survived agile. The model will survive AI. The market will normalize. People who leave firms to start firms come back to firms. They always have. The proposals in this memo are sensible in spirit but radical in execution. Profit-sharing across all employees changes the partnership economics in ways the board has not authorized. The principal-partner tier creates a class of employees with claims on the firm that are not currently in our operating agreement. The four-day workweek is, frankly, a fad. The retainer-pricing transition would put twenty years of client relationships at risk. I would not act on any of these. I would, however, encourage Davis to acknowledge Karen's analysis as careful and to monitor the trends she has identified.*

Margaret Ouellette had asked one question. She had asked, *what is the cost of doing nothing if Karen is right.* Davis had answered that he believed the firm could absorb a higher-than-historical turnover rate for another twelve to eighteen months without strategic impact.

Margaret had nodded. She had not pushed.

Theo Bishop, the Connecticut family-office man, had agreed with Geoffrey.

Walter Kessel, the emeritus member, had said little. Margaret would tell Karen, later, that Walter had looked at the memo's closing paragraph for a long time and had set it down without comment.

The vote — when it came — had been unanimous. Five to nothing. The recommendation: *acknowledge Karen Voss's analysis, thank her for it, monitor the trends, do not act on the proposals at this time.*

The meeting had ended at ten forty-five.

Davis had ridden the elevator down to Karen's office at eleven.

---

He knocked on her door.

"Come in."

He came in. He sat down in the chair across from her desk. He did not close the door behind him, which Karen noted, because the things that Davis said with the door open were things he did not consider private.

"Karen."

"Davis."

"The board appreciates your work. They think we should stay the course. I wanted to tell you directly."

She did not speak for a long moment.

She looked at him.

She looked past him, briefly, at the closed door of the office across the hall — the corner office that had been Eduardo Mendez's, before Eduardo had left in October, and that they had not yet filled.

She looked back at Davis.

"Thank you for telling me directly," she said.

"Geoffrey was emphatic. He thinks the model will hold."

"I understand."

"I wanted to come down myself. I didn't want you to hear it from anyone else."

"I appreciate that, Davis."

He nodded.

He stood up.

He stood, awkwardly, for a beat, as if he might say one more thing. He did not say it.

He walked out.

The door stayed open behind him.

Karen sat at her desk for a long time after he left. She did not cry. She did not stand up. She did not call anyone. She did not close her laptop. She sat in her chair and looked at the closed door of Eduardo Mendez's office across the hall, and she thought.

She thought, first, about quitting. She thought about typing the resignation letter that afternoon and giving it to Davis the following morning. The thought lasted perhaps ninety seconds. She put it down.

She thought about who would write the next memo if she left now. The answer was that nobody would. Brian Wexler did not write memos like this. The next layer of senior management did not have the historical view she had. Karen had been the only person in the firm at her level for eight years who had cared enough about the underlying economics to do the spreadsheet work. If she left, the work would not be redone.

She thought about the people still in the firm.

She thought about Marisol in the Cambridge office, who was thirty-eight and was raising two boys and who had told Karen, over coffee in November, that she did not want to start her own thing and that she liked the firm and that she hoped the firm would be okay. She thought about Tom Beardsley in New York, who was sixty-one and who was three years from retirement and who needed those three years. She thought about the four junior engineers she had personally recruited from MIT in the previous two graduating classes who had no idea what was happening above them.

She thought: *if I leave, who is left to argue.*

She decided, on the inside, that she would stay.

She gave herself a number. Fourteen months. She would stay fourteen more months. She would do what she could from inside. If the firm corrected, she would have helped. If the firm did not correct, she would have given the people still in it fourteen months of someone who was paying attention.

She turned to her laptop.

She opened a new spreadsheet.

She titled it *Hartwell Senior Departures — Phase 2 — beginning February.*

She typed the column headers: hire date, departure date, title, tenure, last salary, reason given on exit interview, reason I actually believe.

She saved it.

She closed the laptop.

She picked up her coat.

She went home.

---

*The Tuesday in February that Karen Voss's memo was rejected by the Hartwell Digital board was the day the firm sealed its own future, although none of the people in the boardroom that morning understood that yet. They believed they were exercising prudent oversight. They believed they were defending what worked. They were not, in the strictest sense, wrong. The model had worked. The model had made all of them comfortable. The model had survived every previous wave. It was simply that this wave was not the same shape as the previous ones, and Karen Voss had been the only person in the room who knew it.*

*Geoffrey Stansfield, in the years after, would maintain — privately, to friends in his club — that he had given Davis Hartwell the best advice he knew how to give. He had not, in his telling, been wrong about the model. The model had been wrong about the market. There was, Geoffrey would say, a difference.*

*Karen Voss, in the years after, would not talk about that Tuesday at all. Not in interviews. Not in private. She would let the spreadsheet do the talking, and the spreadsheet would talk to exactly one person, fourteen months later, when Karen left it on her desk in an envelope for whoever came after her.*

*She walked out of the Hartwell Building that February evening at six fifteen. The wind on Federal Street was cold. She buttoned her coat to the throat. She did not look up at the building. She walked to the T at State Street and rode home to Beacon Hill, where she lived alone, and where she made herself a small dinner of soup and bread, and where she sat in her reading chair until ten thirty with a book she had been meaning to finish.*

*The firm continued.*

*For a little while longer.*
