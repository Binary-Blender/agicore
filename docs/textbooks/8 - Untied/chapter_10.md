# Chapter 10: Hartwell, Eighteen Months In

The eighth floor was dark.

Karen Voss noticed it again on Monday morning as she rode the elevator up from the lobby. The button panel had been refitted in November — the eighth and sixteenth floor buttons were no longer illuminated, and a small adhesive sign next to the directory in the lobby read *Floors 8 and 16 consolidated. Please direct visitors to Floor 22 reception.* The sign had been printed by the building's facilities team. Hartwell Digital had not paid for new signage. Hartwell Digital had not paid for very much of anything new in eight months.

The elevator stopped on twenty-two. Karen got out.

The hallway was quiet. It was a quarter past eight. The hallway had been louder, when she had started here eight years ago, at quarter past eight. There had been four open offices on this floor full of associate consultants drinking coffee and arguing about deck templates. Now there were two open offices on this floor and one of them was empty.

She walked to her office at the corner. She set her bag down.

She had been doing the same thing every morning for fourteen months. The same elevator. The same hallway. The same office.

This was the last week.

---

She knew the numbers because she had been keeping them.

Forty-six employees had left in the last twelve months. The firm was at one hundred and seventy-four people. It had been two hundred and twenty when the book had opened, a little over two years ago. Twenty-one percent gone.

The eighth floor had been the architecture practice, which had hollowed first. The sixteenth had been the New York satellite, which had closed at the end of last quarter — the lease had been bought out, the remaining six employees relocated to Boston or laid off.

The pipeline, in the report Karen had read on Friday afternoon, was at eleven million dollars of qualified opportunity. Eighteen months ago it had been thirty-four million. Two of the firm's top three clients — a Boston-based asset manager and a Cambridge biotech — had not renewed at the end of their last engagements. The asset manager had moved its work to a four-person partnership of ex-Hartwell architects in Watertown. The biotech had taken its work in-house, hiring two ex-Hartwell senior engineers as full-time staff.

The board had begun, finally, to ask Davis Hartwell questions he could not answer.

Karen knew the meeting schedule. Geoffrey Stansfield had asked for time with Davis every Friday morning for the last seven Fridays. The meetings had been getting longer.

None of this was Karen's problem anymore.

She had accepted the offer four weeks ago.

Northpoint Studio was a forty-person firm based in Cambridge — partnership model, retainer-based pricing, three senior partners, eight principal-level associates, the rest junior. Structured exactly along the lines Karen had recommended in her memo to Davis a year and a half ago. The chief operating role had been open for three months. Northpoint's founder, a woman named Eve Connolly who had been a partner at a larger firm before founding Northpoint in 2019, had reached out to Karen through a mutual contact. They had met for coffee in late December. Karen had accepted a verbal offer the second week of January. She had given Davis her notice on the first of February. Her last day was Friday.

She had told nobody else, yet, that she was going. Davis had not announced it. She suspected Davis had not announced it because Davis did not know what he was going to say in the announcement.

She sat at her desk and read her email.

---

Davis asked her into his office on Wednesday afternoon.

She walked the corridor to the twenty-fourth floor. She knew this corridor. She had walked it for eight years. She had walked it the day she had been hired, after her interview with Edward Hartwell in this same office. She had walked it on the day Davis took over. She had walked it on the day she delivered her memo, fourteen months ago, and she had walked it on the day Davis came back from the board meeting and told her the board appreciated her work but they would stay the course.

She had walked it on the days she had attended people's farewells. Forty-six of them, in the last twelve months. Some had been at the conference room. Some had been at restaurants. Some had been brief — a handshake, a thanks, a wish. Some had been long. She had attended every one she had been able to.

She walked it now.

Davis's assistant Maddie waved her in.

Davis was standing at his window, looking at the harbor.

He turned when she came in.

He looked tired.

She had not seen him look tired before. He had always been the kind of man whose face did not show fatigue — the Wharton MBA, the squash habit, the careful diet, the eight hours of sleep, the executive calm. The tired had crept up on him over the last six months. He had lost weight. His suit jacket fit loosely across the shoulders. His tie was slightly crooked, which was not his style. There was something in his eyes that had not been there when she had walked this corridor a year ago.

He gestured to the chair across from his desk.

She sat down.

He sat down behind the desk.

He did not pick up a folder. He did not look at his computer. He folded his hands in front of him on the desk and looked at her.

"Karen," he said. "I want to ask you something. I want to ask it honestly. I don't want a polite answer."

"All right."

He looked at the desk for a moment.

He looked back up.

"If you were me. Right now. What would you do."

She let the silence hold for a second.

She had thought about how she would answer this question if it ever came. She had thought about it more than once in the last eighteen months. She had imagined, in different scenarios, the version of Davis who would ask her this question.

She had imagined the version who would ask it angrily.

She had imagined the version who would ask it as a test.

She had not imagined this version.

This version was just asking.

"Davis."

"Yes."

"What I would do — the same thing I told you in the memo. Eighteen months ago. Equity restructuring. A principal-partner tier with real profit participation. Retainer-based pricing instead of hourly billing. Focus on the relationship value and the embedded-team value rather than commodity development work. Restructure the cost base. The model has not changed since I wrote it. The window for executing it has."

"Smaller."

"Smaller."

"How small."

"I don't know. Maybe six months. Maybe twelve. It depends on what the board will let you do, how fast, and whether you can hold the senior partners you have left while you do it."

He nodded slowly.

She kept going.

"There is one new thing, Davis. I want to say it because nobody else is going to."

"Say it."

"You would also need to lay off about forty of the remaining one hundred seventy-four people. The structural decline has already happened. The firm at the size it was is gone. You would be rebuilding the firm at the size it actually is. You cannot get to the partner model from a one-hundred-and-seventy-four-person base. You can get to it from a one-hundred-and-thirty-person base."

"Forty people."

"Forty people. Carefully chosen. Mostly middle layers. You would need to keep the senior architects you have left and the junior bench. You would need to flatten."

"And the cost of the layoff."

"Several million in severance and transition. Painful. But the recurring cost reduction is double-digit millions a year. It buys you the runway to make the larger change."

He looked at his hands.

"My father started this firm with seven people in a basement in Brookline," he said. "He never laid anybody off in nineteen years."

"I know."

"He had a different market."

"He did."

There was a silence.

Davis spoke again.

"Karen. I think it might be too late."

She did not contradict him immediately.

She thought about it.

She said, "It might be. But your father started this firm because he was willing to try things nobody else was. You could still try."

He looked at her for a long moment.

He said, "Thank you, Karen."

She said, "You are welcome."

He did not say what he was going to do.

She stood up.

"Davis."

"Yes."

"Whatever you decide. I want to say — for what it is worth — I think you have a good firm here. I think the people who are still here are still here because they want to be. I think there is something to build with."

"Thank you."

"Good luck."

"You as well."

She walked out of his office for the last time.

---

Friday was her last day.

She came in at seven thirty, the way she always had. She sat at her desk. She did her email. She had eleven messages, most of them administrative — IT closing her accounts, finance settling her final expense report, Brian Wexler confirming the small farewell gathering at four PM.

She cleaned out the drawers.

She had not kept much in this office. She was not a person who personalized an office. There had been a few photographs in the top right drawer — her parents at her college graduation, her cat asleep on a windowsill, a picture of her and her younger brother on a fishing trip in Maine when she had been forty-three and her brother had been thirty-eight. She put the photographs in a small manila envelope and slid it into her bag.

There were three books in the bottom left drawer. She left them. She had read each of them more than once and she had no use for them at Northpoint.

There was a pen, in the top right drawer, that Edward Hartwell had given her on her first day. It was a Cross fountain pen with a black lacquer finish. She picked it up. She looked at it for a moment. She put it in her shirt pocket.

In the top right drawer, behind the photographs, was the spreadsheet.

She pulled it out.

It was forty-three pages, single-spaced, printed at six-point Times. Eighty-seven rows. Names, dates, reasons, destinations, and the brief notes she had written next to each one — *good engineer, kept saying he could do this work for himself,* and *single mother, finally got the four-day-week request denied one too many times,* and *senior architect, told me directly that the math no longer worked for him.* She had been keeping it on her work laptop and printing a hard copy at the end of every quarter. The current printout was from January.

She read the first page slowly.

She read the names of the people who had left in the first months — Priya Ramaswamy, Aliyah Hassan, Eduardo Mendez, Carla Bowen, Tess Lin, the ones from the original wave. She read the names of the people who had left more recently — names that had appeared after her time as COO had started feeling like a long deathwatch.

She did not read all of them. She turned to the last page and read the bottom row, which had been filled in on Monday — *Maria Okafor, principal architect, fourteen years, left for a three-person partnership in Newton.*

She got an envelope from the supply cabinet across the hall.

She came back. She folded the spreadsheet in half. She put it in the envelope. She sealed the envelope. She wrote on the outside, in pen — Edward Hartwell's Cross pen, the one she had carried in her shirt pocket since she had picked it up off her desk that morning — *for whoever comes next.*

She set the envelope on the desk, centered, in the spot where the photographs had been.

She looked at the office for a moment.

She picked up her bag.

She picked up her plant — a small jade plant about the size of her hand that she had brought into the office in her first month and had repotted twice over eight years. She held it in the crook of her arm.

She took her coat off the hook.

She walked out of the office.

---

The farewell was in the eighth-floor break room. The eighth floor was dark, but Brian Wexler in HR had arranged for the lights to be turned on in the break room and the conference room next to it for the afternoon.

Twenty-three people were there when Karen walked in. She knew all of them by first name. Some had been at Hartwell longer than she had been. Some had been hired in the last two years. There were two principal architects, three project managers, four senior engineers, the head of finance, two people from operations, the office manager, Brian Wexler, and a small cluster of mid-level associates who had come up to her over the last year, individually, to say some version of *thank you for what you are trying to do.*

There was a sheet cake on the table. There was a small stack of cards. There was a bottle of sparkling cider and twenty-three plastic cups.

Brian gave a short toast.

"Karen," he said. "You have done more for this firm than most of us know about. We are going to miss you. We hope wherever you go next is a place that listens."

The room said *here, here.*

Karen did not give a speech.

She thanked Brian.

She thanked the people in the room.

She said, "I am going to remember every one of you. I am proud to have worked with you. I am sorry I could not do more."

She walked around the room. She hugged three people — Brian, the office manager, and one of the senior engineers, a man named Russ Coleman who had been at the firm twenty-six years and who had once told her that she was the best COO the firm had ever had. She shook hands with most of the others. She kissed Russ Coleman's wife, who had come for the farewell.

She left at four thirty.

She walked to the elevator.

She rode it down to the lobby.

She walked across the lobby to the revolving door.

She walked out of the Hartwell Building for the last time.

---

It was four forty-five.

The Boston February afternoon was already going gray. The wind on Federal Street was sharp, and Karen pulled her coat tighter and held the jade plant against her chest. She walked east, toward Atlantic. She did not look back at the building.

She walked two blocks. She passed a coffee shop she had gone into perhaps a thousand times. She did not go in.

She found a cab on Atlantic Avenue. The cab pulled over. She opened the door, set the plant on the seat next to her, slid in, and gave her address in Beacon Hill.

The cab pulled away from the curb.

She looked out the window at the gray afternoon.

She was not sad. She was not relieved. She was something in between — the something that people who have done their best inside an institution that would not move feel when they leave. She had told the truth to Davis in his office on Wednesday. She had left the envelope on her desk. She had thanked the twenty-three people in the eighth-floor break room. She had walked out under her own power and with her plant in her arm.

She was fifty-one years old. She had eight years left, maybe ten, of the kind of full-tilt work she liked best. She had a job to start on Monday at a firm that knew, already, what Davis Hartwell had not been able to learn. She had her health, her plant, her brother in Maine, the pen.

The cab turned onto Cambridge Street.

The river came into view briefly as the cab crossed Bowdoin.

Karen Voss did not look back at the Financial District.

---

*Hartwell Digital would file for Chapter 11 reorganization fourteen months after Karen Voss's last day. The firm's name would be sold for less than the value of the office furniture. The remaining eighty-eight employees would scatter — some to other firms, some to solo work, some to in-house roles at former Hartwell clients. Davis Hartwell would not work in software again. The book has nothing more to say about him. He was not bad. He was not lazy. He was not stupid. He was simply unable to see what was in front of him, and the firm could not survive his unseeing. The other CEO, two hundred miles to the west, in a converted warehouse on the Mississippi River, was at that moment writing a thank-you note to a former employee who had just sent her flowers. Nobody, by then, was even talking about Hartwell.*
