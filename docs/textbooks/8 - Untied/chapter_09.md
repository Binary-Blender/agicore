# Chapter 9: Partners Not Resources

The restaurant was closed for the night.

Carlo had put a hand-lettered sign on the door at four — *closed for a private event* — and locked the front. The dining room was lit by the low pendant lamps over the bar and one wall of votives along the booths. The kitchen was running. Carlo was in there in his apron, prepping a meal he had been planning for two weeks, humming to himself in a way that meant he was happy.

Maya Holm-Russo sat in the back booth with her chief operating officer Aaron Park and her head of people Renata Diaz.

It was a Tuesday in October. The wind outside was the kind of cold Minneapolis wind that meant snow was coming inside the week.

Maya had a manila folder on the table in front of her. The folder was three quarters of an inch thick. She had been adding to it for six months.

She did not open it yet.

"I want to walk you through where I've gotten," she said. "It will take me an hour or two. I want you both to interrupt whenever you have a question. I do not want you to be polite. I have been carrying this around alone for too long and I want the holes found tonight."

Aaron nodded. Renata pulled her notebook out of her bag.

Carlo came out of the kitchen with three glasses of water on a small tray.

"You want me to start the food in twenty," he said, "or do you want longer."

"Forty," Maya said.

"Forty," Carlo said. He went back to the kitchen.

Maya opened the folder.

---

"The first move worked," she said. "I want to start by saying that. Six months ago we did the perks-first thing. Pay restructure, profit-sharing across the company, four-day weeks for engineering, flexible schedules, doubled parental leave, five-year sabbatical. The bleeding slowed. We kept somewhere around half the people who I think would have otherwise left. Renata, your numbers, please."

Renata flipped a page in her notebook.

"Of the twenty-three people I had on a leading-edge attrition watchlist in February, twelve are still here. Of those twelve, nine have signed onto the four-day pilot. Four have taken on profit-sharing-eligible roles they would not have taken before. Two would have left and told me so."

"Good," Maya said. "So that's the win. The win is real."

"And the loss," Aaron said.

"And the loss is also real," Maya said. "The eleven who left in the last six months — Renata, who were they."

Renata read from her notebook.

"Six senior architects. Three principal engineers. Two senior project managers. The architects went to solo work or to two-to-five-person partnerships. The principals same. The project managers — one to a competitor, one to an in-house role at a client."

"Right," Maya said. "So the perks worked on the middle. The perks did not work on the senior layer. And the senior layer is what we cannot replace."

Aaron nodded.

"We knew that going in," he said.

"We did," Maya said. "But I needed to see it. Now I have seen it."

She took a sip of water.

"Here is the thing I have been working on for six months. I want to lay it out in pieces. I want you to push on every piece."

Renata uncapped her pen.

---

"First piece," Maya said. "We have to stop trying to compete with solos on price."

Aaron leaned back.

"Go on."

"A solo at three hundred dollars an hour can charge the client less, in total, than we can charge them at two hundred. Because the solo has no overhead. The solo has no project manager. The solo has no salesperson. The solo has no head of people, no COO, no CEO. The solo eats their own benefits. The solo is one person paying for one person.

"We are one hundred and twenty-eight people paying for one hundred and twenty-eight people, plus this building, plus all of the things that make us a firm. We cannot win the price fight. We should not try."

"Agreed," Aaron said. "I have been saying that."

"You have. I have been agreeing with you slowly. Now I am agreeing with you out loud."

Renata wrote something in her notebook.

"Second piece," Maya said. "Compete on what one person cannot offer. I want to be specific. I do not want to say *value* and *relationships* because those words are mush. I want to name the things."

She held up her hand and started counting on her fingers.

"One. Continuous embedded relationship. Five years of the same five people on a client's account, not a rotating cast.

"Two. Dedicated account teams with named members the client knows by name. The client's CIO knows our senior architect's kids' names. The client's CFO knows our project manager's golf handicap. Those things matter and a solo cannot do them at scale.

"Three. Multi-year partnership engagements. Not statements of work. Not project-based. Year-long, two-year, three-year, five-year retainer relationships where the client and the firm grow together.

"Four. On-site presence on forty-eight hours notice. If a client's plant in Cedar Rapids has a crisis, we can put three of our people on a plane Friday morning. A solo cannot. A solo with a kid in school cannot. A solo with a sick parent cannot.

"Five. Variable staffing. Up and down with the client's needs. A solo is one capacity, all the time. We are a hundred and twenty-eight capacities we can rearrange.

"Six. The senior-junior pairing. Architect plus mid-level plus junior dev plus project manager on the same engagement. The client gets the senior's brain and the junior's hands and the PM's calendar. A solo is one brain, one set of hands, one calendar.

"Seven. The safety of a team behind every commitment. If one of our people gets sick, the work continues. If a solo gets sick, the work stops. The client knows this. Clients with real risk profiles cannot, structurally, depend on solos for the things that matter most."

She put her hand down.

"That is our advantage. Those are the seven things we have that a solo does not have and cannot have. We should stop apologizing for the fact that we are not a solo. We should sell, and price, and structure our work around those seven things."

Aaron had been listening with his head tilted slightly to the side.

"That changes our pricing model," he said.

"Yes."

"Retainer-based. Not hourly."

"Yes."

"Some of our clients will not buy that."

"Some of our clients will not buy that. Some of them will. The ones who will buy it will pay more, more reliably, and stay longer."

"And the ones who won't."

"We let them go to solos. We will refer them to solos. We will be the firm that knows which solos to refer them to."

Renata stopped writing and looked up.

"That is a big move, Maya."

"It is."

---

Carlo brought out the first plates. Roasted squash with sage butter on small white plates. He set them down without speaking. He brought a basket of bread. He brought a bottle of wine and three glasses. He poured one glass of wine and two glasses of mineral water. He knew which was which without asking.

He went back to the kitchen.

Aaron took a bite of squash.

"Third piece," he said. "Go."

Maya set down her fork.

"Third piece. We change the internal model. Employees are not resources. They are partners. I want to make this concrete. I do not want to say it as a slogan."

She pulled a single sheet of paper out of the folder.

"New tier. Senior Partner. Specific elements.

"Profit-sharing. Real. Not a token bonus pool. A percentage of firm profit, allocated annually, that becomes a meaningful fraction of the partner's total compensation. I am thinking in the range of thirty to fifty percent of base for a senior partner in a profitable year.

"Autonomy over engagement selection. A senior partner picks which engagements they take. We do not assign them. We propose. They accept or decline. If they decline often enough that they are not generating revenue, we have a conversation. But the default is that they choose.

"Equity-style compensation. Restricted units that vest over five years. Real participation in the firm's value, not just its profit.

"No billable-hour quotas. The senior partner is paid as a partner, not as a billable resource. We trust them to make their hours valuable. If a senior partner takes a week to write a thought-leadership piece that generates three new clients in the next quarter, that week was worth more than any forty hours of billable would have been.

"Seat at strategic decisions. Senior partners are at the table when we talk about firm direction. They are not just executing what the executive team decides. They help decide.

"Mentorship expectation. This is the only hard one. Senior partners are expected to mentor junior staff. To teach. To make the next generation. That is the responsibility that comes with the autonomy."

She set the sheet down.

"That is the tier. I want it open to current senior employees who qualify. I want it open to former employees who left, tried something else, and want to come back."

Renata had been very still.

She said, "Maya. Do you mean Damon."

"I mean Damon. I mean any of them. Damon texted me three weeks ago. We had coffee on Tuesday. He is not ready to come back. He is close. He will be ready within six months."

"You did not tell me."

"I am telling you now."

Renata wrote *Damon — six months* in her notebook and underlined it twice.

"Maya," Aaron said. "If we open it to returnees we have to be careful. We cannot make them come back at junior. They have been senior."

"We will not. They come back at the Senior Partner tier directly. Whatever their previous role was, that is the floor. We treat them generously. We do not make them earn back what they have already earned."

"That is going to create some noise inside the firm."

"It is."

"People who stayed are going to ask why the leavers get the partner tier first."

"They will. We are going to give the partner tier to the people who stayed too, on the same terms, at the same time. The Senior Partner tier is not for returnees specifically. It is for senior people. Some of them stayed. Some of them left. The tier does not distinguish."

Aaron thought about this for a moment.

"All right."

"Renata."

"All right too. But I am going to need a communications plan."

"You will have one. I want you to write it."

Renata wrote *comms plan — me* in her notebook.

---

"Fourth piece," Maya said. "The one I keep wanting to skip past because it sounds soft."

She took a sip of wine.

"Stop treating the solo economy as a threat. Start treating it as an industry."

Aaron raised an eyebrow.

"Meaning."

"Meaning some of our best future hires will be ex-solos. Some of our best future referral partners will be solos. The book of business comes through relationships, not through capturing market share against people we used to work with. When we refer work to Three Rivers we strengthen our brand. When Three Rivers refers work to us — which they have done twice this quarter, by the way, Aaron, did you see that — we win work we would not have won otherwise.

"The solo founders are not the enemy. They are the rest of the ecosystem. Some of our clients want a firm. Some of our clients want a solo. Some of our clients want both. We can be the firm in that picture without trying to drive the solos out of it. We will be a better firm if we are not trying to drive them out of it."

"That is a culture change," Renata said.

"It is. We have been training our people for fifteen years to think of the firm as the unit. The unit is changing. The unit is now the firm plus the network around the firm. We have to act like that is true."

Aaron looked at his glass of water.

"You know what this is going to cost us."

"I know."

"What is the budget."

"Year one, I am projecting north of four million in incremental cost. Profit-sharing pool, equity vesting, the comms work, the rebrand of how we go to market, the price-model transition, the on-site-presence infrastructure, two new senior hires in business development for the partnership model, the change-management costs internally. Roughly four point two."

"And the margin impact."

"We will run at seven percent operating margin for at least two years. We have been running at sixteen. So we are accepting a structural margin compression for the medium term."

"And on the other side of those two years."

"On the other side, if the model works, we are a healthier firm at higher margins than we have ever run, with a partner base that is impossible for a competitor to poach, with a referral network that feeds us, and with a price model that does not rise and fall with the labor market."

Aaron was quiet for a long moment.

Then he said, "I have been waiting for this conversation for ten months."

Maya looked at him.

"I have been waiting to have it," she said.

---

The board met Wednesday of the following week. Stillwater's board was three people — Maya, Aaron, and Lena Schultz, the Twin Cities investor in her late sixties who had put in early capital fifteen years ago and who had walked two of her other portfolio firms through structural transitions over four decades.

Lena drove herself in from Wayzata. She arrived ten minutes early, walked the floor, said hello to two senior engineers whose names she remembered, drank a cup of coffee, and sat down at the conference table at four exactly.

Maya did not re-pitch the plan. She handed Lena the four-page summary, the financial projections, and the comms timeline that she and Renata had drafted that week. Lena read for twenty-three minutes. Aaron sat across the table with his coffee. Renata sat beside him with her notebook closed.

When Lena was done she set the papers down.

The room was quiet.

Then she said, quietly, "Maya. This is the most expensive thing you have ever done. And it is also the only thing I think can possibly work. Do it."

Maya said, "The budget is north of four million in year one. We will be running a seven percent operating margin instead of sixteen for at least two years."

Lena said, "Yes. Do it."

Aaron Park said, "I have been waiting for this conversation for ten months. I'm in."

Lena pulled a small leather notebook out of her bag and wrote down the names of the four written quarterly reports she wanted to see — partner inflows and outflows, partner-tier compensation in practice, the price-model transition's revenue impact, and client retention at senior-partner-led accounts versus legacy ones. She closed the notebook. She stood up.

"I'm going to drink a glass of wine downstairs at Carlo's and then I'm going to drive home. Maya, you should come too."

"I will. In an hour."

"In an hour."

Lena walked out.

Aaron and Renata stayed at the table for another twenty minutes, working through the comms outline. Then they left.

Maya sat alone in the conference room for a few minutes longer.

She closed the folder.

She thought about her father, briefly, who had worked the ore docks until his back gave out and had retrained as a welder, and who had told her once, when she was nineteen, that the hardest thing about any work that mattered was not figuring out the right answer. The hardest thing was waiting until you had earned the right to be heard before you said it.

She thought, *I think I have just earned the right.*

She put the folder under her arm and walked down to the restaurant.

---

The restaurant was quiet.

It was nine thirty. Carlo had closed the kitchen. The dining room was empty except for two of his line cooks wiping down the surfaces and a single dishwasher running in the back.

Maya sat at the bar.

Carlo poured her a glass of wine. He poured himself a glass. He came around to the bar side and sat on the stool next to her.

"Tell me what you decided," he said.

She told him, briefly. The substance. The partner tier. The board. The four million. The seven percent margin. The November all-hands.

He listened.

He did not interrupt.

When she was done he turned his glass slowly in his hand.

He said, "That's it. That's the thing."

She said, "That's the thing I think it is too."

They sat at the bar in the empty restaurant for a long time, drinking, not talking. Outside the window the first snow of the season had started to fall in small dry flakes that did not stick. The street lamps on the avenue were yellow and the air past the windows was the deep blue of a November Minneapolis night.

Carlo got up and went to the kitchen and came back with a small plate of olives.

He set it between them.

They did not say anything else for a while.

---

*Maya Holm-Russo would describe the night she presented the Senior Partner program to her board, in interviews she would give years later, as the night she stopped being scared of the future. The future, she would say in those interviews, was not something happening TO her firm. The future was something her firm could be part of building. The board had said yes. The capital was committed. The path was hard. She was clear-eyed about the hard part. But she was no longer scared, and the absence of fear, she would tell the interviewer, was its own kind of freedom.*
