# Chapter 2: The Other Office

Maya Holm-Russo was at her desk by seven fifteen on the Monday morning that Aaron Park came up the iron staircase with his coffee mug in one hand and his laptop in the other and the look on his face that meant he had something he wanted to walk her through before the rest of the office arrived.

The Stillwater Software office occupied four floors of a converted brick warehouse on the east side of Nicollet Island, with the Mississippi visible through the tall arched windows on the river side. Maya's office was on the third floor, in a corner, behind a glass wall that she had never asked to have. The previous tenant had built it. Maya had inherited it. She kept the door open most days.

It was open now. Aaron knocked anyway.

"Morning," he said.

"Morning."

"Got a minute."

"I have an hour. Come in."

Aaron Park was forty-seven. He was her chief operating officer and had been for nine of the seventeen years she had been running the firm. He was Korean-American, second-generation, a Best Buy alumnus, methodical in everything he did. When he wanted to talk to Maya about something difficult he always started by sitting down without saying what it was about. He did that now. He set his coffee on the table between them and opened the laptop and then closed it without looking at the screen.

"Ben Kowalczyk is leaving," he said.

Maya did not move for a moment.

"Tell me."

"He told me Friday afternoon. He asked me to hold it over the weekend so he could tell you himself this morning. He's outside. He was going to come up at eight. I wanted you to have a heads up."

"How firm is it."

"It's firm. He's giving us four months. He's not unhappy. He wanted me to make sure I said that part."

"Four months."

"Four months. He says February."

Maya looked past Aaron at the river. The October light was clean and low and pale. There was a tug pushing a barge upstream. The leaves on the cottonwoods along the bank were yellow and red and almost gone.

"All right," she said. "Send him up at eight. And Aaron — thank you."

"For what."

"For telling me before he came up. I want to know how I want to handle it before he sits down."

"I figured."

Aaron picked up his coffee and stood up.

"Maya."

"Yes."

"He's good. I don't want to lose him."

"I know."

Aaron nodded and went back down the staircase.

---

Maya sat at her desk for the next forty minutes and did not do any work.

She thought about Ben Kowalczyk.

He was thirty-eight. Polish-American, third-generation Minneapolis, the kind of person who had grown up on the north side and gone to the University of Minnesota for engineering and never seen any reason to live anywhere else. He had been at Stillwater six years. He was one of the firm's best backend architects. He led the integration practice for their manufacturing clients. He was the kind of engineer that clients asked for by name.

She had hired him personally. She remembered the interview. He had asked her, in his quiet way, what the firm was for. Not what it did. What it was *for*. She had told him she had started Stillwater because she had wanted to build software for the kinds of Midwestern companies that the coast didn't bother with — companies that made things, companies whose CIOs answered their own phones, companies whose CEOs would shake your hand and look you in the eye and remember your name a year later. Ben had nodded once. He had accepted the offer two days after.

She thought about what she knew about him personally. He was married to a woman named Theresa who taught second grade. They had two kids. He played hockey in a Tuesday-night league. He brought his dog into the office sometimes, a black lab named Roosevelt who slept under his desk.

She thought about what Aaron had said. *He's not unhappy. He wanted me to make sure I said that part.*

She got up from her desk and walked to the window and looked down at the parking lot. Ben's car was there. A grey Subaru with a hockey-stick rack. He had been at his desk early too.

She thought: *if he is not unhappy and he is still leaving, then he is leaving because the math has changed.*

She had been reading about this for the better part of a year. Not in trade press. In economics journals. She had a subscription to two of them that she paid for personally. She read them on Sunday mornings with her coffee at the kitchen table while Carlo prepped for his Sunday brunch service. She had been watching the labor-market signals out of the larger AI-augmented firms — productivity curves on individual senior engineers, the per-engineer revenue numbers at firms that were transparent about them, the survey data on independent contractors in the software trades. She had not put her conclusions on paper yet. She had not said any of it out loud to Aaron.

She had been waiting, she realized, for the first instance.

This was the first instance.

She walked back to her desk and sat down and waited for Ben.

---

At eight he knocked.

"Come in, Ben."

He came in. He was wearing a flannel shirt and jeans and the same canvas work jacket he had been wearing in the parking lot of the warehouse the first day she had given him a tour. He sat down across from her.

"Maya."

"Ben."

"I'm sure Aaron told you."

"He did. I appreciated that he did. I assume you wanted him to."

"I did."

"All right."

He took a breath.

"I'm leaving in February. It's me and Eli Choudhry and Marcia Reuter. We've been talking about it for a while. We're going to set up a three-person studio. Manufacturing clients, mostly. I — "

"Ben."

"Yes."

"Let me say one thing first. Then you say everything you came to say."

"Okay."

"You don't owe me an explanation. You don't owe me a sales pitch on why it's the right move. You came up here on a Monday morning to tell me, four months early, that you and two of my best people are leaving. That is a courtesy I have not earned and that I am going to remember for a long time. Thank you."

He looked at her for a moment. He did not say anything.

"All right," he said finally. "Thank you."

"Now. Tell me what you came to tell me. And then I want to take you to lunch. At Carlo's. Are you free at noon."

"I am."

"Good."

He sat back in his chair.

He talked for twenty minutes. He told her, plainly, that he loved Stillwater. He told her that he was not leaving because of anything Stillwater had done or had failed to do. He told her that he had been doing math at his kitchen table on Sunday mornings for six months. He told her that the three of them — himself, Eli, Marcia — had built a small spreadsheet model. They had run it forwards and backwards. They had assumed conservative billing rates and pessimistic ramp times. The model said that they would clear, in their first year, somewhere between sixty and ninety percent more take-home pay than they were making at Stillwater. The model said that in their second year, if they kept their headcount at three and managed their pipeline carefully, they would clear more than double.

"That's the math," he said. "And the math has been the math for six months. I kept thinking I would find a hole in it. I haven't found one."

"All right."

"I want to be clear about one more thing."

"Go ahead."

"The reason I'm telling you in October instead of in January is because I respect you. And because I think there are going to be more people doing this kind of math. I don't think we're the only ones."

She nodded.

"That's what I assumed."

"I figured you would have figured that out."

"Ben. Will you have lunch with me and let me ask you three questions over lunch."

"Yes."

"Good. Noon at Carlo's. I'll drive."

He stood up.

"Thank you, Maya."

"Thank you, Ben."

He left. She heard his boots on the staircase going down.

She did not move for a full minute.

Then she pulled her phone out of her bag and texted her husband.

*Two for lunch at noon. Me and Ben Kowalczyk. He's leaving in February. I want a quiet booth and your good coffee.*

The reply came back inside thirty seconds.

*Back booth, by the kitchen door. Coffee will be on.*

---

Carlo Russo's restaurant was a small Northeast Minneapolis place on East Hennepin called simply Carlo's, in white letters on a green awning over a glass storefront. Twelve tables inside, an open kitchen behind a marble counter, exposed brick on one wall and old framed black-and-white photos of his grandfather's village in Calabria on the other. He served breakfast and lunch and closed by three in the afternoon, six days a week. Carlo had run the place for eleven years. He had run a bigger restaurant in Chicago before that. He had cashed in and moved to Minneapolis the year he and Maya had decided they were going to be married. He liked the smaller place. He liked knowing every regular. He liked being able to walk home for dinner.

Maya parked in the alley at five to twelve. Ben was already at the back booth. Carlo was behind the counter, in his white shirt and apron, plating eggs for the late-breakfast table by the window. He looked up as Maya came in and nodded at her — the small, quick nod that meant *I see you, the booth is yours, I'm not going to hover.*

Maya slid in across from Ben.

"Coffee will be over in a minute," she said.

"Good."

Carlo brought the coffee himself. He set the two cups down. He looked at Ben.

"Ben. Good to see you."

"You too, Carlo."

Carlo looked at Maya for half a second and his eyes did a small thing — a private acknowledgement, a *I'm here if you need me, I'll otherwise be at the counter* — and he went back to the kitchen.

Ben smiled.

"He's a good man."

"He is."

She lifted her coffee. She set it down.

"Three questions, Ben. I told you upstairs I had three. I want to ask them and then I want us to have lunch and I won't ask anything else."

"All right."

"First question. What did you see, six months ago, that made you start running the math."

He thought about it.

"It wasn't one thing. I had been using the toolkit — the same one everybody else at the firm uses, the orchestrator and the chat and the codegen and all of it — for about a year. And one afternoon I was on a call with a client. The Whitfield project. The one we did last spring. And the client said something about how the deliverables were coming in faster than they had expected. And I looked at my own work and I realized that the thing I had built for them in three weeks would have taken me ten weeks two years ago. And it wasn't the firm's productivity that had gone up. It was *mine*. I was the one doing the work. And I started to think — if it's me doing the work, and the work is taking a quarter of what it used to take, and the client is paying the same rate, then where is all the value going."

"Into firm overhead and into the firm's margin."

"Right. And then I started thinking, what if I billed the client directly and kept ninety percent of what they paid. And then I started running the math."

"All right. Second question. What would have kept you here."

He took a long breath.

"For me personally — I want to be honest — nothing. I want this. I want to do this with Eli and Marcia. I want to see what I can do with two people I picked, working only on the things we want to work on, with no firm overhead between me and the client. I want it. I'm not running from Stillwater. I'm running toward something."

"All right."

"But Maya — I think you should know this. Other people are doing the same math. They're not all coming to the same answer I'm coming to. Some of them are going to want to be alone. Some of them are going to want to stay. Some of them are going to want what we want — small partnerships. I think the answer for keeping the people who want to stay isn't going to be 'compete with our math.' You can't. Stillwater can't be cheaper than three people in a loft. The answer is going to be something else. I don't know what."

"All right."

"That's the most honest thing I can give you."

"It is. Thank you."

She drank some of her coffee.

"Third question. Will you tell me, when you're ready, who else is doing the same math."

"Not today."

"I know not today."

"I will tell you in January. Two weeks before the cluster resigns. I owe Stillwater that. I'll tell Aaron, and I'll give Aaron names and dates and what we know. Will that work."

"That works."

"I won't tell you all of them. I only know some of them. But I'll tell you the ones I know."

"Good."

She let the coffee warm her hands.

"That's three. That's all I had."

"Okay."

"Now I'm hungry. I think Carlo made the porchetta sandwich this morning. Have you had it."

"I haven't."

"Have it."

They had lunch. They talked about Ben's kids. They talked about Roosevelt the dog. They talked about the Wild's season prospects. Carlo brought a small plate of cannoli at the end, on the house. He sat down with them for two minutes and asked Ben how his hockey league was going. He did not say a word about anything else. Then he got up and went back to the kitchen and started prepping for tomorrow's breakfast.

Ben paid for his own lunch. He insisted.

They walked out together onto East Hennepin Avenue at one fifteen. The October sun was thin and clean. The maples on the boulevard were the color of new copper. A bus went by. The cold was just starting to be the cold that meant winter rather than fall.

"Maya."

"Yes."

"Thank you for handling this the way you handled it."

"Ben. I have not started handling it. I have started listening to it. That is a different thing."

He smiled.

"Fair."

"Walk you to the corner."

They walked to the corner. He went left toward the office. She went right toward the parking lot.

She got into her car and sat for a moment with the engine off. She watched Ben walk down the sidewalk in the direction of the warehouse — the same canvas jacket, his hands in his pockets, his shoulders square. She thought: *that is a man who already lives in the next chapter of his life. There is nothing I could have offered today that would have changed that.*

She started the car. She pulled out into the street. She drove two blocks. She pulled over. She picked up her phone.

She called Aaron Park.

"Aaron."

"Maya."

"This is the leading edge. There will be more. We need to think about what we're going to do."

There was a small silence on the other end.

"I've been waiting for you to say that."

"I know."

"I'll clear my Wednesday afternoon. You and me. Long block. We'll start."

"Yes. And Aaron — don't tell anybody else yet. Just you and me to start. I want to be sure I'm seeing what I think I'm seeing before I say it out loud to anyone else."

"Understood."

She hung up.

She sat at the wheel for another half minute. Then she pulled back out into traffic and drove the rest of the way to the office.

---

She did not get much done that afternoon.

She sat at her desk. She tried to write an email to a client about a contract renewal. She wrote the same sentence three times. She closed the email without sending it. She got up and walked the floor — down to the second floor where the senior engineering bullpen was, past Ben's empty desk (Ben was in a client meeting), past the kitchen where two of the integration engineers were arguing about a deployment script. She watched her people. She tried to see them the way she had been seeing them for nine years and to also see them the new way, the way Ben's morning had handed her, the way the spreadsheet on Ben's kitchen table had described them.

She thought about her own history.

She was fifty-three. She had been born in Duluth, the only child of a high-school librarian and a longshoreman who had worked the ore docks until his back gave out and he had retrained as a welder. She had been the first person in her family to go to college. She had done two engineering degrees — undergraduate at the University of Minnesota, master's at Carnegie Mellon. She had worked four years at a defense contractor in Pittsburgh and three years at a software firm in Chicago and then, in 2008, in the worst possible year to start a company, she had come home and started Stillwater Software with her friend Joseph Velasquez.

Joseph had been a wonderful partner. He had cashed out in 2017 to buy a vineyard in Sonoma. He sent her a case of his Syrah every year at Christmas. They still talked once a month.

She had been alone at the top of the firm for almost eight years now. Aaron was the closest thing she had to a partner. Carlo was the closest thing she had to a person she did not have to be the CEO around.

She had no children. She and Carlo had decided not to have any. She did not regret it.

She read economics for fun. She read labor-market economics in particular. She had read it long before there was any reason to. She had read it because the structure of how people came together to do work was the most interesting question she could think of. She had read it because she had built a company and she wanted to understand the rules of the game she was playing.

The rules of the game, she thought, had just changed.

She did not know what the new rules were.

She knew that the time horizon was short. She knew that she had caught the first instance instead of the seventh. She knew that Aaron was already there with her, which meant she had at least one person to think with. She knew that her board — Aaron and Lena Schultz — was small enough to move fast. She knew that her balance sheet was healthy. She knew that her clients trusted her.

She did not yet know what she was going to do.

She knew she was going to do something.

---

At five o'clock she stood at her office window.

The October sun was going down over the warehouse roofs across the river. The Mississippi was dark and slow. A train was crossing the trestle bridge to the south. The light on the water was the color of a brass bell.

She thought about her firm. She thought about the one hundred and forty-five people in it. She thought about which ones were already doing the math, and which ones had not yet started, and which ones never would.

She thought: *I have always known the labor market would move on me one day. I had not assumed it would move this fast.*

She thought: *the first thing I have to do is admit I am working on this. To myself, to Aaron, to the board. The second thing I have to do is figure out what I am working on.*

She thought: *I do not have to figure that out today.*

She turned off the light in her office and picked up her bag and walked down the iron staircase and out the front door of the warehouse onto Main Street Northeast.

She walked the eight blocks home.

Carlo was already there. He had made a small fish stew. They ate at the kitchen table with a candle between them and Maya did not say one word about the firm and Carlo did not ask. He understood that there would be a night, in a week or a month, when she would tell him what she had been working on. He had been married to her long enough to know that.

After dinner she sat on the couch with a glass of his Syrah and an issue of the *Journal of Labor Economics* that had been sitting on her side table for three weeks. She read for an hour. She did not retain much of what she read. But the act of reading it — of being a person who read these journals — anchored her.

She slept well that night. Better than she had expected.

---

By the end of that same week, Maya Holm-Russo had begun a conversation with herself that she would continue for the next fourteen months. Ben Kowalczyk's was the first resignation. It would not be the last.

She did not yet know what the second move would have to be. She would not know for almost a year. But the first move — the recognition itself, the willingness to see the pattern in the first instance instead of the seventh — that move she had already made, on a Monday morning in October, in a small Italian restaurant on East Hennepin, over coffee that her husband had poured himself.

The firm, two hundred miles east of her, that would not make this move was at that moment going about its day. Its CEO was at his desk. Its COO was finalizing a memo. Its senior engineers were preparing their resignation letters. None of those things were known to Maya Holm-Russo, who had problems of her own.

But she had begun.

That was the thing that mattered.
