# Chapter 8: The Solo Years

Damon Wright sat at his kitchen table at eleven on a Wednesday night in April and watched the steam rise off a cup of coffee he should not have been drinking.

The apartment was on the third floor of a brick building in Northeast Minneapolis, two blocks from a bakery and four blocks from the river. The kitchen table doubled as his desk. The desk, in turn, doubled as the only flat surface in the apartment large enough to hold both a laptop and a second monitor and a stack of paper, so most of the time it held all three.

He had two proposals due in the morning. He had three invoices to send. He had a follow-up email to a client that had been sitting in his drafts folder for four days because he had not found the right tone.

He took a long sip of coffee.

The math, on paper, looked excellent. Damon had been a senior architect at Stillwater for seven years. He had left in February. He had set his rate at three hundred dollars an hour, which was fair for his level, and he had picked up six active clients in the first ten weeks, which was more than he had projected. At thirty billable hours a week for fifty weeks a year that was four hundred and fifty thousand dollars of revenue. Less his sixty thousand in operating costs — health insurance, accountant, legal, software, the new laptop, the LLC formation, the bookkeeping software, the proposal-management software, the CRM, the conference travel, the membership in the Twin Cities consultant network he had joined because somebody had told him he should — that left three hundred and ninety thousand dollars of take-home. More than he had ever made.

That was the math on paper.

The math in practice was that he was billing eighteen to twenty-two hours a week, not thirty. The other thirty to thirty-five hours of his work week were going to things that did not show up on an invoice.

He had counted them once on a yellow legal pad. Eighteen hours of sales — proposals, prospect calls, follow-ups, networking events he attended because he had read in a book that you had to attend networking events, networking events he sat through with a Diet Coke and a polite smile and left from with his shoulders aching from the small smile-muscles that were not his. Six hours of bookkeeping and contracts. Four hours of collections — clients took thirty days to pay, then forty-five, then sixty, and he had to chase them politely without sounding like he was chasing them. Two hours of tax and legal admin. Plus the proposals.

Fifty to fifty-five hours total. Twenty billable.

His effective hourly rate, run through that filter, was closer to a hundred and ninety thousand a year. Which was, almost exactly, what he had made at Stillwater on a forty-hour week with benefits, a 401(k) match, paid time off, and other people doing the sales and the bookkeeping and the collections.

He drank his coffee.

He thought, *I used to leave Stillwater at five thirty.*

He had not exercised in two months. The gym membership was still being charged on his credit card; he had not been since February. He had not seen his college friends — three guys from Georgia Tech, all in different cities now — in six months. Bri, his girlfriend, had been patient. Bri taught third grade at a school in South Minneapolis and lived in a small apartment across town. She came over on Saturdays. She had started, recently, to ask questions. Not pointed ones. Just *how are you sleeping* and *when's the last time you took a Saturday off.*

Damon opened his laptop.

The two proposals were for clients he did not love. The first was a financial-services firm in Eden Prairie that wanted a custom dashboard built on a stack he did not particularly want to work in. The second was a logistics company in Brooklyn Park that wanted a data-integration project that was going to be one-third technical and two-thirds politics. Both were good money. Both would consume him for the next four months.

He stared at the blinking cursor.

He thought about the client follow-up email. He thought about the three invoices. He thought about the gym. He thought about Bri.

He took another sip of coffee.

He started typing.

Aliyah Hassan was sitting on the edge of her bed in her apartment in Loring Park, on a Sunday night in July, with her phone pressed to her ear.

The apartment was a studio. She had picked it because the rent was eleven hundred dollars and because she could see a slice of the lake from the kitchen window if she leaned. She had been there for eight months. The walls were almost bare. She had hung up two prints from a thrift store and her diploma from the University of Minnesota and that was all.

Her mother's voice was warm and tired on the other end of the line.

"So how is the week, baby."

"It was fine, Mom. I had three demos. Two went well. One went badly."

"What was the bad one."

"The owner of a deli in Saint Paul. He kept telling me he already had a system. I asked him what the system was. He said the system was his wife. I think his wife is the system."

Halima Hassan laughed.

"You can't compete with the wife."

"I can't compete with the wife."

There was a small silence. Aliyah could hear her mother's television in the background — a Somali news channel her mother kept on at low volume in the evenings.

"Aliyah."

"Yes, Mom."

"Are you happy."

Aliyah pulled the phone away from her ear for a second and looked at the ceiling. The ceiling had a water stain in the corner that her landlord had been promising to fix since November.

She put the phone back to her ear.

She thought about the numbers.

She had eleven paying customers. They were paying her thirty-nine dollars a month. Four hundred and twenty-nine dollars of monthly recurring revenue. She had fifty-two thousand dollars of savings remaining out of the seventy-eight she had started with. She was spending forty-eight hundred a month to live, which was the lowest she could possibly run her life — rent, groceries, health insurance from the state exchange, the bare minimum of everything else. Eleven months of runway. Maybe twelve if she ate more rice.

She had been doing customer-development calls every weekday for six months. She was good at the calls. She liked the calls. She liked the moment in a call when a restaurant owner explained their inventory mess and she could see, in her head, exactly what RestoStock could do for them.

She was not good at marketing. She had read four books on it. She had taken two online courses. She did not enjoy any of it. She did not enjoy sending cold emails. She did not enjoy posting on LinkedIn. She did not enjoy writing the weekly newsletter she had committed to writing. She did not enjoy the part of the work that was *getting people to know that the thing existed.*

She had started, in the last month, to suspect something larger.

She did not enjoy running a company.

She enjoyed building software.

Those were not the same thing.

"Mom," she said.

"Yes, baby."

"I miss having a team. I miss having a boss."

The silence on the other end was longer this time. Then her mother spoke, in the voice she used for the things she had been waiting a long time to say.

"Baby. There's no shame in liking a paycheck."

Aliyah felt something loosen in her chest that she had not known was tight.

"I know there isn't."

"Your father and I, when we came here, we worked for other people. For thirty years. I have worked at the same hospital for eighteen years. There is dignity in it. The dignity is not in being the boss. The dignity is in being good at the work."

"I know, Mom."

"You don't have to be the boss."

"I know."

"Will you think about that this week."

"I will."

They talked for another twenty minutes about other things — her cousin's wedding, her father's hip, the new neighbor downstairs who played his music too loud. When Aliyah hung up she sat on the edge of the bed for a long time, looking at the water stain in the corner of the ceiling.

She did not make a decision that night.

But something had moved.

Damon Wright sat at his kitchen table at midnight on a Friday in September and watched the rain hit the window.

It had been a bad week.

The healthcare-data firm — two-hundred-person company, based out of an office park in Plymouth, his biggest client — had spent the week adding requirements to a project that had been scoped in June. The original statement of work was thirty pages. The current set of requirements, if he documented them, would be sixty. The client's CTO was a man named Pete who liked Damon and respected Damon and also, every week, asked Damon to do a little more than the contract said.

Damon had not pushed back. He had wanted to. He had drafted the email three times. Every time he had gotten to the part where he had to use the phrase *change order* and had felt his throat tighten. The client was eighty percent of his current revenue. If he lost them he was looking at three months of runway and a sales pipeline he had not been building because he had been too busy delivering.

He had eaten the overrun.

He had worked seventy-three hours that week. Twenty-one of them on Saturday alone. Bri had come over on Sunday and they had ordered Thai food and watched a movie and she had fallen asleep on the couch at nine thirty because she had a parent-teacher conference at seven in the morning. Damon had gone back to the laptop after she fell asleep. He had worked until two.

He looked at the rain.

He thought about Stillwater.

He thought about the warehouse loft on the Mississippi River. He thought about the third-floor open workspace where his old desk had been, in the corner near the window with the view of the iron bridge. He thought about Aaron Park stopping by his desk on Tuesday mornings with coffee. He thought about Renata Diaz, the head of people, who had remembered his sister's name and his sister's son's name and had asked about both of them at the all-hands every quarter. He thought about the project managers who had handled the things he was not good at. He thought about the junior engineers he had mentored. He thought about leaving the office at five thirty with the work done and getting on his bike and riding home along the river.

He thought about Maya Holm-Russo.

He picked up his phone.

He typed: *can we have coffee.*

He stared at the message for a long time before he sent it.

He sent it.

The message went green and then blue.

He set the phone down. He drank a glass of water. He went to bed at twelve forty-five.

At seven the next morning he picked up the phone and saw Maya's reply, sent at six fifty-two.

*Yes. Tuesday.*

Damon set the phone face down on the nightstand.

He went back to sleep.

He slept until eleven.

When he got up he made himself eggs and toast and sat at the kitchen table and looked, for a long time, at the chair across from him where nobody was sitting.

He thought about Maya's text. He thought about the freedom he had bought himself in February. He thought about the cost of that freedom — the exhaustion, the loneliness, the not-making-more-money, the gym he had not been to, the friends he had not called, the girlfriend he had not been able to be present for. He thought about the freedom he had thought he was buying and the freedom he had actually purchased and the gap between them.

He thought, *I am forty-two years old. I am supposed to know myself by now.*

He thought, *maybe I am learning.*

Aliyah Hassan put down the phone in her studio apartment in Loring Park on a Tuesday evening in November and let herself smile.

She had taken the call standing up. She had paced, in the four steps of clear floor between the bed and the kitchen counter, while a woman named Jenna Park from Brunswick Capital had walked her through the offer.

Salary one hundred and forty-five thousand.

Full benefits — medical, dental, vision, life, disability, four-oh-one-K with six percent match.

Thirty-two-hour week option, if she wanted it, or forty hours with proportional pay.

Work from anywhere two days a week.

Equity grant — restricted stock units, vesting over four years.

Three weeks of PTO, plus the firm's standard holidays.

Start date: December second.

Aliyah had said *thank you. I would like to accept the offer.*

Jenna Park had said *we are delighted.*

They had set the start date.

Aliyah had hung up.

She sat on the edge of her bed and breathed.

She thought about the eleven paying customers of RestoStock.

She opened her laptop and drafted the email.

She wrote, *I am sorry to share difficult news.* She wrote, *I have decided to shut down RestoStock effective December thirty-first.* She wrote, *I will provide a full data export by November twenty-fifth. I will keep the application accessible read-only through June of next year as a six-month grace period. There will be no further billing after November.* She wrote, *I am sorry for the inconvenience this will cause your business. I have done my best to build something that would serve you. I have decided that I am not the right person to continue it.* She wrote, *thank you for trusting me with your business. I will not forget the courtesy.*

She read the email three times.

She sent it to all eleven customers.

Within twenty-four hours she had eight responses. Two were from owners who said they understood and wished her well. Three said they had appreciated the honesty and would she recommend a competitor. Two said they would miss the app and were sorry to see it go. One — the owner of a small barbecue place in Robbinsdale — wrote a long email saying *thank you for being honest with us. Most software companies just disappear. You told us. We respect that.*

Aliyah cried, briefly, reading the barbecue email.

She wiped her face.

She filed the paperwork to dissolve the LLC.

She paid her final accountant invoice.

She closed her business bank account.

She walked to Lake of the Isles in the November cold and stood at the bridge over the channel and watched two geese pick at something on the path.

The next day she packed an overnight bag and drove out to her parents' house in Eden Prairie for Thanksgiving.

The family had gathered in the dining room — her mother and father, her older brother Yusuf and his wife and their two kids, her younger sister Hodan home from medical school in Iowa, two cousins in from Saint Cloud. The turkey was on the table next to a platter of sambusas her mother had been making since seven that morning.

Aliyah waited until the second round of plates was being filled.

She cleared her throat.

"I want to tell everyone something," she said.

The table looked at her.

"I'm starting a salaried job in two weeks. At a financial-services firm called Brunswick Capital. I'm closing down RestoStock. The solo thing is over. I'm — I'm taking a regular job."

There was a small silence.

Her father — Mohamed Hassan, retired computer-science professor at MCTC, sixty-six years old, gray-bearded and slow-moving and gentle — set his fork down.

"Aliyah."

"Yes, Baba."

"How do you feel about it."

She paused.

She had thought about how she would answer this question for two weeks.

"Like I tried the thing," she said. "The thing was not for me. The thing not being for me does not mean the thing is wrong. It means I needed to try the thing to know."

Her father nodded slowly.

He picked up his fork.

"That is a wise thing to say, daughter."

Her mother put a sambusa on her plate.

Her brother said, "Brunswick has good benefits, I hear."

Her younger sister said, "Can you write me a recommendation letter for residency."

The family laughed.

Aliyah ate her dinner.

She did not feel like she had failed.

She felt like she had learned.

---

*The solo economy, in the third year of its emergence, was not a monolith. Some people loved it. Some people hated it. Some people made it work for two years and then made something else work after that. The story of any individual decision to go solo was always a story of personal fit — temperament, life stage, relationship to ambiguity, relationship to other people, relationship to work itself. Damon Wright had not been built for being alone with the work. Aliyah Hassan had not been built for the company-running side of running a company. They had each tried, and they had each learned, and they were each, in November of Year 2, on the verge of finding their own way back to a shape that fit them. They did not yet know it. But they would.*
