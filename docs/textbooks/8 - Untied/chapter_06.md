# Chapter 6: Stillwater's First Move

Maya Holm-Russo had been in her office since six thirty on a Wednesday morning in March, and by eight she had the printouts laid out on her conference table in the order she wanted to walk them through. Six sheets. Each one a single page. She had condensed three months of work into six pages because she had learned, over twenty-some years of running things, that no working session went better than the working sessions where the inputs were tight.

She set out three coffee mugs. Hers, Aaron's, Renata's. She poured coffee from the carafe Carlo had sent up from the restaurant that morning, the way he did every Wednesday, in a steel thermos he brought back at the end of the week.

She sat down at the head of the small table.

She waited.

Aaron Park came in at five before eight. He sat down at her right. He picked up the first printout and looked at it without speaking.

Renata Diaz came in at three before. She sat at Maya's left. She set down a yellow legal pad. She looked at the same printout Aaron was looking at.

Maya let them read.

"All right," she said, after a beat. "We have been working on this for three months. I want to make a decision today on the first move. We have all looked at the data. We have all looked at the modeling. I want to walk through the package one more time and then I want us to say yes or no in this room before lunch."

"Yes," Aaron said.

"Yes," Renata said.

"Okay."

---

The package had six components.

Maya read them off the first sheet, slowly, one at a time.

ONE — pay restructure. Top of market plus ten percent across all engineering and design roles, indexed to the latest Twin Cities tech-salary benchmark survey. The increase took effect July first.

TWO — profit-sharing. A quarterly profit-share pool, distributed across all employees above six months of tenure, calculated as a percentage of net profit above a baseline threshold. Distribution weighted modestly by tenure and role but with a floor that meant even the most junior staff received a meaningful share. Not just executives. Not just senior architects. Everyone.

THREE — four-day workweek pilot. Engineering only, six-month pilot, beginning July first. If utilization held, the pilot expanded firm-wide in January. Maya had budgeted the lost Friday hours against the expected retention benefit.

FOUR — flexible schedules. Fully flexible remote-versus-in-office, no manager approval required, no quotas on office days, no questions asked about where anyone was working from. Renata had pushed back on this in February — she had worried about culture drift — and Maya had told her plainly that they were not going to be a firm that policed where people sat.

FIVE — parental leave doubled. Twelve weeks to twenty-four. Same policy for any new parent, regardless of role or gender or how the child entered the family. The doubling applied to existing pending leaves as well.

SIX — sabbatical at five years. Four weeks paid, taken in a continuous block, fully detached from work. The five-year clock would be retroactively applied — anyone over five years tenure as of July first was eligible immediately.

Aaron was the first to speak after Maya finished reading.

"It's a lot."

"It is a lot."

"I'm not pushing back. I'm noting it."

"Noted."

Renata flipped to the second sheet. She looked at the line for projected first-year cost.

"Two-point-eight million."

"Two-point-eight million."

"That's about eleven percent of last year's net."

"That's about eleven percent of last year's net."

"And we've signed off on this in the budget."

"We've signed off on this in the budget. Lena has signed off. The board has voted."

"Okay."

Renata flipped to the third sheet. The retention modeling. She had built this one herself, with Maya, in February.

"We are projecting this slows turnover at the senior level from roughly two departures per month to roughly one per quarter."

"That's the model."

"That's a six-X improvement."

"For about six to nine months. Then the curve flattens. The people who really want to be solo will leave anyway. The people on the fence will give us six months. Some of them will stay past six months. Some of them will leave at seven."

"And the people we are clearly going to lose."

"We will lose them. They are not in this model."

"Damon."

"Damon."

"Tomás."

"Tomás. Probably."

"Sora."

"Sora is going to leave. Sora has already mentally left. Sora is going to spend six months figuring out what she's doing next and then she's going to leave. The package will not change that."

Aaron set the sheet down.

"You are being clearer about who we are not going to retain than I have heard you be."

"Because I want us to be clear about it. The package is for the people who are on the fence and the people who would have left in disgust at not seeing us notice. It is not for the people who have already chosen solo. We are not trying to win them back. We are trying to make this place worth staying for the people who actually want a place."

Renata wrote a single line on her legal pad. Maya could not see what it said.

"That tracks," Renata said.

"It tracks," Aaron said.

"All right," Maya said. "Yes or no on the package as written."

"Yes," Aaron said.

"Yes," Renata said.

"Yes. Let's go."

---

They spent the rest of the morning on the announcement plan.

Maya wanted to do an all-hands on Friday. Aaron pushed for a written internal communication first, then the all-hands. Renata wanted the all-hands first, with the written communication going out the same hour. Maya sided with Renata. *People should hear it from me before they read it from anybody.*

Renata took the writing. Aaron took the operational sequencing — the comp-system updates, the PTO-policy rewrites, the benefits-broker conversations. Maya took the all-hands itself, which she would do without notes.

By eleven thirty they had the plan.

By noon they had broken for lunch.

At one Maya was back at her desk, looking out the window at the Mississippi River through the gap in the warehouse roofline across the alley. The river was high. The ice had broken up the previous weekend. The water moved fast and gray. Maya watched it for a long time.

She thought, idly, that she did not actually know, yet, what the second move was going to be. She had a shape. She had a direction. She did not have the move.

She thought: *that is fine. The first move is not for the second move. The first move is for the first move.*

She picked up her phone and texted Carlo. *Six tonight. Bar seat. Bring the kitchen book.* Carlo wrote back thirty seconds later. *I'll save you the corner.*

---

Friday afternoon at four. The all-hands.

Stillwater's converted warehouse held one hundred and forty-three of its one hundred and forty-five employees that afternoon. The two who were absent were on client sites — one in Wisconsin, one in Iowa — and were dialed in on the company video bridge from hotel rooms. Aaron had walked the floor at three forty-five and asked everyone to come up to the third-floor open workspace, where the long brick walls and the exposed beams made the room look more like a community hall than an office.

The third floor had been the firm's all-hands space since 2014. There was a stage that was really just a low riser. There was a microphone on a stand. There were folding chairs that had been borrowed from a community theater in Northeast Minneapolis fifteen years earlier and never returned.

Maya walked up to the riser at four oh-five.

She did not have notes.

She had a small index card in her right pocket on which she had written four phrases in pencil. She did not take it out.

"Hi everyone," she said.

She waited for the room to settle.

"I'm going to tell you what we are doing, when, and why. I want this to take about fifteen minutes. I will take questions after. Some of the questions I will answer right now. Some of them I will answer in the next two weeks. Some of them I do not know the answer to yet, and I will tell you when I do."

She paused.

"Here is what we are doing."

She read off the six components. She did not embellish them. She did not sell them. She read them. She let the room absorb each one before going to the next. After the third component there was a small audible noise in the back of the room — somebody had said something quietly to a neighbor — and Maya looked up briefly and smiled and went back to reading.

When she finished the six components she set her hands at her sides.

"Why we are doing this. I'm going to be direct. We are doing it because the labor market in our industry has changed and we have been losing senior people for the better part of a year because of it. We could pretend that's not happening. I'm not going to pretend that's not happening. The first thing I have to demonstrate to all of you is that I noticed. That is what this package is. It is the first thing. It is the *noticing* package."

She let that sit.

"Here is what I want to be honest about. This is the *first* thing we are doing. It is not the *only* thing we are doing. The package will slow the bleeding. The bleeding will not fully stop. Some people who would have left will stay because the math is now different for them, and I am glad about that. Some people who were on the fence will give us six months to see if there's a second thing. There will be a second thing. I am working on it. I do not have it yet. I will when I have it."

She paused again.

"And some people who really want to be solo, or who really want to start their own firm, or who really want to do something different — those people are going to leave anyway. I am not trying to talk them out of it. I think there is a version of this industry in which people leave firms and start things and some of those things work and some of them don't, and the people who started them are better off for having tried, and the firms they left can still be on good terms with them, and that is fine. I would like Stillwater to be a firm that people leave well, when they choose to leave. And I would like Stillwater to be a firm that people *return to* well, if and when they choose to return."

There was a small ripple of something — recognition, Maya thought — through the room.

"What I want you to know is that I see what is happening. I don't have all the answers. I am working on this. And I am not going to pretend that I am not working on it."

She stepped back from the microphone.

The room was quiet for what felt to Maya like a long time and was, on the recording later, four seconds.

Then there was applause.

It was not raucous applause. It was not standing-ovation applause. It was the applause of a hundred and forty-three people who had been waiting, for some number of months, to hear their CEO say what Maya had just said. The applause was sustained for perhaps fifteen seconds. Some people clapped harder than others. A few people did not clap at all, which Maya noticed without judgment.

Aaron, in the back of the room, gave her a small nod.

Renata, at the side of the riser, was writing something on her legal pad.

Maya took questions for thirty-five minutes.

She told them when the changes took effect. Sixty days. She told them which questions she could not answer yet. She told them what the second thing might look like and that she would tell them more when she knew. She told them — when somebody asked — that she did not know whether they would lose specific clients because of the four-day workweek and that she had budgeted for the possibility and that she thought the risk was acceptable.

At four fifty-five she stepped down from the riser.

People came up to her, one at a time, for the next forty minutes.

By six the third floor was nearly empty.

---

Aaron and Renata came to Maya's office at six fifteen.

Maya was on the couch by the window. She had taken off her shoes. The river outside was darker now and the lights had started to come on along Hennepin and across the river toward downtown.

Aaron sat in the chair across from her.

Renata sat at the other end of the couch.

"Two people who were going to leave just told me they're staying," Renata said.

"Who."

"Lina Bjornson. And Reggie Park — no relation."

"Lina was going to leave."

"Lina had a resignation letter drafted. She told me directly. She tore it up."

"Good."

"Three people who were on the fence said they're going to give it six months to see what the second thing is," Aaron said.

"Who."

"Theo Halberg. Maddy Reyes. Jin Chu."

"Those are people I want to keep."

"They want to be kept."

"Good."

Aaron paused.

"And one person — Damon — still gave notice."

Maya did not move.

"Damon was always going to leave," she said. "Damon needs to try the solo thing. Damon will be back."

"You sure?"

"I am pretty sure."

Aaron looked at her.

"You sound pretty sure."

"I have known Damon for seven years. Damon is brilliant. Damon is also someone who needs to try things until he knows. He is going to go do the solo thing because he needs to know if it is for him. It is not going to be for him. He is going to find that out the hard way. And then he is going to come back to us, if we have made it so that coming back is possible."

"Have we made it so that coming back is possible."

"Not yet. That's the second thing."

Renata wrote a single line on her legal pad.

"All right," Renata said.

"All right," Aaron said.

They sat for a few minutes without talking. The lights along Hennepin were fully on now. The river was nearly black. Somewhere down on the street level a car honked, briefly, twice, and then was quiet.

"Carlo's expecting me at six thirty," Maya said. "He's saving me the corner seat."

"Go," Renata said.

Maya got up. She put on her shoes. She picked up her coat and her bag.

At the door she turned.

"Thank you both," she said.

"For what."

"For working with me on this. For sitting in the meetings. For all the spreadsheets. For believing the labor market actually changed when I told you it had. I would not have done any of this with anyone else."

Aaron looked at her.

"We know," he said.

She smiled.

She left.

---

*Stillwater Software's first move worked exactly as Maya Holm-Russo predicted it would. The bleeding slowed. Several people stayed who would have otherwise left. The firm went from losing senior engineers at a rate of two per month to losing them at a rate of one per quarter. It was not enough. Maya had said it would not be enough. The board had agreed it would not be enough. The point of the first move was not to be enough.*

*The point of the first move was to make the second move possible.*

*Maya did not yet know what the second move was. She knew its shape. She knew it had to do with treating people as partners and not as resources, and she knew it had to do with not trying to win a price war Stillwater could not win. She did not yet know how to put those two things into a structure her firm could operate inside.*

*She had six months, more or less, to figure it out. The people who had given her six months were going to come back, at the end of those six months, with their question. The question was going to be the same question, phrased a hundred different ways. The question was: was there a second thing.*

*If the answer was yes, Stillwater had a future.*

*If the answer was no, the first move would be the last move, and the firm would be a kinder version of the firm Davis Hartwell was already, by then, presiding over from his corner office on Federal Street.*

*Maya did not intend to let the answer be no.*

*She walked down the steps from her office to Carlo's restaurant on the ground floor of the building next door, that Friday evening in March of Year 2, and she took the corner seat at the bar that Carlo had saved her, and she ordered the same dish she ordered every Friday, and she sat and thought, and the lights on Hennepin came up brighter as the night fell, and the river behind the building moved on toward the south as it had moved on for a long time and would continue to move on for a long time after.*
