# KDP Listing — Untied: A TAO Future Case Study

## Description

The third book of the TAO trilogy of practice — and the first one set in the future.

By the time *Untied* opens, both of the companies at its center have already completed their AI rollouts. Their best employees have learned to use AI tools at a level the firms paid them to learn. And then a quiet thing happened that nobody at either firm had built a plan for: those employees started realizing they could do the work themselves, alone, with the same tools, and bill it directly to the same clients, and keep all of the money, and not have to sit through a single status meeting ever again.

The exodus began.

*Untied* is a business novel about what two CEOs did with that exodus.

**Davis Hartwell** is the third-generation CEO of Hartwell Digital, a 220-person Boston consultancy founded in 1998 by his father Edward. Davis is not lazy. He is not stupid. He is not malicious. He is the steward of an institution that has survived every previous wave of industry change, and he reads the AI-augmented exodus from his firm as another such wave. *Normal turnover. The market will normalize. The model has worked. The model will work.* His board agrees with him. His chief operating officer, Karen Voss, writes him a five-page memo explaining why he is wrong. The board reads it for forty-five minutes and votes unanimously to do nothing. Eighteen months later, Hartwell Digital is a husk.

**Maya Holm-Russo** is the founder and CEO of Stillwater Software, a 145-person Minneapolis firm that occupies a converted brick warehouse near the Mississippi. She gets her first resignation early — four months early, because the engineer who is leaving respects her enough to give her runway. She does not panic. She does not yet know what to do. She knows it is real and it will get bigger. Within three months she has called her board and said: *we are going to lose between 20 and 40 percent of our staff over the next 18 months. This is not turnover. This is structural. I want to talk about what we are going to do about it.*

What Maya does, across two and a half years, is the spine of this book. She makes two moves. The first move is the perks-first move — equitable pay, four-day weeks, profit-sharing, real flexibility — which slows the bleeding but does not stop it. The second move is the deeper one. She stops trying to compete with the solos who left on price. She stops thinking of them as competitors at all. She starts thinking of them as the other half of an ecosystem. She creates a new tier inside her firm — **Senior Partner** — that is open to current employees AND to former employees who tried solo and want a team again. She stops treating employees as resources and starts treating them as partners. She stops competing on price and starts competing on the one thing one person cannot offer: continuous embedded human contact, dedicated relationships, the safety of a team behind the work.

*Untied* follows four named employees who leave their firms. **Priya** stays solo and builds a quiet kingdom. **Ben** finds a tiny partnership and builds a tight, profitable studio. **Damon** tries solo, exhausts himself, comes home as a Senior Partner. **Aliyah** tries solo, finds out it is not her thing, takes a salaried role at a different company, and is honest with herself about preferring the paycheck. None of these outcomes is the right outcome. All of them are the right outcomes for the people who chose them.

The book closes in May of Year 3. Stillwater is stable. Maya is at her desk. Carlo, her husband, is in his restaurant. Damon is at his old/new desk teaching a 26-year-old engineer he had never met. Priya is in her attic in Cincinnati with her dog. Ben is at his loft on Hennepin Avenue answering a referral call from Maya. Aliyah is on a run along the Mississippi at 6 PM.

The firms that did not adapt are not mentioned by name. They are mentioned in industry retrospectives now and then. They are mentioned less often each year.

Nobody remembers them.

*Untied* is a business fable in the lineage of *The Phoenix Project* and *Who Moved My Cheese?*, with a folkloric closing register and a 2.5-year time horizon. It is the third book of the TAO trilogy of practice. Stand-alone.

## Reading order

The TAO trilogy of practice can be read in any order, but the natural sequence is:

1. ***Anchor*** — restoration after an AI failure (literary novella)
2. ***Multiplied*** — a successful greenfield AI rollout (business fable)
3. ***Untied*** — what happens to firms and employees after the rollouts (multi-protagonist business fable)

All three stand alone. Together they form a complete portrait of organizational AI transformation: how to recover, how to do it right, what comes after.

## Back-Cover Copy (~150 words)

The exodus began quietly. The best engineers learned to use AI tools at the level their firms paid them to learn, and then they realized they could do the work alone, faster and cheaper, with the same tools. They started leaving.

One CEO called it normal turnover. His firm did not survive.

The other CEO saw what was happening. She slowed the bleeding with better pay, four-day weeks, real flexibility. Then she made the deeper move: she stopped treating employees as resources and started treating them as partners. She stopped competing with solo founders on price and started competing on what one person cannot offer — continuous relationships, dedicated teams, the human touchpoints.

*Untied* is a business fable about the labor market consequences of widespread AI adoption, told through two software consultancies and four employees who left. The third book of the TAO trilogy of practice.

Some lived happily ever after. Some did not. The other company is not remembered.
