# Chapter 1: The First Wave

The first resignation letter was on Davis Hartwell's desk when he came in at seven thirty on a Tuesday morning in October. It was sitting on top of the day's printouts that his assistant Maddie always laid out in the same neat stack — the previous day's billing summary, the project-status dashboard, the calendar printout, the press clippings, and now, on top of everything, a folded sheet of cream-colored stationery with the Hartwell Digital letterhead at the top.

He sat down at his desk. He took off his coat. He picked up the letter.

It was from Priya Ramaswamy. He read it twice. It was short, professional, courteous, and final. Her last day would be Friday, November 7th. She thanked him for four good years. She thanked specifically her project lead Gerald Hsu and her old mentor in the architecture practice, Vince DiCamillo. She said she had decided to start her own company and that she had thought hard about the timing and the courtesy. Her transition plan was attached. She offered to help train her replacement.

Davis set the letter down.

He drank his coffee.

He looked at the dashboard printout. Q4 was tracking 6% above budget. The Stansfield engagement was on schedule. The pipeline was healthy. The Cambridge office had two new senior hires starting on the first.

He looked at the letter again.

Priya Ramaswamy. Senior engineer. Four years. Healthcare-compliance specialist. He could not recall her face exactly. There were, he reminded himself, two hundred and twenty people in this firm. He did not have to be able to picture all of them.

He pressed the intercom.

"Maddie."

"Yes, Davis."

"Get Brian Wexler on the phone whenever he comes in."

"He's in. Want me to put him through?"

"Please."

The phone rang on Davis's desk thirty seconds later.

"Davis."

"Brian. I have a resignation letter on my desk from a Priya Ramaswamy. Senior engineer. Four years. What's the protocol look like?"

"I have her exit interview on the calendar for Thursday. The transition plan she sent over is reasonable. We'll backfill her work across two senior engineers we have rotating off other projects."

"Good. What's the trailing average on resignations, by the way? We doing okay there?"

There was a small pause.

"We're a little above the trailing twelve-month average."

"How much above."

"Q4 to date — seven weeks in — we've had nine resignations. Last year same period was four. The full-year trailing average runs around eighteen to twenty-two."

"So we're high but not crazy."

"That's right."

"Any pattern in why?"

"Some of them are AI-curious. Three of them have said they're going to try something on their own. Two said they're going to other consultancies. One went in-house at a client. Two were retirement. One — Aliyah Hassan, the junior — said she wanted to work on a startup of her own."

"All right. Keep me posted."

"Will do."

He hung up.

He picked up the Ramaswamy letter again and put it in the file tray on the right side of his desk where the HR-related papers went.

The day's first call was at eight thirty.

---

By eleven the second letter was on his desk. Aliyah Hassan, junior developer, two years. She had given six weeks instead of two. She said she wanted to give the firm time to backfill her role on the Brigham project. She thanked her team lead. She wrote, in a paragraph that Davis registered without considering, that she was leaving to start a small software company of her own and that she expected the next year of her life would be difficult and educational.

He set the letter on top of the Ramaswamy letter in the right tray.

At eleven forty-seven the email came in.

It was from Eduardo Mendez, senior engineer, eight years, the lead architect on the firm's largest insurance-industry account. Subject line: *Resignation*. Eduardo had attached the formal letter as a PDF.

Davis read the email twice.

He did not read the PDF.

He picked up the phone and called Karen Voss, his chief operating officer.

"Karen."

"Davis."

"Eduardo Mendez just resigned."

She was quiet for a beat.

"I know," she said. "He told me yesterday."

"You knew."

"He wanted to tell you himself. He sent the email this morning because he didn't want me to be in the awkward position of knowing before you did."

"What's he doing."

"He's starting a small firm with another senior — I think Tom Reilly, who left in August. They've got two clients already committed."

"Two of our clients."

"Two of our clients."

"Which ones."

"He didn't say. He said he wouldn't approach anyone he was actively working with for ninety days. He said he'd put it in writing."

"That's something, I suppose."

"It is."

"Karen. We've had ten resignations this quarter."

"I know."

"What do you think is going on."

There was another pause. Davis heard her chair creak. He pictured her sitting up.

"I think we should talk about it, Davis. I've been wanting to."

"Come up. I'm clear from one to two."

"I'll be there."

He hung up.

He stood up from his desk and walked to the window.

The Hartwell Building sat on Federal Street, and from the corner office on the twenty-fourth floor he could see the harbor and the wharves and, on a clear day, the planes climbing out of Logan into the Atlantic. He had stood at this window — or one almost identical to it, on the same floor of a previous building, before his father moved the firm here in 2007 — for most of his adult life. He had been an associate in this firm when he was twenty-eight. He had been a vice president when he was thirty-six. He had been the chief operating officer when he was forty-two. He had been the chief executive officer for five years.

He looked at the harbor.

He thought, briefly, about his father. Edward Hartwell would be eighty-one this December. He was on Cape Cod, where he had a small house with a view of the bay, and where he spent his days reading Civil War history and watching the boats. Davis had called him last Sunday. They had talked, for an hour, about the firm. Edward had asked about the Stansfield account. Edward had asked about the Cambridge office expansion. Edward had not asked about resignations because Edward had not known there were resignations to ask about.

Davis turned away from the window.

---

Karen Voss came in at one o'clock with a notebook.

She was fifty-one. She had been at Hartwell Digital eight years. She had spent the previous decade at a competing firm in New York where she had risen to a senior vice president role and where she had, in her early forties, decided that she wanted to move to Boston for personal reasons and that she wanted to work somewhere smaller. She had taken a step down to come to Hartwell. Davis's father had hired her over Davis's mild objection — Davis had wanted someone with more direct consulting experience. Edward had said that what the firm needed was someone who knew how firms ran, not someone who knew how consulting ran. Karen had been one of Edward's last major hires.

She sat down across from Davis. She did not open the notebook.

"Davis," she said, "I want to walk you through what I think is happening. It will take ten minutes. Will you give me ten minutes without interrupting."

He raised his eyebrows.

"Sure."

"All right. Three things. First. The resignations we've had this quarter — and last quarter, and to a lesser extent the quarter before — are not random. Eduardo Mendez is the seventeenth person to leave this calendar year. The two before him this month are Priya Ramaswamy and Aliyah Hassan, plus the email you got last week from Carla Bowen in the New York office and the call I had with Tess Lin in Cambridge last Friday. Of the seventeen, ten have left to start their own firms. Three have gone to other consultancies — two of those at our same tier, one at a smaller boutique. Two have gone in-house at clients. One retired early. One left to take care of a parent."

"All right."

"Second thing. The ten who left to start their own firms — they are not random in their seniority. Six of them were senior engineers or above. Three of them were principal architects. One was a director. These are the people we cannot easily replace. They are not the easy backfills. The trailing-twelve-month average that Brian is reporting against does not distinguish levels. It just counts heads."

Davis nodded.

"Third thing. I have been talking to some of the people who left, off the record, over coffee. I have done five of these conversations in the last six weeks. Every single one of them said some version of the same sentence to me. The sentence is: 'I realized I could do the work I do here with AI tools, and bill it directly to a client, and keep all of the revenue, and not have to deal with the firm overhead.' Every one of them said that. Almost word for word."

She paused.

"Davis. This is not a labor-market blip. This is structural. The economics of being a senior engineer at a firm like ours just changed, and the people who got there first are leaving."

She stopped.

She looked at him.

"That's what I wanted to walk you through."

He looked back at her.

"Karen, I appreciate the analysis. I really do. But I want to push back on the framing a little."

"All right."

"We've been in this business for twenty-seven years. We have been through several waves of *the work is changing.* We went through the offshoring wave in the early two-thousands. We went through the cloud-migration wave in the twenty-tens. We went through the agile transformation. Every one of those was supposed to be the wave that disintermediated firms like ours. None of them did. Why? Because clients don't actually want to manage individual contractors. They want a firm. They want a name. They want a partner."

"That's true. They still do."

"So the AI thing — I'm not saying it isn't real. I'm saying it isn't different. The waves come. The firm adjusts. The work continues."

Karen nodded slowly.

"I'd like to be wrong about this, Davis. I want to make that clear. I would love to be wrong."

"But?"

"But the difference between the previous waves and this one is that the previous waves required the worker to get *better tools.* This wave is making the worker *equivalent to the team.* It is a different shape of change."

Davis tilted his head.

"What are you suggesting we do."

"I have some ideas. I'd like to write you a memo. Give me three weeks. I'll have a written recommendation. I'd want you to take it seriously."

"I will."

"Will you actually."

He looked at her for a long second.

"Yes, Karen."

"All right."

She stood up. She tucked her notebook under her arm.

"Davis, one more thing. I want to be careful how I say this. I think the time horizon on this is shorter than you think. I don't think we have a year to figure out what we're doing. I think we have a quarter."

"Noted."

She left.

---

Davis sat at his desk after Karen left. The afternoon light was beginning to slant. He could hear the building making its small late-afternoon sounds — the HVAC cycling, the faint murmur of the office beyond his closed door, a printer somewhere down the hall starting up.

He thought about what Karen had said.

He thought about Eduardo Mendez. He had hired Eduardo. He remembered the interview. Eduardo had been thirty-three. He had been doing financial-services work at a smaller firm and Davis had recruited him with a forty-thousand-dollar bump and the promise of a fast track to principal. He had made principal in three years. He was good. He was better than good.

Eduardo had been at the firm eight years. He was leaving with another senior who had been at the firm eleven years. They were taking two of Hartwell's clients with them.

Davis thought, with a kind of detached corporate calm, that this was the natural rhythm of consulting firms. People came, people went. The firm continued. The market normalized.

He thought about his father. Edward had run this firm for nineteen years. He had hired Davis's first boss. He had hired Karen Voss. He had hired the lead architect on the firm's biggest active engagement. He had made the call to move to Federal Street. He had stepped down at seventy-six because, he had said, *the firm needs a CEO with another twenty years in him, not another five.*

Davis had been forty-three when his father had retired. He had been ready. He had been good at the operational work and reasonable at the strategic work and his father had trusted him.

The five years since had been smooth, on the whole. The firm had grown. The clients had been steady. The pipeline had been healthy. Nothing dramatic had happened. He had done what a competent CEO did, which was to keep the operation running and not break what worked.

He thought: *we have a quarter.* He had heard Karen say it. He had registered it. He had let it pass.

He picked up his phone and called his wife.

"Davis."

"Audrey. I'm going to be home by seven. I'll pick something up for dinner."

"Sushi from the place on Centre Street?"

"Sushi from the place on Centre Street. Big day?"

"Mild day. Some resignations. Karen wants to write me a memo."

"Karen's a good memo writer."

"She is."

"See you at seven."

He hung up.

He picked up the third resignation letter and added it to the file tray. He looked at the file tray. There were three letters there now. He turned away from it and pulled the Stansfield engagement file out of the left drawer of his desk and opened it and began to read.

By six thirty he had not thought about the letters again. By six forty-five he had taken his coat off the back of the door, said goodnight to Maddie, ridden the elevator down to the underground garage, and driven out onto Federal Street into the early-evening Boston traffic. He listened, on the radio, to a financial-podcast segment about EBITDA optimization in mid-market services firms. The host's argument was that the firms with the cleanest cost structures would have the best decade ahead of them. Davis found himself nodding along. He thought, briefly, that Hartwell Digital's cost structure was clean. He thought, with a small satisfaction, that the firm was in a good position.

He turned onto Storrow Drive.

The Charles River was dark beyond the windshield.

He drove home.

He picked up sushi from the place on Centre Street.

He did not think about Karen Voss's memo for the rest of the evening.

By the end of that same week, two more senior engineers in the New York office would resign. Davis Hartwell would not be told about either of them until the Monday following.

By the end of the quarter, the number would be twenty-one.

Davis would still believe it was normal turnover.

Two hundred miles to the west, in a city he had rarely thought about, another CEO was reading her own first resignation letter and reaching a different conclusion. Her name was Maya Holm-Russo. Her firm was Stillwater Software. She had built it from nothing. She would be the one who saw what was happening, and she would be the one who decided to do something about it.

But Davis Hartwell would not know any of that, because Davis Hartwell did not know that there was anything to know.

He went to sleep at eleven fifteen that Tuesday night, satisfied with his day's work.

The firm continued.

For a little while longer.
