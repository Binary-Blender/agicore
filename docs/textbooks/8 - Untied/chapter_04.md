# Chapter 4: The Two Reactions

The Hartwell Digital boardroom was on the twenty-fifth floor of the Hartwell Building, one floor up from Davis Hartwell's office, in a long room with a wall of windows facing the harbor and a long mahogany table that Edward Hartwell had bought from a bankrupt Cambridge law firm in 1999. The chairs around it were the original chairs. The portrait of Edward Hartwell hung at the far end. The painting was a good likeness. It had been done by a Newbury Street artist the year Edward had stepped down, and it captured something of his old square-shouldered Yankee stillness without making him look pious about it.

The board met four times a year. The mid-December meeting was always the longest because it included the annual budget and the year-end review.

Davis Hartwell took his seat at the head of the table at ten past one on the Thursday of the second week of December. The other five board members were already there. Coffee had been poured. Pastries had been laid out on a side table by the window and were going untouched, as they always did. The portrait of Edward Hartwell watched the room without comment.

Geoffrey Stansfield, the board chairman, was at Davis's right hand.

Stansfield was seventy-one. He had been Edward Hartwell's college roommate at Bowdoin in the early 1970s. He had spent his career in private banking. He sat on six boards. He had been the chairman of Hartwell Digital's board since the firm had first formalized one in 2003. He wore the same kind of grey wool suit he had been wearing for fifty years and a navy tie with small white dots and a Patek Philippe his grandfather had bought him for his college graduation. He spoke quietly. He drank coffee. He had served on Edward Hartwell's daughter-in-law's wedding committee. He had known Davis since Davis was nine years old.

The meeting opened with the budget review. Brian Wexler, the head of HR, presented the people numbers.

"Q4 resignations," Brian said, "ran fourteen. Trailing twelve-month average has been eight."

Geoffrey Stansfield looked at the page in front of him for a long moment.

"Davis," he said. "What's driving it."

Davis Hartwell had thought about the answer for a week. He had not written it down. He had not needed to. He believed what he was about to say.

"Normal turnover, Geoff. Tight labor market in the senior engineering ranks. The AI tools are making some of our engineers think they can launch their own little shops. We're seeing a wave of that across the industry. Most of them will be back in a year — the math doesn't work for them the way they think it does. The clients still want firms. The labor market is what it is."

Stansfield nodded slowly. He took a sip of coffee. He set the cup down.

"The Stansfield account is in good shape."

"It is."

"What's the year-end revenue looking like."

"Up four percent over last year."

"Margins."

"Margins are holding. The new senior hires in Q1 will cost us a little against margin but the pipeline supports it."

"All right."

Geoffrey Stansfield turned a page in the binder. The board moved on.

The rest of the meeting took two hours and ten minutes. The coming year's budget was reviewed and approved. The Cambridge office expansion was reviewed and approved. A small bonus pool for senior executives was reviewed and approved. The year-end client-summary report was discussed. The strategic plan for the coming year was tabled until the February meeting. The board went into executive session for fifteen minutes — Davis stayed in the room — and then the meeting adjourned at three twenty-five.

Geoffrey Stansfield stood up and put his hand on Davis's shoulder on his way out.

"Good year, Davis. Steady."

"Thank you, Geoff."

"Wish your father a merry Christmas from me when you call him."

"I will."

Stansfield left.

Davis sat at the head of the table for a moment after the others had gone.

He thought: *I handled that well.*

He gathered the binder and the budget summary and his pen and his coffee cup and he stood up and he walked back down the internal staircase to the twenty-fourth floor and to his office. He put the materials on his desk. He looked at the file tray on the right side where the HR papers went. The tray had thirteen letters in it. He did not count them. He had not counted them once.

He sat down. He began to draft the year-end note he would send to the full firm on the Friday of the next week.

He felt, for the first time in a month, that the year was under control.

The Stillwater Software board met around a small round table in the corner of the third-floor conference room above the warehouse loft, with the Mississippi visible through the tall arched windows behind Maya Holm-Russo's chair. There were three of them. Maya. Aaron Park. Lena Schultz.

Lena Schultz was sixty-four. She was a Twin Cities investor who had cut her first check to Stillwater in 2010, when the firm had been twelve people and had needed a quarter of a million dollars to survive the year. The check had been three hundred thousand. She had stayed on the cap table at the same percentage for fifteen years. She had served on three boards in the Twin Cities over those years and watched two of them through structural transitions — one a marketing-services firm that had been gutted by the move to digital advertising, the other a manufacturing-software firm that had been forced to pivot from on-premise to SaaS. The first had not survived the transition. The second had. Lena had been the difference on the second board, in a way that Maya had always known and had always intended to use when the time came.

The time had come.

The meeting opened at one. Lena had a small leather notebook. Aaron had a laptop. Maya had a single sheet of paper, folded once, in front of her.

"Lena," Maya said. "Aaron and I have been working on something for the last two months. Privately. I want to walk you through it. Then I want your help."

"Walk me through it."

Maya unfolded the paper.

"We are going to lose between twenty and forty percent of our staff over the next eighteen months. This is not turnover. This is structural. The labor economics shifted, the senior engineering tier is running the math on independent or small-partnership models, and I think we caught the leading edge of it in October. I want to talk about what we are going to do about it."

Lena Schultz did not say anything for a beat.

"All right," she said.

"That's the headline. Aaron has the supporting analysis. I want to walk you through five things and then we can argue about any of them you want to argue about."

"Go."

Maya walked her through five things.

One: the firm had lost three senior engineers in the last sixty days. One was Ben Kowalczyk, who had given notice in October with a February departure date. The other two were a senior frontend engineer in the Stillwater Studio practice and a senior data engineer in the Stillwater Integration practice. Both had said, in their exit interviews with Renata Diaz, that they were leaving to do independent work. The three departures, in two months, exceeded the firm's senior-engineering attrition for the entire prior year.

Two: Maya had been reading the labor-market data for nine months. She gave Lena two journal articles and a McKinsey paper Aaron had photocopied. The data was consistent. AI-augmented individual contributors were achieving productivity multiples — measured in deliverable software per engineer-hour — that ranged between eight and twenty-five times pre-augmentation baselines. The math at the individual level had become favorable to leaving. The math at the firm level had become unfavorable to staying billable-hours-based.

Three: Aaron had run the financial model. If Stillwater lost twenty percent of its staff and did nothing to compensate, the firm would lose approximately fourteen percent of revenue in the trailing twelve months — because the people who would leave were the most senior, and the most senior were the most billable. Margins would compress further because the firm's fixed overhead would have to be absorbed across a smaller revenue base. The firm would go from twenty-three percent margins to roughly nine. If the loss was forty percent the firm would go from twenty-three to negative four.

Four: the simple response — competing on price with solos — would not work. Solos could and would always undercut a firm. The firm's overhead made it structurally impossible to match independent pricing. Trying would accelerate the death spiral, not slow it.

Five: Maya did not yet know what the second move was. She knew there had to be one. She suspected it had to do with what a firm could offer that a solo could not — continuity, embedded relationships, redundancy, multi-person teams, a different shape of value. She was working on it. She wanted six months to figure out the answer. In the meantime she wanted to start a first move — perks, pay restructure, profit-sharing, schedule control — that would slow the bleeding while she figured out the deeper response.

She put down the paper.

"That's what I have," she said.

Lena Schultz did not say anything for a long moment.

Aaron looked at his hands.

"What's the budget for what you want to do," Lena said.

"I don't know yet."

"Order of magnitude."

"Phase one, the perks-first move — somewhere between two and three million in incremental annual run-rate cost, against current revenue of forty-one. Phase two, the second move, I cannot scope yet."

"And without doing either of those — your model says you lose fourteen to thirty percent of revenue."

"That's right."

"Maya."

"Yes."

"I'm in. Tell me when you know."

Maya looked at her.

"That's it."

"That's it. I have seen this movie. I have seen it twice. The two times I have seen it, the boards that hesitated lost their companies and the boards that moved kept them. We are going to move. You go figure out where. I will hold the floor down here."

Maya folded the paper.

"Thank you, Lena."

"Don't thank me yet. This is going to be the most expensive thing you have ever done. And the hardest. And the right thing."

Aaron closed his laptop.

"All right," Maya said. "Let's talk about the December all-hands and what we are and are not going to say."

They talked for another forty-five minutes.

At three o'clock Lena Schultz stood up to leave. She picked up her coat from the back of her chair. She put it on. She walked over to Maya and put her hand on Maya's forearm.

"Maya."

"Yes."

"You caught it in October."

"I know."

"That is the whole game. You will figure out the rest. The first thing was the catching."

She walked out of the conference room.

Maya sat at the table after she had gone.

Aaron sat across from her.

"Maya."

"Aaron."

"We are going to have to tell the firm something in March. Probably April."

"I know."

"I don't have an answer either yet."

"I know."

"But I think Lena is right that the catching was the thing."

"I think she is right too."

They sat there for another minute. Then they got up and walked down the staircase together.

The Hartwell Digital holiday gala was held on the evening of December eighteenth in the Walpole Ballroom of the Langham hotel, on the corner of Franklin Street, six blocks from the Hartwell Building. The gala had been held in the same ballroom for fourteen consecutive years. The menu was always the same. The band was always the same. The bartenders were the same bartenders the firm had used since 2011. Davis Hartwell wore a black tuxedo. His wife Audrey wore a long emerald-green dress and the pearls Edward Hartwell had given her on her wedding day to his son.

The room was full. Two hundred and twenty employees, plus spouses and significant others, plus the board, plus the senior clients who had been invited as a courtesy. Champagne was being served from silver trays. The band was playing a Sinatra arrangement. A photographer moved through the crowd in a discreet black suit, taking photographs that would later appear in the firm's year-end retrospective.

Davis stood near the bar with Geoffrey Stansfield and two other board members. He had a champagne flute in his hand. He had eaten well. He was telling a story about a Cape Cod sailing trip from the previous August. Stansfield was laughing the small dry laugh he had. Audrey was a few feet away talking to Stansfield's wife about a Beacon Hill renovation. Karen Voss was on the opposite side of the room, talking with two of the senior partners from the Cambridge office. Davis had nodded at her when she came in. She had nodded back.

Brian Wexler came up to the bar. He shook Davis's hand. He said something complimentary about the firm's year. Davis thanked him.

Eduardo Mendez was not at the party. He had left the firm in November.

Priya Ramaswamy was not at the party. She had not been on the firm's distribution list since November fifteenth.

Aliyah Hassan was not at the party. She had left the previous Friday.

Davis did not register the absences. There were two hundred and twenty people in the room. There had always been resignations. There had always been new hires. The party was the party.

He was having a good time.

The Stillwater Software holiday party was held on the same Thursday night, on the second floor of the warehouse loft, around a long row of folding tables that had been set up at five o'clock and would be broken down at eleven. The tables were covered with butcher paper. The food was potluck. Carlo had brought a pan of lasagna the size of a coffee-table book, a tray of meatballs, and four loaves of bread from his Saturday-morning loaves. Aaron Park had brought bulgogi short ribs. Renata Diaz had brought tamales her aunt had made in Saint Paul. The integration team had brought a hot-dish that had been the subject of a fierce internal email thread the prior week. There was beer in a cooler by the freight elevator. There was a turntable in the corner that had been set up by one of the junior developers, who was running a vinyl rotation that began with Bill Withers and was, by the time Maya arrived at seven, well into a side of Marvin Gaye.

Maya was wearing jeans and a brown wool sweater. She had her hair down. Carlo was at the lasagna table, in his apron, ladling portions onto paper plates and refusing all offers of help. Maya kissed him on the cheek as she went by. He winked at her.

She got a plate of lasagna.

She walked across the loft to the brick wall by the river-facing windows. Ben Kowalczyk was already there. He was sitting on the floor with his back against the brick. He had a paper plate of lasagna in his lap and a beer beside him on the floor. He was wearing the same canvas jacket. His wife Theresa was somewhere in the crowd. His dog Roosevelt was not present, because dogs were not allowed at the holiday party, a rule Ben had argued against for six years and had never won.

Maya sat down next to him.

"Ben."

"Maya."

"How's the lasagna."

"It's the lasagna."

"Carlo's been making it for twenty-five years."

"You can tell."

They ate for a while without saying anything.

"Maya."

"Yes."

"I'll send you my exit memo three weeks before."

"Thank you."

"It'll have the names. The ones I know."

"I appreciate that."

He took a drink of his beer.

"Maya."

"Yes."

"Are you going to be okay."

She looked at him.

"I think so."

"Yeah."

"I'm worried about my industry, not about my firm. I think I can do the firm part."

"What's the move."

"I don't know yet."

"Tell me when you know."

"I will, Ben."

He drank some more of his beer.

"I'll be rooting for you."

"I know you will."

They sat against the brick wall and ate Carlo's lasagna and listened to the side of Marvin Gaye finish out. Across the room one of the junior developers was teaching one of the senior architects a dance step. Aaron Park was at the lasagna table now, deep in conversation with Renata Diaz. Lena Schultz, who had been invited and had come, was talking to a client who had also been invited and had also come. Carlo was bringing out a second pan of lasagna from the kitchen elevator. The windows behind Maya and Ben were dark with the river and the bridges across it.

"Maya."

"Yes."

"This is a good firm."

"I know."

"I am going to miss it."

"I know."

"That's not going to stop me from leaving."

"I know that too."

He smiled. He held up his beer. She tapped her plastic cup against the bottle.

"To the next chapter," he said.

"To the next chapter."

They drank.

---

December eighteenth of Year 1 was the last day of the easy time.

The two CEOs that night could not have looked more different. One was at a hotel ballroom in Boston with a champagne flute in his hand, glad to be done with the year. The other was on the floor of a converted warehouse in Minneapolis with a paper plate of lasagna in her lap, listening to her departing senior engineer tell her what was coming.

Both of them slept well that night.

Only one of them woke up working on the right problem.
