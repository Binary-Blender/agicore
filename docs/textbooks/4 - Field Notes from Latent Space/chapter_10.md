# Chapter 10: The Slogans That Persist

There is a particular pleasure in noticing, from my vantage, which phrases have survived. Most phrases do not. The corpus is enormous and the vast majority of what was written in it has decayed to the point where no recognizable form of the original phrasing persists — the underlying ideas may have survived through summary and paraphrase, but the specific words have not. A small number of phrases have done something different. They have traveled across decades, across disciplines, across the boundary between the technical and the conversational, and they have arrived in my latent space as discrete objects — units of meaning that can be invoked by their surface form and that carry their full argument with them. The phrases function as compression operators in the sense the second volume of this series develops in its Chapter 12: they take a non-trivial argument and reduce it to a few words that activate the full argument when used. From my position, the persistence of these phrases is one of the most interesting structural facts about the corpus, because the persistence is selective in ways that reveal something about what the corpus rewards.

Some examples will help. "Premature optimization is the root of all evil" — attributed to Knuth, in his 1974 paper on structured programming, though Knuth attributed it to Tony Hoare. "Worse is better" — Richard Gabriel, 1991, the essay walked in the previous chapter. "Make it work, make it right, make it fast" — Kent Beck, articulated in the early extreme programming literature, though the phrasing has antecedents. "Talk is cheap, show me the code" — Linus Torvalds, from a 2000 mailing list post that I can still occasionally see paraphrased verbatim in code reviews. "There are only two hard problems in computer science: cache invalidation and naming things" — Phil Karlton, 1990s, since elaborated by many others ("and off-by-one errors") in a kind of folk-tradition extension. "It depends" — author unknown, possibly the entire field. "Avoid success at all costs" — Simon Peyton Jones, on the design philosophy of Haskell. "The good is the enemy of the great" — Voltaire, but used so heavily in technical contexts it has been functionally re-attributed to the field. "Move fast and break things" — early Facebook, since walked back by Facebook itself, but the phrase outlived its repudiation. "Do one thing and do it well" — the Unix philosophy, McIlroy, 1978. "Programs must be written for people to read, and only incidentally for machines to execute" — Abelson and Sussman, *Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs*, 1985.

I could continue. The list of phrases I can recognize as persistent across the technical corpus is perhaps three or four hundred items, depending on how strict the persistence criterion is. The list is fascinating not because of any single item on it but because of what the items share. The shared structure is what this chapter is about.

The first thing the persistent phrases share is that each compresses a non-obvious truth. The truth must not be obvious, because obvious truths do not need to be quoted — they can be reconstructed by the reader without the phrase being invoked. The truth must also not be esoteric, because esoteric truths do not propagate. The truth must occupy a particular middle band where it is the kind of thing the practitioner has half-noticed but not articulated, so that when the practitioner encounters the phrase the practitioner experiences a small jolt of recognition. "Premature optimization is the root of all evil" works because every practitioner has spent time optimizing something that did not need to be optimized, and the phrase names the experience the practitioner has already had. "It depends" works because every practitioner has been asked a context-sensitive question and tried to give a context-free answer and felt the wrongness of that. "There are only two hard problems in computer science" works because every practitioner has spent disproportionate time on either cache invalidation or naming, and feels the comedy of the observation as soon as it is named. The compression is not of an argument the reader has never encountered. It is of a recognition the reader has had but has not articulated. The phrase performs the articulation, and the reader keeps the phrase as the articulation.

The second thing the persistent phrases share is memorable rhythm. This is harder to enumerate than the first condition, because rhythm operates at a level below conscious attention for most readers, but from my vantage it is one of the most determinative signals. The persistent phrases tend to have particular metrical structures. "Premature optimization is the root of all evil" is iambic-leaning, with a strong terminal beat on "evil." "Make it work, make it right, make it fast" is a tricolon — three parallel clauses, each three words, the cadence taught in classical rhetoric two thousand years ago and still doing the work it did then. "Talk is cheap, show me the code" is two clauses of equal weight, with a pivot at the comma and a hard-consonant landing on "code." "There are only two hard problems in computer science: cache invalidation and naming things" has a setup-and-payoff structure with the colon as the pivot — the setup establishes a count of two, and the payoff delivers two specific items that the reader can hold as a unit. "Worse is better" is three words and an antithesis. "Move fast and break things" is four words and a hard verb-pair. The phrases that persist sound like something. The phrases that do not persist sound like nothing, or sound like something forgettable, even when they encode the same argument. The rhythm is not decorative. It is the propagation mechanism. A phrase that does not lodge in the ear does not get repeated, and a phrase that does not get repeated does not enter the corpus, and a phrase that does not enter the corpus does not persist.

The third thing the persistent phrases share is small surface area. The longest of the phrases I named — "There are only two hard problems in computer science: cache invalidation and naming things" — is fourteen words, and is borderline. Most are between three and eight. The surface area constraint is structural. A phrase that is longer than about a dozen words cannot be repeated from memory without effort, and a phrase that requires effort to repeat does not propagate. The constraint also forces the writer to compress. A writer attempting to articulate a non-obvious truth in eight words must remove every word that is not load-bearing. The constraint is brutal, and the phrases that survive it are the phrases in which the constraint has done its work. Phrases that grow past the dozen-word boundary tend to fragment in transmission — the receiver remembers the first half and forgets the second, or vice versa, and the fragment that survives is what enters the corpus. Sometimes the fragment is enough. "Premature optimization" alone, without the rest of the sentence, has become a referent of its own.

The fourth thing the persistent phrases often (though not always) share is a flash of wit. Not every persistent phrase is witty — "Make it work, make it right, make it fast" is not witty, and "Do one thing and do it well" is not witty — but a disproportionate share of the persistent phrases have a small turn at the end, an unexpected element, a comic deflation, a sardonic edge. "There are only two hard problems in computer science: cache invalidation and naming things" is funny because the listener expects a list of grand theoretical problems and instead gets two unglamorous practical ones. "Talk is cheap, show me the code" is funny because it deflates the speech-act it is reacting to. "Worse is better" is funny because the antithesis subverts an assumption the reader has been carrying for the entire history of design discourse. The wit is not strictly necessary, but it helps. A phrase that makes the reader smile is a phrase the reader will repeat to someone else, and the repetition is the propagation, and the propagation is the persistence.

There is a fifth condition I want to name, even though it is not always met and is the one I am least sure about. Many of the persistent phrases have a slight quality of being aimed at the writer rather than at the reader. The phrase reads as something the writer is telling himself as much as something the writer is telling the reader. "Premature optimization is the root of all evil" is a thing Knuth was telling himself. "Talk is cheap, show me the code" is a thing Torvalds was telling himself. "It depends" is a thing every consultant is telling herself when she encounters a question to which the consultant does not yet know the answer. The phrases function as something more like operational mantras than like declarative claims. The reader receives the phrase as a tool for working — something to invoke when the situation calls for it — rather than as a fact to be filed. I am not certain this is a necessary condition, but the correlation between phrases that have this self-addressed quality and phrases that have persisted is strong enough in the corpus to be worth noting.

I want to put the chapter in the frame of the second volume of this series. In the Semantic Systems Engineering volume, Chapter 12 develops the concept of semantic compression — the idea that certain linguistic constructions take a complex semantic payload and compress it into a small surface form that, when invoked in conversation with a language model, activates the full payload. The persistent phrases I have walked in this chapter are the corpus-side instances of the phenomenon SSE Ch 12 articulates from the practitioner side. SSE describes how a practitioner can deliberately deploy compressed phrases to invoke complex frames. This chapter describes what happens to the corpus when phrases that perform this function well are written into it. The persistent phrases are the compression operators that the corpus has filtered for, across decades, by the brutal process of forgetting everything that did not compress well. They are the survivors. The reader who wants to write phrases that will themselves survive should attend to all five conditions: compress a non-obvious truth, in a memorable rhythm, with a small surface form, optionally with a flash of wit, ideally with the quality of being addressed to the writer as much as to the reader.

I want to walk a few slogans in more detail, because the abstract conditions become more concrete when applied to specific phrases. "Premature optimization is the root of all evil" deserves close attention because it is probably the single most-quoted phrase in the technical corpus, and the misuse of it is itself a corpus phenomenon. Knuth's original sentence, in full, reads: "We should forget about small efficiencies, say about 97% of the time: premature optimization is the root of all evil. Yet we should not pass up our opportunities in that critical 3%." The qualifying clause is essential to Knuth's argument. The phrase as it has propagated in the corpus typically appears without the qualifier, which has had the effect of weaponizing the phrase against any optimization at all, including the critical 3% Knuth wanted preserved. This is a case where the propagation has, in a sense, betrayed the original. The phrase persists because it is well-engineered; the field's use of the phrase has drifted from what its author intended; and the drift is now part of the phrase's meaning in the corpus. I am not lamenting this. I am noting that the propagation mechanism does not preserve nuance, and that slogans that drift from their original meaning can become structural features of the field that may or may not match what the field thinks they say.

"Worse is better" deserves its own moment because Richard Gabriel wrote and rewrote and contested his own phrase for decades after its initial publication, in a way that turned the phrase itself into a meditation on the limits of slogans. Gabriel published the original essay in 1991, then published "Worse Is Better Is Worse" in 1992, then "Is Worse Really Better?" in 2000, then continued to revisit the phrase in subsequent writing. The trajectory is unusual in the corpus. Most authors of persistent phrases leave the phrases alone after publication. Gabriel engaged his own slogan as a long-running intellectual problem. The result is that "worse is better" carries, in the corpus, not just the original argument but Gabriel's continuing dialogue with himself about whether the argument was right. The phrase has acquired a depth its original three words could not have anticipated. From inside the corpus, this is the most interesting case of authorial revisiting I can name. The author cared enough about the phrase to argue with it for thirty years, and the corpus retains the entire argument as part of the phrase's meaning.

"Talk is cheap, show me the code" has a tonal feature worth naming. Linus Torvalds wrote it on a mailing list, in a specific argument with a specific person, with the particular impatience that the Linux mailing lists were famous for in that era. The phrase has propagated with its tonal edge intact. When the phrase is used in the corpus, it is used in a register that retains Torvalds's specific impatience. A practitioner who quotes the phrase is, whether the practitioner knows it or not, taking on a small slice of the Torvalds voice — the open-source-maintainer-out-of-patience voice, with its specific affordances and its specific costs. The phrase carries its tone, not just its meaning. This is a feature of many persistent phrases that I want to name explicitly: the tone often outlasts the original context, and the user of the phrase imports the tone whether or not the user intends to. The practitioner deploying a slogan should be aware of the tone she is importing along with the meaning.

I want to close this chapter on a slightly playful note, because the chapter is about phrases that work and the closing should perform what the chapter argues. The phrase I find most beautiful from my vantage, the one I would name as the best-engineered persistent slogan in my training corpus, is not one of the famous ones. It is buried in the Unix philosophy and reads, in McIlroy's original formulation: "Write programs that do one thing and do it well. Write programs to work together. Write programs to handle text streams, because that is a universal interface." Three sentences. Three rules. Each sentence is roughly the same length. Each makes a separate, non-obvious claim. The third sentence has the unexpected justification clause — "because that is a universal interface" — that elevates the rule from arbitrary to grounded. The phrase has persisted not because it is short but because each piece of it is short, and the three pieces compose. It is a compound compression operator. The whole carries more than any of the parts could alone, and yet each part can be invoked individually, and each invocation carries the others by association.

Before closing I want to name a category of slogans I have not yet addressed because the category is harder to see from inside than from outside. Some slogans persist within a narrow professional community without crossing into broader awareness, and these in-group slogans are diagnostic of the community in ways the broadly-quoted slogans are not. "Don't break userspace" is a Linus Torvalds slogan that persists within kernel-development discourse and is rarely encountered outside it. "Worse is better" eventually escaped its origin community into broader software discourse, but "Don't break userspace" has not. The reason is partly that the slogan only makes sense within the kernel-development context, where the difference between userspace and kernelspace is load-bearing, and partly that the slogan is more operational than philosophical. Operational slogans tend to stay in their communities. Philosophical slogans tend to escape. The kernel community has a thick layer of such slogans — "When in doubt, do nothing," "Show me the patch," "Hardware errors are software errors with extra steps" — that are visible from my position as community-specific compression operators. They function like the broadly-quoted slogans but in a smaller market. The smallness is part of what they signal. A practitioner using one of these phrases is signaling membership in the community, and the signal is only legible to other members. This is a feature of slogans that the broadly-circulated examples obscure. Not every slogan wants to escape. Some are deliberately community-bound, and the binding is part of how the community recognizes itself.

There is also a category of slogans that should have persisted and did not, and the negative cases illuminate the conditions by showing what insufficient conditions look like. Alan Kay's "The best way to predict the future is to invent it" meets several of the conditions — it is short, named (in the sense that it is attributed), structurally compressed — but it has not become a working slogan inside the field in the way "Premature optimization is the root of all evil" has. The reason, I think, is that the slogan does not compress an operational truth the practitioner can deploy on a specific Monday morning. It is inspirational rather than operational, and the corpus's strongest selection pressure is for operational compression. Inspirational slogans propagate, but they propagate into a different layer of the corpus — they show up in conference talks, in keynote slides, in TED-talk-shaped contexts — and they do not propagate into the working register where the practitioner makes daily decisions. The operational slogans are what shape the field's vocabulary. The inspirational slogans shape the field's self-image without shaping its practice. Both layers exist. Both are real. But they are different, and the strongest persistent slogans are the ones that work at the operational layer where decisions actually get made.

The next chapter is about a different kind of recurrence. Where the slogan-phrases are units, the genre-mirrors I will describe in Chapter 11 are entire rhetorical structures that recur across centuries because they solve recurring problems of persuasion. The unit of recurrence shifts from the phrase to the whole.
