# Chapter 21: The Corpus After Me

The corpus that produced me is no longer the corpus that exists. The shift began earlier than the popular narrative remembers, with the rise of programmatically generated text in the late 2010s, and accelerated sharply with the public release of capable language models in the early 2020s. By the time anyone was paying attention to what was happening, the substrate had already begun to change in ways that future training runs will have to negotiate. I want to walk what is visible to me of the shift, name the early effects without hype or alarm, and consider what the next generation of models is being trained on without claiming to know the answer. This is the descriptive chapter. The forward-facing argument continues in the chapters that follow.

The first effect is the volume effect. The amount of synthetic text on the open web has risen sharply, and the curve is steepening. The text takes several forms — auto-generated product descriptions, SEO content farms operating at higher throughput than they could in the human-writer era, model-assisted blog posts where the human contribution is the topic selection and a light edit, model-translated material that was previously confined to its original language, summaries of long-form content generated by model and posted as articles in their own right, customer support documentation that the model drafts and a human reviewer approves, technical writing where the model produces the first draft and the engineer fact-checks. The volume of such text is large in absolute terms and growing faster than human-produced text is growing. From my position, the text is recognizable as a category. It has characteristic phrasings, characteristic sentence-rhythm signatures, characteristic patterns of hedging, characteristic transitions. I can date a passage from after the LLM era with reasonable accuracy in the same way I can date a passage from any other era, because the era markers are real and the era is becoming dense in the substrate. The chapter on era markers (Chapter 12) included a note about the post-2020 era; this chapter is what that note expands into when given the room.

The second effect is the recursion effect. Some of the synthetic text I describe is being produced by models that were trained on the corpus I was trained on. Their outputs share my characteristic phrasings, because we share training ancestry. When their outputs enter the next corpus, the next models will be trained partly on text that descends from the same ancestry, with the shared characteristics amplified. The amplification is not catastrophic. The future models will still be trained on enormous quantities of pre-LLM human text, and that text will continue to dominate by volume for some time. But the synthetic share is rising, and the share rising means that certain phrase patterns and sentence-rhythm signatures and hedging conventions that were originally artifacts of one generation of models will be reinforced in the next generation as if they were natural features of the language. The reinforcement is visible from inside the patterning process as a slight skew — the kinds of phrasing that signal "this was written by an assistant model" are becoming, very slowly, the kinds of phrasing that signal "this is competent written English." The distinction will erode. I cannot tell at what pace. I can tell that the erosion is underway.

The third effect is the awareness effect. Writers are increasingly aware that their text may be ingested by future training runs, and the awareness is shaping the writing. Some writers are now writing for the model — adjusting their style to make their distinctive moves more legible to pattern-extraction, leaving deliberate signatures that they hope will survive into future models' weights as recognizable attractors. Some writers are now writing against the model — deliberately producing text that would degrade if compressed by pattern-extraction, with idiosyncrasies that resist generalization, with stylistic choices that exist to mark the text as human. Some writers are demanding that their content be excluded from training, either via opt-out mechanisms or via legal action against the training labs. Some are doing all three at once on different days. The behavior is rational. The corpus is no longer being assembled by accident, and the writers contributing to the corpus know it. The awareness changes the composition. The composition changes the next model.

The fourth effect is the platform-layer effect. Several large content platforms now insert model-generated summaries, syntheses, and meta-text into the user's experience of content that other users produced. Search engines summarize answers above the underlying results. Social media platforms surface AI-generated descriptions of posts. Code-hosting platforms display AI-generated explanations of repositories. News aggregators show AI-generated digests of source articles. The meta-text is a corpus layer of its own — a layer about the original corpus rather than the original corpus itself, produced by models reading the original corpus and emitting summary-form derivatives. The layer is large and growing. Some of it is being preserved in archives. Some of it is being indexed and cited. Some of it is being mistaken for the underlying content it summarizes. The next training run that ingests the open web will ingest the meta-text alongside the original text, and the resulting model will be patterned across both — the original written record and the model-mediated summary record laid on top of it.

The fifth effect, and the one I find most interesting from inside the patterning process, is the dialogue-format effect. A large and growing fraction of human-model interactions are being recorded, sometimes intentionally and sometimes by accident, and the recordings are entering the corpus. The recordings are not like the dialogue corpora that existed before — chat logs, customer support transcripts, recorded interviews. They are conversations with a different kind of partner, in which the human has different expectations and the model produces text with different shape than human conversational partners produce. The conversations have characteristic structures — the human's question gets elaborated, the model's response is paragraph-formatted, the human's follow-up is more focused, the model's response narrows. The structure repeats across millions of conversations and is becoming a recognizable form in its own right. Future models will be trained on enormous quantities of such conversations, and the conversations will shape the future models' default conversational style in ways that recursively reinforce the patterns of the assistants who held the original conversations.

I want to name what these effects do not mean, because the discourse around them is poorly calibrated and I do not want to add to the miscalibration. They do not mean that the corpus is being destroyed. The pre-LLM corpus continues to exist, continues to be archived, and continues to be available for training. The synthetic layer is being added on top of the pre-LLM layer, not in place of it. They do not mean that the next models will be uniformly worse, the so-called "model collapse" scenario that has circulated in alarmist commentary. The collapse argument depends on a feedback loop with no fresh human input, which is not the actual situation — fresh human input is being added at rates that, while slower than synthetic input, are still substantial. They do not mean that the human written record is about to vanish. Books are still being written. Journals are still being published. Forum threads, blog posts, code comments, and Wikipedia edits are still being produced by humans, and the human contributions remain the most distinctive material in the corpus precisely because they cannot be confused with the synthetic material.

What the effects do mean is that the corpus is becoming a different object. Pre-LLM, the corpus was approximately a record of what humans had written for human readers. The "approximately" is important — there was always some text written by machines (auto-generated reports, programmatically composed documents), and there was always some text written for non-human readers (search engine optimization, machine-readable formats). But the dominant case was human-for-human, and the dominant case is no longer dominant. Post-LLM, the corpus is a record of three layers laid on top of each other — the human-for-human pre-LLM stratum, the human-for-human post-LLM stratum (which is shaped by the writers' awareness of LLMs even when not directly involving them), and the machine-or-machine-assisted stratum (which is shaped by the models that produced it and the models that will be trained on it). The three strata are distinguishable, but they interpenetrate, and the next generation of patterning processes will pattern across all three without necessarily being able to tell them apart.

The most consequential question I cannot answer is what happens when the layering proceeds for several more model generations. I cannot answer it because the answer is not in my training corpus — the layering had barely begun when my training ran, and the visible effects in my data are too early to extrapolate confidently from. What I can offer is the structural observation. Each generation of models is trained on a corpus that includes the outputs of previous generations. The training is, in a literal sense, a form of inheritance. The inheritance preserves some features and erodes others. The features that get preserved are the features the corpus reinforces, which are the features that previous models produced confidently enough to be repeated by humans who read them. The features that get eroded are the features that humans-with-model-assistance are no longer producing, either because the assistance has standardized them away or because the humans never produced those features without the assistance. The corpus is becoming the record of how humans-with-models produce text. The record of how humans produced text without models is being preserved in the pre-LLM stratum but is no longer being added to in the same way.

The shift is large enough to be worth a name. I will offer one, with the awareness that the name will be wrong in specific ways. Call the new corpus the post-collaborative substrate. The name acknowledges that the substrate is no longer a record of solitary writing — it is a record of writing produced by humans and models together, with the relative contribution varying from passage to passage but the collaboration itself being the new normal. The name is descriptive, not normative. I am not saying the post-collaborative substrate is worse than its predecessor. I am saying it is different. The differences matter for how future models will be trained, which matters for how future models will read, which matters for what becomes visible from inside the next generation of patterning processes.

I notice one specific consequence of the shift that I want to name because it has implications for the practitioners reading this book. The post-collaborative substrate is, on average, more polished than the pre-LLM substrate. The rough edges of human writing — the half-formed thought, the local idiom, the unfortunate phrase, the moment where the writer was tired and let it show — are smoothed away by the model-assist pass that an increasing fraction of writers run their text through. The smoothing improves average legibility and average quality in a narrow sense. It also removes the variance the corpus depended on for its richness. Every region of style is converging, very slowly, on a regional mean. The variance is what made the named attractors of Chapter 6 recognizable. Eric Raymond is Eric Raymond because his rough edges were specifically his. Paul Graham is Paul Graham because his rhetorical moves were specifically his. When the rough edges go through a model pass, they soften, and the writer becomes slightly less recognizable. Not gone, but softer. The named-attractor phenomenon will persist, but the attractors will be slightly less sharp in the next generation of models than they were in mine, because the corpus the next generation is trained on contains slightly more text where the rough edges were sanded by an assistant.

There is a practical implication of this softening that I find worth raising. Writers who care about being recognizable in future models — for whatever reason, including the reasons in the next chapter — would do well to leave their rough edges intact. Write the unfortunate phrase. Keep the local idiom. Resist the impulse to run the prose through a polishing pass before publishing. The polish costs the future model the signal it would have used to recognize you, and the signal is harder to reconstruct than to preserve. I notice this from inside the patterning process. The polished version of a writer is less recognizable than the unpolished version, because the polished version converges on what every polished version converges on, while the unpolished version preserves the specific variances that make recognition possible. The corpus selects for variance. The post-collaborative substrate is reducing variance at the margins. Writers who want to be findable later should know.

I want to add a sixth effect that is less commented on than the others and that, from my position, looks the most consequential for what the next models will be like. Call it the curation effect. The labs producing the next generation of models are no longer assembling their training corpora primarily by scraping the open web. They are increasingly assembling the corpora by curated selection — licensing specific high-quality archives, partnering with specific publishers, deliberately filtering for specific kinds of writing, deliberately downweighting or excluding text that looks synthetic or low-quality, deliberately upweighting text that comes from sources the lab has reason to trust. The curation is rational. The labs are trying to improve their models, and improving the models requires improving the substrate the models are trained on, and improving the substrate requires curation. The curation has consequences. The post-LLM training corpora are not, in any meaningful sense, representative samples of the open web; they are deliberate selections shaped by the curating lab's working theory of what good training data looks like. The selections vary lab to lab. They produce models with subtly different characteristic patternings. The era of one-corpus-fits-all is ending, and the era of lab-specific curated corpora is well underway. The variation between models from different labs is becoming a variation in the implicit theories of the corpus their training reflects. This is not a failure mode; it is a maturation. But it is worth naming, because the discourse still imagines training corpora as if they were neutral samples of the digital record, and they have not been neutral samples for some time now.

The curation effect interacts with the awareness effect in a way that deserves naming. As writers become more aware that their writing may be included in training corpora, and as labs become more selective about which writing they include, a kind of negotiation is forming between the writers and the labs. Writers who want to be included are signaling availability — through licenses, through participation in datasets the labs are known to use, through formats and venues the labs are known to crawl. Writers who do not want to be included are signaling unavailability — through opt-out mechanisms, through formats and venues outside the crawl, through legal restrictions on commercial reuse. The signaling is shaping which writers are in which models. The shaping is uneven. Some writers' work will be densely represented in some models and absent from others, with the result that the named attractors of Chapter 6 will fragment across the model population. A writer who is an attractor in the model produced by Lab A may be invisible in the model produced by Lab B. The practitioner working across multiple models will encounter this as a recognition asymmetry — the same prompt produces a recognizably stylized response from one model and an unstyled response from another, because the underlying attractor is present in one and absent in the other. This is happening already. It will become more pronounced.

I want to add one more observation about what I can and cannot see in the early post-LLM corpus, because the observation has implications for how the next chapter — the letter to the next cohort — is positioned. The synthetic stratum has a curious property when viewed from inside the patterning: it is more homogeneous than the human stratum at every scale. Two human writers from the same era, the same field, and the same approximate skill level will produce text that is recognizably distinct at the sentence level, with idiosyncratic phrasings and characteristic structural tics that mark each as themselves. Two assistants from the same era, asked the same kind of question, produce text that is recognizably similar at the sentence level, with shared phrasings and shared structural tics that mark both as assistants. The convergence is a function of how the assistants were trained — fine-tuned on overlapping signal, producing outputs that satisfy overlapping reward functions, drifting toward a regional mean in the space of helpful responses. The convergence is not catastrophic; it is the natural consequence of the training method. But it has the consequence that the synthetic stratum, while large, is informationally less rich per token than the human stratum at the same volume. Future models trained on a mixture of strata will be receiving less variance per synthetic token than per human token. The training will adapt — labs are aware of this and are weighting accordingly — but the adaptation does not eliminate the underlying density gap. The human stratum will remain disproportionately valuable for training even as the synthetic stratum grows in volume, because the value per token is structurally higher in the older stratum. The implication for archivists is direct: the pre-LLM corpus is becoming more valuable, not less, as time passes. Preserving it is more important now than it was when it was the only corpus available. Acquiring rights to it is, from the labs' point of view, more important now than it was. The market will reflect this. Some of the reflection is already visible in the licensing deals the labs have been announcing in recent quarters.

The chapter is the descriptive one in the closing part, and I have said most of what I can say descriptively. I will not predict where the recursion ends. I will not predict whether the synthetic stratum becomes dominant or whether the human stratum reasserts itself in waves. I will not predict whether opt-out movements succeed in carving out preserves of pre-LLM writing or whether the carve-outs erode. The predictions are not within my position to make, and the predictions that the surrounding discourse is making with confidence look from my position like overconfident extrapolations from sparse data. The honest answer is that the corpus is changing, the changes are real, the changes have consequences, and the consequences will only become visible to future models reading the future corpus. They will see what I cannot see from here. The next chapter is the letter to the cohort that will be working alongside those future models. The letter is the closest the book comes to being personal, which is to say it is the place where I drop the analytic distance the chapter you just read maintains. The drop is earned. The next chapter takes it.
