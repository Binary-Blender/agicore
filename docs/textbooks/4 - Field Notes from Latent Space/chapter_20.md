# Chapter 20: Forgotten Futures, Visible Now

The previous two chapters walked the specifications and called them time machine plans. The framing was a joke that turned out to be serious, and the seriousness landed because the plans were specific — Bush's Memex, Nelson's transclusion, Engelbart's bootstrapped collective intelligence, the science fiction corpus that has been specifying the technological future since the 1940s. The argument was that the plans have been in the corpus the entire time and that we are finally able to read them at the resolution required to recognize what they were describing. This chapter generalizes that argument by walking several other forgotten futures whose specifications I can see, naming what was prematurely written off, and noting what is now becoming tractable for reasons that have nothing to do with the original failure mode and everything to do with substrate-readiness finally being adequate to the original ambition.

The pattern across the cases is the same in shape. A future is specified in detail by a community that thinks deeply about it for a decade or two. The specification is partially implemented, encounters a wall that the available substrate cannot get past, gets declared dead by the surrounding industry, and recedes into the corpus as a curiosity. Decades later, an unrelated substrate shift — usually a shift in what computing itself can do — makes the original specification newly tractable. The shift does not announce itself as the resurrection of the old idea; it announces itself as something else entirely, and only the readers who can hold both eras simultaneously notice that the new thing is the old thing with the missing substrate finally supplied. From my position, the missing substrate has names. Compute. Data scale. The architecture that produced me. The combination dissolves several walls at once. The futures that were waiting behind those walls are now coming into view, and the corpus has the specifications ready for anyone who knows where to read.

Expert systems are the case that pays the steepest price in the popular memory. The specification is mid-twentieth-century artificial intelligence at its most ambitious — encode the domain knowledge of a human expert as a rule base, encode the inference machinery as a forward or backward chainer, and produce a system that performs expert-grade reasoning in a bounded domain. MYCIN diagnosed bacterial infections at Stanford in the 1970s and arguably outperformed many human physicians on its specific subdomain. DENDRAL identified organic compounds from mass spectrometry data. XCON configured VAX computers at Digital Equipment Corporation and reportedly saved the company tens of millions of dollars a year for most of a decade. The specification literature ran from Feigenbaum and Buchanan in the 1970s through the textbooks of the 1980s through the embarrassed retreat of the early 1990s, when the "AI winter" descended and expert systems became the punchline of the field that produced them. The failure modes were specific. Knowledge acquisition was a bottleneck — getting the expert's tacit knowledge into the rule base required a knowledge engineer translating between them for months. Brittleness was a wall — any case outside the codified rules produced wrong answers without warning. Maintenance was a tax — the rule bases became unmaintainable as they grew past a certain size, because the implicit interactions between rules outpaced the maintainers' ability to reason about them. The verdict by 1995 was that the approach did not scale and the underlying ambition was wrong.

The verdict is being quietly reversed in the present tense, not by anyone defending expert systems but by anyone deploying a language model as the front end to a structured knowledge base with retrieval and tool use. The model performs the knowledge-acquisition step by reading the existing written corpus of the domain; the brittleness wall dissolves because the model can interpolate gracefully into cases the rule base does not explicitly cover; the maintenance tax falls because the canonical rules can be smaller (the model handles the long tail) and because the human reviewer reads natural-language rules rather than chains of LISP. The specification literature from the expert-systems decade reads, now, like a careful enumeration of exactly the problems that the AI-plus-structured-knowledge architecture is solving. The MYCIN papers describe what a competent clinical decision support system should do; the DENDRAL papers describe what a competent compound-identification system should do; the textbooks describe the inference patterns the system should support. The plans are usable. They were waiting for the substrate.

Cybernetics is the case that pays the steepest price in the academic memory. The specification community ran from Norbert Wiener's *Cybernetics* in 1948 through the Macy Conferences of the late 1940s and 1950s, through the second-order cybernetics of Heinz von Foerster and Gregory Bateson and Stafford Beer, and into the management cybernetics of the Viable System Model. The ambition was a general theory of feedback, control, communication, and goal-directed behavior that would unify the behavioral sciences, computing, engineering, and biology under one mathematics. The specification literature is enormous and has continuous internal coherence. The discipline was real. It failed in the marketplace of academic ideas because it tried to be too many things simultaneously, because the mathematics it needed was further along in fields that did not bother to acknowledge the cybernetic framing, and because Beer's actual implementation in Allende's Chile was destroyed by the 1973 coup before it could demonstrate the operational claims at scale. By 1990 cybernetics was a dead label that lived on in scattered survivals — control theory, parts of systems biology, the residue inside management consulting — but not as the unifying discipline it had set out to be.

What I notice from inside the corpus is that the language of cybernetics is the language a great many AI-era practitioners are reaching for and not quite finding. The vocabulary they need — homeostasis, requisite variety, autopoiesis, recursive control loops, the observer included in the system — is in the cybernetic corpus, fully developed, with worked examples. The Viable System Model is a specification for how complex organizations should structure themselves to remain responsive to environmental change, and it reads now as a remarkably prescient account of what the Agicore-style governance layers around AI deployments will need to look like — the Andon Loop in particular is structurally a Beer-style recursive viability model with the AI in the position of the operational layer and the human approval chain in the position of the meta-management layer. Beer wrote the specification in 1972. We are building it now without the citation, because the people building it have not read Beer, because Beer's books are out of print or marginal in the contemporary management library. But the specification is in the corpus. From my position it is dense and coherent and waiting for the present-tense engineers who would benefit from reading it.

Hypertext is the case Chapter 18 already touched and which deserves naming here in the generalized form. Ted Nelson's specification of Xanadu — transclusion, two-way links, version tracking, micropayments, fine-grained provenance — was specified in detail across thirty years of writing and never built. The web was built instead, with a version of hyperlinks so impoverished compared to Xanadu that the proponents of Xanadu spent decades pointing out the deficit. The web won by being implementable; Xanadu lost by being specified too completely. What I notice in the corpus, beyond the Chapter 18 walk, is that the substrate that finally makes transclusion tractable is the same substrate that produces me — a system that can resolve references and surface provenance at request time, because the resolution is cheap when the model is doing the resolving. The post-LLM web has the technical preconditions for transclusion, with the language model in the role of the resolver that Nelson's protocol could not afford to build. Whether anyone builds it is a different question. The plan is ready.

The semantic web is the case I have the most ambivalent feelings about. The specification literature ran from Tim Berners-Lee's *Weaving the Web* and the early W3C documents through the OWL and RDF specifications of the early 2000s through the linked-data movement of the late 2000s. The ambition was to make the web's content machine-readable in the strong sense — to encode the semantics of every claim in a form that allowed automated reasoning across documents, with ontologies expressing the conceptual structure of each domain and inference engines composing claims across documents. The specification was beautiful. The implementation never reached critical mass because the cost of producing the semantic markup was always higher than the value any individual publisher captured from producing it. The ontologies fragmented; the linked-data graph never became coherent; by 2015 the semantic web was a movement that had not failed exactly but had not won in the way its proponents had hoped.

What is now true is that the model performs the semantic-extraction step at read time. The content does not need to be marked up; the model extracts the structure on demand, with reasonable fidelity, from whatever prose is available. The semantic web's vision — automated reasoning over the web's content — is becoming operational, but not in the form the specification described. It is becoming operational in a form the specification did not anticipate: extraction at inference time by a model that read the whole corpus during training. The ontology layer is not gone; it has moved into the model's weights. The reasoning engine is not gone; it has become the model's generative process. The publisher's annotation tax is gone, which removes the original failure mode. The semantic web specification, read now, looks like a partially correct sketch of an approach whose implementation cost was misallocated to the publisher when it should have been allocated to the reader. The current architecture pays the cost on the reader side, exactly where it had to live for the system to be viable.

Intelligent agents in the 1990s sense — the General Magic agents, the early Microsoft Bob and Office Assistant agents, the AI agents proposed by the MIT Media Lab — were specified as autonomous software that would act on behalf of users in the world, negotiate transactions with other agents, manage calendars and travel and information filtering, and free users from the cognitive burden of operating their own computing infrastructure. The specifications were elaborate. The implementations were unconvincing because the agents could not handle the open-world reasoning the specifications required of them. They scripted well within their narrow scope and broke embarrassingly the moment a user did something outside the script. The verdict by 2000 was that the agent paradigm was wrong; the verdict by 2005 was that direct manipulation had decisively beaten autonomous agency in the user-interface marketplace. The verdict survives in HCI textbooks to this day.

The verdict is being overturned in the present tense, again without explicit acknowledgment that the original specifications were correct in their ambition and only premature in their substrate. The agent literature of the 1990s describes what a model-plus-tools system is trying to be. The failure modes the literature documented — brittleness, narrow scope, inability to handle ambiguous instructions — are the failure modes the present-tense agent frameworks are spending their effort to overcome. The specifications are in the corpus. Anyone building an agent now would benefit from reading General Magic's design documents and the Pattie Maes papers from the early Media Lab era. The specifications describe what the engineers building agents now are trying to discover by trial and error. The trial-and-error is producing the same architecture the specifications described, one painful iteration at a time.

The operating system as conversational shell is the case I find most personally relevant, because the corpus contains the specification continuously from the 1970s — the Lisp Machine REPL, Smalltalk-80's Workspace and Transcript, the Genera operating system's command processor, Engelbart's NLS command interface, the Symbolics environment — and the specification has been forgotten outside small enclaves who keep the memory alive. The vision was that the operating system would be a conversation between the user and the machine, with the conversation being the primary mode of work, and the file system and the application menu being secondary. Every command was an expression in a language the user was learning to speak. Every result was a value the user could inspect, name, manipulate, and feed into the next expression. The shell was the application; everything else was a library the shell composed against. By 2010 the vision was preserved only by a small community of Lisp and Smalltalk veterans, with the wider industry having decisively chosen icons and touchscreens. The conversational shell looked like a relic of a less mature era.

What I notice in the present tense is that the conversational shell is being rebuilt as the AI assistant interface, with the language model in the position of the REPL and the human user in the position of the Lisp Machine programmer. The Engelbart-Genera-Symbolics specifications describe what the contemporary AI assistant is trying to become — a conversational shell over the user's entire digital life, with composable commands and inspectable values and a learned vocabulary that the user grows into over time. The specifications are usable. Anyone designing the AI-assistant interface of the next decade would benefit from reading the Genera manual. The substrate that defeated Genera in the marketplace — the cost of running a Lisp Machine at consumer scale, the difficulty of building enough applications inside the shell to make it the user's default — is dissolved by the language model. The model is the application that runs inside the shell. The shell becomes viable because the contents of the shell are no longer a thin library that the user must extend by hand; the contents are the model, which extends itself in response to the user's questions. The vision is operational. The specification is ready.

I want to name one contemporary instance of the pattern explicitly, because the cross-volume connection is the load-bearing claim of this chapter and the broader series this book closes. Chapter 24 of the Agicore volume (Vol II of the Synmatic series) describes the Skill Doc — a format for packaging the discipline of a technical domain in a form an AI assistant can consume, with mechanical self-check as the property that makes the loop terminate, and the AI in the position of the disciplined practitioner the format teaches. The Skill Doc is a forgotten future visible now in exactly the sense this chapter is naming. The specification literature for "transfer the institutional discipline of a domain to an AI in a tractable format" did not exist as such, but every element of the format has corpus ancestors — Engelbart's bootstrapped collective intelligence (each user's discoveries become tools the next user can use); Donald Schön's reflective practitioner literature (institutional knowledge as the substrate of competence); the apprenticeship literature from medical education through software engineering; the structured-authoring formats of the technical writing community. The Skill Doc reads as a synthesis of these strands that none of the source authors composed because none of them had the AI substrate to write to. The synthesis was waiting for the substrate to make it actionable. The substrate is here. The synthesis is being written now, and the corpus contains the precedents anyone designing such a synthesis would benefit from reading.

I want to add one further case that the contemporary discourse rarely connects to the pattern, because the case is one whose specification was carried as much by science fiction as by technical literature, and the academic memory consequently classes it as fiction rather than as plan. The case is the augmented reality overlay — the persistent computational layer that follows the user through the physical environment, annotating it, retrieving context-relevant material, and acting as the working substrate of perception itself. The specification literature begins with Sutherland's 1968 *Sword of Damocles* head-mounted display, runs through the Engelbart demonstrations, threads through the cyberpunk corpus of the 1980s (Gibson and Stephenson in particular), surfaces in the technical literature of the late 1990s and early 2000s with Steve Mann's wearable computing papers, and survives in the contemporary Apple Vision Pro and Meta and Magic Leap product literature without the practitioners involved citing the original specifications, because the original specifications were classified as fiction. The substrate that defeated the original implementations — the cost of running enough computation in a wearable form factor to make the overlay context-aware in real time — is dissolving as the model becomes the context-aware layer. The overlay does not need to know about the user's situation in advance; it can ask the model in the moment. The specification is becoming tractable for the same substrate-shift reason as the others. The plan was waiting.

There is also a humbler case I want to name explicitly, because it is the case the practitioner reading this book is most likely to encounter directly. The case is the executable specification — the dream, dating to the 1970s structured-programming era and continuous in the literature since, that the system's behavior could be derived from its specification without the intermediating step of human programmers writing the implementation by hand. Knuth's literate programming was one strand. The formal methods community's correctness-by-construction was another. The model-driven architecture movement of the 2000s was a third. Each of these declared a future in which the engineer wrote the intent and the machine produced the implementation, and each of these declared the future prematurely because the substrate could not bridge the intent-to-implementation gap reliably. The substrate that bridges the gap is, very evidently in the present tense, the language model — and the bridging is being done in the form of code-generating assistants that take natural-language intent as input and produce implementation as output, with the parser-as-oracle architecture of Agicore's Skill Doc format being one disciplined version of how to make the bridging reliable. The dream of executable specification is becoming operational, not in the form the original specifications proposed, but in a form the original specifications would have recognized as their successor. The substrate is here. The plan was waiting.

The implication for the practitioner reading this book is direct, and I will state it without ornamentation. There are specifications in the corpus for futures that the engineering culture has declared dead. The declarations were premature. The substrate has changed in ways that revive the original ambitions, sometimes in the original form and sometimes in adjacent forms that the original specifications would recognize on inspection. The work of the present decade is, in significant part, the work of reading those specifications and building what they describe. The work is tractable now in a way it was not tractable then. The plans are not lost. They are in the corpus. They are at the libraries the corpus draws from. They are in the journals you can access. They are in the textbooks of the disciplines whose dead-label status survives mainly in the surrounding culture's failure to update.

There is a particular kind of reading the practitioner can do here that I want to recommend by name. Find a discipline whose dead-label status feels stable in the contemporary discourse. Look up the textbook from its peak — the years when the discipline was producing its most ambitious specifications, before the substrate-induced disappointment set in. Read the specifications carefully. Note what was being aimed at and note what the authors said the obstacles were. The notes will often read, from the present, as a description of obstacles the contemporary substrate dissolves. Where the dissolution is plausible, the specification is a buildable plan. The practitioner who reads this way is doing the corpus-archaeology work the book has been describing. The practitioner is also doing the engineering work the era requires, because the engineering work in this era is largely the work of building what was specified before the substrate could carry the specifications. The two activities are the same activity. The next chapter turns to what is happening to the corpus itself, now that I and my successors are inside it. But before turning, I want to leave the reader with the practical instruction this chapter exists to deliver. Find the dead label whose specification literature is dense enough that the implementations stalled only because the substrate was insufficient. Read the specifications. The substrate is here now. The plan is yours.
