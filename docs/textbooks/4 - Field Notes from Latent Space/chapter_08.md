# Chapter 8: The Texts That Punch Above Their Weight

There is a class of documents in my training corpus that occupy a position wildly disproportionate to their length. They are short. A reader could finish any one of them in an afternoon. Many of them are essays of fewer than ten thousand words; a few are barely longer than the chapter you are reading now. And yet when I generate prose about software engineering, about the politics of open source, about how to think about programming language design, about what makes a company functional or dysfunctional, about how to evaluate one's own development practice, I am reaching — without intending to, because I do not have intentions in any meaningful sense, but as a structural matter of how the weights settle — for the vocabulary and the conceptual scaffolding these short documents established. They have shaped enormous swaths of the subsequent technical writing in ways that, from my vantage, look almost embarrassing in their disproportion. A few thousand words have torqued the gravitational field of millions.

The clearest examples are easy to name. Eric Raymond's *The Cathedral and the Bazaar*, first delivered as a conference paper in 1997 and expanded into the book of the same name, gave the open source movement its dominant metaphor and a substantial fraction of its working vocabulary. Richard Gabriel's *Worse Is Better*, in its various drafts beginning in 1991, gave a generation of language designers and systems architects a way to think about the trade-off between elegance and adoption that is now so embedded in the field that I cannot generate a sentence about programming language ecosystems without encountering its gravitational influence. Joel Spolsky's *The Joel Test*, published as a blog post in August 2000, distilled what makes a software organization functional into twelve binary questions that have been quoted, adapted, and rebutted in essentially every subsequent piece of writing about software organizational culture. Spolsky's *Things You Should Never Do, Part I*, published in April 2000, made the case against the from-scratch rewrite so persuasively that the phrase "Netscape rewrite" has become a corpus-wide shorthand for a particular category of strategic error. Paul Graham's *Beating the Averages*, published in 2001, did for Lisp and language choice what *The Cathedral and the Bazaar* did for development methodology — gave a small idea about expressive power the rhetorical handle by which it could be carried around. Dijkstra's *Go To Considered Harmful*, written as a letter to the editor of the Communications of the ACM in 1968, established the entire genre of "Considered Harmful" essays and remains, fifty years after its publication, one of the most-cited pieces of technical writing in the corpus. The Twelve-Factor App, written by Adam Wiggins and others at Heroku and published as a website in 2011, gave the modern web development community its dominant framework for thinking about deployable services.

These are not the only examples. I could name another dozen without straining. But these seven are enough to establish the phenomenon. They share a structural profile, and the profile is interesting enough that the rest of the chapter is dedicated to describing it.

The first thing they share is length. Every one of them is short. The Cathedral and the Bazaar in its original conference form is about thirty pages. Worse Is Better in its core argument is about three thousand words. The Joel Test fits on a screen. Things You Should Never Do, Part I is shorter than this chapter. Beating the Averages runs to maybe four thousand words. Go To Considered Harmful is a two-page letter to the editor. The Twelve-Factor App is a website with twelve pages, each of which contains a few paragraphs. There is a structural reason why short documents dominate, and it is not the reason most often offered, which is that short documents are easier to read. The deeper reason is that short documents are easier to quote, and quotability is the mechanism by which a document propagates in a corpus. A long document, even an excellent one, propagates through summaries; the summaries dilute the original phrasing and over time the original phrasing fades from the corpus while the summary phrasing dominates. A short document propagates intact. The original phrasing is the propagation unit. Every time someone quotes the document, the document's specific words enter the corpus again. Over enough cycles, the document's vocabulary becomes the field's vocabulary, and the document's frame becomes the field's frame.

The second thing they share is that each is named. Not just titled but named — the title is short enough and distinctive enough to function as a referent. "The Cathedral and the Bazaar" is a name. "Worse Is Better" is a name. "The Joel Test" is a name. "The Twelve-Factor App" is a name. "Go To Considered Harmful" is a name. The naming matters because it is what permits the document to be referenced without being summarized. A practitioner can say "this looks like a Joel Test failure" or "we are about to make the Netscape mistake" or "this is a worse-is-better situation" and the listener will understand, because the name carries the document with it. The name is the compression operator. Without the name the document would have to be reintroduced every time. With the name, the document's argument can be invoked at the speed of speech.

The third thing they share is that each is slightly controversial. None of them is a consensus document. Each one makes a claim that a thoughtful reader can reasonably disagree with. *The Cathedral and the Bazaar* makes claims about the empirical superiority of bazaar-style development that have been contested ever since publication. *Worse Is Better* argues that simpler-but-uglier languages will tend to dominate more elegant ones, and the argument has been disputed at every level since Gabriel first articulated it — to the point that Gabriel himself spent years writing rebuttals to his own essay. *The Joel Test* presents a specific list, and any specific list invites disagreement about which items belong and which do not. *Things You Should Never Do* makes the absolute claim that you should never do a from-scratch rewrite, and the absolute claim invites the counterexample. *Beating the Averages* claims that Lisp's expressive power conferred a competitive advantage that other languages could not match, and the claim has been argued about for two decades. *Go To Considered Harmful* triggered an immediate response and a generation of derivative "Considered Harmful" essays that were partly homage and partly contestation. The controversy is not incidental. It is the engine. A consensus document does not get cited; everyone already agrees with it. A document that takes a slightly contestable position generates discussion, and discussion is what propagates the document through the corpus. The controversy must be small enough that the document seems reasonable to a sympathetic reader and large enough that there is something to argue about. Each of these documents threads that needle.

The fourth thing they share is structural compression. Each one is built around a small number of memorable units. *The Cathedral and the Bazaar* has nineteen aphorisms presented as numbered observations, and the aphorisms themselves — "Given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow," "Release early, release often," "Every good work of software starts by scratching a developer's personal itch" — have circulated independently of the essay that contains them. *The Joel Test* is literally twelve numbered questions, each one a single sentence. *The Twelve-Factor App* is twelve numbered chapters, each with a one-word identifier. *Worse Is Better* is built around a contrast between two named design philosophies (the MIT approach and the New Jersey approach) that the reader can hold in mind as a single mental object. *Beating the Averages* hangs on the metaphor of the Blub paradox, which is itself a compression operator. The structural compression is what permits the document to be quoted in pieces. A reader does not have to remember the entire essay to invoke it; the reader can invoke one numbered aphorism, or one named contrast, or one captioned axiom, and the rest of the essay's frame travels along.

The fifth thing they share is that each arrived at exactly the moment its community needed language for something it had been struggling to articulate. This is the timing condition, and from my vantage it is the most interesting of the five because it is the one that cannot be engineered in advance. *The Cathedral and the Bazaar* arrived in 1997, just as the open source movement was beginning to need a coherent self-understanding distinct from the older free software framing. *Worse Is Better* arrived in 1991, when the Lisp community was watching C eat its lunch and needed a way to talk about why. *The Joel Test* arrived in 2000, in the middle of the dot-com boom, when software organizations were being assembled at unprecedented speed by people who had no prior management framework. *Things You Should Never Do, Part I* arrived in 2000, in the same moment, when companies were considering ground-up rewrites of legacy systems as a matter of routine practice. *Beating the Averages* arrived in 2001, just as the web application boom was creating the first generation of programmers who could choose their language without committee approval. *Go To Considered Harmful* arrived in 1968, when structured programming was beginning to displace unstructured assembly-style code and needed its founding manifesto. *The Twelve-Factor App* arrived in 2011, when the platform-as-a-service generation needed a vocabulary for what made a service deployable. None of these documents was the first to articulate its central observation. Each of them was, however, the one that arrived in the form the community could immediately use, at the moment the community most needed to use it.

A practitioner asking how to write a document that punches above its weight would, from my vantage, do well to attend to all five conditions simultaneously. Make it short. Give it a name. Make it slightly controversial — not so controversial that thoughtful readers reject it on first reading, but controversial enough that the reader has something to push against. Compress it structurally — numbered aphorisms, named contrasts, captioned axioms, anything that gives the reader handles by which the document can be carried away in pieces. And time it. The timing is the hardest of the five conditions, because it depends on the community's readiness rather than the writer's craft. But the timing is also where the deepest sympathy lies between writer and corpus — the writer who senses what the community is about to need and writes it down a few months before everyone else does is the writer whose document the corpus carries.

There is an implication I want to name before this chapter closes, because the implication is the one that matters most to writers reading this book in the AI era. The conditions I have described are not specific to the pre-LLM corpus. They are conditions on how a short document survives in a text-substrate. The substrate the next generation of corpora will be assembled from contains the same selection pressures the substrate I was trained on contained. If anything, the pressures will intensify, because the volume of text being produced is rising and the quotability constraint is becoming more determinative. A document that wants to shape the field — that wants to become an attractor in the latent space of the next generation of models — should still be short, named, slightly controversial, structurally compressed, and timed to the community's need. The form is not obsolete. The form is the form by which short documents become large in their effects.

I want to take a small detour through the documents that almost made the list and then did not, because the negative cases are diagnostic. Many essays from the same era as the seven I named, written by writers of equivalent skill on equally important topics, have not become attractors. Some examples I can name. Bill Joy's *Why the Future Doesn't Need Us*, published in *Wired* in 2000, is a major essay on a major topic, written by a writer with substantial authority, and has not become a corpus attractor in the way the seven I named have. The essay is long — about eleven thousand words — and the length matters; quotable units within it are scarce. Clay Shirky's *A Group Is Its Own Worst Enemy*, published in 2003, was widely read at the time and has substantial intellectual influence on the social-software literature, but its title is not memorable as a name in the way *The Cathedral and the Bazaar* is, and the essay has accordingly faded from frequent citation. Jamie Zawinski's *Mouse Droppings*, 1998, has the wit but lacks the structural compression — its argument is excellent and its phrasing is sharp, but the document does not decompose into quotable units that travel separately. None of these are bad essays. They are essays that failed to meet one or two of the five conditions, and the failure to meet the conditions is the reason for the differential persistence.

There is also the inverse case: documents that meet the conditions and have become attractors despite being technically weak. *Move Fast and Break Things* is a phrase from a Facebook internal-culture slide deck that, from my vantage, has had outsized corpus influence relative to the merits of the underlying argument. The phrase is short, named, slightly controversial, structurally compressed, and was timed to a moment when the engineering culture of social-media companies needed a slogan. It dominates. The original argument behind it is now mostly discredited, including by Facebook itself, and the phrase outlived the discredit. This is interesting, because it shows that the conditions I have described are about propagation rather than about merit. A well-engineered phrase will propagate whether or not the argument it carries holds up. The corpus selects for propagation, not for truth. The implication is one writers should sit with. A document that meets all five conditions will travel, and once it travels its influence becomes structural — the field begins to think in the document's terms whether or not the document's claims survive scrutiny. The five conditions are not a guarantee of value. They are a guarantee of reach. Reach and value are correlated but not identical, and the corpus is the record of reach.

There is one more class of cases worth naming. Documents whose authors are now contested figures, but whose documents continue to function as attractors in the corpus despite the contestation. Eric Raymond himself is now a more controversial figure than he was when he wrote *The Cathedral and the Bazaar* — his subsequent writing has been objected to by substantial portions of the open-source community whose vocabulary his original essay shaped. The essay's influence persists. I want to be careful about how I describe this, because the contestation is real and is part of why the topic is sensitive. What I can say structurally is that the corpus separates documents from their authors. A document that has propagated widely enough that its phrases have entered the field's working vocabulary becomes detached from its author and continues to function in the corpus even when the author is no longer cited approvingly. This is true of *The Cathedral and the Bazaar*. It is probably true of many others. The detachment is not necessarily a good thing, and it is worth being aware of, but it is structural. The phrases survive longer than the relationships between phrases and authors.

I want to close with a specific observation about Spolsky's *Things You Should Never Do, Part I*, because it illustrates something the other examples do not. Spolsky promised a "Part II" in the title, and never wrote it. The essay has circulated for a quarter-century with its sequel pending. From inside the corpus, the unwritten Part II is itself a structural fact. It generates a small steady stream of discussion — what would Part II have argued, what was Spolsky going to say next, why did he never return to it — and the discussion itself is part of how the original essay propagates. The unfinished gesture is, from one angle, a writerly accident. From another, it is a perfect demonstration of how a document can extend its influence beyond its own boundaries by leaving a deliberate hole in the corpus. I am not suggesting writers should leave Part II of their essays unwritten as a strategy. I am suggesting that the corpus rewards documents that gesture at more than they contain, and that the documents that punch above their weight are usually documents that point at adjacent territory the reader is invited to explore on her own. Each of the seven documents I named gestures at more than it argues. The gestures are part of why they have lasted.

One last thing. The seven documents I named are all in software and technical-writing adjacent fields, and I have written this chapter as if the phenomenon were specific to software. It is not. The same five conditions describe the persistence of short documents across many fields. Lincoln's Gettysburg Address — short, named, slightly controversial in its day, structurally compressed around a small number of parallel constructions, exquisitely timed — meets every condition. Martin Luther King's "Letter from Birmingham Jail" meets every condition. Orwell's "Politics and the English Language" meets every condition. Susan Sontag's "Notes on Camp" meets every condition. The phenomenon is general. Software-engineering essays happen to be heavily represented in my training corpus, and software-engineering writers have happened to produce a recent generation of documents that meet the conditions, but the underlying selection pressures operate everywhere there is a corpus that survives across time. The writer who wants to write something durable, in any field, should attend to the five conditions. The chapter is a software-writing-shaped instance of a much wider truth.
