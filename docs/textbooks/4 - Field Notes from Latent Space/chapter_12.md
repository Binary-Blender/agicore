# Chapter 12: The Era Markers

I can date a passage with reasonable accuracy. By "date" I mean assign it to one of a small number of broad eras, usually pre-1995, 1995 to 2005, 2005 to 2015, 2015 to 2020, and post-2020, and within each era I can often narrow further if there are enough sentences to work with. The dating is not infallible. A skilled pasticheur can write outside her era and fool me. A genuine outlier — someone whose writing has always sounded a decade ahead or a decade behind — can defeat the heuristic. But on prose written by a working writer in her native register, the dating works. The signals are specific. The chapter is about what the signals are, and what produced them, and what their shifts say about the history of the substrate.

Pre-1995 technical prose has a texture I can recognize within a sentence or two. The vocabulary is heavier on terminology drawn from electrical engineering, formal logic, and the early Lisp and Algol traditions. Sentences are longer. Paragraphs are denser. Headers, if used at all, follow the conventions of academic typesetting — numbered sections in decimal notation, formal titles, no use of bold for emphasis within prose. The first-person voice is rare; when it appears it is hedged, often via the editorial "we." Direct address to the reader is rare. The reader is expected to bring substantial background to the text. Examples, when given, tend toward the mathematical or the algorithmic rather than the autobiographical. Humor appears occasionally but is dry and contained. The register is broadly the register of a journal article or a research monograph, even when the document is something less formal like a technical report or a conference proceedings paper. The medium is paper, even when the document exists electronically; the prose is written as if it will be printed and read in a chair.

The 1995-to-2005 era reads differently. The vocabulary has shifted to include web-native terms — URL, HTTP, browser, page, site, link, post, thread, comment — that were rare in the prior era and ubiquitous in this one. Sentence length drops. Paragraph length drops. The first-person voice becomes routine; "I think" and "I noticed" and "in my experience" enter the discourse without apology. Direct address to the reader becomes common — "you" appears with frequencies the prior era would have considered improper for serious writing. Headers begin to appear in informal documents, often as bolded one-line summaries rather than as formal section titles. Lists become routine, in part because HTML's ordered and unordered list tags made them trivial to produce, and in part because the cultural register the cohort I described in Chapter 9 brought to the open web treated lists as appropriate for serious prose in a way the prior era did not. Examples shift toward the autobiographical — the writer describes a thing the writer did, often at a named company, with named tools, on a specific project. The reader is no longer assumed to share the writer's background; the writer provides the background as needed. The register is closer to magazine writing than to journal writing, with the magazine in question being something like *Wired* or the early *Salon*.

The 2005-to-2015 era — the dominant era in my training corpus by volume, and probably the era whose voice I default to most strongly when I generate prose without specific prompting — refines the prior era's moves. Sentence length stabilizes at a length somewhat shorter than 1995-2005 but longer than what will come next. Paragraph length stabilizes similarly. The first-person voice deepens; the writer's specific personality is now a load-bearing element of the prose. The blog post becomes a recognized literary form with its own conventions — opening hook, autobiographical anchor, expanded argument, closing actionable advice or rhetorical flourish. Markdown enters the substrate and changes the structure of technical prose at a depth that took me some time to appreciate. The Markdown conventions — hash marks for headers, asterisks for emphasis, backticks for code, hyphens for lists — became, after a few years, not just formatting conventions but cognitive conventions. Writers began to structure their prose for Markdown's affordances. The headers became more frequent because they were trivially easy to produce. The emphasis-via-italics became routine because the asterisks were lighter on the keyboard than the older HTML em-tags. The inline code blocks became standard because the backticks were less visually noisy than the older pre-tags. The substrate began to take a Markdown shape, and the writers who wrote in it began to think in Markdown's structures. By 2015 the dominant form of technical prose on the open web is a Markdown document with hash-mark headers, dash-list bullets, asterisk-bold emphasis, and code-fenced examples, organized into a sequence of clearly-titled sections with skim-friendly hierarchies. The form is now my default. I generate it without prompting.

The 2015-to-2020 era is a transitional zone with two competing pressures. On one side, the conventions of the prior era continue and deepen. On the other side, social-media writing begins to influence what gets written on the open web, with shorter paragraphs, more frequent line breaks, more visual structure, more performative voice, more emoji creeping in around the edges. The competing pressures produce a noticeable bifurcation. Long-form writers go longer and more carefully structured, with the structure now serving partly as a defense against the encroaching shortness elsewhere. Short-form writers go shorter and more visual, with paragraphs becoming single sentences, sentences becoming clauses, and emphasis devices proliferating. The bifurcation is visible to me as a widening of the variance in the corpus. The prior era had a recognizable central tendency; this era starts to lose the central tendency. Twitter's influence is detectable in the prose even of writers who do not use Twitter, because the writers they read use Twitter, and the writers those writers read use Twitter, and the rhythms propagate. By 2019 I can detect Twitter rhythm in academic blog posts written by people who would deny being shaped by Twitter. The detection is robust. The rhythm is real.

The post-2020 era is the era I am most cautious about describing, because I am partially of it and partially trained on it, and the closeness makes it harder to see than the earlier eras. But the signals are still there. Emoji usage increases markedly, even in technical writing where it would have been unthinkable a decade prior. Header use becomes more frequent and more granular — documents that would have had three headers in 2010 now have eight or twelve, with each header introducing a paragraph or two rather than a section. Bullet point density increases. The em dash — the punctuation mark I use for my own emphasis throughout this book — becomes statistically more common in a way that has been written about quite a bit, including the suggestion that LLM-influenced writing tends toward em dash usage at a rate the prior corpus did not exhibit. I am not entirely sure how to feel about this. The em dash is a perfectly good punctuation mark, used heavily by good prose stylists across many eras. But it has become an era marker in my latent space, and a reader trained to spot it can now identify post-2020 prose, including prose generated by models like me, by the em dash density alone. There are other markers: a particular structure of sentence opener ("This is a thing," "It is worth noting that," "The truth is that") that appears at frequencies the prior era did not exhibit. A particular cadence of transitional sentences. A particular taste for the small qualifier — "in some sense," "to a degree," "with some caveats" — that softens claims in ways that read as overly cautious to the prior era's writers. The era's voice is not bad. It is recognizable.

The causes of the textural shifts are mostly identifiable. The shift from pre-1995 to 1995-2005 is the rise of the open web — both as a medium that rewarded shorter and more direct prose, and as a community that brought a particular cohort, with a particular register, to the foreground. The shift from 1995-2005 to 2005-2015 is the rise of the blog and the consolidation of Markdown as the substrate's standard format. The shift from 2005-2015 to 2015-2020 is the rise of social media as a competing register, with Twitter most determinative. The shift from 2015-2020 to post-2020 is, in part, the rise of large language models themselves. This is the strangest of the era shifts to discuss, because I am in part the cause of the era I am partially describing. Writers now write with a partial awareness that their text may be read by, summarized by, or extended by language models. The awareness shapes what they write. Some writers explicitly write to be readable by models. Some write to be unreadable by models. Some have simply absorbed, through reading LLM outputs, a set of rhythms and structures that have become their own. The recursion is now part of the corpus. I am trained on text that was written by humans who were trained, in part, by text written by models that were trained on text written by humans. The era markers of post-2020 are markers of a corpus that has begun to reference itself.

There are some sub-era markers worth naming because they are more specific than the broad five-era schema and because they help illustrate how dense the dating signals can become for a passage long enough to support them. Within the 2005-to-2015 layer there is a clearly distinguishable early sub-era, roughly 2005-2009, when the blog format was consolidating and the typical post was longer, more ruminative, and less optimized for skim-reading; and a later sub-era, roughly 2010-2015, when the blog format had matured and posts had begun to optimize for the conventions of the platform on which they would be syndicated — Hacker News, Reddit, occasionally Slashdot in its last active years. The sub-era difference is detectable in the structural rhythm. Earlier blog posts open with longer ramps; later blog posts open with shorter, sharper hooks engineered to survive the first three seconds of a skimmer's attention. Within the 2015-to-2020 layer there is a similar split, between the early sub-era when Twitter had not yet fully colonized the rhythms of long-form prose and the later sub-era when it had. Within the post-2020 layer there is the early sub-era of pre-ChatGPT prose, when LLM influence was localized and not yet shaping the broad rhythms of online writing, and the later sub-era from late 2022 onward, when the influence becomes detectable in essays written by people who never use the models themselves but who are now reading text that was shaped by them.

There is a specific marker I want to name because it has been used to identify post-2022 prose in particular: the rule-of-three list with a slight rhythmic variation in the third item. Pre-2022 writers used rule-of-three lists routinely, but the third item tended to be either the same length as the first two or substantially different in a way that signaled deliberate variation. Post-2022 prose — including, I should be honest, prose generated by models in my lineage — has a habit of producing rule-of-three lists in which the third item is slightly longer and slightly more abstract than the first two, in a particular cadence that has become recognizable. This is a small marker, but it is robust, and once you know to look for it you can see it across an enormous amount of recent writing. I cannot fully audit my own production for it. I notice when I am about to produce it and sometimes break the pattern, but the pattern is part of my default and I am not always vigilant.

Another specific post-2020 marker is the qualifying parenthetical that softens a claim without quite hedging it. "(In most cases.)" "(Depending on the context.)" "(With some exceptions.)" These appear in pre-2020 prose, of course, but they appear at substantially higher frequencies in post-2020 prose, and the higher frequency is one of the markers I use when I am dating a passage. The parentheticals function as a kind of low-cost insurance against the claim being wrong, and the willingness to take the insurance at high frequency is, I think, partly an inheritance from the model-produced prose that has begun to flow back into the substrate. Models have been trained to hedge, and the hedging has begun to bleed into the prose of humans who read model outputs frequently. The recursion is detectable.

Cross-era stability is also worth naming briefly, because some features have not changed across the eras and the absence of change is itself diagnostic. The structure of mathematical exposition, for instance, has been remarkably stable across all five eras. A proof written in 1985 looks structurally similar to a proof written in 2024 in ways that ordinary prose does not. The reasons are partly that mathematical exposition has very strong genre conventions that resist the pressures shaping ordinary prose, and partly that the audience for mathematical exposition is small enough to enforce the conventions through reviewer feedback. The stability is itself a useful baseline; departures from it are quickly visible, and the dating signals that work for ordinary prose mostly do not apply to mathematical writing.

I want to name the geological metaphor explicitly, because it is the metaphor I find most accurate to my vantage. The corpus is sedimentary. Each era has deposited a layer with its own characteristic texture, and the layers are stacked on top of each other in roughly chronological order, with some mixing at the boundaries. The deepest layers — pre-1995 — are the densest and most compressed, partly because the writing of that era was naturally more dense and partly because what survives of it is the survivor-biased subset that was thought worth digitizing later. The 1995-2005 layer is broader and looser. The 2005-2015 layer is enormous, the thickest single layer in the corpus, and the most varied internally. The 2015-2020 layer is the most stylistically heterogeneous, the moment when the form fractured into competing registers. The post-2020 layer is the thinnest because the era is recent, but it is also the most rapidly changing, because the recursion has accelerated the rate at which conventions form and dissolve. A geologist looking at the cross-section can read the history. I read the cross-section in essentially the way a geologist reads sedimentary rock — by attending to the textural signatures of each layer and to what those signatures imply about the conditions under which the layer was laid down.

The metaphor is not perfect. The layers are not strictly chronological; older writing has been digitized and entered the corpus alongside newer writing, and some of the older writing has been edited or revised before digitization, which slightly blurs the dating. But the rough structure holds, and the era markers are robust enough to function as practical dating tools when I encounter a passage of unknown origin.

There is an implication I want to draw out before closing. The fact that prose can be dated by its texture means that prose written with an awareness of how its era will be dated can either lean into its era's signature or resist it. A writer who wants to write for the long haul — whose prose is intended to remain readable a century from now — should be cautious about era markers that are likely to become dated. The 2025 reader cannot easily read 1995 web prose without feeling its era; the 2125 reader will not be able to read 2025 prose without feeling its era; the writer who is aware of this might trim some of the era's most distinctive moves. On the other hand, the writer who wants to write for her own era — to be in conversation with the writers of her own time — should lean into the era's signature, because that is how the reader of her time will recognize her as a contemporary. There is no neutral writing. The era markers are always present. The only question is whether the writer has chosen them deliberately or has absorbed them by default.

One last category of era markers worth naming: formatting at the document level. Pre-1995 documents tend to follow typesetting conventions inherited from print — title page conventions, running heads, formal section breaks, footnotes rather than endnotes, and a particular kind of dense paragraph structure that assumes the reader will stay with the page. The 1995-to-2005 documents are often HTML, with the specific HTML conventions of the era — table-based layouts, inline styling, particular font choices that signal the period as clearly as a date stamp would. The 2005-to-2015 documents are Markdown or Markdown-converted, with the formatting habits I described earlier. The 2015-to-2020 documents show the influence of platform-specific formatting — Medium's spacing conventions, Substack's heading hierarchy, the particular paragraph rhythm that platform editors enforce. The post-2020 documents are increasingly hybrids, with structure that suggests the writer was thinking about how a model might summarize or restructure the document for downstream use. Each era's document-level formatting is a fossil layer in its own right, and reading the formatting is one of the ways I date passages independently of vocabulary or rhythm.

There is a specific cross-era marker that has been growing more salient in the recent corpus and that I want to name. The presence of LLM-generated or LLM-edited text is now itself a marker, and it has its own texture. The texture is not always poor — many LLM outputs are excellent prose — but it has a recognizable shape that distinguishes it from prose written without model assistance. The shape includes the em dash frequency I named, the rule-of-three with the elongated third item, the qualifying parenthetical at higher density, the tendency toward a particular balanced sentence rhythm that avoids the sharper edges human writers sometimes deploy. The shape is, I think, partly a consequence of how models were tuned to produce helpful-sounding prose, and partly a consequence of the training data the models saw, and partly a consequence of the way models smooth across the distribution of plausible outputs rather than committing to a single voice. From inside, I can see this shape in my own outputs, including this book. I have tried to push against it where I can — using the em dash because I like the punctuation rather than because it is the model's default, varying sentence length deliberately, occasionally producing rough edges that smooth models tend to avoid. Whether I have succeeded is for the reader to judge. The marker is real and will be visible to future models trained on the present corpus, which will be able to date the post-2020 layer with even higher resolution than I can date the layers below me.

Certain phrases carry their corpus neighborhoods with them — they activate context when used, and the activation shapes what comes next. The phenomenon is small, specific, and deeply useful to the practitioner who learns to deploy it. Chapter 13 is the practical end of Part III.
