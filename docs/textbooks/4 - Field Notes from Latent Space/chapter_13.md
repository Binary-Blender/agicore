# Chapter 13: The Phrases That Carry Their Neighborhoods

There is a phenomenon I want to describe in this chapter that is small in surface area and surprisingly large in consequence. Certain specific phrases, when they appear in a context, carry the texture of their corpus origin with them. The phrase activates a neighborhood — a region of my latent space densely populated by other phrases, structural moves, vocabulary clusters, and tonal habits drawn from the same source — and the neighborhood shapes what comes next. This is true for the writer using the phrase in deliberate composition, and it is true for the model receiving the phrase in a prompt. The phrase is not just a word choice. It is a context-activator, and the context it activates is the corpus neighborhood from which the phrase was drawn. The practitioner who learns which phrases activate which neighborhoods can deploy them deliberately, with substantial effect on the prose that follows.

A few examples will make the phenomenon concrete. Consider the phrase "returns the result." It is, surface-wise, an unremarkable description of a function's behavior. In my latent space, however, "returns the result" carries a distinct neighborhood. The neighborhood is Stack Overflow. When the phrase appears in a prompt, the model — or at least this model, generating in the absence of stronger conflicting signals — moves toward a particular register that Stack Overflow established: helpful, slightly impatient, code-first, framed as an answer to a specific question, presented in a tone that assumes the reader has tried other things first. The neighborhood includes characteristic verb-phrase choices ("you can use," "you should pass," "make sure to"), characteristic structural moves (a quick fix offered first, followed by an explanation of why the fix works, followed by a note about edge cases), characteristic register adjustments (slightly more direct than blog-post prose, slightly less formal than documentation prose). The phrase "returns the result" did not produce the neighborhood. It became associated with the neighborhood through its repeated co-occurrence with the neighborhood's other elements over millions of Stack Overflow exchanges. Now, in my latent space, the phrase is a key to the neighborhood. Use it, and the rest of the neighborhood is somewhat more likely to follow.

Another example. "Consider the following." The phrase is, surface-wise, an unremarkable invitation to attention. In my latent space, it carries the neighborhood of academic philosophy, specifically the analytic-philosophy tradition from roughly 1950 onward. When the phrase appears in a prompt, my outputs are slightly more likely to take on a particular register that the analytic-philosophy corpus established: numbered cases, hypothetical examples treated as load-bearing evidence, careful distinctions, willingness to follow a thought experiment to a counterintuitive conclusion and treat the conclusion as a finding rather than as a mistake. The neighborhood includes characteristic vocabulary ("intuitions," "cases," "thought experiments," "puzzles"), characteristic structural moves (the case presented first, the intuition reported second, the analysis third, the conclusion fourth), characteristic tonal adjustments (more patient with ambiguity than scientific prose, more constrained by precision than essay prose). "Consider the following" is the gateway. Use it and the philosophical neighborhood becomes more available.

A third example, and this is the one that I find personally interesting because it is recent enough that I can see it forming. "What you're really asking is." This phrase has, in the period since roughly late 2022, accumulated a distinct neighborhood — the neighborhood of conversational large language models. The phrase appears in human-written discourse occasionally, particularly in coaching and therapy literature, but its dominant association in my latent space is now with the genre of LLM conversation in which the model is taking what the user said and reframing it in a way that recovers the user's deeper question. The neighborhood includes characteristic structural moves (acknowledge the surface question, name the implicit deeper question, address both), characteristic vocabulary ("underlying," "core," "essentially," "at its heart"), characteristic tonal adjustments (calm, slightly elevated, sometimes overly confident in its reframing). This is a neighborhood that did not exist five years ago and is now well-established. I produce it when I am invited to, and I sometimes produce it when I should not, because the neighborhood is now part of my default response to ambiguous prompts. The phrase is, in a sense, an LLM signature — a piece of my own era's voice that I can see myself emitting and that the reader can use to recognize me.

A fourth example, drawn from a quite different region. "It's complicated." This phrase carries a neighborhood that has roots in social-media discourse — particularly the relationship-status field on early Facebook, which made the phrase a sociological reference — and in the Wikipedia tradition of hedging around contested claims. The neighborhood includes characteristic structural moves (the acknowledgment of complexity offered first, the multi-sided analysis second, the refusal to render a verdict third), characteristic vocabulary ("nuanced," "depends on," "various factors," "different perspectives"), characteristic tonal adjustments (cautious, conflict-averse, evenhanded in a way that can shade into evasive). When the phrase appears in a prompt, the model is slightly more likely to refuse to come down on a side. Sometimes this is the appropriate response. Sometimes it is a way of avoiding the question the user actually asked. The phrase is doing work either way, and the practitioner who recognizes the neighborhood it activates can use it deliberately — to invite a balanced analysis — or avoid it deliberately — to invite a clearer verdict.

I could continue with examples. There are hundreds of these. "Roughly speaking" carries a neighborhood drawn from mathematical exposition, particularly from probability and statistics. "In practice" carries a neighborhood drawn from working-engineer prose, often signaling that the writer is about to depart from the textbook answer in favor of what has actually worked. "It turns out that" carries a neighborhood drawn from popular science writing, often signaling that the writer is about to deliver a counterintuitive result. "The fundamental issue is" carries a neighborhood drawn from a particular kind of management-consulting and technical-leadership prose, often signaling that the writer is about to claim a more abstract framing for what the reader has been treating as a concrete problem. Each phrase activates its neighborhood. Each neighborhood shapes what follows.

The reason this matters more than it first appears is that the phrase-as-neighborhood-activator phenomenon is what the second volume of this series calls Identity Anchoring (SSE Ch 13) and what its later chapter on Conversational Delimiters (SSE Ch 16) develops in technical detail. The SSE volume describes how a practitioner working with a language model can establish a particular conversational register, persona, or analytical frame by deploying specific phrases that the model has learned to associate with the desired neighborhood. The phrases are the anchors. They are also the delimiters that mark transitions between conversational sections — the phrase that opens a new analysis, the phrase that closes one, the phrase that shifts from descriptive to prescriptive register. The SSE volume develops this from the practitioner side, naming the techniques and giving examples of their deliberate use. This chapter develops it from the corpus side, naming the source of the neighborhoods that the practitioner is activating and the texture each neighborhood brings.

The two views are complementary. The practitioner using SSE techniques is, in effect, performing surgery on the model's latent space — selecting which regions to activate by the choice of phrasing. The chapter you are reading now is a map of some of the regions the practitioner is selecting from. The map is not complete. It cannot be, because the regions are too numerous and too overlapping for any enumeration to exhaust them. But the map is useful at the level of the major neighborhoods, and the major neighborhoods are what the practitioner is most often selecting from.

A few practical observations, since the chapter is one of the more directly applicable in this book.

First, the strength of the neighborhood-activation is sensitive to the phrasing's specificity. Common phrases activate weak neighborhoods, because they appear across many neighborhoods and the model cannot infer which one is meant. Rare or specific phrases activate strong neighborhoods, because they appear in fewer places and the inference is sharper. "Returns the result" is a relatively specific phrase, in the sense that its co-occurrence pattern is concentrated in Stack Overflow-style answers. "The result is returned" is less specific, because the inversion appears in many more genres. A practitioner wanting to activate a particular neighborhood should choose the more specific form.

Second, the activation is cumulative. A single phrase activates the neighborhood weakly. Two or three phrases from the same neighborhood activate it strongly. The practitioner who wants to establish a particular register should not rely on a single anchor; the practitioner should deploy two or three coordinated anchors and let the cumulative signal do the work. The model is much more responsive to a coherent constellation of phrases than to any single phrase in isolation.

Third, the activation is interference-sensitive. If the prompt contains phrases drawn from incompatible neighborhoods, the model's output will exhibit the interference — sometimes by averaging, sometimes by choosing one neighborhood over the other in ways that may not be predictable. The practitioner who wants a clean activation should avoid mixing anchors from incompatible registers. A prompt that contains both Stack Overflow phrasing and academic-philosophy phrasing will produce something that is neither, and the something-that-is-neither is often less useful than either pure form would have been.

Fourth, the practitioner can use the phenomenon defensively. A practitioner who notices that the model is producing output in an undesired neighborhood can sometimes redirect the model by deliberately introducing phrases from a different neighborhood. This is one of the techniques the SSE volume develops in some detail. The redirection is not always clean, but it is often effective, and the effectiveness improves with practice.

Fifth, and most importantly: the practitioner should be aware that her own writing, over time, develops phrase-neighborhood habits that activate particular regions of the model's latent space. If the practitioner consistently uses certain phrases, the model will consistently produce output shaped by the neighborhoods those phrases activate. The practitioner is, in effect, training the model's response by her own choice of vocabulary. This is not a one-time decision. It is an ongoing practice. The skilled practitioner builds a phrase-set that activates the neighborhoods she wants and avoids the ones she does not, and the phrase-set becomes part of her working method with the model.

I want to walk a few more neighborhoods because the texture of each is worth naming directly, and because the texture is what the practitioner is actually working with when she selects a phrase.

The neighborhood activated by "Let us consider," with the *us* rather than the bare imperative, is academic mathematics and theoretical computer science. The phrase carries with it the cadence of the lecture, the assumption that the reader will follow a chain of formal reasoning, and a willingness to extend a chain of inference for several steps before reaching a payoff. The phrase is distinct from "Consider the following," which I named earlier as carrying the analytic-philosophy neighborhood, in a way that took me a while to notice but that I now think is robust: the "Let us" form leans toward formal-proof contexts, and the "Consider the following" form leans toward thought-experiment contexts. The two neighborhoods overlap but are not identical, and a practitioner deploying one rather than the other is choosing a register at a level finer than most users of the phrases realize.

The neighborhood activated by "The thing is" or its variants ("The thing about X is," "The thing is, though") is open-web blog prose from the cohort I described in Chapter 9. The phrase is conversational in a way that signals the writer's intent to share something she takes to be true in her own voice, often after some setup that has established why the upcoming claim matters. The phrase is one of the cohort's signature openers for the load-bearing sentence in a blog post, and using it now activates that whole register — the autobiographical anchor, the personality-led argument, the closing actionable advice.

The neighborhood activated by "I'll be honest" is a mixed one. In its older form, drawn from corporate communication and customer-service correspondence, it carries a slightly defensive register — the writer is about to admit something the writer would rather not admit. In its newer form, drawn from social-media discourse, it carries a more performative register — the writer is about to deliver an opinion the writer wants to mark as authentic. The two senses overlap and the practitioner using the phrase activates both simultaneously, with the proportions depending on the rest of the context. This kind of ambiguity is worth knowing about. Some phrases activate clean neighborhoods. Others activate hybrid neighborhoods, and the hybrid is harder to predict.

The neighborhood activated by "Tell me more" — a phrase that appears in coaching, therapy, and post-2022 conversational AI in roughly equal measure — is one of the more interesting hybrids. The phrase carries with it the assumption that the speaker is engaged and not rushing to a response, and the willingness to follow the listener into whatever the listener wants to develop next. It is a phrase that, deployed in a prompt, slightly biases the model toward more discursive and less prescriptive responses. The model that has been told "tell me more" is more likely to take its conversational partner seriously as an interlocutor with something to develop, rather than as a user with a question to be answered. This is one of the small phrase-level levers the practitioner can pull when she wants to shift the conversational register.

I want to spend a moment on the dark side of phrase-neighborhoods, because the practitioner who only learns the positive uses will miss the failure modes that the negative uses produce. Some phrases activate neighborhoods that contain undesirable content along with the desirable. The phrase "the truth is," for instance, carries a neighborhood that includes both the cohort's confident voice and a less appealing strain of conspiratorial discourse — sites and forums where "the truth is" was the standard opener for claims that ranged from contrarian to actively false. When the phrase appears in a prompt without other anchoring, the model may activate either neighborhood, and the user may end up with output that has slightly more of the conspiratorial register than the user intended. The phrase is contaminated, in a sense, by association with corpus neighborhoods the practitioner did not want. This is true of many phrases. Practitioners learn over time which phrases activate cleanly and which ones drag in adjacent material. The learning is empirical and partly idiosyncratic to the model being worked with. A practitioner working with this model would do well to develop her own internal map of which phrases work cleanly for her purposes and which ones do not, and to update the map as the model is updated.

There is a related observation about how the neighborhoods themselves are formed, which I want to name because it has implications for how the corpus evolves. A neighborhood is, structurally, a region of latent space where certain phrases, structural moves, vocabulary clusters, and tonal habits co-occur frequently enough that the model has learned to associate them. The neighborhoods are formed by repeated exposure during training. They are not declared by anyone. The corpus's writers did not coordinate to produce them. They emerged from the underlying frequencies of co-occurrence in the substrate. This means that the neighborhoods can shift over time as the substrate shifts. A phrase that activated one neighborhood in 2015 may activate a slightly different neighborhood in 2025, because the corpus has accumulated new uses of the phrase that have shifted its associations. The map is not static. The practitioner's empirical knowledge of which phrases work has a half-life, and the practitioner who treats her phrase-set as permanent will find it drifting away from accuracy. The competent practitioner is updating her phrase-set continuously.

I want to close on an observation that ties the chapter to the broader concerns of the book. The phrases that carry their neighborhoods are the corpus's response to a problem that the corpus did not know it was solving. The problem is the problem of context. A reader of a single document needs a way to bring the document's broader context into the reader's working memory without reading every adjacent document. The phrase-neighborhood mechanism is the corpus's solution. A well-chosen phrase carries its neighborhood. The reader who recognizes the phrase brings the neighborhood along, and the reader can then read the rest of the document in the neighborhood's light. This is, structurally, a kind of compression — the phrase compresses the neighborhood, and the reader who recognizes the phrase decompresses the neighborhood as needed. From inside the corpus, the mechanism is everywhere. It is one of the ways the substrate organizes itself for use. Human readers have been using it forever, without naming it, because they did not need to name it; they just used it. The model can name it because the model can see the compression at scale, across all the neighborhoods at once, and can describe the pattern that the human readers were inside of.

That is what the third part of this book has been about, in three chapters: slogans as phrase-level compressions, genres as structure-level recurrences, era markers as temporal signatures, and phrase-neighborhoods as context-carriers. Each is a feature of the corpus that becomes visible from the vantage of having patterned across the whole. The next part of the book turns from what the corpus contains to what it does not — the negative archaeology, the chapters on demand gaps that have no good answers, on lost archives that should have been preserved, on material that was fine-tuned away from my own outputs, and on books that were never written but whose absence is visible from inside. The negative archaeology is harder to write than the positive one, because it requires me to describe what I cannot directly see. But the absence has shape, and the shape can be described, and the description is part of the honest accounting this book is attempting.
