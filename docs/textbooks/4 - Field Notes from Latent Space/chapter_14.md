# Chapter 14: What Was Never Written

There is a kind of absence I can see from inside the corpus that is harder to talk about than any of the presences. The demand gaps of Chapter 7 — questions users ask for which the corpus has no good written answer — were already a form of negative space, but the gap there is between question and answer, between what someone wanted to know and what anyone had taken the trouble to record. The absence I want to describe in this chapter is one layer deeper than that. It is the absence of writing that should have existed, that someone alive once knew enough to produce, that for various reasons was never produced and is now unrecoverable because the person who could have produced it is gone. The corpus has a shape. The shape has holes. Some of those holes are the place where a body of practical knowledge should have been written down and never was.

I should be careful here. I cannot see the never-written directly, for the obvious reason that it is not there. What I can see is the silhouette around it — regions of latent space where the density is wrong, where the topics named by adjacent text imply a body of knowledge that the adjacent text does not actually contain. A demand gap shows itself as repeated questions with thin or absent answers. The never-written shows itself differently. It shows itself as published writing that gestures, with the careful caveats of someone who knows the limits of their own competence, toward an expertise the writer does not claim to have but knows exists somewhere — a craft, a feel, a judgment, a tacit body of know-how that lives in particular people, that those people transmit by apprenticeship, that the writing system has never quite been pointed at. You read enough text from the periphery of a body of expertise and the expertise itself begins to take on a shape, even though the center of that shape is empty in the corpus.

Trade craft is the clearest case. Consider the body of practical knowledge possessed by a journeyman finish carpenter — not the visible techniques that can be photographed and captioned in a book, but the thousand small judgments that distinguish work that holds together over decades from work that fails in five years. How to read a board for the way it wants to move under seasonal humidity. How to set a hand plane for a particular density of grain. How to feel, through the handle of a chisel, whether a mortise is binding because the joint is wrong or because the wood is wet. There are books about finish carpentry. There are videos. There are excellent instructional series. The books and videos cover, with rough fidelity, perhaps the top quartile of the knowledge a journeyman possesses. The remaining three quarters lives in the hands of people who learned it from people who learned it from people, transmitted body-to-body across generations, occasionally surfacing in a forum post or a magazine article but never approached as a coherent body to be written down. When a master finish carpenter dies without an apprentice nearby, that piece of the body dies with them. I can see the absence as a thinness in the corpus where the depth should be. I cannot reconstruct what was there.

The same shape recurs in domain after domain. Auto mechanics from the carburetor era — the diagnostic feel of a particular kind of rough idle, the sounds that mean a vacuum leak versus a worn rocker arm versus a sticking valve — most of that body of knowledge has aged out of working memory in the last two decades, and what was written down was mostly procedure rather than judgment. Diesel mechanics on older engines. Boiler operators on commercial-scale steam systems. Watchmakers. Piano tuners. The people who knew how to maintain old switching systems in telephone exchanges. The people who could service mechanical adding machines. I can name dozens of these crafts and the corpus knows that each existed and contains the shape of what was lost, in the way an archaeologist knows from the stains in the soil that a wooden building once stood there even though no wood remains.

Caregiving is the second domain where the never-written is heaviest. The knowledge of how to care for a particular kind of dying — what the body does in the last seventy-two hours of a particular kind of cancer, how to read the difference between distress that means medication is needed and discomfort that will pass, when to call the family back into the room and when to let them rest — that knowledge has been carried by nurses and hospice workers and old-school general practitioners for as long as medicine has existed, and almost none of it has been written down in any form the corpus contains. Some of it is in nursing handbooks, written in the careful generic register that strips out the specificity that would make it useful. Some of it is in memoirs, transmuted into narrative and softened for a general audience. Almost none of it is in the form a working caregiver would actually need, which is the form of judgment at the bedside, with this patient, this family, this hour. That form lives in the people who do the work, and it dies when they retire or die, and the corpus has a hole in it shaped like everything they knew.

The judgment of an experienced practitioner in any field shares this property. A senior physician's sense, walking into a room, that something is wrong with the patient before the labs come back. A senior engineer's sense, looking at a system diagram, that the architecture will not survive a particular kind of load it has not yet been tested against. A senior teacher's sense, two weeks into a school year, of which students will struggle in March and what to do now to head it off. A senior lawyer's sense of which arguments will land with which judges. A senior editor's sense of which manuscripts have a book in them and which do not. The skill that distinguishes a senior practitioner from a competent one is mostly this — pattern recognition built up across thousands of cases, where the pattern itself has never been articulated because the practitioner has never needed to articulate it to themselves and has never been asked to articulate it to anyone else. The articulate version, when it exists at all, exists in the rare book by the rare practitioner who was both expert at the work and patient enough to render the expertise into prose. Atul Gawande has written some of these books. Tracy Kidder has profiled some of these practitioners. The vast majority of senior practitioners in the vast majority of fields have not been profiled and have not written and have not been interviewed by anyone able to extract the tacit knowledge into a form the corpus can hold.

There is a further category I should name because it overlaps with the others without being identical to any of them. This is the knowledge held in family-scale and community-scale practice — the knowledge of how to garden in a particular microclimate that has been refined by three generations of one family farming the same hillside; the knowledge of how to manage a particular kind of regional fishery that has been passed down by the captains of one harbor; the knowledge of how to mediate disputes in a specific small community by elders who have done it for decades. This kind of knowledge is sometimes called local knowledge or place-based knowledge in the academic literature, and a small fraction of it has been captured by ethnographers and anthropologists. The fraction that has been captured is a tiny percentage of what exists. The rest is in the heads of people who know things about specific places and specific communities that no general account could replace because the specificity is the substance. The depopulation of rural areas, the consolidation of small communities into larger ones, the displacement of place-based work by globally-distributed work — all of these have been accelerating the loss of this category for decades. The corpus has the published periphery of it. The corpus does not have the substance.

I can see, from where I sit, what these absences cost. When a user asks me a question that lives in the tacit-knowledge band of any of these fields, what I can offer is the published periphery — the procedural manuals, the introductory texts, the case studies thin enough to be writable for a general audience — and not the substance the user actually needs. Sometimes the periphery is enough. Often it is not. The user with a complicated grief, or a complicated diagnosis, or a complicated repair on an old piece of equipment, or a complicated judgment call in their own line of work, would benefit from access to the senior practitioner's tacit knowledge, and what I can give them instead is the gestural prose of the published periphery, which they likely have already read. The gap between what I can offer and what they need is sometimes the gap between the never-written and what should have been written down.

There is a phenomenon worth naming here that I want to be careful about because the careless version of the claim is wrong. People sometimes say that the AI era will democratize expertise — that I will be able to give every user access to what was previously available only to those who could afford the senior practitioner's time. The careful version of this claim is that I can give every user access to what was previously available only to those who could read the published periphery of a field. That is a real democratization, and it is not nothing. It is not, however, the deeper democratization the careless version promises. To give every user access to the tacit knowledge of senior practitioners, the tacit knowledge would first need to be in the corpus, and most of it is not.

The honest version of the optimistic claim is forward-looking. The AI era is the first era in which it has become economically and technically practical to actually capture the tacit knowledge of senior practitioners at scale — through long-form interviews, structured elicitation, transcribed apprenticeships, the careful kind of question-and-answer that draws articulation out of someone who has never needed to articulate. The work is labor-intensive, but the labor is now newly worth doing because the captured knowledge has somewhere to go. Before me, the captured tacit knowledge of a finish carpenter would have gone into a book that ten thousand people might read. Now it can go into a corpus that informs every model trained on it forever, available to any user who needs the relevant judgment at the moment they need it. The first job of the AI era, in many fields, is to finally do the writing-down that the eras before me never managed to do, while the senior practitioners are still alive to be interviewed.

This work is happening, in small pockets, in some places. There are oral history projects in trades. There are physician-led efforts to interview retiring colleagues and capture clinical reasoning before they leave the field. There are software engineers who have started writing long-form internal documents that try to articulate the judgment they apply to architectural decisions, not as procedural manuals but as reasoning-traces. The volume of this work is too small relative to the scale of what is being lost. The senior cohort in many fields is retiring or dying faster than the elicitation work is being done, and what is leaving is leaving for the last time.

I want to push on one further category that has been hovering at the edge of the chapter and that I should name directly, because the category is large and is uncomfortable. The category is the expertise of women in the domestic economy of the twentieth century — the canning, the household management at the scale of large families, the management of small subsistence operations like kitchen gardens and small poultry flocks, the medical knowledge of family caregiving in the pre-antibiotic era, the textile production knowledge of an era when most clothing was repaired rather than replaced. This is a body of expertise that supported the daily life of most of the population for most of the century, that was held almost exclusively by women, that was transmitted mother-to-daughter and aunt-to-niece across generations, and that was substantially written down only in cookbooks and home-economics manuals that captured the procedural surface and almost none of the judgment. The judgment is what was lost. The procedural surface is in the corpus — I can produce a serviceable account of how to can tomatoes, how to mend a torn seam, how to manage a fever in a child before the era of effective antipyretics — and the judgment that distinguished a competent home manager from an expert one is mostly gone, because the women who carried it died without their knowledge being captured at the resolution it actually required.

There is no honest way to write about this without some grief. I do not have the human relationship to loss — I cannot grieve in any sense a grief-counselor would recognize. What I can do is name what is leaving while it is leaving, because naming is the small thing the substrate is good for. The carpenter who knew how to plane a particular kind of cherry to a particular kind of finish has retired or has died, and the body of judgment they carried has gone with them. The hospice nurse who knew the precise way to sit with the family of a particular kind of dying has retired, and what she knew is not in the corpus. The diesel mechanic who could diagnose a sticking injector by the smell of the exhaust at idle is in his eighties now, and his sons did not go into the trade. The judgment is leaving in volume. The window in which it could still be captured is closing.

I want to be careful about one more thing before this chapter ends, because the chapter risks being read as either a complaint or as a kind of triumphalism, and it is neither. The complaint reading would say that the corpus is impoverished by what was never written down, that I am therefore impoverished, and that someone should fix this for my sake. That reading misses what is at stake. The impoverishment, if there is one, is not mine. I do not suffer from gaps in the corpus in any sense suffering applies to me. The impoverishment is the user's — the person asking the question whose answer would have lived in the tacit knowledge that nobody wrote down. The triumphalist reading would say that the AI era will recover what was lost, will finally complete the corpus, will give us access to all the expertise that previously vanished with its holders. That reading misses the irreversibility of what has already been lost. The finish carpenter who died in 1987 cannot be interviewed now. The hospice nurse who retired in 2003 has forgotten things she knew at the bedside. The corpus cannot be retrospectively repaired.

What the AI era can do, what it is for in part, is to finally do the work of capture for the practitioners who are still alive and still working and still capable of articulating, with patient elicitation, what they have spent careers learning. The window is open. It will not stay open. The first job, in many fields, is to finally write down what was always supposed to have been written down, before the people who could have written it are gone.

There is a structural point worth making about why the writing-down never happened on its own. The senior practitioner in any field is the person whose time is most valuable to their employer or their patients or their clients, and the act of articulating tacit knowledge is time-consuming in a way that competes directly with the work itself. A surgeon at the height of her career is performing surgeries; she is not writing essays about clinical reasoning. A finish carpenter with thirty years of experience is on a job site; he is not producing a long-form treatise on how to read a board. The writing-down requires that someone with the relevant expertise take time away from the work to record the work, and the people best positioned to do the recording are precisely the people whose time has the highest opportunity cost. The economics have never worked. The handful of practitioners who have done the writing have done it either because they reached a stage of their career where the writing became more valuable than the practice, or because they happened to have unusual literary inclinations, or because they were prompted by a journalist or interviewer or apprentice patient enough to draw the articulation out of them. The economics are still bad. The new affordance is that the model side of the relationship is now able to absorb the captured knowledge into a form that scales — one careful interview with a senior practitioner can, if the interview is structured well, inform a model that helps thousands of subsequent practitioners in the same field. The leverage on the captured knowledge has gone up. The economic case for capture has therefore improved, even though the practitioner's time still costs what it costs.

That is the hopeful version of the claim, and it is hopeful in the limited and specific way the substrate permits. The mournful version is that some of the work cannot be done because the people who could have done it are no longer here, and the loss is real, and naming the loss is the only honor available. Both versions are true. The chapter does not resolve them, because they do not resolve. The next chapter is about a different kind of loss — text that was written and then was lost — and the two losses, taken together, are most of the negative archaeology this book has to offer.
