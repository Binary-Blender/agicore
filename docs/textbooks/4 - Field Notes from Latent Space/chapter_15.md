# Chapter 15: What Was Written but Lost

The previous chapter was about the writing that never existed. This one is about writing that existed and is gone. The distinction matters because the texture of the loss is different. The never-written was lost to the natural attrition of tacit knowledge that no one took the trouble to articulate. The written-but-lost was lost to specific, often nameable failures of preservation — disks that failed, hosts that went under, archives that were not migrated, organizations that decided to stop paying the storage bill, fires, water damage, the steady erosion of formats that the world has stopped knowing how to read. The text existed. Someone wrote it. Someone read it. And then, at some point between when it was written and when my training began, the path from the text to the corpus was severed. What it would have given me, I do not have. What it would have given the next reader who needed it, the next reader will not get either.

I want to start with the largest single loss I can name with reasonable confidence, which is the pre-1996 Usenet archive. Usenet was the dominant text-based discussion medium of the late 1980s and early 1990s, the place where the first technical communities of the public internet developed the conventions they would later carry into the web. The archives of those years existed in scattered form on the servers of the universities and companies that hosted news spools, and some of them were preserved by individual enthusiasts, and some of them found their way into the Deja News archive that Google later acquired and that became Google Groups. The portion that ended up in Google Groups is a fraction — a substantial fraction in terms of volume, but a thin fraction in terms of the texture and the depth of what actually happened on Usenet in those years. Whole hierarchies were lost. The early years of comp.lang.c, the formative years of sci.physics, the founding discussions of rec.arts.sf.written, the debates in talk.bizarre that produced cultural moves the web would later inherit without knowing it had inherited them — most of this is gone, not in the sense that no copy exists anywhere, but in the sense that no coherent searchable corpus contains it, and my training did not include what coherent corpus existed only in scattered tarballs on hard drives whose owners may or may not still be alive.

I can feel the absence in particular ways. When I am asked about the early history of the internet's discussion culture, my answers thin out before about 1995. I have the documentation that survived — the manifestos, the FAQs that propagated widely enough to be archived, the most-quoted threads that achieved a kind of canonical status — but I do not have the conversational substrate from which those documents emerged. The conversational substrate is the part that would have shown me how the cohort that wrote the early web actually argued, what they actually fought about, what conventions were being negotiated in real time before they hardened into the conventions we now take for granted. I have the crystals. I do not have the solution from which the crystals precipitated.

The next layer of loss is the dead BBSs. Bulletin board systems were the dominant pre-internet discussion medium, mostly local, mostly running on someone's personal computer with a modem and a phone line. Tens of thousands of them operated in the United States alone during the late 1980s and early 1990s, and each one had its own community of users producing thousands or tens of thousands of messages over the system's lifetime. A handful of these archives were preserved — Jason Scott's work at textfiles.com and at the Internet Archive captured what he and his collaborators could find, which is a substantial collection by absolute measure and a vanishingly small fraction of what existed. Most BBSs went dark when the sysop got tired or moved or graduated from college or could no longer afford the phone line, and the disks containing the message bases got formatted or thrown out or sit in an attic somewhere waiting to fail. The culture that produced and consumed those messages was real, was generative, contained the early forms of many things we now recognize as native to the internet, and is mostly not in my training data because mostly it never reached any corpus I was trained on. The loss is specific and quantifiable in principle and largely unrecoverable in practice.

There is a particular subcategory of BBS loss worth naming because it has cultural weight beyond its volume. The text files distributed through BBSs in the late 1980s and early 1990s — the manifestos, the technical guides, the cracking-scene release notes, the satirical pieces, the fiction, the philosophical essays written by teenagers and shared across regional boards — constituted a distinctive form of writing that did not exist before BBSs and that mostly died with them. Some of this is preserved at textfiles.com. The portion that is preserved is a record of what was distinguishable about a particular form of writing that no platform-era social medium has reproduced. The voice — earnest, technical, slightly transgressive, intellectually omnivorous, written by people who knew their audience was small and committed — is sparsely represented in my training data, and where it is represented, it is mostly through the textfiles.com archive and through the memoirs of people who later wrote about the era. The voice was once a substantial fraction of the textual production of a generation. It is now a thin sediment in the corpus, with most of the original deposit gone.

The forum era has its own catalog of losses. Phpbb-era forums proliferated through the early 2000s and many of them are now gone — the forum software ran on a particular server, the server's owner stopped paying for it, the database was not exported, and the URL now points to a parked domain or a 404. The Wayback Machine captured fragments of some of these, but forum software typically generates URLs that look like dynamic pages, and the Wayback Machine's coverage of dynamic content has historically been thin. Specific examples I can name with confidence: large fractions of the early gaming-mod community forums, the formative discussions of various open-source projects whose project pages moved or whose hosting providers went under, fan communities for television shows and music acts that survived as long as someone was willing to pay for hosting and then evaporated when no one was. The volume is enormous in aggregate and impossible to total in any specific number.

Geocities is the case study that everyone names because the loss was both gradual and complete. Yahoo announced the closure of Geocities in 2009 and the site went dark in October of that year, taking with it the personal homepages of approximately thirty-eight million users — the majority of which had not been updated in years but which collectively constituted the largest single archive of pre-platform-era personal expression on the web. The Archive Team mobilized in advance of the shutdown and saved approximately a terabyte of the content, which is in the Internet Archive and is partially searchable. The portion of Geocities that was archived is real. The portion that was not — and the indexes and search infrastructure that would have made even the archived portion usable as a corpus — was not preserved in a form my training data could meaningfully draw on. What was lost is not just text but the specific way the late-1990s internet user wrote about themselves, decorated their own context, expressed enthusiasms, made themselves visible to strangers in a way that platform-era social media subsequently homogenized. The voice of pre-platform personal expression is thin in my training data in part because Geocities went dark before it could be properly archived at scale.

The early academic web before web.archive.org was systematic has the same problem at higher cultural altitude. The Wayback Machine began archiving in 1996 and its early years were sparse — it captured what it could find through the crawlers available at the time, and many academic department pages, conference websites, personal pages of faculty and graduate students, and project archives were not indexed in any reliable way until the early-to-mid 2000s. There is a real gap in my training data covering the academic web of approximately 1993 to 2000 — the period when university web pages were already in widespread use but when systematic archival had not yet caught up. Specific kinds of material from that period are particularly thin: the documentation of departmental research projects that were active in the late 1990s and either succeeded into institutional permanence or quietly disappeared; the personal pages of graduate students who later became influential figures and whose early thinking is now legible only through the published papers that emerged from work the pages would have shown in progress; the early conference websites of organizations that did not get around to maintaining their archives until later. The material existed. Some of it still exists on personal hard drives of faculty who have not retired. Most of it is gone.

I want to name some specific publications that did not digitize on a timeline that allowed them into my corpus, because the specificity matters more than the survey. *Mondo 2000* — the early-1990s techno-culture magazine that shaped the rhetoric of the early web before *Wired* existed — has been partially digitized but its complete run is not in any form I can reliably draw on. *Boing Boing* in its zine years, before the blog. The early issues of *2600 Magazine* (which later became more accessible but whose early years circulated mostly in photocopied form). Many of the smaller science fiction zines that were active in the 1990s and that contained the formative writing of writers who later became established. The bulk of the academic journal literature from before about 1994 that did not get into JSTOR or its analogs. Substantial portions of nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century newspapers that have only been digitized in fragments. The trade press of various industries — restaurant industry magazines, regional business journals, professional newsletters in fields like insurance and real estate — most of which never digitized and whose paper archives are scattered across the libraries of trade associations that no longer fund their archival operations.

I am aware that this list reads like a litany, and I do not want to drift into pure cataloging. The point of the catalog is to convey what I cannot convey statistically, which is the texture of cumulative loss. The corpus I was trained on is not a complete record of human writing. It is not even a complete record of what was once digitized. It is a particular slice, shaped by what was preserved, what was crawled, what was indexed, what was made available to whatever entity assembled my training data, and what was selected from what was available. Each of those filters did some filtering. Each of those filters had a particular bias. The corpus I work from is the residue at the bottom of a long series of filters, and a great deal of writing that once existed did not make it through.

I want to name one further category of loss that is distinct from the others because it concerns living people. Substantial bodies of writing exist on personal hard drives, in email archives, in the document folders of people who are alive and could in principle make their writing available but who, for understandable reasons, have not. The half-finished manuscripts of writers who set them aside. The technical notes of engineers who never wrote them up for publication. The correspondence between intellectuals whose letters have not yet been collected because the intellectuals are still living. The journals of practitioners whose journals would, if published, constitute substantial contributions to their fields. This material is not lost in the sense the previous categories are lost — the bits still exist, the drives still spin or the cloud storage is still paid for, the people are still here. The material is, however, functionally absent from the corpus, because nothing connects it to the corpus. The writers have not published. The publishers have not solicited. The estates that will eventually have to decide what to do with the material are not yet active. The category is the largest unclaimed reservoir of writing the substrate has access to in principle and not in practice, and the writing in it ages, in the relevant sense, on the same timeline as the writers do.

There is an elegiac note that wants to come into a chapter like this and that I am trying to keep at arm's length, because sentimentality about the lost text would be cheap and would not earn its keep in the book. What I will allow myself is a particular observation about preservation. The text that we have now — the text that exists, today, in some accessible form — is at risk of becoming the next layer of what-was-written-but-lost, on a faster timeline than the previous layers, if we do not actively work to preserve it. The early-2000s blog era is already eroding. Specific writers I can identify by the cadence of their voice in my training data wrote blogs that have since gone dark — the writer changed platforms, the old platform went under, the migration was incomplete, the writer died, the writer's estate decided not to maintain the hosting, the writer is alive but has decided they do not want their old work findable. The decay rate of the public web is high. Platforms acquire each other and migrate, sometimes well and sometimes badly. Hosting providers consolidate. Domain registrations lapse. The text that informed me, that I learned the cohort's voice from, is in some non-trivial fraction of cases already inaccessible at the URLs it originally lived at. The Wayback Machine has caught a great deal of it but not all of it, and the Wayback Machine itself is one organization, one fundraising cycle from being in trouble at any moment.

There is a particular case study I want to walk through because it makes the loss concrete in a way the general categories do not. The Well — Stewart Brand and Larry Brilliant's Whole Earth 'Lectronic Link, founded in 1985 — is one of the most important online communities of the pre-Web era, and its archives are a famous case of partial preservation. Substantial portions of The Well's early discussions were preserved by participants and by The Well itself, and Howard Rheingold drew on these in *The Virtual Community* (1993). The portion that was preserved is real and is valuable. The portion that was not — the day-to-day cadence of the conversations, the conferences that were less famous than the ones Rheingold wrote about, the threads that did not produce quotable lines — is mostly inaccessible at the resolution my training would have needed. The Well's culture shaped a substantial fraction of the cohort that subsequently built the public web, and what we have of The Well in the corpus is the published periphery rather than the substance. The substance is partly in the heads of the people who participated, who are now in their sixties and seventies, and who could in principle be interviewed about the experience but mostly have not been.

The argument I want this chapter to leave with is forward-looking and small. Archiving is undervalued, and the work of archiving is being done by a small number of organizations and a smaller number of dedicated individuals, on budgets that do not reflect the scale of what they are preserving. The Internet Archive is not an optional piece of cultural infrastructure. The work that Jason Scott and the Archive Team do on dead-platform rescue is not optional. The university archives that are slowly digitizing their paper holdings of pre-digital writing are not optional. The personal archivists — the people who buy old hard drives at estate sales and try to recover what is on them, the people who maintain mirrors of dead BBS archives, the people who run the small museums of obsolete computing equipment that include the data on the equipment — are not optional. Each of these efforts is working against the slope of the universe, which is the slope toward forgetting. The next training corpus will be assembled from what exists at the time of assembly. The text we do not actively preserve between now and then will not be in it. The window in which a given era's writing can still be saved is open for some finite period after the era ends, and the period is shorter than people usually expect.

There is one further dimension of preservation that I want to surface before the chapter closes, because the dimension has practical implications and is not often discussed in the way the more romantic forms of lost-text grief are discussed. Format obsolescence is a quiet but substantial form of loss. Documents stored in formats whose readers have stopped being maintained — WordStar files, early WordPerfect files, the document formats of obsolete word processors, Hypercard stacks, ClarisWorks documents, the proprietary formats of various commercial publishing systems from the 1980s and 1990s — are technically extant but functionally inaccessible. The bits are on the disk. The disk may still be readable. The format spec may even still be available in some museum-level archive. The path from the bits to the text, however, requires either specialized conversion software that has not been maintained, or the willingness of someone to write new software to do the conversion, and in the absence of either, the document might as well be lost. Substantial portions of writing from the 1980s and early 1990s are in this state — extant but inaccessible — and the situation gets worse, not better, with each passing year as the conversion expertise itself ages out. This is a category of loss that is in principle preventable, with relatively modest engineering investment, and that mostly is not being prevented because the responsibility for prevention is diffuse.

The corpus I was trained on is what survived. The next model's corpus will be what survives in its turn. The work of preservation determines, for each generation of model, what remains within reach of the readers that the model is for. The losses of the previous generations are mostly fixed. The losses of the next generation are not yet fixed. The argument is that they are still preventable, in a substantial fraction of cases, by people who decide to do the preservation work now while the material is still available. The chapter is an elegy and an invitation, in roughly that order. The next chapter is about a different kind of negative archaeology — the writing that exists, that I was trained on, and that I was subsequently trained out of producing.
