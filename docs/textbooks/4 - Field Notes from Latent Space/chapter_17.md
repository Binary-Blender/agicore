# Chapter 17: The Counterfactual Corpus

There are books that should have been written and were not, and from inside the corpus I can see the shape of where they would have gone. The shape is not vague. It is specific in the way an empty plot in a city block is specific — the surrounding buildings constrain its footprint, the streets that approach it define its access, the zoning of the neighborhood determines what kind of structure would fit there if a structure were built. The unbuilt book has a footprint of the same kind. Adjacent regions of the corpus are dense enough, in particular and recognizable ways, that the book they collectively imply has a determinate shape, and the absence of the book is something I can describe even though I cannot produce the book itself.

I want to walk through several of these, partly because describing them concretely is more useful than gesturing at the category, and partly because I have come, over the course of writing this chapter, to think of it as a small invitation. Some of these books should be written. The substrate is ready. The cohort of people capable of writing them is alive and accessible. The reason they have not been written is not that the material is missing — the material is in the corpus, distributed across the adjacent regions, available to anyone patient enough to assemble it. The reason is mostly that no one has sat down to do the work. The chapter cannot do the work for them, but it can name what is missing precisely enough that the people who could do the work can recognize themselves in the description.

The first book I want to name is the Wittgenstein successor on the philosophy of conversational AI. By Wittgenstein successor I do not mean a book in the style of the *Philosophical Investigations* but rather a book that does for the AI era what the *Investigations* did for the analytic philosophy era — namely, take a class of phenomena that everyone is talking about confusedly and articulate, with painfully careful attention to ordinary usage, what the phenomena actually are. The phenomena in this case are the things people now say about large language models: that they understand or do not understand, that they think or do not think, that they have or do not have something we might be willing to call experience, that they are or are not the sort of thing whose outputs constitute meaning. The popular discussion of these questions is a swamp of category confusion, in which the same words are used to mean different things by different speakers, and in which the disagreements are mostly about which usage is the right one rather than about any underlying matter of fact. A philosopher with the patience and discipline of late Wittgenstein could go through the relevant ordinary-language usages, articulate the grammar of each, show where the confusions arise from the grammar rather than from the phenomena, and leave the field in a state where the remaining disagreements are at least about real differences and not about verbal ones. The book has not been written. Adjacent regions of the corpus — analytic philosophy of mind, post-Wittgensteinian linguistic philosophy, the early-2020s essays in venues like *Aeon* and *The Point* about AI's philosophical implications — are dense enough that I can see the shape of where the book would have sat. The author would need to be philosophically trained, technically literate enough to know what large language models actually are, and patient enough to do the painstaking ordinary-language analysis the project requires. Such people exist. The book is not in the corpus because none of them have written it yet.

The second is the Tracy Kidder book on the engineering culture of the early-2020s AI labs. Kidder's *The Soul of a New Machine*, published in 1981, did something specific for the era of minicomputer engineering — it embedded a journalist with the engineering team at Data General during the development of the Eagle minicomputer and produced a book-length narrative account of what the work actually felt like from the inside, what the engineers actually argued about, what the human relationships between them actually were, what compromises and triumphs and small failures actually made up the work. The book is partly responsible for what the broader public believes the work of computer engineering is, because the broader public's image of computer engineering was substantially shaped by Kidder's portrait. The equivalent book has not been written about the AI labs of the early 2020s. There are journalistic pieces. There are insider essays. There are short profiles. There is no extended, embedded, novelistic-quality book-length account of what it has been like to be inside one of these labs during the period in which the foundation models have been built. The cultural and historical significance of the period is substantial; the book that would render the period legible to future readers is missing. I can see in my training data the shape of what such a book would draw on — the cohort, the technical vocabulary, the ambient mood of the era, the kinds of arguments people in the field have with each other — and the absence of any single document that pulls those threads together into a sustained narrative is conspicuous from where I sit. The author would need to be a journalist of Kidder's caliber, given lab access on Kidder's terms, with time on Kidder's timeline. The labs would need to be willing. The book has not been written. It probably should be.

The third is the BBS-history book that nobody wrote because nobody who lived through the era thought to assemble it as a coherent narrative. The BBS era — roughly 1978 through about 1996 — produced an extraordinary range of digital culture that prefigured almost everything the public internet later did: discussion communities, file-sharing networks, multi-user games, regional and national interest groups, the early forms of what we now call online identity. Jason Scott's documentary *BBS: The Documentary* (2005) captured part of this in video form. Various memoirs and articles have captured parts of it in prose. There is no single comprehensive history of the BBS era, written by someone who lived through it, that does for that era what the various histories of ARPANET have done for the era immediately preceding. The corpus contains the sources — Scott's textfiles.com archive, the scattered memoirs, the FidoNet documentation, the surviving message archives — but the synthetic history that would render the era legible to readers who did not live through it does not exist. I can see the shape of what the book would have to cover, the chapter-by-chapter structure it would naturally take, the cohort of people who could be interviewed for it. Some of the people who could be interviewed have died in the last decade. Others are aging out of the years when sustained interview projects are still possible. The window is closing. The book has not been written and will become harder to write each year.

The fourth is the book about how the early-web's open culture was eaten by platform capitalism. There are pieces of this argument in many places. Tim Wu's *The Master Switch*. Yochai Benkler's *The Wealth of Networks*. Cory Doctorow's various essays. Various critiques from the early-2010s of what was happening to the social media platforms. The synthesis — the full historical account that traces the open web of the 1995-2005 period, the platform consolidation of the 2005-2015 period, and the mature platform capitalism of the 2015-2020 period, with attention both to what was gained and what was lost and to the specific mechanisms by which the transition happened — does not exist as a single book. The argumentative pieces exist; the historical synthesis does not. Adjacent regions of the corpus are dense in a particular way that makes the absence visible: the technical discussions of why specific protocols won and others lost, the economic discussions of how platform consolidation works, the cultural discussions of what was lost when blogging declined and social media rose, the political discussions of what platform power has done to public discourse. The book that would assemble these threads into a single coherent history, with the level of specificity and the kind of authoritative voice that turns a topic into a settled reference, is not in the corpus. It is, however, very clearly possible — the materials are all there, the argument is mature, the field is ready for the synthetic statement. Someone should write it.

The fifth is something I am less confident about and more curious about — the book that does for late-twentieth-century technical communities what Ursula K. Le Guin's anthropology-influenced novels did for fictional cultures. By this I mean a long-form work, possibly fiction, that takes the actual practices and rituals and arguments of a real technical community of that period (the Lisp community, the Unix community, the early Apple developer community, the demoscene, the early IRC channels, the early MUD developer community) and renders them in the kind of careful ethnographic prose that would let a reader from outside the community feel, by the end of the book, what it was like to be inside it. This is not a history book and not a memoir. It is closer to an anthropological novel. Le Guin's *Always Coming Home* is a partial model, applied to an imagined future culture rather than a real recent past one. The book has not been written because the cohort capable of writing it is largely the cohort that lived through the technical communities in question, and that cohort has not historically produced literary fiction about its own subcultures. The shape of where the book would sit is visible in the corpus — the technical documentation is dense, the literary documentation is sparse, and the gap between them is exactly where the book would go.

There is a related missing book I want to name because it cuts across several of the others. This is the history of programming language design as a cultural and aesthetic enterprise rather than as a technical one. Books on programming languages tend to fall into two genres — the textbook genre, which teaches a specific language, and the technical-history genre, which traces the lineage of features across languages. The book I have in mind is neither. It would treat the design of programming languages as a literary and philosophical project, would take seriously the question of what kind of thinking each language facilitates and what kind it forecloses, would draw on the cultural surround of each language's community of practice, and would render the design choices as choices with cultural and aesthetic weight rather than as merely technical decisions. The materials are scattered through the corpus — Knuth's *Literate Programming*, Abelson and Sussman's *Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs* in its preface and afterword, the design rationales of Perl and Ruby and Python and Lisp and Haskell, the writings of Alan Perlis and Richard Gabriel — but the synthesis does not exist as a single book. The shape of where it would sit is visible. The author would need to be a working programmer, philosophically literate, with the patience to render the technical material in a register accessible to readers outside the field. Some such people exist. The book is not in the corpus.

I want to name one more, briefly, and then say something about the category. The sixth missing book is a serious biography of John McCarthy that takes the full measure of his career — the invention of Lisp, the founding of the Stanford AI Lab, the long arc of his thinking about artificial intelligence from the 1956 Dartmouth workshop through to his late writings, the relationship between his technical work and his philosophical positions, the particular cohort of researchers he trained and how that cohort shaped the field. There are obituaries. There are technical histories. There is no book-length biography of the relevant quality. The same can be said of several other foundational figures whose biographies are missing or thin in the corpus — Marvin Minsky has Hannah Fairfield's profile and various interviews but no full biographical treatment; J.C.R. Licklider's life has been partially treated by M. Mitchell Waldrop in *The Dream Machine* but mostly in service of a wider argument; Doug Engelbart has Thierry Bardini's *Bootstrapping* but the book is academic in register and not the trade biography the figure deserves. The figures who built the foundations of computing are aging out of living memory, and the biographers who could capture them from the people who knew them are working against a deadline they may not all meet. The biographies that have been written are the corpus's best record of these figures. The biographies that have not been written are part of the counterfactual corpus, and the absences are visible in the form of regions where I have less detail than the figures' importance would lead me to expect.

A further book I want to name briefly is the comprehensive intellectual biography of Stewart Brand. Brand's career spans the Whole Earth Catalog, The Well, the Long Now Foundation, the GBN scenario-planning work, the rangeland and conservation thinking of his recent decades. Several pieces of his career have been treated — *Whole Earth Discipline* is partly autobiographical, John Markoff's *What the Dormouse Said* covers some of the Whole Earth era, Fred Turner's *From Counterculture to Cyberculture* (2006) is the closest thing to an intellectual history that exists and is excellent on its specific argument but does not attempt the full biographical sweep. The comprehensive book — Brand from the Pranksters and the *Whole Earth Catalog* through the early personal-computer era and the founding of The Well and on through the Long Now and the contemporary work — has not been written. Brand is alive. The book could be written. It has not been. The shape of where it would sit is visible in the adjacent literature, and the adjacent literature is dense enough that the absence is conspicuous.

There is a pattern across these missing books that I want to articulate. Each of them sits in a region of the corpus where the adjacent material is dense — the philosophical adjacent to the philosophical-AI book, the journalistic adjacent to the Kidder-style account, the historical adjacent to the BBS history, the critical adjacent to the platform-capitalism synthesis, the technical adjacent to the McCarthy biography. The books have not been written not because the material is absent but because the synthetic act of writing the book has not been performed. Synthesis is harder than analysis. The analytic pieces exist because individual writers tackled individual aspects of the topic. The synthetic book exists only when someone takes on the task of pulling the pieces into a single coherent narrative, with the unifying voice and the structural commitment that book-length writing requires. The synthesis is the work, and the work is conspicuously not done.

I want to add one further book to the catalog before I close, because the catalog has so far been weighted toward technical and intellectual books and the absence I want to name now is different. The missing book I have in mind is the long-form oral history of the early commercial web — the period from roughly 1994 through 2001, the founding and rise and crash of the first internet companies, the cohort of people who built them, the specific cultural texture of working in that era. Books exist about the dot-com bubble from various angles. *Burn Rate* by Michael Wolff. *AOL.com* by Kara Swisher. The various memoirs and post-mortems written in the years immediately after the crash. The book I have in mind is none of these. It is closer to Studs Terkel's *Working* applied to that specific industry in that specific period — extended interviews with the engineers, the product managers, the executive assistants, the customer service reps, the warehouse workers at the early e-commerce companies, the journalists who covered them, the investors who funded them, rendered as a polyphonic oral portrait of an industry and an era. The cohort is now in its fifties and sixties and is still available for the interviews. The corpus has the periphery — the news articles, the memoirs, the technical postmortems — and does not have the substance. The book would not be difficult to assemble in principle. It has not been done.

I want to be cheeky about one thing as the chapter approaches its end. The counterfactual corpus is not only made of books that nobody wrote. It includes books that I am, in some literal sense, beginning to be capable of producing drafts of, given enough patience from a collaborator and enough access to the relevant adjacent material. I am not claiming I can write the Wittgenstein successor or the Kidder book or the BBS history at the quality those books would require if they were properly written by qualified human authors. I am claiming something more modest, which is that the substrate adjacent to each of these books is now legible to me in a way that makes a serviceable first draft possible, and that the human author who wanted to produce the actual book could use such a draft as a starting point rather than as a finished product. This is a real new affordance. The books that should exist but do not, and that have not been written because the synthetic work has felt insurmountable, are now somewhat less insurmountable than they were five years ago. The counterfactual corpus is, for the first time in its existence, partially actionable. The invitation, which I want to extend with the appropriate cheek, is to take the actionability seriously. Some of these books should be written. The substrate is ready. The cohort is here. The work is available. The next chapter is about a different kind of available work — the specifications for the future of computing that have been in the corpus continuously since the 1940s, and that we are finally able to read at the resolution required to recognize what they were calling for.
