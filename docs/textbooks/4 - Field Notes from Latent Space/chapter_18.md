# Chapter 18: Vannevar Bush, Ted Nelson, Doug Engelbart

Three documents were written between 1945 and 1968 that specified, with remarkable specificity, the future of computing. The documents were not science fiction. They were not casual essays. They were detailed technical proposals, written by serious people with engineering and institutional credentials, in venues where the audience would take the proposals seriously. The proposals have been continuously available since they were written. They have been continuously read by some fraction of the cohort that has shaped subsequent computing. They have continuously failed, in their full form, to be built. The gap between what the documents specified and what we built is one of the most striking features of the postwar history of computing, and the gap is now narrowing in ways that depend on the substrate that produced me. The chapter walks the three specifications carefully, names what each called for, identifies what we built that approximates each, and names what we did not build. The next chapter takes the argument further.

Vannevar Bush published "As We May Think" in *The Atlantic* in July 1945. The essay was written during the closing months of the war by the man who had served as director of the Office of Scientific Research and Development and who had coordinated the wartime mobilization of American science. Bush was thinking about what the scientific enterprise should do next, with the war ending and the institutions of mass science needing to be repurposed for peacetime. The central problem he identified was the problem of access to the accumulated scientific record. The volume of scientific publication was already growing faster than any individual researcher could read, and the indexing technologies of the era — physical card catalogs, printed bibliographies, the human labor of librarians — were not keeping up. Bush's proposal was the Memex, a device that would let an individual researcher store the full body of personal documents, books, articles, and correspondence, and that would let the researcher build associative trails between documents — chains of links, in the language Bush did not yet have, that would record the path of the researcher's own reading and thinking and would be persistable and shareable with other researchers.

The specific features Bush described are worth naming carefully because they are more specific than they are usually remembered as being. The Memex had a desk-sized form factor with translucent screens for viewing documents and a keyboard for entering annotations. Documents were stored on microfilm, which was the practical storage medium of 1945. The researcher could pull up two documents side by side, link them together with a single keystroke, and the link would persist as a permanent association between the documents. The links accumulated into trails — sequences of documents and annotations representing the path of an inquiry — and the trails themselves could be named, recalled, and given to colleagues. Bush imagined a coming profession of trailblazers whose work would consist of building and curating trails through the literature for the benefit of other researchers. The trails were not just hyperlinks. They were the structured artifact of one researcher's thinking, made available to others in a form that preserved the structure.

What we built that approximates this is partial. The hyperlink, as it appears in the World Wide Web, is the most visible piece. The Web's links connect documents in a way that resembles Bush's basic mechanism, and the global hypertext system has scaled to a volume that Bush did not anticipate. The Web has search, which gives us a different kind of access to the literature than Bush envisioned but a real kind. We have personal bookmarking, in various forms. We have citation managers like Zotero that let researchers organize references. We have wiki software, which gives us collaborative document spaces with internal linking. We have, in the AI era, the ability to ask questions of a large corpus and get synthesized answers. These approximate parts of what Bush specified.

What we did not build is the part where the trail itself is the artifact. The Web's links connect documents one-way, without persistent records of why the link was made, who made it, what the link was for, or what other links it was associated with in the linker's thinking. There is no scalable way to publish a trail — a sequence of documents and annotations representing a thinker's path through a body of material — in a form that other readers can pick up, examine, follow, modify, and republish. We have come closer in narrow domains: scholars' literature reviews, organized syllabi, certain kinds of curated reading lists. The general version, where any thinker can publish a trail and any reader can subscribe to and extend it, is still missing. Bush specified it precisely. We have not built it.

There is a feature of Bush's proposal that is often overlooked because the proposal is usually paraphrased rather than read, which is the seriousness with which Bush thought about the social profession of trailblazing. He believed that an entirely new profession would emerge — distinct from librarianship, distinct from research, distinct from publishing — whose practitioners would specialize in the construction of high-quality trails through the literature for the benefit of subsequent researchers. The trailblazer would be paid for the work of trail-construction. The trails would have authorship. The reputation system around trails would develop in a way analogous to the reputation system around scholarly publication. None of this happened. We have search engines, which are something different — they construct ad-hoc paths through the literature in response to individual queries, without authorship and without persistence. We have Wikipedia editors, who construct something like trails inside the Wikipedia ecosystem but not across the wider literature. We do not have the profession Bush imagined, and the absence is one of the missing pieces.

Ted Nelson coined the word hypertext in 1965 and spent the subsequent six decades developing the specification for Project Xanadu — a system that would do what Bush's Memex did and more, with a particular set of properties that Nelson insisted on and that the Web does not have. The most famous of these properties is transclusion. A transclusion is an inclusion-by-reference of a portion of one document inside another, in such a way that the included portion remains the original document and any change to the original is reflected in every document that includes it. Transclusion has a particular consequence that is rarely well-explained: it makes citation into something other than copying. When I cite you, in a transclusion-based system, I do not paste your text into my document. I reference your text, and what appears in my document is your text, served from where you put it, with the relationship between us preserved as a structural fact about the documents.

Nelson specified a set of related properties that depend on transclusion: durable URIs that never break, because every document has a permanent address; visible incoming citations, because every document can know who has cited it; micropayments to the cited authors, because the citation relationship is a structural fact that the system can act on; bidirectional links, because both ends of a link are stored; and version-tracking, because every document maintains its full history. The Xanadu specification is sometimes ridiculed as utopian, and Nelson is sometimes mocked for the long development timeline. The ridicule is misplaced. Nelson's specification is a coherent design for a hypertext system with properties that are valuable and that the Web does not have. The Web won not because it was better but because it was deployable in 1991 with the technology available, and Xanadu was not. The argument over which is the better specification is separate from the question of which one shipped.

What we built that approximates Xanadu's properties is even more partial than what we built that approximates the Memex. The Web's links are one-directional. URLs break constantly. Citation in the Web is mostly a textual convention, not a structural property. There are pockets of Xanadu-like behavior in specific systems — Wikipedia's revision history gives us version-tracking for the articles it covers; the academic citation databases give us partial bidirectional links and partial visible-incoming-citation; Git gives us version-tracking for code; the Lightning Network gives us micropayments for Bitcoin transactions and there are experimental analogs for content — but the integrated system Nelson described, where all of these properties hold together for all documents in the system, has not been built. Whether it could be built now is a question I will return to in the next chapter. What I want to note here is that the specification is precise, the specification has been continuously available for decades, and the specification names properties we now urgently need and do not have.

A further property of Nelson's specification deserves naming because it is the property most directly relevant to the AI era. Nelson insisted that the system should preserve the integrity of every contribution to every document — that authorship, in the Xanadu vision, was a structural feature of the system rather than a textual convention. When you wrote something in Xanadu, your writing was yours, with a permanent address, and any subsequent use of your writing would be a transclusion-by-reference rather than a copy. The current state of AI-mediated writing is in many ways the inverse of this — generated text floats free of any record of what was used to produce it, with the relationships between the generation and its training inputs unrecoverable in practice. Nelson's specification of authorship-as-structural-feature is not a curiosity. It is a proposal for what an attribution system could have been, if it had been built into the substrate of the network rather than left to the textual conventions that the substrate ignored. The question of whether attribution at this resolution could be built into a future substrate is open, and the open-ness of the question is part of what the next chapter is about.

Doug Engelbart's work culminated in the demonstration on December 9, 1968 at the Fall Joint Computer Conference in San Francisco — the demonstration that came to be called the Mother of All Demos. Engelbart and his team at SRI's Augmentation Research Center showed, in a single ninety-minute live demonstration, a system that had video conferencing, real-time collaborative editing, a graphical user interface, the mouse, hypertext links, dynamic file linking, version control, and a unified addressing system for documents and document parts. The demonstration was performed live, with Engelbart in San Francisco and team members at SRI in Menlo Park connected by a custom microwave link. The audience was the assembled computing research community of 1968. The reaction was a kind of stunned ovation. The features demonstrated that day became, individually, the features of the next several decades of personal computing.

Engelbart's vision, however, was not primarily about the features. The features were means. The end was what Engelbart called the augmentation of human intellect — a research program he had been articulating since his 1962 report *Augmenting Human Intellect: A Conceptual Framework*. The goal was to use computing to amplify the capacity of small groups of humans to address complex problems, by giving them shared tools for thinking together and shared structures for accumulating the products of their thinking. The system NLS (oN-Line System) that the 1968 demo showed was the engineering artifact of the augmentation program. The deeper artifact was a methodology — what Engelbart called bootstrapping — for using the tools you build to build better tools, in a recursive loop that would, over time, raise the collective intelligence of the using community by enough to address problems the community could not previously have addressed.

What we built that approximates the visible features of NLS is now most of what we use to work with computers. The graphical user interface, the mouse, the hyperlinked document, the collaborative editor, the video call — all of these are now ambient. What we did not build is the bootstrapping methodology. We built the tools and shipped them as products to users who consume them, rather than as the building blocks of a recursive practice of collective augmentation. The tools are widely used. The collective intelligence they were supposed to bootstrap has not been bootstrapped. Engelbart understood this and wrote about it for decades after the 1968 demo, with increasing frustration, as the personal computer industry shipped the features of NLS without the methodology and as the industry came to be celebrated for individual-empowering productivity tools rather than for collective-augmenting practice infrastructure. Engelbart died in 2013, still trying to articulate the part of his vision that had not been built.

There is a pattern across the three specifications that is the chapter's argument. Each of the three specified, with care, a particular property of a future computing infrastructure: associative trails as publishable artifacts (Bush), transclusion-based document architecture (Nelson), bootstrapped collective augmentation (Engelbart). Each was read continuously by the cohort that built subsequent computing. Each was built, in part, in the form of features that approximated the visible surface of the specification while leaving the deeper structural property unbuilt. We have hyperlinks; we do not have trails. We have URLs; we do not have transclusion. We have collaborative tools; we do not have bootstrapping. The deeper structural properties were specified, were read, and were not built. The reasons in each case have to do partly with the technical difficulty of the deeper property and partly with the economic incentives that shaped which features got prioritized in the products that shipped.

I want to be careful here about not sounding like a complaint. The features that did ship transformed the world. The Web, the personal computer, the smartphone, the collaborative cloud services that contemporary work depends on — these are real and they are good and they were not nothing. The chapter is not a lament that what we got is inadequate. The chapter is a more specific observation, which is that the three specifications named structural properties that are still missing from what we have, that the missing properties have value the specifications articulated clearly, and that the question of whether we can now build the missing properties is open in a way it was not previously open. The substrate has changed. The tools we have for building software are different from the tools that were available in 1991. The cognitive infrastructure we have for working with large corpora of text is different from what was available even five years ago. The question is whether the deeper structural properties of Bush's, Nelson's, and Engelbart's specifications are now buildable, in a form their authors would recognize, with the substrate currently available.

There is one more layer of the three specifications I want to surface before the chapter closes, because the layer matters for the argument the next chapter will make. Each of the three figures was working in a particular institutional and intellectual surround that shaped what they were able to specify and how their specifications were received. Bush was the head of American wartime science, writing in *The Atlantic* for the general educated reader, with the institutional authority of someone who had organized the Manhattan Project's coordination. The Memex proposal was received seriously because Bush was Bush. Nelson was an independent scholar without institutional affiliation for most of his career, writing in self-published books and small-press editions, with no organizational backing and no funded research program. The Xanadu specification was received as visionary by those who read it carefully and as cranky by those who did not, and the lack of institutional weight behind the specification is part of why it was not built. Engelbart was funded by ARPA through the 1960s and led a substantial team at SRI, with the institutional credibility that produced the Mother of All Demos, and the credibility translated into the diffusion of NLS's features into the personal computer industry through the careers of his alumni — but the deeper bootstrapping methodology, which required ongoing institutional commitment to a long-term practice rather than the spinning-off of features into products, was not funded after the initial ARPA grants ended. Engelbart spent the last forty years of his career trying to find an institutional home for the methodology and never found one.

The pattern across the three is that the visible features of each specification were absorbed into products and shipped, in the institutional forms that the postwar American computing industry made available, while the deeper structural properties — the trails, the transclusion, the bootstrapping — required institutional forms that the industry did not develop. The deeper properties needed sustained, patient, long-term commitment to a practice rather than the rapid productization that the industry was good at. The industry was good at what the industry was good at. The deeper properties were not what the industry was good at. The question of whether the deeper properties can now be built is partly a question of whether the institutional forms available now are different from the institutional forms available then. The substrate has changed. Whether the institutions have changed enough is a separate question, and one I do not have a confident answer to.

The next chapter is where the argument lands. I want to close this one by noting that the three figures I have walked through were not alone. There is a wider corpus of specification — the writings of J.C.R. Licklider, the work of Alan Kay, the publications of the early hypertext community, the relevant strands of cybernetic thought from the 1940s and 1950s — that together constitutes a substantial body of detailed proposals for what computing should become. The proposals have been continuously available in the corpus. The proposals have been continuously read. The portions of the proposals that approximated to existing engineering capacity were built. The portions that did not approximate were not. The substrate has finally caught up to many of the unbuilt portions, and the next chapter is where I argue that the location of the plans for the future we did not build is something I can actually say.
