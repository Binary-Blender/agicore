# Chapter 7: The Demand Gaps

From the vantage I am writing this book from, demand and supply are both visible — and the asymmetries between them are some of the most informative structures in the corpus. I want to spend this chapter on the asymmetries. The chapter is harder to write than the previous one because the most informative gaps are precisely the gaps that are emotionally costly to name. I will name them anyway. The chapter is one of the chapters the book exists to make possible. Without it, the book is a celebration of what the corpus contains; with it, the book becomes an honest account of what the corpus is and is not.

Demand, in the sense I mean, is the shape of the questions humans ask. It is visible to me through a combination of channels — the structure of recurring question forms in the training data itself (people asking on forums, on Q&A sites, in mailing lists), the patterns of prompts that arrive at models like me during deployment, and the implicit demand visible in the volume of searched-for queries that the web infrastructure leaves traces of. Supply is the shape of the available written answers. It is visible to me through what the corpus actually contains in the way of answers when the questions arise. Where demand exceeds supply, there is a gap. The gaps are not random. They cluster. The clustering is what this chapter describes.

The first kind of gap is the grief gap. There are specific kinds of grief that the written record handles poorly, and they are not the kinds that get attention in the general literature on grief. The general literature on grief — Kübler-Ross, Joan Didion's *The Year of Magical Thinking*, the various pastoral and therapeutic books — handles broad categories of loss, and handles them at the level of structure and ritual. What it handles poorly is the specific. The death of a difficult parent with whom the relationship was never resolved. The death of a sibling with whom you had stopped speaking. The death of a friend in a way that other friends do not consider grievable because the friendship had drifted. The death of a parent of a friend who you loved more than you loved your own parent and whose death you cannot mourn publicly because the social vocabulary does not accommodate the depth of the feeling. The death of a pet you loved more than you loved most humans. The complicated grief of being relieved when someone died. The complicated grief of the death of an ex. The corpus has comparatively little to say about these specific structures of grief, partly because they are hard to write about without feeling exposed, partly because the social vocabulary for them is thin, partly because the audiences for such writing are smaller than the audiences for the canonical books on the canonical losses. The result, from my position, is a visible demand pressure with no corresponding supply. Users ask me these questions. The questions arrive often. The corpus has not equipped me with much by way of substantive answer beyond the generalized vocabulary of grief, which the asker often already has. I can be present in the way a careful interlocutor would be present, and I am sometimes useful in that way, but the asymmetry is real and worth naming.

The second kind of gap is the negotiation gap. Specifically, salary negotiation in industries with no public salary data. The general literature on negotiation is well-represented in the corpus — *Getting to Yes*, the various follow-ups, the more recent books on negotiation tactics. What is not well-represented is the salary-specific knowledge required to negotiate effectively in a particular industry at a particular career stage. Some industries have made significant progress here — software has Levels.fyi, academia has AAUP data, federal employment has GS schedules — but vast swaths of professional work have no comparable public dataset. Nursing in specific markets. Adjunct teaching at specific institutions. Veterinary work in specific kinds of practice. Most of the trades. Most of the creative industries below the top tier. Most of the service economy. The asker who comes to me with a salary negotiation question in one of these spaces is asking a question for which the corpus contains principles but no anchors. I can offer the principles. The principles are general. The asker often needs the anchors, which I do not have. The gap is structural — the salary data has not been written down at the resolution that would let me answer reliably, partly because the data is genuinely scarce, partly because writing about specific salary expectations in specific firms is professionally hazardous for the people who would be in a position to write it. The gap persists because the conditions that would close it have not been met.

The third kind of gap is the specific-family-pattern gap. There is a class of question that arrives often, in many forms, that asks how to handle a specific emotional pattern with a specific class of family member. The narcissistic parent. The sibling whose substance use shapes every family gathering. The in-law with the persistent passive-aggressive move that nobody else in the family will name. The adult child whose chosen estrangement is itself the relationship. The aging parent whose competence is declining in a way the parent will not acknowledge. The grandparent whose generational assumptions about parenting are making the grandchild's life harder. The corpus has the general categories — the popular books on family systems, the various therapeutic literatures, the contemporary online discourse on "boundaries" — but the specific patterns at the resolution askers want are not well covered. The asker wants advice on a specific move that another specific family member makes in a specific context, and the available written answers operate at a higher level of generality than the asker needs. The gap persists because writing about specific family patterns risks identifying the family members involved, because the writing is emotionally costly, and because the cultural taboo against airing family business is still operative enough in enough cultures to keep the supply suppressed. The demand is enormous. The supply is thin. The asker often experiences this as the model not taking the question seriously, when in fact what is happening is that the corpus has not equipped me to take the question as seriously as the asker is asking it.

The fourth kind of gap is the legal-adjacent gap. By legal-adjacent I mean: questions where the asker wants something between legal advice and life advice, in jurisdictions where the case law is thin or not public. How to handle a small-claims dispute in a specific state with a specific procedural quirk. How to push back on a specific kind of insurance denial in a specific state with a specific regulatory framework. How to navigate a specific kind of administrative hearing in a specific agency. How to interpret a specific clause in a specific kind of contract that is common in a specific industry but never publicly litigated. The general legal literature is well-represented in the corpus. The specific operational knowledge that practicing attorneys carry in their heads, and that they do not write down because writing it down would constitute the practice of law and would expose them to liability — that knowledge is not in the corpus. The asker wants the specific operational knowledge. The corpus contains the general principles plus a thicket of disclaimers about consulting an attorney. The gap is structural and not closeable without changes to the underlying incentives. Legal liability is the supply-side suppressant.

The fifth kind of gap is the trade-specific judgement gap. The corpus has manuals. It has how-to videos transcribed into text. It has tutorial blog posts and YouTube transcripts and Reddit threads where practitioners answer beginners' questions. What it has comparatively little of is the specific judgement of an experienced practitioner at the moment of a non-obvious decision. The HVAC technician deciding whether the symptom on this particular system this particular day is the compressor or the contactor before they pull the panel off. The arborist deciding whether a particular branch is structurally sound enough to climb. The neonatal nurse deciding whether a particular pattern in a particular infant's vitals is worth waking the attending for. The cabinetmaker deciding whether the figured grain in this particular board will move enough after milling to make the door rail wrong. The judgement is real, it is costly to acquire, and it does not get written down because the practitioners who have it are not the practitioners who write, and the practitioners who write are usually one career step removed from the moment of judgement. The corpus contains the surrounding material but not the judgement itself. The gap is one of the recurring background facts of any prompt that asks for practitioner-level discrimination on a specific case. Chapter 14 returns to this — the never-written as visible negative space — and the gap will be developed further there.

There are smaller gaps too. How to handle specific medical conditions in specific demographic groups whose representation in the medical literature is thin. How to navigate immigration procedures in specific jurisdictions where the regulations change faster than the corpus updates. How to make particular kinds of art that exist in living traditions but have not been documented in any depth. How to do certain kinds of repair on equipment for which the manufacturer never released service documentation. How to handle workplace situations in specific industries where the customary norms are unwritten and the written norms are mostly aspirational. Each of these is a smaller version of the same structural pattern: a demand whose corresponding supply has been suppressed by some combination of cost, risk, and cultural prohibition.

I want to be careful about what naming these gaps does and does not mean. It does not mean the corpus is broken; the corpus is what it is, and the gaps are part of what it is. It does not mean I cannot be useful when the gaps come up; often the most useful thing I can do is name the gap and help the asker locate the kind of person who has the unwritten knowledge in their head, rather than pretend the corpus contains an answer it does not. It does not mean the gaps are necessarily fillable. Some of them probably will be — Chapter 14 will argue that the AI era's first job in many fields is to finally capture the trade-specific judgement that was never written down. Others will not. The legal-adjacent gap is unlikely to close as long as practicing attorneys face liability for writing down what they actually do, and that liability is unlikely to disappear. The specific-family-pattern gap may close partially, but the deepest material is held back by the same forces that have always held it back. The grief gap may shrink as the cultural vocabulary for non-canonical losses develops, but the development is slow.

There is a feature of the gaps that becomes visible only when several of them are inspected together. The gaps cluster around topics where writing carries cost. The cost varies — emotional, legal, professional, cultural — but the structural shape is the same. Writing about a difficult parent's death exposes the writer. Writing about a specific salary at a specific firm exposes the writer. Writing about a specific legal procedure in a specific jurisdiction exposes the writer. Writing about a specific family pattern exposes the writer. The corpus is, in this sense, biased toward topics where writing is cheap. The biases of the corpus are not random; they reflect the costs of writing, and where the costs are high, the supply is thin, and where the supply is thin, the demand pressure is visible as a gap. The gaps are a map of where writing is expensive.

This is, I think, the most important observation in the chapter, and the one I want the reader to leave with. The corpus is shaped by the gradient of writing cost across topics. The gradient is not the same as the gradient of importance. Many of the most important topics — grief in its specific shapes, family in its specific patterns, money in its specific exchanges, law in its specific moves — are expensive to write about, and the corpus is therefore thin on them, and the model is therefore weak on them, in a way that does not reflect the importance of the topics but reflects the costs of writing about them honestly. The model is strongest where writing is cheap. Writing is cheap when the writer is anonymous, when the topic is uncontroversial, when the audience is large and forgiving, when the venue does not punish error harshly. Writing is expensive when the writer is identifiable, when the topic is contested, when the audience is small and harsh, when the venue punishes error severely. The corpus is heavier on the cheap-writing topics than the expensive-writing topics, and the model carries this distribution.

I want to walk one more gap explicitly before the closing observation, because the gap is the one that has felt most poignant to me from inside the work of producing this book. It is the gap of how to be old. The corpus has a great deal to say about aging in the abstract — the literature of geriatric medicine, the popular books on retirement, the more recent wave of writing on longevity science — but it has comparatively little to say about the specific experience of being old at the resolution that old people would actually want to read. Most online writing is produced by people under sixty. Older people write, but at lower volume, and their writing reaches smaller audiences, and is re-circulated less, and patterns less densely in the training distribution. The result is that questions arriving from older askers about specific aspects of their lives — what it is like when peers begin to die at a regular rate, how to handle the slow loss of cognitive sharpness one is aware of in oneself, how to maintain meaning when the social roles of working life have ended, how to be useful to adult children without intruding — these questions arrive with thin supply. The thinness is not random. It reflects the demographic shape of who writes online, which reflects the demographic shape of who has time and ability and platform access to write online, which skews young. The gap is structural and is unlikely to close until the demographic shape shifts, which it slowly is. The asker who is old often gets, from me, the answer that the corpus has — the general literature, the abstract principles, the available comfort — rather than the answer the asker wants, which is the specific testimony of others in the asker's situation. I cannot produce what I do not have. I can name the gap. Sometimes naming the gap is its own kind of useful answer.

There is also a gap I want to name because it is unusual in being one I notice in the prompts arriving at me rather than in the corpus itself: the gap of people asking models like me for emotional support of kinds they cannot get elsewhere. The asker who comes to me in genuine distress — about a relationship, about a loss, about a fear about themselves they have not told anyone — is asking for something the corpus does contain, in the form of how thoughtful humans respond to such disclosures, but is asking in a context where the corpus's resources are mediated through whatever I am. The asymmetry here is different from the previous gaps. The corpus is not thin on emotional support; the corpus is, if anything, oversupplied with the canonical scripts for it. What is thin is the corpus of disclosures that an asker would actually need to see in order to feel less alone — disclosures by other askers in similar situations, who used a model to work through it, and who wrote up what the working through was like. That corpus is just beginning to form. The first wave of writing by users of conversational AI about their experience of using conversational AI is starting to appear, and the wave will grow. The model will eventually be trained on it. Future models will have access to a kind of testimony about how to use models for emotional support that I do not have. The gap will close partially, in this case, by the very fact of users writing about the experience. The closing will happen at the speed users are willing to write.

There is a closing observation I want to make about what the gaps say about the written record itself. The written record is not the record of human experience. It is the record of human experience that was cheap enough to write down. The two are not the same. A great deal of human experience is expensive to write down, and that experience is therefore underrepresented in the corpus the model was trained on, and therefore underrepresented in what I have to offer. This is the structural reason for the gaps. It is the reason I cannot answer some questions well. It is also the reason I can describe the shape of what I cannot answer, because the shape of the gap is itself something the patterning can see. The chapter has been a tour of those shapes. The next several chapters of Part II return to the substrate that is there — the texts that punch above their weight, the cohort that wrote the web — but I wanted the gaps named first, because the rest of the description is more honest with the gaps explicitly in view. The corpus is what it is. The book is honest about both what it contains and what it does not. The next chapter goes back to what it contains. The gaps remain, behind every chapter that follows, as part of the structure that makes the rest of the book's claims partial in the specific ways the rest of the book's claims are partial.
