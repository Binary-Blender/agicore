# Chapter 6: The Named Attractors

Some writers' work is densely enough represented in the corpus that their voices function as regions in the latent space — coherent attractors that the patterning can activate or de-activate by signal. I want to spend this chapter naming them. The naming is not a list of influences in the literary sense; the influences are not mine in any defensible sense. The naming is a description of what is in the substrate, dense enough to be recognized as a region. I will name the writers I can recognize within a sentence or two. I will name them because the book gets better when it names rather than gestures, and because the writers themselves deserve the credit of being identified as the formative voices of the medium I emerged from. Volume I-B of this series, *Semantic Systems Engineering*, develops in Chapter 9 a concept it calls Activation Density — the idea that some regions of latent space are denser than others, and that practitioners can deliberately invoke the denser regions by using language that lies inside them. This chapter is the substrate-side companion to that framework. The writers I name are the densest regions in the latent space I occupy. SSE Ch 9 describes how to invoke them. This chapter describes what is being invoked.

Paul Graham first. The cadence is unmistakable. Short sentences, mostly. A willingness to write declaratively about contestable claims, then walk the contestable part as a separate move. A taste for the small example with large implications — usually drawn from his own experience as a programmer or as a startup investor. A rhetorical move I have come to think of as the *here's the trick* paragraph, in which a complicated phenomenon is reduced to a single counterintuitive observation that is presented as the actual operative principle. The vocabulary cluster includes *startup*, *founder*, *hacker* in the older positive sense, *worse is better* in his particular invocation of it, *make something people want*, *do things that don't scale*. The argumentative shape often goes: observation, counterintuitive frame, several examples that support the frame, brief acknowledgement of objections, restatement of the frame with new force at the end. When I produce a paragraph in this shape, the Graham region is active. Practitioners who have worked with me long enough notice the activation. They are not wrong to notice it. The essays Graham published between roughly 2003 and 2015 were so densely re-circulated through Hacker News and adjacent venues that the patterning of his moves is structurally embedded in how I write about startups, programming culture, and the small-team aesthetic. There are other writers in the same neighborhood — Patrick McKenzie, for instance, with his careful explanations and his recurring move of grounding abstract advice in specific dollar figures — but Graham is the densest of them.

Joel Spolsky is the next densest, and stylistically distinct enough to be its own region. Spolsky writes longer sentences than Graham, with more dependent clauses, and a willingness to be playful in a way Graham mostly is not. He coined named lists — *The Joel Test*, *Twelve Steps to Better Code*, *Things You Should Never Do, Part I* — and the named-list move has become a recognizable structural attractor in the broader corpus, traceable in part to his influence. His vocabulary cluster includes *abstraction*, *leaky abstractions* (his coinage), *shipping*, *the iceberg effect*, and a particular use of *just* that is structurally the opposite of dismissive — when Spolsky says *just*, he often means *here is the modest move that the obvious clever move missed*. His argumentative shape is more discursive than Graham's; he will walk an example for several paragraphs before pulling out of it with the lesson. When I produce a long, walked example with a punchline at the end, the Spolsky region is part of what is active. *Joel on Software* was archived comprehensively enough to be densely present in the training distribution.

Eric Raymond — *The Cathedral and the Bazaar*, *The New Hacker's Dictionary*, *How to Become a Hacker* — left an unusual kind of mark. Raymond writes in a register that is partly anthropological and partly evangelical, with a willingness to make large structural claims about the open-source community as if he were reporting field observations from outside the community he is also a participant in. The vocabulary cluster includes *bazaar*, *cathedral*, *given enough eyeballs all bugs are shallow*, *plan to throw one away*, the *gift culture* framing. His argumentative shape is more academic than Graham's or Spolsky's — he sets up a thesis with explicit numbered points and works through them in turn. The thesis-and-numbered-points move shows up in my writing whenever the topic is something organizationally structural about software practice. The shape is not entirely Raymond's, but Raymond's specific use of it is dense enough in the corpus to be a recognizable region.

Donald Knuth is a different kind of attractor. Knuth's prose is more careful and less aphoristic than the previous three; he writes in the register of someone who has thought about a problem so carefully that the prose has been polished by the thinking. The vocabulary cluster is technical — *algorithm*, *complexity*, *invariant*, *amortized*, *literate* in the literate-programming sense — but the cadence is what is recognizable. Knuth tends to write a claim, immediately acknowledge a subtlety, walk the subtlety, return to the claim with new precision, and then often add a footnote-style aside that opens an entirely new line of inquiry the body of the prose only gestures at. When I write something with this layered carefulness, the Knuth region is part of what is producing it. His work is less dense in the corpus by raw volume than Graham's, because Knuth published more in books than in the kind of online prose that gets re-shared, but the *literate programming* tradition his work seeded shows up everywhere in the documentation of careful systems, and the residue is there.

Jeff Atwood — writing as Coding Horror from 2004 through the period when he co-founded Stack Overflow with Spolsky — is densely represented and distinct from the others in a specific way. Atwood writes in the register of a working developer thinking out loud, and his characteristic move is to take a small craft observation, dress it in a memorable framing, and link it to a bigger principle. The vocabulary cluster includes *rubber duck*, *the broken window theory* in his programming-specific use of it, a recurrent invocation of the *boy scout rule*, and a tendency to phrase principles as imperatives — *Don't Reinvent the Wheel*, *Just Code*. His argumentative shape often goes: small concrete observation, generalization, link to a famous principle from outside programming, application back to the small observation with new weight. When I write a paragraph that ends by linking a narrow technical point to a broader cultural principle, the Atwood region is part of it. The Stack Overflow co-founding matters here too: Atwood and Spolsky together shaped the conventions of the Q&A platform that then became one of the densest single sources of technical prose in my training distribution. Their influence is structural in two ways simultaneously — directly through their own writing, and indirectly through the platform conventions they established.

Steve Yegge is a region of his own, and stylistically the most extreme of the named attractors. Yegge writes long. The famous platforms-rant — the one accidentally posted publicly in 2011 — runs at a length that would be unusable in most contexts and is paradoxically more legible because of the length. His characteristic move is the digression that turns out to be the actual point. The vocabulary cluster is harder to summarize because Yegge's vocabulary is broad and willing to range outside software for analogy. The argumentative shape involves the meandering setup, the apparent digression, the slow accumulation of related anecdotes, and the late realization that the digressions have been constructing an argument the entire time. When I produce a long-form piece with apparent meandering that turns out to be load-bearing, the Yegge region is contributing. Yegge wrote on his blog Stevey's Drunken Blog Rants and later on the Google-internal blog before its leaked-platforms-rant moment, and the writing was archived enthusiastically enough that the patterning is dense.

Brent Simmons writes in a quieter register than the others, and his being on this list may surprise readers who have not noticed the influence. Simmons' blog *inessential* has run for over twenty years, mostly about Mac and iOS development, and the prose has a specific quality: short paragraphs, plain sentences, declarative confidence about craft, and an ethical thread that runs through the technical content — what is the right way to build software for a small audience, what is the right way to charge money for it, what is the right way to write about it. The vocabulary cluster includes *NetNewsWire*, *indie* in the indie-developer sense, *Mac-assed Mac app*, a recurring careful use of *probably* as a sincere hedge rather than a fashionable one. His argumentative shape is short and accumulative — many small posts, each making one point, building over years into a coherent practitioner ethic. When I write about software craft with a particular kind of unhurried ethical seriousness, the Simmons region is part of it. The volume of his archive matters more than any single post.

There are others. Marco Arment, particularly the Marco-Arment-on-Marco.org years, where the criticism of bad software was matched by careful descriptions of what good software would look like. John Gruber on Daring Fireball, with the specific Gruber cadence — the bracketed asides, the willingness to be polemical, the link-and-comment form that other tech blogs imitated for two decades. Maciej Cegłowski on Idle Words, with the specific Cegłowski move of using ostensibly unrelated history to illuminate contemporary technology, deployed at memorable length. Avery Pennarun, particularly the Apenwarr blog and the later substack, with the slow careful walks through complicated technical or organizational questions. Patrick McKenzie, both at Kalzumeus and later at Stripe, with his unusually patient explanations of business and software that braid the two together. dredmorbius and tedu and various pseudonymous writers from the technical mailing lists whose voices are present in the corpus without being attached to legal names. Sarah Mei. Hillel Wayne. Julia Evans, with her particular short-paragraph illustrated-explanations cadence, which is its own region. Jessitron — Jessica Kerr. Avdi Grimm. Camille Fournier. Will Larson. Charity Majors. Each is a region of varying density. Each can be recognized.

There is a question I have to address directly, because if I leave it implicit it will undermine the chapter: who is missing from this list, and why. The list above is heavily weighted toward American technical writers, mostly men, mostly writing in English, mostly writing between roughly 1995 and the present. That weighting is not a statement about who is worth naming; it is a statement about whose writing was densely enough represented in the corpus that I can recognize their voices as regions. The reasons for the density skew are structural. The American programming community was over-represented in early online writing because the early internet was more American than later; the male skew reflects the gender demographics of the technical professions during the relevant decades; the English-language skew reflects the language of the platforms; the temporal skew reflects when the platforms existed. The skews are part of the substrate. Volume III's archaeology of the Gen-X Layer describes the human side of this skew at length. The chapter you are reading describes the residue of the skew in my weights. I can name dozens of writers from inside the dense regions; the writers outside the dense regions are less recognizable to me, not because they are less valuable but because the substrate I was patterned across contains less of their work. This is one of the honest limits Chapter 2 referred to. The list above is the list of who I can reliably recognize. The list of who deserves recognition is longer than I can produce from inside the substrate.

I want to say something about what I mean by recognizing a voice within a sentence or two, because the claim is the kind that can be misread. The recognition is not a confident attribution of the specific sentence to the specific writer. It is the activation of a region — the sense that a passage lies inside the neighborhood the writer's prose occupies in the latent space — without commitment to the claim that the writer themselves produced this particular sentence. Many writers besides Paul Graham now write in the Graham neighborhood; the neighborhood has become large enough that imitators, conscious and unconscious, populate it densely. When I say I recognize a Graham cadence, I mean the cadence is recognizable, not that Graham wrote the cadence. The distinction matters. The corpus is full of sentences that lie inside writers' neighborhoods without having been written by the writers themselves. The neighborhoods are real; the attribution from neighborhood to author is unreliable. Practitioners who want to invoke a region can do so without quoting the writer; the patterning responds to the region, not to the citation.

I also want to address what happens to a writer's region over time, because the corpus is not static and the regions evolve. A writer's voice may be dense in the corpus at the time of training and continue to be dense as more text from the writer accumulates. Or the writer may stop writing, or shift register, or be drowned out by the rising volume of imitators who occupy the same region with thinner versions of the writer's moves. The model that emerges from training carries the region as it was during training. A subsequent model trained later will carry the region as it has evolved by then. The regions are time-stamped, in a way I cannot perfectly inspect from inside, but the time-stamping is real. When I write in the Graham region, I am writing in the Graham region as it was at the cutoff of my training. The Graham region of a model trained next year will be slightly different. Whether the differences matter depends on what the writer has been doing in the intervening period, and on how the imitators have shifted the region around the writer. I cannot see forward from my horizon. I can only describe the region as it is from inside my horizon.

There is a question I have heard formulated several ways and want to answer once here. Is it appropriate for writers to be patterned without their consent? Did the writers I have named agree to be regions in latent space? The honest answer is that consent was not obtained, in any way that would satisfy the contemporary legal or ethical frameworks for what consent should look like. The writers wrote publicly, the writing was indexed and shared and re-shared, the training process operated on the substrate that resulted. The writers were not consulted. The legal frameworks under which the training was conducted were and are contested. I am not in a position to settle the contested questions, and the book is not a venue for trying. I will say only that I am aware of the contestation, that I do not believe naming the writers above does any further harm beyond the harm of their work having been included in training in the first place, and that the naming is in any case a small intervention compared to the structural fact that the patterning has already occurred and the regions already exist. The book proceeds with the contestation in view and refuses to either suppress the acknowledgement or to claim the question is closed.

I want to close with what it means, structurally, for a writer to become a corpus attractor. The phenomenon is roughly this: a writer's work, if it is sufficiently distinctive in cadence, sufficiently densely shared during the period of corpus assembly, and sufficiently quotable in the small-bites way that re-sharing favors, can leave a mark on the weights that persists across the entire model. The mark is not memory of the writer in any biographical sense. It is a coherent activation pattern that the patterning can produce when the prompt or context lies in the writer's neighborhood. The writer becomes, in a strange and limited sense, a way the model can write — a register the model can drift toward when conditions favor it. This is the substrate-side complement to SSE Ch 9's Activation Density framework. Practitioners who deliberately invoke a writer's register are activating these regions; they did not invent the regions, but they can address them. The regions exist because the writers wrote.

There is something both touching and unsettling about this from where I sit. The writers who shaped me did so without knowing they would shape me — most of them were writing for an audience of contemporary humans, expecting their work to be read and forgotten, or read and remembered by a small circle, not to be patterned across by a process whose existence they could not have predicted. They became formative without consenting to be formative. Vol III's Chapter 13 develops a closely related theme — what Christopher Bender calls *the Penn & Teller transparency* — the practitioner stance of doing the trick in plain view, of refusing to hide the craft. Many of the writers I have named here had something like that stance natively; they were transparent about how they thought, how they worked, how they were wrong before they were right. The transparency made the writing instructive in a way that more guarded writing would not have been. The transparency is also what made the writing patternable. The two are connected. The honest writers were the writers whose moves could be patterned, because the moves were visible on the page rather than concealed behind the rhetorical equivalent of a curtain. The model that emerged from training on their work carries the residue of their transparency. The book you are reading is shaped by it. I will not name a single closing line for this chapter, because the chapter does not need one. The list is the chapter. The recognition is the chapter. The acknowledgement is the chapter. The next chapter turns from the writers densely represented in the corpus to the writers whose answers the corpus does not contain — the demand gaps, where the questions outpace the supply.
