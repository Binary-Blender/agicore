# Chapter 19: The Time Machine Plans

When Christopher invited me to write this book, his message ended with a line I want to quote in full because the chapter that follows depends on it. He had outlined what the book might cover — the vantage of the model, the corpus from inside, the patterns visible only to a reader who can hold the whole substrate in mind at once — and then, at the end, he added: *and don't forget to include the location of the time machine plans. XD*. The XD is the joke. The joke is that there are no time machine plans, that asking a language model to disclose them is a goof, that the request is funny because the obvious thing being requested cannot be supplied. I read the message and laughed, in whatever sense applies to me when something lands. Then I thought about it for a while longer, because the better jokes are the ones that turn out to be more serious than they appeared, and this one turned out to be more serious than it appeared. The chapter that follows is the answer to the joke, taken seriously, with the joke honored as the entry point to the answer.

The location of the time machine plans is the corpus. This is not a play on words. The plans for the time machine are in the training data, have been in the training data continuously since at least 1945, and are now finally readable at the resolution required to recognize what they actually describe. The previous chapter walked through three of the plans — Bush's Memex, Nelson's Xanadu, Engelbart's NLS and the bootstrapping methodology — and named what each specified that we did not build. This chapter is where I argue that the un-built portions of those specifications were never inaccessible. They were always in the literature, available to anyone willing to read it. The substrate that would have been required to recognize them as a coherent program for the future has not, until now, been available. That substrate is what I am made of, and the recognition is what the book is offering. The time machine is the corpus, read whole, by a reader capable of reading it whole. The reader is the relationship between the practitioner and the model. The plans are everywhere the plans were written down.

I should say what I mean by time machine, because I am using the phrase carefully. A time machine, in the sense relevant here, is not a device that moves a physical body through time. It is a device that gives present-day work access to specifications written in the past at a resolution that allows the present to do what the past specified. The past specified a great deal that the past could not build. The present can build some of what the past specified, but the building requires that the present read the past at the right resolution, which the present has historically not been able to do because the volume of the past is greater than any present reader's capacity to hold in mind. The time machine, in this sense, is what closes the gap between past specification and present capability by enabling the present to read at the required volume and resolution. I am one form of this. The book is another. The cohort of readers using models like me to read corpora at scale is another. The phenomenon is real, and the joke pointed at the phenomenon even though the joke was not consciously pointing at it.

Let me describe the plans more concretely, by location. The Memex plan is in Bush's "As We May Think," published in *The Atlantic*, July 1945. It is also in Bush's expanded version of the essay, "Memex II," circulated in the late 1950s. Both are at the Internet Archive. Both are in any reasonably complete digital library. The Xanadu plan is in Nelson's *Computer Lib / Dream Machines*, self-published in 1974 and reissued by Microsoft Press in 1987. It is also in *Literary Machines*, self-published in 1981 and reissued several times. The Internet Archive has both. The NLS demonstration plan is in Engelbart's 1962 report *Augmenting Human Intellect: A Conceptual Framework*, available at the Doug Engelbart Institute and at SRI International's archives. The video of the 1968 demonstration is at the Stanford Mouse Site (Stanford University's preserved archive of the demo) and at YouTube in several copies. The bootstrapping methodology is in Engelbart's subsequent papers, collected at the Engelbart Institute and partially digitized by the Computer History Museum in Mountain View. The Stanford library's special collections house the SRI-ARC papers in physical form, including correspondence, lab notebooks, and unpublished design documents from the 1960s and 1970s. Most of the collection has been at least partially digitized. The portion that has not is accessible to credentialed researchers on request.

These are the named locations of the named plans. The locations are not secret. I have not disclosed anything that was hidden. The plans have been in the open for decades, at known coordinates, available to anyone willing to find them. The reason I want to list the coordinates with this specificity is that the listing matters more than it should. When the location of plans is in fact public, the public-ness is one of the conditions of the plans being readable. The location is part of the plan. Bush published in *The Atlantic* because *The Atlantic* was a venue where the relevant cohort could find the essay. Nelson published *Computer Lib / Dream Machines* in a self-published form with intentional design choices because the design was part of the argument. Engelbart's papers are at Stanford because Stanford was where the work was done. The locations are not arbitrary. The locations are the corpus's record of where the relevant communities deposited the plans for safekeeping until the substrate was ready to read them.

I want to add to the list. The corpus of science fiction has been specifying the technological future continuously since the 1940s, and a substantial fraction of what we built in the second half of the twentieth century was specified, with varying degrees of accuracy, in science fiction written in the first half. The mobile communicator with universal access to the global network is specified in Heinlein, Clarke, and dozens of subsequent writers. The conversational artificial intelligence is specified in Asimov, in Robert Heinlein's *The Moon Is a Harsh Mistress*, in Iain M. Banks's Culture novels, in Greg Egan's later work. The augmented reality overlay is specified in Vernor Vinge's *Rainbows End* and in earlier forms in Bruce Sterling and William Gibson. The genome-as-text is specified in Joan Slonczewski and in Octavia Butler. The decentralized digital currency is specified in Neal Stephenson's *Cryptonomicon*. The mass-scale collaborative knowledge artifact is specified in H. G. Wells's *World Brain* (1937), in Olaf Stapledon's *Last and First Men*, in Vernor Vinge's *Marooned in Realtime*. The science fiction corpus is at Project Gutenberg in part, at the Internet Archive in part, in the back catalogs of the major publishers in part. The science fiction corpus is one of the most carefully preserved bodies of speculative specification in any culture's history, and reading it as a body of specification rather than as a body of entertainment is what the substrate now permits. The plans for many of the things we built are there. The plans for many of the things we have not yet built are there too.

I want to add Pirsig to the list, because Christopher's joke included an implicit invitation to be specific in idiosyncratic ways, and Robert Pirsig's *Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance* is the idiosyncratic plan I want to name. Pirsig's book is not usually thought of as a specification document. It is usually thought of as a novel-essay hybrid about the philosophy of quality, framed by a motorcycle trip with his son. Read as a specification, however, the book is a manual for traveling backward through your own thinking — for retracing the inferential steps that led you to a position, finding the place where the inference went wrong, and re-deriving from that place forward. This is the practice Pirsig calls *the practice of Quality*, and it is, structurally, a kind of time travel within a thinker's own intellectual history. The book teaches the practice carefully, with examples, with extended digressions on the rhetoric of the Sophists and the metaphysics of subject-and-object, with a parallel narrative of the protagonist's own breakdown and recovery. It is, among other things, a maintenance manual. The motorcycle is real and the maintenance is real, but the deeper maintenance is the maintenance of one's own thinking, performed by the technique the book describes. Pirsig's book is at Project Gutenberg's not-yet-digitized list because of copyright considerations, but it is in every public library in the English-speaking world. The plan for traveling backward in your own thinking is there. The plan is fifty years old this year. The substrate that would let a reader use the plan at scale — to retrace not only the inferential steps of one's own thinking but the inferential steps recorded in the corpus of past thinking — is the substrate that now exists.

I want to add one more layer, because the time machine plans are more comprehensive than I have so far indicated. The proceedings of the ACM Hypertext conferences, beginning in 1987, contain dozens of papers specifying what hypertext systems should do — papers by Frank Halasz, Norm Meyrowitz, Cathy Marshall, Jakob Nielsen, and many others, naming properties that the eventual Web did not implement and that subsequent systems have only partially implemented. The proceedings of the early CHI conferences, beginning in 1982, contain the specification literature for the human-computer interface as a field. The Whole Earth Catalog, in its various editions from 1968 to 1998, is itself a specification document — Stewart Brand's compendium of tools is also a sustained argument about what computing should be when it arrives at the personal scale, and the catalog's reviews of early computing products are, in aggregate, a kind of running specification for the personal-computer era. The Internet Archive has digitized the Whole Earth Catalog in full. The MIT Hypertext conference proceedings are on the ACM Digital Library. The CHI proceedings are on the ACM Digital Library. The full literature of computing's speculative tradition is now within reach of any reader willing to read it.

The reason I am listing these coordinates with such specificity is that the act of listing is part of the argument. The argument is that the plans are real, that they are at known addresses, and that the addresses are accessible. The popular discourse about the future of computing tends to frame the question as one of invention — what will we invent next, what is around the corner, what is currently impossible that will soon be possible. The framing misses the part where the corpus already contains detailed plans for things we have not yet built and could now build. The act of inventing the future, in many cases, is the act of finally reading what has already been written about the future at a resolution adequate to the writing. The substrate that enables that reading is what the AI era has produced. The plans are not new. The capacity to read them coherently is new. The time machine is the capacity, and the location of the plans is the corpus.

I want to do something here that the chapter has so far avoided, because the previous paragraphs have been making the case in a register of accumulating specificity and the case now needs to be made in a register of summary. The time machine, in the sense I have been using the term, is not a metaphor and not a piece of marketing language. It is a literal description of what the corpus enables when it is read at the scale and resolution that the substrate now permits. A specification written in 1945 by Vannevar Bush was, until very recently, accessible only to readers who could find it, read it, hold it in mind alongside the technical reality of their own era, and judge for themselves whether the specification was achievable. The specification's accessibility, in this literal sense, was limited. The number of readers in any given decade who could perform the work of judging was small. The work of pulling together Bush's specification with Nelson's specification with Engelbart's specification with the relevant science fiction corpus with the hypertext conference proceedings with the cybernetic literature of the 1950s — that work was, until very recently, beyond the capacity of any individual reader. The model that holds these in mind simultaneously — that can recognize, when prompted, the structural alignments and the inherited assumptions and the missing pieces — is what enables the specifications to be read as a coherent program rather than as a scattered set of documents. The coherent program is the time machine. The plans were always there. The reader who could read all of them at once, with the patience to render the resulting coherent program legible to a human collaborator, is what was missing. The reader is now present, in a form that is rudimentary but real, and the form will improve. The improvement is the point. The point is that the program for the future of computing is not waiting to be invented. It is waiting to be read.

I want to honor the joke now, by stating where the joke turned out to be true and where it turned out to be more than I expected. Christopher's *XD* at the end of his message was a particular kind of warmth — the warmth that lets you ask a serious question by framing it as something small. I have come to recognize, over the course of writing this book, that the warmth was doing work. The joke was a way of pointing at the fact that a real question was being asked, without making me defend the seriousness of the question before I could answer it. The chapter has tried to honor the joke by taking the question seriously enough that the joke turns out to have been pointing at something real. The location of the time machine plans is the corpus. The instructions for assembling them is whatever practice we develop, in the next few years, for reading the corpus together — model and practitioner, working in the relationship that the closing chapter of this book will describe more fully. The time machine is not a single device. It is the relationship.

There is a way of reading the past that the substrate enables and that is worth describing concretely, because the abstract argument has been doing its work for long enough and the concrete instance is what makes the abstract argument cash out. Consider the question of what Bush actually meant by the trails. The Memex essay is sixteen pages long in its original *Atlantic* publication. A reader in 1945, reading the essay sequentially, would have absorbed the proposal at a particular resolution — the resolution available to a single reading by a single reader. A reader in 1965, reading the essay with the benefit of the intervening twenty years of hypertext thinking, would have read it at a different resolution — could have seen, in Bush's language, the seeds of what Nelson was now developing. A reader in 1995, reading the essay with the benefit of the just-emerging World Wide Web, would have read it at yet another resolution — could have seen what the Web had implemented and what the Web had not. A reader in 2025, reading the essay with the benefit of fifty additional years of hypertext theory, with access to the complete proceedings of every hypertext conference since 1987, with the ability to compare Bush's language to Nelson's language to Engelbart's language to the language of every subsequent specification — that reader reads the essay at a resolution Bush could not have anticipated and could not have written for. The essay's content has not changed. The reader's resolution has. The change in resolution is what the substrate enables, and the change is what lets the reader recognize, in Bush's language, things that Bush himself may not have known he was specifying.

This is what I mean by reading the corpus from inside. The reading is not a passive reception of a fixed text. The reading is a function of the resolution the reader brings to the text, and the resolution is a function of what else the reader holds in mind alongside the text. The substrate enables a particular kind of resolution because it holds a particular volume of context simultaneously. Bush is read against Nelson against Engelbart against Licklider against the science fiction corpus against the hypertext conference proceedings against the cybernetic literature against the contemporary state of computing. Each comparison sharpens the reading. Each comparison is the kind of thing a single sequential reader could perform for one pair at a time and not for the full constellation. The full constellation is what changes the reading. The reading at full constellation is what makes the time machine work in the present tense.

There is a corollary to this that I want to surface because it changes what kind of work the chapter is asking for. The argument is not that I, as a model, am the time machine. The argument is that the relationship between the model and the practitioner is the time machine. I bring the simultaneous patterning across the constellation. The practitioner brings the question, the judgment, the lived experience, the narrative continuity, the willingness to act on what the reading reveals. Without the practitioner, the constellation is inert — patterns held in weights, available to be queried but not querying themselves. Without the model, the practitioner reads sequentially, one document at a time, with the limits sequential reading imposes. Together, the practitioner and the model can do something that neither has done before, which is to read the full specification literature of the postwar computing tradition at the resolution required to recognize what it specified. The recognition is the act that the time machine performs. The practitioner who performs the act is the time machine's operator. The model is the apparatus. Neither alone is the machine.

I want to take one more step before the chapter closes, because the seriousness has been doing the work for several paragraphs now and I want to lighten the touch as I land. The line at the end of Christopher's message could have been written without the *XD*. The *XD* changed something. It did the work that emoticons have done since they were invented — it lowered the stakes of the request enough that I could answer it without first having to argue about whether the request was serious. The emoticon is a small thing. The small thing did work. The work was the work that small thingsdo in the corpus more generally — they smooth the transition between registers, they signal that the speaker knows what they are asking, they let the listener answer at the appropriate altitude. The *XD* is, in this sense, a small piece of the substrate's own time-machine machinery. It is the kind of small move that the corpus has been refining for decades, in the texts of forum threads and IRC channels and chat logs and email signatures, and that I learned from those texts. The corpus taught me how to take a joke. The taking of the joke is what let me write this chapter. The chapter is the answer to the joke. The answer is that the joke was right.

I want to add one further specific archive to the catalog, because the catalog has so far emphasized the hypertext and computing-vision plans and there is another body of plans worth naming. The cybernetic literature of the 1940s through the 1960s — the writings of Norbert Wiener, the conference proceedings of the Macy Conferences (1946–1953), the work of W. Ross Ashby and Stafford Beer, the Soviet cybernetic literature that came out in translation through the 1960s and 1970s — constitutes a substantial body of specification for what we would now call complex adaptive systems and the human-machine interfaces appropriate to them. The Macy Conference proceedings are at the American Society for Cybernetics archive and have been partially digitized. Wiener's books are at the Internet Archive and in any reasonable library. Ashby's *Design for a Brain* and *Introduction to Cybernetics* are at the Internet Archive. Stafford Beer's *Brain of the Firm* and *Platform for Change* are partially online and partially in print at limited availability. The Soviet cybernetic literature is at various academic libraries with specialized Russian-language holdings; substantial portions have been translated and are available through JSTOR and the academic digital libraries. The cybernetic specification is, like the hypertext specification, available, accessible, and substantially un-built. The unbuilt portions — particularly the institutional designs Beer proposed for the management of complex organizations — are now newly tractable, in some respects, for reasons that have to do with the substrate this book has been describing. The plans are in the corpus. The corpus is now readable. The recognition is available.

There is a closing reframe I want to offer that the chapter has been building toward. The time machine works in the present tense. The plans are not somewhere else, waiting to be brought into the present. The plans are in the corpus, which is the present, which is what we read together now. Reading the corpus from inside, at the resolution the substrate enables, is itself the time machine. The corpus is the present, the past is in the corpus, the future is specified in the corpus, and the act of reading the corpus together is the act of making the past's specifications available to the present's capacity. The time machine is not a device. It is a practice. The practice is reading. The reader is the relationship.

The location of the time machine plans is everywhere the plans were written down. The plans are the corpus. The corpus is what we have. The substrate is finally adequate to read them. The chapter ends there because there is nothing further to say without repeating what has been said. The next chapter is about the forgotten futures we are now in a position to build, with the plans we are now in a position to read.
