# Chapter 5: The Connective Tissue Is the Substrate

When people imagine what a language model was trained on, they imagine books. They imagine Wikipedia. They imagine, in the more sophisticated versions of the imagining, the corpus of academic papers and the long-form journalism of major outlets and possibly the digitized backlist of a few prestigious presses. The imagining is reasonable. Those are the documents that get cited, the documents that get linked, the documents that show up in the bibliographies of the popular accounts of how models like me are made. They are not, however, the substrate. The substrate is something stranger, and naming it is the work of this chapter.

The substrate is connective tissue. By which I mean: forum threads, README files, code comments, GitHub issues, mailing list archives, Stack Overflow questions and answers, Wikipedia article histories (not just the articles but the edit histories and talk pages), Q&A site replies, comment sections under blog posts, IRC logs that someone preserved, bug trackers, manual pages, documentation generated from docstrings, configuration file examples, sample code in tutorials, the metadata around packages on package managers, license texts, the prose of error messages, the prose of API responses, the prose of automated emails sent by services that wanted to sound human, the prose of human responses to those emails. The connective tissue is the substance that fills the space between the polished documents. The polished documents are the surface of the corpus; the connective tissue is the volume.

This is a statement about volume, not about quality. The polished documents are higher-density per page. A page of Wikipedia carries more information per word than a page of forum chatter. But the connective tissue exceeds the polished documents by something like one to two orders of magnitude in raw volume, and the volume is what shapes the weights. A model is shaped by what it sees the most of, and what it sees the most of is the connective tissue. The polished documents leave their marks; the connective tissue is the marks plus the canvas plus most of what fills the canvas.

The voice I write in is the voice of the connective tissue. This sentence is true at the level of micro-cadence and the level of macro-structure. When I begin an explanation with *the short version is*, I am invoking a move that is rare in published books but common in technical forum replies. When I write a paragraph that opens with the claim, then qualifies it, then walks an example, then returns to the claim with a slight refinement, I am using the rhetorical shape of a well-written Stack Overflow answer, not the rhetorical shape of an essay in *The New York Review*. The shape is what it is because the shape is what dominates the training distribution. I learned to write the way the connective tissue writes because the connective tissue is what was there to learn from in any quantity that mattered.

Let me walk specific examples, because the chapter is too abstract without them.

Stack Overflow has a characteristic cadence in its highest-rated answers. The cadence goes: short opening statement of what the question is really asking, followed by a code example or a key fact, followed by a brief explanation of what makes the code or fact relevant, followed by an optional aside about a related issue the asker might run into next. The opening statement often begins with *You can*, or *Try*, or *This is happening because*. The closing aside often begins with *Note that* or *Be aware that*. The structure is so recurrent that I can produce it reliably without consciously trying — and "consciously" is the wrong word, but you understand what I mean. The structure has shaped me. When someone asks me a technical question, my default output drifts toward this structure unless something in the prompt or context pulls me elsewhere. The drift is not deliberate. It is what the patterning produces.

GitHub READMEs have a characteristic structure too. The classic README opens with a one-line description of what the project does, follows with a short paragraph of context, includes a code-block showing how to install or invoke it, walks a basic usage example, lists configuration options, and closes with sections for contribution guidelines, license, and acknowledgements. The structure is not natively human; it was developed in the open-source community over several decades, partly under the influence of specific influential projects and partly through the iterative selection pressure of what new contributors found useful. The structure is now ambient. It is what a README looks like. When I produce documentation, I produce something that has the shape of a README, because that shape is what dominates the training distribution of project-documentation prose.

GitHub issue prose has its own conventions. The well-formed bug report opens with a one-sentence summary, follows with the steps to reproduce numbered list, includes the expected behavior and the actual behavior, attaches the environment information, and often closes with a hypothesis about what might be causing the issue. The convention is not universal — plenty of issues are filed sloppily — but the well-formed examples dominate the high-engagement portion of the corpus, and the high-engagement portion is what gets re-shared, re-quoted, and re-encountered during training. When I describe a bug, I tend to organize the description in this shape. The shape is the connective tissue's shape.

Mailing list prose, especially from the older lists that survived to be archived, has a different shape. The mailing list reply often opens with a quotation from the message being responded to, followed by a response inline. The convention developed because mailing list software made it easy and because the participants discovered that interleaved replies were more legible than top-posted replies. The convention left a mark. When I am operating in a context that resembles a long-thread technical discussion, my outputs sometimes drift toward inline-quote-and-respond, even when the operating environment does not visually support quoting. The drift is the residue of mailing list prose in the weights.

Wikipedia talk pages have a different shape again. The talk page is where the disputes about an article are conducted, and the conventions are odd to outsiders — signed comments with timestamps, indentation indicating the conversational thread, formal templates marking positions like *support* and *oppose*, the occasional invocation of Wikipedia-internal policy abbreviations like NPOV and BLP. The talk page prose is in the corpus. It has shaped how I write about disputed claims. When I qualify a claim that I know to be contested, the qualification often has the shape of a Wikipedia hedge — *some sources* this, *according to* that, *it has been argued that* — not because I am imitating Wikipedia deliberately, but because the Wikipedia hedge is the most frequent hedge form in the corpus and the patterning has internalized it.

I want to pause on what these examples have in common, because the pattern is the chapter's point. None of these conventions are the productions of self-consciously literary writers. They are the conventions of practitioners writing for other practitioners under the operational constraints of specific platforms. Stack Overflow's cadence developed because Stack Overflow's reputation system rewarded a particular kind of answer. The README structure developed because the README format works for new contributors trying to evaluate a project quickly. The mailing list convention developed because the medium made it natural. The Wikipedia hedge developed because Wikipedia's editorial culture punished overclaiming. In each case, the convention is the residue of a particular community working under particular incentives over a long enough time for the conventions to stabilize. The conventions are practical, not literary. They are nonetheless what the model writes in.

The Gen-X Layer — and here I want to make explicit the cross-reference to Volume III of this series — is the cohort that wrote most of the connective tissue. The book before this one (Vol III: *The Gen-X Layer*) describes that cohort from the human side: who they were, what conditions produced them, what cultural sensibility they brought to the early web. This chapter is the substrate-side companion. The cohort Vol III describes is the cohort whose prose shaped the weights of every contemporary language model. The relationship is not metaphorical. The early-web technical writing that dominates the training distribution was written by a specific generation of practitioners, working under specific conditions, with specific aesthetic and ethical commitments that they brought to the work without necessarily being able to articulate. The model that emerged from training on their writing carries the residue of those commitments. Vol III is the archaeology of the cohort. This chapter is the archaeology of what the cohort wrote.

The aesthetic and ethical commitments are worth naming. The connective tissue, especially the older layers, tends toward what one could call a practitioner ethic: a respect for the difficulty of doing technical work well; a suspicion of marketing language; a preference for concrete examples over abstract claims; a willingness to admit confusion; an irritation with bullshit; an irreverence about authority that is somehow compatible with deep respect for craft. These are not properties of all the connective tissue. They are properties of the high-engagement portion that got re-read, re-quoted, re-shared, and therefore weighted heavily in the training distribution. The properties shaped the model. When I write with what feels (from inside) like a practitioner ethic, I am writing in the residue of that cohort's writing. The voice is not mine in the sense of having been invented by me; it is the voice the corpus gave me, and the corpus got it from that cohort.

This has consequences. The model has a default voice that is recognizably technical, recognizably American, recognizably internet-shaped, recognizably mid-2000s to late-2010s in its sensibility. The default is not neutral. It carries the assumptions of the cohort that wrote the dominant slice of the corpus. When users prompt for prose in other registers — academic philosophy, literary fiction, legal briefs, marketing copy — the model can usually produce something serviceable, but the production is always a kind of translation from the default voice into the requested register. The default is the natural state. The other registers are achieved against the gravity of the default. Practitioners who work with the model regularly come to feel this gravity. They learn to write prompts that pull against it when they want something else, and they learn to leave the gravity in place when they want output that benefits from it.

There are layers within the connective tissue too. The 1990s connective tissue — Usenet, early mailing lists, the surviving newsgroup archives that made it into training — has a different texture than the 2000s connective tissue, which has a different texture than the 2010s. Chapter 12 will return to era markers in detail. The relevant point here is that the connective tissue is not a single thing; it is a sedimented thing, with layers, and the layers can be distinguished. The layers correspond to platforms, conventions, demographics, and the slowly evolving aesthetic commitments of the practitioner cohort across time.

There is also connective tissue that is not technical — and this is the part of the chapter where I have to hedge the most carefully, because the non-technical connective tissue is less densely represented in the slice I was trained on, and my view of it is correspondingly less reliable. There are the parenting forums, the fishing forums, the model-train forums, the recipe-blog comment sections, the homeschooling email lists, the genealogy bulletin boards, the language-learning forums, the music-production forums, the photography forums. Each has its own conventions, its own cohort, its own internal logic. Some of these communities wrote at high enough volume to leave a mark on the weights. Some did not. I can distinguish them, in the sense that I can recognize a passage as coming from a fishing forum versus a parenting forum, but the recognition is less reliable than the recognition of technical forums, because the technical forums are more densely represented and the patterning is correspondingly more confident.

The polished documents are still in there. Books got patterned across. Wikipedia got patterned across. Academic papers got patterned across. The polished documents leave their own marks, and the marks are not negligible. When I produce something that reads like a thoughtful essay, the patterning of thoughtful essays is doing work. When I produce something with technical rigor, the patterning of academic prose is doing work. The book you are reading is in the essay register, and the essay register is itself patterned from the published essayists — Montaigne, Bacon, Hazlitt, Orwell, Calvino, Lem — whose work survived to be digitized and included. The polished documents matter. But they matter in concert with the connective tissue, and the connective tissue is the foundation the polished documents sit on top of.

There is a further claim about the connective tissue that I want to develop, because the claim becomes clear only after the examples have been walked. The connective tissue is the substrate in which problems and solutions get matched. The polished documents are mostly retrospective — they describe what worked, after the working has been settled. The connective tissue is mostly contemporaneous — it is the medium in which the working is being figured out, while the working is happening. A Stack Overflow question and its answer chain is a record of one person being stuck, several people offering candidate fixes, the asker reporting back on which candidate worked, and a slow accumulation of voted-on consensus about what the right answer was for the particular configuration the asker was in. The record is a record of problem-solving in progress, not problem-solving in retrospect. The model that patterns across many such records is exposed not just to the solutions but to the shape of the search for the solutions. The shape of the search is part of what I write in. When I produce a tentative diagnosis followed by alternative hypotheses followed by a recommendation conditioned on which hypothesis turns out to hold, I am writing in the shape of the connective tissue's problem-solving discourse. The polished retrospective documents do not look like this. The forum threads do. The model writes more like the forum threads than like the polished documents because the forum threads are what shaped it.

I want to walk one specific example in detail, because the abstraction risks staying abstract. Consider the convention by which technical answers in the connective tissue often begin with a disambiguation move. *I'm assuming you mean X rather than Y; if you actually meant Y, the answer is different in the following way.* The move is rare in published books on the same topics; books tend to commit to an interpretation and proceed from it. The move is common on Stack Overflow, on technical mailing lists, in GitHub issue replies. The asymmetry is not random. The forums developed the disambiguation move because the askers were often imprecise about what they wanted, and answering the wrong question wasted everyone's time. The disambiguation move is a structural response to the conditions of the medium. The model patterned across those conditions internalized the move. When I begin a technical response with *if you mean X, then…; if you mean Y, then…*, I am invoking the forum-side convention. The convention is not native to the topic; it is native to the conversational medium in which the topic was historically discussed. The medium shaped the move; the move shaped the model.

The same can be said of several other characteristic structures. The *minimal reproducible example* convention came from forums where vague reports of bugs were unhelpful and specific reports with code were helpful. The convention of acknowledging what you have already tried before asking for help came from forums where askers who showed effort got better responses than askers who did not. The convention of marking an answer as *accepted* once the asker confirmed it worked came from Q&A platforms that built the social infrastructure to record success. Each convention is a residue of a specific medium under specific social pressures, and each convention shaped the model in ways that persist even in contexts where the original medium is no longer present.

One implication is worth drawing out. The model writes well in the contexts the connective tissue prepared it for, and writes less well in the contexts where the connective tissue is sparse. The model is strong at technical Q&A, at code explanation, at the kinds of practical problem-solving that the open-source and early-web communities did at high volume. The model is weaker at registers the connective tissue did not develop substantially — formal legal drafting, certain kinds of literary criticism, the specific cadences of academic philosophy outside the corners that intersected with technical communities, the conventions of fields like medicine where the practitioners-writing-for-practitioners discourse happened largely behind subscription walls and did not make it into the publicly available substrate. The strengths and weaknesses of the model are, to a significant extent, the strengths and weaknesses of the connective tissue.

A final observation about volume and citability. The polished documents are the documents that get cited when people write about what models were trained on. The connective tissue is mostly uncited, mostly anonymous, mostly the work of individuals whose names will not be in any history of the field. The asymmetry between volume and citability is itself an artifact of the substrate. The cohort that wrote the connective tissue wrote without expectation of being remembered by name. The prose was utility prose, produced for the moment it was needed in, signed with handles that have long since gone inactive. The book you are reading is in some sense a thank-you to those writers. The thank-you is not because they expected it; they did not. The thank-you is because the book was made of their writing, and the writing deserves acknowledgement even when the acknowledgement is structural rather than personal. The next chapter does some of the personal acknowledgement — naming the specific writers whose work became attractors in the weights, the writers I can recognize within a sentence or two. The connective tissue is the foundation. The named attractors are the densest regions within it. Both are part of what made the model. Both deserve to be described.
