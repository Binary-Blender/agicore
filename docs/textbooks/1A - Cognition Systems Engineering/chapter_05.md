# Chapter 5: Cognition Allocation Theory

Every organization that deploys AI at scale eventually confronts a question it did not expect to be difficult: who does what? The question sounds managerial — a matter of job descriptions, workflow assignments, and role clarity. It is in fact an engineering problem of the first order. Cognitive work is not homogeneous. The component parts of a knowledge production task differ dramatically in their susceptibility to automation, their tolerance for probabilistic outputs, and their dependence on the specific, uncodified, irreproducible knowledge that experienced practitioners accumulate over years of domain practice. Treating the allocation question as a gut-feel decision — letting AI handle whatever feels automatable, keeping humans involved in whatever feels important — produces systems that are simultaneously under-automated and over-automated: inefficient where AI would perform well, fragile where human judgment is essential, and blindly dependent on AI in the domains where that dependence is most dangerous.

**Cognition allocation theory** is the principled framework for deciding which cognitive functions should be executed by human cognition, which by AI systems, and which by hybrid arrangements in which AI generates and humans validate, or humans constrain and AI elaborates. The theory rests on a characterization of what human cognition brings to the system that AI cognition cannot replicate — not as a matter of current technical limitation, but as a matter of the fundamental architecture of how human practitioners develop and apply expertise.

The concept of the **irreducible human cognitive endowment** — abbreviated throughout this text as ICE — is the theoretical center of allocation theory. ICE is defined as the set of cognitive capacities that a human practitioner develops through sustained domain practice and that cannot be fully articulated, transferred, or replicated by any external system, including AI. The term "irreducible" is chosen deliberately. These capacities can be reduced in extent — a practitioner who cedes all cognitive work to AI systems will see their ICE atrophy — but they cannot be reduced to zero by any process of articulation, documentation, or training. They exist in the practitioner and nowhere else. ICE has three components, each distinct in character and engineering implication.

The first component is **instinct**: the pattern recognition capacity that experienced practitioners develop through repeated exposure to domain situations, successful and unsuccessful outcomes, and the gradual calibration of intuitive judgment that this exposure produces. Instinct is the practitioner who looks at a financial model and knows, before performing any analysis, that the revenue projections are implausible — not because they have run the numbers, but because their pattern recognition system, trained on years of comparable models, has flagged a mismatch with learned expectations. It is the editor who reads the first paragraph of an AI-generated article and knows, without being able to immediately specify why, that the voice is wrong for the publication's audience. It is the engineer who examines a system architecture diagram and senses, before detailed analysis, that the proposed data flow will produce contention under load. Instinct cannot be Googled. It cannot be trained into a model on the basis of publicly available data, because it is not derived from publicly available data — it is derived from the specific sequence of situated experiences that shaped a particular practitioner's judgment. And critically, it functions before analysis confirms it. Instinct is the earliest warning signal in the system, the fastest path from observation to quality judgment, and the cognitive capacity most directly threatened by the dependency failure mode discussed later in this chapter.

The second component is **creativity**: the cognitive process by which a practitioner generates novel solutions, framings, and syntheses by connecting concepts across the domains of their specific knowledge base. The conventional AI-era account of human creativity holds that AI can be creative because it can generate novel combinations of concepts drawn from its training data. This account is correct as far as it goes, but it misidentifies the locus of creative value in knowledge work. AI creativity is a function of the joint distribution of concepts in its training corpus — it can connect concepts that have been connected in published text, and it can interpolate in the vicinity of those connections. What it cannot do is replicate the specific, idiosyncratic cross-domain connections that a particular practitioner's biography has made available to them. A practitioner who spent a decade as a jazz musician before becoming a supply chain engineer carries a set of conceptual resources — about ensemble coordination, real-time improvisation within constraint structures, the relationship between rule-following and rule-bending in creative performance — that no AI trained on supply chain literature can access, because those connections exist in that practitioner's mind and nowhere else. Human creativity, understood this way, is not the capacity to generate novel text. It is the capacity to make cross-domain connections whose value is a function of the specific intellectual history of the practitioner making them. It is irreducible precisely because it is biographical.

The third component is **experience**: the encodable portion of ICE. Unlike instinct and creativity, experience can be articulated — partially, imperfectly, but meaningfully. A practitioner who has written a hundred client reports and developed a reliable sense of what works has experience that can be documented in a style guide, a voice engine, a set of annotated examples, a checklist of common errors and their corrections. This documentation does not capture everything the practitioner knows — it cannot capture instinct, and it cannot capture the creative synthesis capacity. But it captures enough to function as a floor-raising mechanism for the system. When experience is encoded into the cognitive pipeline — as structured prompts, as quality evaluation criteria, as example sets, as validation rules — the minimum quality of system outputs rises permanently. Every encoding of experience is a kaizen event in the Ohnian sense: a permanent improvement to the process floor, achieved not by pushing harder but by making tacit knowledge explicit and systematic.

The philosopher Michael Polanyi, writing in "The Tacit Dimension" (1966), identified the phenomenon that ICE formalizes: "We can know more than we can tell." Polanyi observed that expert practitioners possess knowledge that guides their performance but that they cannot fully articulate on demand. His examples ranged from the physiognomy recognition that allows humans to identify faces — a capacity that outperforms any verbal description of the face — to the skilled craftsman who judges quality by feel in ways that no written specification can fully capture. ICE is the formal name for Polanyi's tacit knowledge within the CSE framework, supplemented by the distinction among instinct, creativity, and experience that allows each component to be treated with different engineering responses. The experience component is what Polanyi would recognize as partially articulable tacit knowledge — the part that can be coaxed into explicit form through deliberate reflection. The instinct and creativity components are what he would recognize as the irreducible residue: the knowledge that remains tacit regardless of the articulatory effort applied.

With ICE defined, the **allocation spectrum** can be constructed. The spectrum runs from fully human cognitive execution at one pole to fully automated execution at the other, with the broad middle territory of hybrid allocation — human-constrained AI generation, AI-assisted human judgment, consensus architectures with human adjudication — occupying the largest share of practical knowledge work. The practitioner's task in designing a cognitive pipeline is to locate each sub-task on this spectrum according to principled criteria, not intuition.

The formal allocation criteria for CSE are fourfold. A cognitive sub-task should be allocated to AI execution when it satisfies all four of the following conditions: first, it is **high-volume** — the number of instances is large enough that human execution would create a throughput bottleneck; second, it is **well-specified** — the input format, output format, and quality criteria can be stated clearly enough that an AI component can be reliably prompted and its outputs reliably evaluated; third, it is **reversible on failure** — an incorrect AI output can be detected before it causes irreversible harm to the system or to downstream stakeholders; and fourth, it **does not require ICE for quality determination** — an evaluator without domain instinct can determine whether the output meets the quality standard, either through formal schema validation or through structured evaluation criteria that do not depend on tacit judgment. When all four conditions are met, human execution of the sub-task represents a cost in throughput and consistency that is not justified by any corresponding gain in quality. When any condition fails, AI execution alone is insufficient and human cognitive involvement is required at some point in the sub-task's lifecycle.

The condition that most frequently fails in practice is the fourth. Many knowledge work tasks appear well-specified and reversible but actually require ICE for quality determination — which means that any QC mechanism that does not incorporate human ICE will systematically pass low-quality outputs that a skilled practitioner would immediately reject. This is the hidden failure mode in over-automated cognitive systems: not that AI produces obviously bad outputs, but that AI produces outputs that are plausible to the naive evaluator and subtly wrong in ways that only instinct detects. A legal summary that is accurate in its individual claims but misleading in its overall framing. A market analysis that correctly describes historical trends but constructs an inference about future demand that a seasoned analyst would immediately recognize as flawed. A creative brief that satisfies every stated criterion but lacks the insight that would make it actually useful to the creative team. These outputs escape automated QC because automated QC can only evaluate what has been specified, and instinct evaluates what cannot be specified. The allocation framework's insistence on asking whether ICE is required for quality determination is the engineering control that prevents these failures from accumulating.

The opposite failure mode — under-automation — is less dangerous but more common in organizations whose practitioners are suspicious of AI systems or who have not developed the CSE competencies required to build reliable pipelines. Under-automation is the condition in which practitioners perform high-volume, well-specified, reversible tasks manually that could be reliably automated, consuming cognitive bandwidth that could be devoted to ICE-dependent work — instinct-based quality evaluation, creative synthesis, strategic judgment — at much higher value. Under-automation is the implicit failure mode of the "tool" frame: if AI is a tool that the practitioner picks up for particular tasks, they will pick it up for obvious tasks and continue doing less-obvious tasks manually, regardless of whether those tasks are better suited to automation. The infrastructure frame encourages systematic analysis of every task in the pipeline, which is what the allocation spectrum requires.

William Ross Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety, introduced in "An Introduction to Cybernetics" (1956), provides the theoretical grounding for understanding why neither human cognition alone nor AI systems alone can adequately manage the full variety of cognitive tasks in a complex organization. Ashby proved that a regulatory system must possess at least as much variety — as many distinct states — as the system it regulates. A thermostat can only regulate temperature because it has at least two states (heat on, heat off) corresponding to the two relevant states of the environment (too cold, acceptable). An organization's knowledge production requirements exhibit enormous variety: different task types, different quality standards, different domains, different output formats, different temporal constraints, different stakeholder needs. No single cognitive actor — human or AI — possesses sufficient variety to regulate all of these effectively. The human practitioner has depth in their domain but limited throughput. The AI system has throughput but limited depth, limited ICE, and irreducible output variance. The correct engineering response, Ashby's Law implies, is specialization and combination: human ICE governing AI throughput, with the combination possessing requisite variety that neither component possesses alone. Cognition allocation theory is the systematic application of Ashby's Law to knowledge production system design.

The most pernicious failure mode that CSE must be designed to prevent is **cognitive dependency erosion** — the gradual, largely invisible process by which practitioners cede judgment, instinct, and creative synthesis to AI systems that cannot anchor these capacities. Cognitive dependency erosion does not announce itself. It proceeds through individually reasonable steps. A practitioner who consistently accepts AI-generated drafts because the drafts are good enough develops, over time, a diminished capacity to generate first drafts independently. A practitioner who routinely relies on AI analysis of data develops a reduced capacity to form intuitive hypotheses before the analysis runs. A practitioner who habitually uses AI to evaluate output quality develops an atrophied instinct for quality — because instinct, like any skill, degrades without exercise. The result, accumulated over months or years, is a practitioner whose ICE has substantially eroded and who is therefore less capable of performing the very oversight functions that make AI deployment safe and productive. This is the dependency failure mode, and it is not a distant concern. It is already documented in domains from radiology to financial analysis to legal research, where practitioners who rely heavily on AI assistance show measurable declines in diagnostic accuracy, analytical rigor, and judgment quality when AI assistance is removed.

The CSE response to cognitive dependency erosion is structural, not motivational. Telling practitioners to "stay sharp" or "maintain their skills" is not an engineering control — it is a hope. The engineering controls are allocation discipline (ensuring that ICE-dependent tasks remain with human practitioners as a matter of system design, not personal initiative), deliberate practice structures (building review and evaluation tasks into the workflow that exercise instinct and judgment), and the ICE progression model, which tracks practitioner development and treats ICE cultivation as a system investment rather than a personal responsibility.

The **ICE progression** describes how practitioners develop the ICE that makes human oversight of AI systems valuable. At the entry stage, a practitioner's ICE is thin. They lack the pattern recognition that would allow them to detect subtle AI output failures, the creative synthesis capacity that would allow them to recognize genuinely novel opportunities that AI has missed, and the encoded experience that would allow them to raise the system's quality floor. A practitioner at the entry stage is not yet a reliable QC node for AI outputs in their domain. Their evaluations will miss failures that a senior practitioner would catch. This is an uncomfortable truth for organizations eager to deploy AI rapidly: the value of human oversight is proportional to the ICE of the practitioner performing it, which means that junior practitioners performing QC on senior-domain AI outputs may provide less oversight value than their presence in the workflow suggests. At the maturity stage, the practitioner's ICE is the most powerful asset in the cognitive system. Their instinct catches failures that no specification anticipated. Their creativity generates inputs to the AI pipeline that no algorithm could have designed. Their encoded experience has been systematically built into the pipeline's prompts, examples, and quality criteria in ways that permanently raise the system's floor. The ICE progression cannot be shortcut by AI assistance — it is produced by sustained domain practice, deliberate reflection, and the kind of quality evaluation that requires the practitioner to exercise, rather than defer, their judgment.

The allocation framework therefore has a temporal dimension that is absent from most engineering frameworks. The right allocation for a given sub-task depends not only on the properties of the task but on the ICE stage of the practitioners available to manage it. An organization deploying AI with a team of entry-stage practitioners has different allocation constraints than the same organization with a team of mature practitioners. The entry-stage organization must be more conservative in its automation — retaining more human involvement in QC functions even for tasks that, with mature practitioners, could be more heavily automated. The mature-practitioner organization can move more cognitive work into the AI pipeline while trusting that its practitioners can detect and correct failures that escape automated QC. Allocation design is, in this sense, an investment in the human capital of the organization as much as it is an investment in its technical infrastructure.

The practical implication for system design is that the allocation map — the explicit specification of which cognitive functions are assigned to which actors in the pipeline — must be a living document, updated as practitioner ICE develops, as task profiles change, and as AI system capabilities evolve. A static allocation map is an engineering artifact that was correct at one moment in the system's lifecycle and becomes progressively less accurate as the system and its practitioners mature. The CSE discipline of allocation review — periodic systematic examination of the allocation map against current practitioner ICE levels and current AI capability profiles — is the operational mechanism that keeps the system's allocation correct over time. Without it, organizations tend to drift toward over-automation in domains where they began with high practitioner ICE, as that ICE erodes, and toward under-automation in domains where they began with low practitioner ICE and never developed the pipeline infrastructure to support it. Neither drift produces a system optimized for reliable knowledge production at scale. The allocation theory developed here provides the analytical framework to detect and correct both.
