# Chapter 1: Mudhouse

The 2018 Toyota Corolla had a small persistent rattle in the rear passenger-side wheel well that Dax had been planning to address since the second week of September.

He had not addressed it because, on his honest internal accounting at sixteen years old, the rattle did not affect the driving and the driving was the work.

The Corolla had been his since the fourth of October of Year Sixteen, a year ago last Wednesday, when his father had bought it for him with the difference between Carl's old wage and the new wage Carl had been making since Year Fifteen as night-shift floor lead at the Walmart distribution center in Cabool. The car was silver. It had a hundred and seventy-eight thousand miles on the odometer. It had been previously owned by a woman in Mountain Grove who had bought it new in 2018, driven it on a regular twenty-mile commute to a credit union in West Plains for seven years, and then traded it in at the dealer in Springfield where Carl had found it on Facebook Marketplace and had driven up at six AM on a Saturday morning to look at it.

Dax was driving the Corolla now.

It was nine eighteen AM Central on a Saturday morning in the second week of October of Year Seventeen.

The dashboard clock read nine eighteen.

Highway 65 north of Bolivar was the kind of highway that had not changed in fifty years — two lanes of cracked asphalt running through low rolling hills of brown October pasture, a few small farms with hand-painted For Sale signs at the gate, a Casey's General Store every fifteen miles, and a sky that was the kind of pale Ozark blue October sometimes opens onto for a single weekend before the gray sets in for the season.

Dax had the radio off.

He had been driving the route from Mountain View to Springfield once a week since his sixteenth birthday in May. The route ran from County Road 211 to Highway 17 to Highway 60 to Highway 65, then north into the city. The drive was two hours and eleven minutes if traffic was clean. It was usually clean.

The drive was, on his standing operational practice on Saturday mornings, the time he used to think about what he was going to work on at Mudhouse.

Today he was going to work on the parser.

The parser was a small open-source contribution he had been writing for the Synmatic's specification-language project across the previous six weeks. The parser improvement was, in operational substance, a way to make Synmatic's DSL handle a particular kind of nested constraint expression that had been giving trouble to three of the Plant Cognition Engineers in Synmatic's user-feedback channel since August. Dax had volunteered to write the fix because, on Christopher's standing assignment policy for the apprentice Cognition Systems Engineers, the engineers who volunteered for community-feedback fixes were the engineers Christopher trusted to do the work.

Dax was, in operational reality at sixteen years old, two years into the apprenticeship.

The apprenticeship had started on the eleventh of January of Year Fourteen, on the gravel turnaround of the workshop in Ava, when Christopher Bender had handed him a small kraft pamphlet and said *Come back next month.*

Dax had come back next month.

He had come back every month since.

He had, on Christopher's standing operational practice, spent one Saturday a month at the workshop in Ava for the past forty-six months. The Saturday was the second Saturday of every month. The drive was thirty-six miles. The day was, on the standard practice of the workshop, eight AM to five PM, with lunch at the kitchen table at noon.

Today was not a Bender Saturday.

Today was a Mudhouse Saturday.

The Mudhouse Saturdays had started in October of last year, when Dax had been fifteen and had not yet had the Corolla, and when Sage had been driving them both to Springfield on the second and fourth Saturdays of every month in her uncle Tate's old delivery truck so that Sage could spend the day at the Springfield-Greene County Library researching her MIT admission portfolio and Dax could spend the day at the coffee shop two blocks south of the library on Pickwick Avenue.

The coffee shop was called Mudhouse.

The Mudhouse Saturdays were the Saturdays Dax used for the work that did not happen at the workshop in Ava.

The work that did not happen at the workshop in Ava was the work of building things on Synmatic's open-source repositories that nobody had specifically asked him to build.

The parser was the current example.

Dax had pushed the first version of the parser at three forty-one PM Pacific on the sixteenth of August. He had iterated on it across eight weekends. The current version — version 0.4.2 — was at a state Dax believed was ready for review. He was going to push it to the public PR queue this morning at Mudhouse.

The push would be the work.
