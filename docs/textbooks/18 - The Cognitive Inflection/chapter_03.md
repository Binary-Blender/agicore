# Chapter 3: McGrath's Inflection Points, Made Sharper

Rita McGrath's *Seeing Around Corners*, published in 2019, makes the most important contribution to strategic-foresight literature of the last two decades. The book's central proposition is that strategic inflection points — moments at which the basis of competition in an industry shifts in a way that disadvantages incumbents and advantages new entrants — are visible in advance to a strategist who knows where to look. McGrath's contribution is the systematic identification of where to look. Her framework moves the discipline from the post-hoc analysis of inflection points to their detection in advance, and the detection is the part of the strategic problem that boards and management teams most consistently fail.

McGrath's three-stage model holds that inflection points propagate through an industry in a sequence. The first stage occurs at the **edges** — the small, often-dismissed corners of the market where new behaviors, new technologies, or new customer preferences first appear. The second stage is the **periphery** — the expansion of the new phenomenon beyond its initial edge case into a recognizable adjacent segment. The third stage is the **mainstream**, at which point the phenomenon is no longer dismissable and the incumbent firms either respond or are displaced. McGrath's diagnostic instrument is the systematic monitoring of edges, so that the strategist sees the phenomenon during stage one rather than during stage three, when the response window has closed.

The model is correct and the diagnostic is sound. For most inflection points in the industrial economy of the late twentieth and early twenty-first century, the model's three-stage propagation describes the empirical record accurately. The personal computer began at the hobbyist edge in the mid-1970s, expanded to the small-business periphery through the late 1970s and early 1980s, and reached mainstream enterprise adoption by the late 1980s. The internet followed a similar arc through the 1990s. The smartphone followed it through the 2000s. The strategist trained on McGrath's framework, monitoring the relevant edges for each of these inflections, would have had years of advance warning. The framework gave management what it most needed: time.

The chapter's argument is that the cognition-systems inflection violates McGrath's three-stage model in a specific, identifiable way. The edges and periphery do not unfold as separable stages. They compress into a single visible event, often within a single quarter, and the mainstream stage begins before the periphery stage has been recognized as such. The compressed timeline is the load-bearing difference. McGrath's framework, applied without adjustment, produces the right read on the substrate of the phenomenon and the wrong read on its propagation timeline. The strategist who uses the framework correctly will detect the edges of the cognition-systems inflection. The strategist who relies on the framework's implicit propagation assumptions will assume the periphery and mainstream stages will arrive on the timelines characteristic of prior inflections, and will conclude that the response window is two to four years when in fact it is two to four quarters.

The mechanism that produces the compression is worth examining in detail. McGrath's three-stage model assumes that propagation requires the recruitment of additional adopters, that recruitment occurs through demonstration and social proof, and that demonstration and social proof require time. These assumptions held for the personal computer, the internet, and the smartphone for material reasons. Each of these technologies required physical artifacts to be manufactured and distributed before adoption could occur. The personal computer required factories and retail channels. The internet required service providers and physical infrastructure. The smartphone required handsets and carrier relationships. The physical-artifact requirement bounded the propagation rate, and the bound matched the three-stage cadence McGrath observed.

Cognition-systems capability does not require physical artifacts to be manufactured and distributed. The capability propagates through documents, code repositories, and online communication channels at zero marginal cost. The recruitment of additional adopters does not require demonstration to a customer who must then purchase a physical artifact; the recruitment requires only that another practitioner read a document or watch a video and apply the technique to their own work. The friction in the recruitment loop is approximately the time required for a serious practitioner to absorb a technical idea and apply it. That time, for the practitioner population the inflection most depends on, is measured in days. When a new technique emerges, the practitioner population that will absorb it is already configured to absorb it — connected through online communities, monitoring the relevant feeds, accustomed to integrating new techniques into their daily work. The propagation rate is bounded by the speed of human attention, not by the speed of physical distribution.

This produces the phase-transition shape introduced in Chapter 1. The capability is below the practitioner-saturation threshold for some period, often months. During this period the capability is the property of a small number of innovators and is invisible to the strategist relying on McGrath's edge-monitoring. The capability then crosses the saturation threshold, and within days to weeks, it becomes the standard practice of the entire serious-practitioner population. The crossing event is the phase transition. From the strategist's point of view, the capability appeared overnight. From the perspective of the practitioner community, the capability has been visible for months and the strategist's surprise reflects the strategist's failure to monitor the right channels.

The empirical record on AI-era capability propagation since the beginning of 2024 supports this characterization. Consider four cases, each of which exhibits the phase-transition shape.

The first case is skill documents. The named concept — markdown files structured to inject domain expertise into AI model context, with specific sections for domain context, key entities, metrics, decision frameworks, and customer context — did not exist as a recognized pattern in early 2024. By mid-2024, scattered practitioners had begun to circulate the structure informally. By late 2024 the structure had crystallized into a standard pattern with a small number of documented variants. By the first quarter of 2025 the pattern was in daily use by approximately every serious AI practitioner and had been integrated into the documented workflows of the major frontier-AI providers. The propagation from "novel pattern circulated among a small community" to "industry standard absorbed into the major providers' documentation" occurred in approximately two quarters, with the bulk of the absorption concentrated in a single quarter.

The second case is agentic coding. The capability — an AI model invoked in a loop, equipped with tool-use capability, executing multi-step tasks autonomously toward a stated objective — existed as a research curiosity in 2023 and was demonstrated in research papers and academic settings. In 2024 the capability moved from research to engineering practice in a sequence of releases by frontier AI providers. By the second quarter of 2025, agentic coding had been operationalized in widely-available developer tools and was in daily use by an estimated several hundred thousand professional developers. The crossing from "research capability with no production use" to "daily standard practice for hundreds of thousands of practitioners" occurred in approximately three quarters, with the visible phase-transition event concentrated in a single one.

The third case is Claude Code, the developer tool that integrates agentic coding capability into the daily workflow of a working software engineer. The tool was released to the public in early 2025. Within ninety days of release it had been adopted by an estimated fifteen to twenty percent of professional developers working with AI tools, and within six months it had become the dominant pattern for AI-assisted software development among engineers who had adopted any AI-assisted pattern at all. The propagation curve has the phase-transition shape: months of latency during which the practitioner population evaluated and integrated the tool, followed by a quarter of rapid adoption across the population.

The fourth case is the determinism contract — the architectural pattern in which AI is paid for at build time, generates deterministic runtime artifacts, and is excluded from per-request execution. The pattern was articulated, in something like its current form, in 2024 by a small number of practitioners working independently on the same problem. By 2025 the pattern had begun to appear in documented architectures and in deployed systems. The propagation is still in progress at the time of writing; the early phase-transition signature is visible but the full propagation has not yet completed. The chapter takes the in-progress status as illustrative: the strategist who detects the pattern now, while it is in the latency phase of its phase transition, has the response window that the strategist who detects it after the transition will not have.

These four cases share a common structure. In each, the capability existed below the saturation threshold for a measurable period during which it was visible to careful observers and invisible to most strategists. In each, the capability crossed the saturation threshold in a single quarter, after which point its adoption was universal among serious practitioners and its absence in a firm's competitive positioning became a measurable disadvantage. In each, the strategist who monitored the right channels had ample warning. In each, the strategist who relied on standard industry-monitoring channels had no warning at all.

The adjustment required to McGrath's framework is now expressible. The framework's edge-monitoring instrument must be replaced with an instrument that monitors phase-transition latencies. The instrument requires three modifications.

The first modification is to the monitoring channels. The standard edge-monitoring set — small-firm activity, niche customer behavior, peripheral analyst coverage, scientific and engineering publications — must be supplemented with channels appropriate to cognition-systems propagation. The relevant channels include: technical blog posts from practitioners working at frontier-AI providers and their direct customer base, GitHub repository activity for tools that operationalize emerging capabilities, technical conference talks that introduce named patterns, and the practitioner-community channels (Discord servers, technical Substacks, Twitter-equivalent feeds) where new techniques circulate before they are formally published. The strategist who is not monitoring these channels has no leading-indicator capacity for cognition-systems inflection. The strategist who is monitoring them has approximately one to two quarters of latency in which to act before the phase transition.

The second modification is to the propagation-stage taxonomy. McGrath's three stages — edges, periphery, mainstream — should be replaced, for cognition-systems inflection, with a two-stage taxonomy: **latency** and **transition**. The latency stage is the period during which the capability exists, is visible to monitoring, and has not yet crossed the saturation threshold. The transition stage is the quarter in which saturation occurs and the capability becomes universal among serious practitioners. The two-stage model is empirically more accurate than the three-stage model for the propagation shape this inflection exhibits, and the strategist who uses it will produce more accurate response-window estimates.

The third modification is to the response cadence. McGrath's framework assumes that the firm, having detected the edges, has time for a multi-quarter response — typically a year or more of strategic planning, capability building, and product development before the periphery stage demands response. Under phase-transition propagation, the response cadence must be compressed to match. The firm that detects a capability in latency stage has, at most, the duration of the remaining latency in which to begin its response. The latency period varies by capability but is typically two to four quarters from emergence to transition. The firm's response window is correspondingly two to four quarters. The implication for organizational design is non-trivial: the firm whose standard strategic-planning cycle is annual will, by construction, miss most of these inflections, because the cycle's resolution is coarser than the inflection's response window.

The chapter has, in its analysis, made one additional claim that requires examination. The claim is that the response window for the present cognition-systems inflection — the broad inflection of which skill documents, agentic coding, Claude Code, and the determinism contract are component capabilities — is on the order of four to seven quarters at most, rather than the four to seven years of classical Christensen-style disruption. The claim is essentially a forecast about the propagation shape of the broader phenomenon based on the observed propagation shape of its components. The forecast has three premises that should be made explicit. The first is that the broader phenomenon will continue to propagate by the mechanism the components have propagated by, namely document-and-tool-mediated peer-to-peer transmission among an already-configured practitioner community. The second is that the practitioner community capable of operationalizing the broader phenomenon — capable of standing up the deterministic substrate, mastering the cognitive pipeline pattern, building production systems on the new substrate — is large enough at present, or will reach sufficient size within the next quarter or two, to constitute the disruptor population at the scale Chapter 1 forecast. The third is that the economic incentives for the practitioner community to operationalize the broader phenomenon are sufficient to overcome the friction of doing so.

Each of these premises is defensible, but the defense should be inspected. The first premise is the most solid; the mechanism of component-capability propagation has been observed across enough cases to be treated as the base rate for cognition-systems capability propagation in general. The second premise is more open; the size of the practitioner community capable of operationalizing the broader phenomenon at production grade is currently in the low thousands and growing rapidly. Whether the community will reach the ten-thousand-operator scale Chapter 1 forecast within the six-month window stated is the load-bearing empirical question for the forecast. The chapter's view, supported by the propagation rates of the component capabilities, is that the community will reach that scale. The reader is entitled to a different view. The third premise is the most secure; the economic incentives for individual practitioners to operationalize the broader phenomenon are well-established and increasing. The income differential between a senior practitioner working as an employee at an enterprise software firm and the same practitioner operating independently at the new substrate is large enough to motivate the migration on its own, and the migration is observable in the labor-flow data referenced in Chapter 1.

The synthesis of the chapter's argument is that McGrath's framework survives the inflection with the modifications stated. The strategist who uses the modified framework has a working instrument. The strategist who uses the unmodified framework will produce systematically late reads on the inflection, and the lateness will be of the same magnitude as the response window — that is, the strategist will detect the inflection at the moment the response window closes. This is the failure mode the present chapter most wishes to prevent. The reader who has absorbed the chapter has the basis for a different outcome: a strategist who detects the inflection during its latency stage, who has remaining latency in which to respond, and who uses the response window the latency provides.

The next chapter takes up Christensen's framework. Christensen's contribution is to the mechanics of disruption rather than to its detection, and the mechanics-of-disruption analysis is the load-bearing piece of the response strategy. McGrath tells the strategist that the inflection is occurring. Christensen tells the strategist what the disruption will look like when it arrives. Grove tells the strategist what to do. The three frameworks together constitute the diagnostic instrumentation of Part I. Each requires modification for the cognition-systems substrate. Each is tractable to modify. Each produces, post-modification, a working instrument the strategist can apply.

## Key Concepts

- **Phase Transition vs. Sigmoid.** Two distinct propagation shapes for technology-driven inflection points. Sigmoid propagation provides multi-quarter to multi-year response windows; phase-transition propagation collapses the windows to one or two quarters.
- **Latency Stage.** The period during which a cognition-systems capability exists, is visible to monitoring, and has not yet crossed the practitioner-saturation threshold. The strategic-response window.
- **Transition Stage.** The quarter in which the saturation threshold is crossed and the capability becomes universal among serious practitioners. The window closes here.
- **Practitioner Saturation Threshold.** The level of adoption at which a capability becomes the default practice in a community, after which non-adoption becomes a measurable disadvantage rather than a neutral choice.
- **Compressed Response Cadence.** The strategic-planning cadence required to operate inside the response windows characteristic of cognition-systems inflection. Typically quarterly or finer.
