# Chapter 13: 10x Force or Going-Out-of-Business Sale

Andrew Grove's central operating insight, derived from his experience at Intel through the memory-business exit and the microprocessor-business commitment of the mid-1980s, can be stated in a single sentence. **When a 10x force has arrived in your industry, the management's task is to act as if the firm has been put up for a going-out-of-business sale.** The framing is operating-CEO direct in a way the academic strategy literature rarely matches, and the framing is correct. The chapter opens Part III by demanding that the strategist apply the framing to the strategist's own firm and produce, in writing, the answer.

The going-out-of-business framing has two analytical functions. The first is that it forces the management to discount sunk costs. Sunk costs — the firm's prior investments in product, in organization, in customer relationships, in market position — are not recoverable through the going-out-of-business sale and are consequently irrelevant to the going-out-of-business price. The going-out-of-business price reflects the firm's forward-going expected value, calculated on the assumption that the new owner will not be bound by the prior owner's commitments. The management that values the firm at the going-out-of-business price has, by construction, discounted the sunk costs. The management that values the firm at its book value has not. The first valuation is the one Grove's framework demands; the second is the valuation the firm's standard accounting produces.

The second analytical function is that the framing forces the management to imagine itself as new ownership. The new owner is not bound by the existing strategy, the existing product roadmap, the existing organizational structure, or the existing customer commitments. The new owner can dismantle any of these in pursuit of the post-inflection position. The new owner's only constraint is the inflection itself, against which the new owner must produce a coherent response. The management that imagines itself as new ownership has, by construction, granted itself the same freedom of action. The management that does not imagine the transition remains bound by the existing commitments, which is to say bound by the past basis of competition, which is the basis the inflection has invalidated.

The chapter's task is to walk the strategist through the new-CEO exercise as it should be conducted at the strategist's own firm. The exercise has four steps. The strategist who completes the four steps has, at the end of the exercise, either the structured response of the post-inflection management team or the explicit acknowledgment that the firm should be sold. Both outcomes are acceptable to Grove's framework; what is not acceptable is the third outcome, in which the management neither structures the response nor explicitly acknowledges the going-out-of-business position.

The **first step** is the brutal-honesty diagnosis. The new-CEO management writes down, in plain language, what the firm currently sells, who it sells it to, what the firm's competitive position has been historically, and what the inflection does to each of these. The diagnosis is not a strategic-planning document. It is a one-page write-up that any competent observer would produce if asked to describe the firm in the context of the inflection. The write-up is not optimized for internal political acceptability; it is optimized for accuracy. The discipline of the first step is the discipline of writing the diagnosis the consultant would write if the consultant had no stake in the firm's continued operation. The firm's incumbent management cannot, by construction, write this diagnosis without effort, because the firm's incumbent management is implicated in every diagnostic finding. The exercise's value depends on overriding the implication. The exercise produces value only if the diagnosis is the one a stranger would produce.

The **second step** is the no-firm option. The new-CEO management asks, given the diagnosis, whether the firm should continue to exist. The question is not rhetorical and is not a posture. The question has real content: there are firms in the present moment for which the honest answer is no, and the management's responsibility to its shareholders is to identify them, transact the wind-down or sale at the highest available price, and return the proceeds. Firms for which the honest answer is yes are firms whose post-inflection position can be coherently constructed; firms for which the honest answer is no are firms whose post-inflection position cannot be coherently constructed and whose continued operation will destroy more shareholder value than it preserves. The discipline of the second step is the willingness to answer the question honestly. Most firms whose management completes this step honestly find that the firm should continue, with substantial restructuring. A meaningful minority find that the firm should not. The minority's correct response is to sell, and the sell-decision conducted at the moment the management identifies it is invariably at higher price than the same decision conducted eighteen months later, when the firm's deteriorated position is apparent to all bidders.

The **third step** is the candidate-strategy generation. Assuming the second step has produced a yes, the new-CEO management identifies, in writing, the candidate strategies for post-inflection position. The candidate strategies should include: a strategy in which the firm builds the cognition-systems-native product line as a parallel offering, a strategy in which the firm restructures its existing product line around the new substrate, a strategy in which the firm narrows to a defensible high-end segment and concedes the disruption-exposed segments, a strategy in which the firm pivots to a complementary service offering that depends on the disruption rather than competing with it, and a strategy in which the firm pursues a defensive consolidation by acquiring adjacent incumbents whose positions are weaker. The candidate strategies should be elaborated to the level of detail at which they can be evaluated; the elaboration is not the final strategic plan, but it must be specific enough that the management can assess each strategy's feasibility, cost, and likely outcome. The discipline of the third step is the willingness to consider strategies that the existing organizational structure would normally reject. The new-CEO management, by construction, has no prior loyalty to the existing structure.

The **fourth step** is the selection-and-commitment. The new-CEO management selects the candidate strategy with the strongest expected outcome and commits to it in writing. The selection is not a planning exercise; it is a commitment. The commitment is the announcement to the firm's staff and stakeholders that the strategic position is being restructured. The commitment is non-reversible in the strong sense that reversing it within the response window is equivalent to having no commitment at all. The discipline of the fourth step is the willingness to commit before the analytical work is complete, because the response window does not provide time for the analytical work to be complete before commitment is required. Grove's framework treats this as the load-bearing decision moment; the firms that produce successful post-inflection positions are the firms whose management committed early and adjusted within the commitment, against the firms whose management deferred commitment and ran out of response window.

The chapter has now described the new-CEO exercise. The strategist who has read the chapter and has not conducted the exercise is in the position of the river guide who has been taught to read the boil and has not yet looked at the water. The exercise is the looking. The remainder of Part III assumes the exercise has been conducted, the second step has returned yes, and the candidate strategies of the third step are under consideration. Chapters 14 through 18 develop the analytical material the candidate-strategy selection requires: the labor-market untying, the Hartwell counterfactual, the board's failure to read, what Davis Hartwell should have done, and the Stillwater choice. The reader who has not conducted the exercise will find the chapters informative but not actionable; the reader who has conducted the exercise will find the chapters directly applicable to the candidate selection.

A note on the register Part III adopts. The register tightens further from Part II. The chapters of Part III address the management team directly rather than the analytical reader. The pronouns shift slightly; the strategist who is reading is now treated as the strategist who must act. The footnote density decreases; the references are still present but the chapters do not pause to develop them. The voice becomes operator-CEO rather than research analyst. The shift is Grove's; the present book follows him.

The chapter closes with the framing the rest of Part III will use. The strategist's firm is either responding to a 10x force or holding a going-out-of-business sale. The new-CEO exercise tells the strategist which. The candidate-strategy selection determines which post-inflection position the firm pursues. The chapters that follow give the strategist the analytical material to make the selection well. The selection is the strategist's. The chapters do not make it for the strategist; the chapters give the strategist what is required to make it. The book proceeds.

## Discussion Questions

1. Conduct the four-step new-CEO exercise for your firm. Write the diagnosis at step one. Answer the yes-or-no question at step two honestly. Generate at least three candidate strategies at step three. Select one at step four. Submit the four-step write-up to a colleague whose judgment you trust and ask whether they would have produced the same answers.

2. The chapter argues that the going-out-of-business framing forces management to discount sunk costs. Identify, for your firm, the specific sunk costs that are currently constraining the firm's response posture. For each, articulate what the response posture would be if the sunk cost did not exist.

3. The second step of the exercise — the no-firm option — requires honest assessment of whether the firm should continue. For your firm, conduct the assessment as if you were the firm's largest outside investor. What is your honest answer? What evidence supports it?

4. The chapter holds that the commitment at step four is non-reversible in the strong sense. Identify, for your firm, the organizational practices that would tend to soften or reverse a step-four commitment. What modifications to those practices would harden the commitment?

5. The chapter notes that a meaningful minority of firms whose management completes the exercise honestly find that the firm should not continue. For your industry, identify the firms whose position is, on the visible evidence, weakest. What would the going-out-of-business price be for each? What is the spread between that price and the current public-market valuation?

## Key Concepts

- **The Going-Out-of-Business Framing.** Grove's central operating instrument for forcing management to discount sunk costs and act as new ownership.
- **The New-CEO Exercise.** The four-step procedure — diagnosis, no-firm question, candidate generation, selection-and-commitment — that operationalizes the framing.
- **Brutal-Honesty Diagnosis.** The discipline of writing the diagnosis a competent stranger would write, regardless of internal political acceptability.
- **The No-Firm Option.** The honest answer to whether the firm should continue, considered as a real option rather than a rhetorical position.
- **Early Commitment.** The discipline of committing to a candidate strategy before the analytical work is complete, because the response window does not permit completion before commitment.
