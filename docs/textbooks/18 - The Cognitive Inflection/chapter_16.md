# Chapter 16: What Davis Hartwell Should Have Done

The chapter is a counterfactual analysis. Given the information available to Davis Hartwell at the start of Year 1, given the institutional constraints he operated under, given the board composition he had inherited, and given the firm's financial position at the time, what decisions would have produced a different outcome? The chapter walks through the decision tree branch by branch, identifies the actual fork at each branch, and develops the alternative branch that, on the available evidence, would have produced a materially better outcome. The chapter's purpose is to convert the diagnosis of Chapter 14 into the actionable prescription that the reader can apply to the reader's own firm.

The counterfactual is structured around five decision points, each corresponding to a moment at which Davis Hartwell could have acted differently and at which the difference would have compounded across the subsequent period. The decision points are: the first-week recognition decision in October of Year 1; the Q4 board reporting decision in December of Year 1; the Karen Voss memo response decision in February of Year 2; the Year-2 strategic review decision in midyear; and the Year-3 exit-or-restructure decision. The chapter takes each in turn.

## Decision Point One: The First-Week Recognition

The actual decision: Davis treated each resignation as a discrete event, accepted Brian Wexler's "within normal range" characterization, deferred Karen Voss's request for a working session to the following week, and proceeded with the standard CEO agenda.

The counterfactual: Davis treats the three resignations on a single Tuesday as a pattern requiring immediate analysis. He accepts Karen's offer of a working session that afternoon, asks Brian Wexler to produce a senior-tier-concentrated departure analysis by end of week, and adjusts the Stansfield call to the following day. He commits, in writing, to a sixty-day exit-interview restructuring that adds the "departure to independent operation" category and that captures, in addition to the standard categories, the practitioner's specific expected next-step circumstances.

The counterfactual's cost: approximately one CEO-day of attention in the first week, displacing one standard meeting in the standard agenda.

The counterfactual's value: the recognition that occurred would be eighteen months earlier than the recognition Davis actually produced. The recognition would not, on its own, produce the response. The recognition would, however, be the precondition for every subsequent decision in the counterfactual tree. The eighteen-month head start on recognition is the largest single value capture in the counterfactual analysis.

## Decision Point Two: The Q4 Board Reporting Decision

The actual decision: Davis accepted Brian Wexler's standard quarterly HR update — fourteen resignations, characterized as elevated but within tight-labor-market norms — and presented the standard quarterly slide deck to the board. The board's discussion of the resignation pattern lasted approximately three minutes.

The counterfactual: Davis, having recognized the pattern in October, prepares for the December board meeting with a substantially different reporting package. The package includes: the senior-tier-concentrated departure analysis (which shows that eleven of fourteen Q4 departures were senior-tier), the destination-distribution analysis (which shows that approximately half of the departures went to independent operation rather than to other employers), and a preliminary impact analysis (which estimates the revenue exposure from the departed senior tier's client relationships). The package is structured to make the pattern visible at the board's reading resolution rather than to dilute it as the standard reporting does.

Davis's presentation to the board accompanies the package with an explicit framing: this is a structural labor-market change rather than elevated turnover; the firm's exposure is real and material; the firm should commission a strategic review to be completed by the end of Q1 of Year 2 with response options for the board's consideration.

The counterfactual's cost: approximately one week of CEO attention and two weeks of HR analytical work in the months leading up to the December meeting. The cost of the strategic review, which would be commissioned at the December meeting, is approximately one to two hundred thousand dollars over the subsequent quarter.

The counterfactual's value: the strategic review's completion in Q1 of Year 2 produces, at minimum, the option set the firm needs to consider. The board's exposure to the option set in Q1 of Year 2 — eighteen months before the Hartwell case's actual Q3-of-Year-2 acceptance failure — provides the response window the firm requires. The board may or may not act on the option set; the counterfactual provides the option set in time for action to be possible.

## Decision Point Three: The Karen Voss Memo Response

The actual decision: Davis brought Karen's memo to the board, accepted the board's rejection, and reported the outcome to Karen without further advocacy.

The counterfactual: Davis, having received Karen's memo, treats it as the strategic review document the December counterfactual board meeting commissioned. He convenes a working session with Karen, Brian Wexler, the firm's CFO, and the firm's head of business development to refine the memo's five proposals into a board-ready strategic plan. The plan adds three components the memo did not: a pricing structure analysis (showing the unsustainability of the firm's current hourly model against post-inflection competition), a customer-relationship inventory (showing which clients are most exposed to the departing-senior practitioners' new firms), and a financial projection (showing the firm's three-year trajectory under both the status-quo path and the proposed-restructuring path).

Davis presents the refined plan to the board in March of Year 2 with explicit advocacy: the plan is, in his judgment, the firm's best available response to the inflection; the plan requires the board's approval to execute; the alternative — continued status quo — has, in his judgment, a materially worse expected outcome. Davis frames his advocacy in the going-out-of-business language Grove's framework prescribes: the firm is either responding to a 10x force or holding a going-out-of-business sale; his recommendation is to respond.

Geoffrey Stansfield's response, in the counterfactual, is identical to his actual response: skepticism, reference to the firm's prior successful navigations, recommendation to stay the course. The counterfactual's branch is in Davis's response to Stansfield. Davis, in the actual case, accepted the rejection. Davis, in the counterfactual, advocates further. He requests a one-week deferral, returns the following week with revised analysis, and presses the case explicitly. If the board still rejects, Davis frames the board's rejection as a confidence-vote question: does the board retain confidence in his judgment as CEO. If yes, he presses for execution of the plan; if no, he resigns and proceeds to acquire a competing firm or join a small partnership in the disruptor population.

The counterfactual's cost: substantial executive courage, the possibility of CEO departure, the possibility of an extended board-management conflict.

The counterfactual's value: the plan's execution begins in Q2 of Year 2, eighteen months before the actual case's effective dissolution. The firm's Q4 of Year 2 financial position, under the counterfactual, includes the early effects of the restructuring (modest profit-margin compression due to investment, materially improved senior retention, the beginnings of a repositioned customer base) rather than the actual case's continued exposure deterioration. The Year-3 financial position, under the counterfactual, is approximately twenty percent below the firm's prior peak rather than the actual case's sixty percent below.

## Decision Point Four: The Year-2 Strategic Review

The actual decision: there was no strategic review at midyear; the standard quarterly cycle continued.

The counterfactual: Davis, having executed the restructuring beginning in Q2, conducts a formal strategic review at midyear to assess execution progress and adjust the plan. The review's findings, in the counterfactual, are mixed: the firm's senior retention has improved but not stabilized; the firm has retained three of the five departing senior employees who would have otherwise gone independent; the firm's hourly billing has been partially converted to retainer pricing with modest customer pushback; the firm's costs have increased modestly due to the restructuring investment.

The midyear review's principal output is the identification of the second-move requirement: the perks-first restructuring has slowed the bleeding but has not stopped it, and the firm requires a deeper restructuring of its compensation and partnership model to retain the senior tier durably. The midyear review identifies the Senior Partner tier — anticipating the Chapter 22 innovation that Maya Holm-Russo will develop in the Stillwater case — as the appropriate second move and commissions a design effort for board review in Q4 of Year 2.

The counterfactual's cost: approximately one quarter of executive attention and design work.

The counterfactual's value: the Senior Partner tier's introduction in Q1 of Year 3 captures the return-as-partner archetype's first returners — practitioners who attempted independent operation in late Year 1 and early Year 2 and who, by early Year 3, have discovered that independent operation does not suit them. The firm's Year-3 position, under the counterfactual, includes meaningful senior recapture rather than the actual case's continued senior depletion.

## Decision Point Five: The Year-3 Exit-or-Restructure Decision

The actual decision: Davis continued to characterize the trajectory as elevated turnover through Q1 of Year 3 and did not initiate exit or restructure discussions.

The counterfactual: the counterfactual reaches Year 3 with the firm in materially better position than the actual case, and the exit-or-restructure decision is consequently a different decision. The counterfactual's Year 3 firm has approximately 180 employees (against the actual case's 90), four of the top twenty client engagements at modified retainer pricing (against the actual case's transfers to small partnerships), and the Senior Partner tier in active operation. The firm's continued operation, in the counterfactual, is not in question. The exit-or-restructure decision is, instead, a strategic-optimization decision: at what scale does the firm operate going forward, what mix of staffed and partner-tier capacity, what segments retain and which concede.

The counterfactual's Year-3 firm is, on the available evidence, a successful post-inflection mid-market consultancy. The firm is smaller than the pre-inflection firm and structured differently. The firm is profitable, growing modestly, and competitive with the disruptor population through the ecosystem complementarity arrangements that have developed with the departed senior practitioners' small partnerships.

## Synthesis

The counterfactual analysis demonstrates that the actual case's outcome was not inevitable. Davis Hartwell, with the same information and the same institutional constraints, could have produced a materially different outcome by acting differently at each of five decision points. The decisions, in aggregate, required no information he did not have at the time. The decisions required executive recognition, executive courage, and executive willingness to advocate against the board. Each of the three was, in the actual case, the limiting factor.

The reader who is reading this chapter is, in most cases, not Davis Hartwell. The reader is the CEO, COO, or senior executive of a firm currently in approximately the position Hartwell occupied at the start of Year 1. The reader's five decision points are approximately the five Davis faced. The reader's response at each decision point is the reader's. The chapter has shown that the actual decisions produce dissolution and the counterfactual decisions produce successful repositioning. The chapter does not relieve the reader of the decision; it shows the reader what each branch produces.

The next chapter takes up the Stillwater contrast — the firm whose CEO produced approximately the counterfactual sequence of decisions, in real time, in the same period the Hartwell case covers. The Stillwater case demonstrates that the counterfactual is not theoretical; it has been executed. The reader proceeds to Chapter 17, then to Part IV's constructive treatment.

## Key Concepts

- **The Five Decision Points.** First-week recognition, Q4 board reporting, Karen Voss memo response, Year-2 strategic review, Year-3 exit-or-restructure. The decision tree the chapter walks through.
- **The Recognition Head-Start.** The value of recognizing the inflection eighteen months earlier than the standard reporting would surface it.
- **The Confidence-Vote Framing.** The CEO's framing of a board rejection as a confidence-vote question. The strategic logic of the framing under conditions where the rejection is, in the CEO's judgment, materially wrong.
- **The Three Limiting Factors.** Executive recognition, executive courage, and executive willingness to advocate against the board. The factors that, in aggregate, determine whether the counterfactual decisions are executable.
- **The Sequential Compounding.** The value capture mechanism by which each decision's value depends on the prior decisions having been made correctly. The earliest decisions have the largest leverage.
