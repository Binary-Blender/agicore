# Chapter 18: The Stillwater Choice

The chapter introduces the constructive contrast to the Hartwell case. Stillwater Software is a mid-market enterprise software consultancy based in Minneapolis, founded in 2008 and operating at approximately 145 employees at the start of the case period. The firm's CEO, Maya Holm-Russo, executed approximately the counterfactual sequence Chapter 17 developed, in real time, in the same period the Hartwell case covers. The chapter introduces the firm, the CEO, and the strategic choice the CEO made; the operational detail of the choice is developed in Chapters 22 and 23 of Part IV. The chapter's purpose is to establish that the counterfactual is not theoretical — to demonstrate, by reference to a firm that exists, that the response can be executed by a competent CEO with appropriate institutional support, on the timeline the inflection requires.

The Stillwater case is, like the Hartwell case, composed. The firm's name and the specific identifying details are not those of any single actual firm; the patterns the case dramatizes have been observed across multiple actual firms whose responses to the inflection have followed the Stillwater trajectory. The composition permits the same analytical clarity the Hartwell case provided. The case's instructional value is in the pattern, not in the firm.

## The Firm and the CEO

Stillwater Software was founded in 2008 by Maya Holm-Russo and her then-partner Joseph Velasquez, both former engineers at a Twin Cities financial-services firm. Velasquez cashed out in 2017 and now runs a small vineyard in Sonoma. Maya remained as CEO and sole principal. The firm has occupied a converted four-story brick warehouse near the Mississippi in Northeast Minneapolis since 2014; the building was acquired during a real-estate downturn and serves as both office space and a balance-sheet asset. The firm's customer base is Upper Midwest mid-market — financial-services firms in the Twin Cities, agricultural-technology firms across the surrounding farm belt, and a steady roster of mid-size manufacturers across Wisconsin, Iowa, and the Dakotas. The firm's reputation is smart, fair, deeply embedded with its clients, mid-tier pricing, no flash.

Maya Holm-Russo at the start of the case period was fifty-three years old. She holds two engineering degrees, an undergraduate from the University of Minnesota and a master's from Carnegie Mellon. She built Stillwater from a two-person shop into the 145-person firm of the case period. She is married to Carlo Russo, a chef who runs a small restaurant in Northeast Minneapolis; she has no children by choice. She reads economics for relaxation. She knows her industry's labor market in a way most CEOs do not, in part because the Twin Cities tech-and-services labor market is small enough to be knowable and in part because she has spent more time talking to senior practitioners — at every level of the firm and outside it — than most CEOs at her scale of firm allocate.

The firm's COO at the start of the case period is Aaron Park, forty-seven, Korean-American, second-generation, hired from Best Buy's enterprise division nine years prior. He is, in the case material, Maya's right hand and the executor of every move Maya decides to make. The firm's head of people is Renata Diaz, forty-four, Mexican-American, Twin Cities native, twelve years at the firm. The firm's board is three people — Maya herself, Aaron Park, and a Twin Cities investor named Lena Schultz who put in early capital and who has, in the period since, served three other Twin Cities firms through structural transitions. The board's composition, by contrast with the Hartwell board, includes substantial direct exposure to organizational transitions of substantial magnitude and is consequently capable of reading the inflection's signals at the resolution the standard board does not.

## The First Departure and the Recognition

The case opens on the same week in October of Year 1 that the Hartwell case opens. Stillwater receives, on a Monday morning, the news of a pending resignation from Ben Kowalczyk, a senior engineer with six years at the firm. Ben is one of Maya's best people; his departure has the specific weight that the best people's departures always carry. Maya does not panic. She walks down to Ben's desk and asks him to lunch.

The lunch is at Carlo's restaurant. Carlo brings them coffee and pretends not to listen. Ben tells Maya, plainly, that he is not unhappy at Stillwater — he loves Stillwater — but he has been running the math for six months and he and two colleagues believe they can do better as a tiny partnership. The colleagues will resign in February. Ben is telling Maya four months early because he respects her. Maya thanks him for the courtesy. She does not try to talk him out of it. She tells him that, when the time comes, she would consider it a privilege to refer Wisconsin manufacturing clients to his new firm.

Walking back to the office she calls Aaron Park from the sidewalk: *Aaron. This is the leading edge. There will be more. We need to think about what we're going to do.* Aaron's response, on the case material, is: *I've been waiting for you to say that.*

The case's first instructional point is the recognition. Maya read the pattern in the first instance. She did not require a quarter's worth of departure data, a Brian Wexler standard report, or a Karen Voss memo to recognize what was happening. She had been monitoring the indicator categories — at her own resolution, with her own informal instruments — for the previous six months. When Ben's resignation arrived, the data slotted into a pattern she had already prepared to recognize. The recognition was not Maya's analytical brilliance; it was Maya's prior investment in monitoring. The investment was small and the value was disproportionate.

## The First-Quarter Response

The case continues through November and December of Year 1 with Maya and her senior team developing the firm's response. The response is structured in two phases, which the case material identifies explicitly: the first phase is the perks-first response, intended to slow the bleeding and demonstrate that the firm has noticed; the second phase is the partners-not-resources response, intended to durably restructure the firm's relationship with its senior practitioner population. The first phase is announced at the firm's all-hands meeting in March of Year 2. The second phase is announced at the firm's all-hands meeting in November of Year 2. The chapter's preview of the two phases will be expanded into operational detail in Chapters 22 and 23.

The phase-one announcement at the March all-hands is, in the case material, explicit about its limitations. Maya tells the staff: this is the first thing we are doing. It will slow the bleeding. It will not stop it. We have to do something else after this. But we have to do this first because the first thing we have to demonstrate is that we noticed. The framing — explicit acknowledgment of the move's insufficiency — is, by contrast with the Hartwell case's denial of the underlying problem, the framing the case treats as load-bearing. The Stillwater staff hears the framing and recognizes the framing as truthful; the recognition is the precondition for the staff's willingness to defer judgment on the firm's response while the second phase develops.

The Q4 board meeting of Year 1, held at the converted-warehouse office in mid-December, is the case's second instructional contrast with Hartwell. Maya presents to her board the same diagnosis Karen Voss would later present to the Hartwell board: structural labor-market change, twenty-to-forty-percent senior-tier exposure over the next eighteen months, requirement for substantial restructuring. Lena Schultz, who has seen three other firms through structural transitions, asks the question that distinguishes the Stillwater board from the Hartwell board: *what's the budget for what you want to do.* Maya: *I don't know yet. But I think it is going to be significant and I think it is going to be the most important investment we make this decade.* Lena: *I'm in. Tell me when you know.*

The board's response, in the case material, is the response a properly-composed board produces. The composition produced the response, not the eloquence of the presentation. The case's third instructional point is consequently the board composition. Lena Schultz is, in the Stillwater case, the inflection-specialist director Chapter 16 prescribed; the chapter did not invent the concept, the case material did. The Stillwater board's capacity to read the inflection at the resolution Maya brought to it depended on Lena Schultz's prior experience with structural transitions, and the board's willingness to fund the response at the magnitude Maya identified depended on Lena Schultz's confidence in the framing. The board's other member — Aaron Park, in his dual role as COO and director — provided the operational confirmation that the response was executable. The board's two non-CEO members were, between them, sufficient governance for the response the firm required.

## The Choice

The Stillwater case's central instructional point is what the chapter calls the Stillwater choice. The choice is not the choice between perks-first and partners-not-resources — those are sequential phases of the same response, not alternatives. The choice is the choice between treating the inflection as a temporary deviation requiring tactical adjustment and treating the inflection as a structural change requiring strategic repositioning. Maya made the latter choice at the moment Ben Kowalczyk's resignation arrived. Davis Hartwell made the former choice at the same moment in his own case. The two choices, made in the same week, produced the diverging trajectories that the cases ultimately demonstrate.

The choice is, in the case material's language, a choice about the firm's identity. The firm that treats the inflection tactically remains, in its own self-understanding, the firm it was — temporarily inconvenienced by labor-market noise, awaiting the noise's subsidence. The firm that treats the inflection strategically becomes, in its own self-understanding, a different firm — one whose relationship to its senior practitioner population, its customer base, its competitive position, and its strategic posture must be reconstructed. The reconstruction is expensive and uncomfortable. The reconstruction is the substance of the firm's continued existence on the other side of the inflection.

Maya's choice was the strategic choice. The case's Part II — the chapters that follow Maya through the development and execution of the perks-first and partners-not-resources phases — is the dramatization of the choice in action. The chapter does not develop those chapters here; they are the subject of Part IV. The chapter's task has been to introduce the firm, the CEO, the choice, and the contrast with the Hartwell case. The reader proceeds to Part IV with the contrast in place.

## The Bridge to Part IV

The chapter closes Part III. The Part has, across six chapters, established the strategic-inflection framing (Chapter 13), the labor-market untying that the inflection produces (Chapter 14), the cautionary case of the firm that did not respond (Chapter 15), the structural reasons boards systematically fail to read the inflection (Chapter 16), the counterfactual analysis showing what the cautionary firm could have done (Chapter 17), and the constructive case of the firm that responded as the counterfactual prescribes (the present chapter). The reader has, by this point, the full diagnostic and the full strategic framing required to understand the response. What the reader does not yet have is the operational detail of the response itself.

Part IV provides the operational detail. Chapter 19 introduces Transformative AI Operations as the named transformation framework that any firm — Stillwater, the reader's firm, or any post-inflection mid-market consultancy — can use to convert the strategic choice into operational execution. Chapters 20 and 21 develop the analytical and audit instruments of the framework: the Multiplication Doctrine and the Blueprint Audit. Chapter 22 develops the perks-first response — Stillwater's first move — at operational depth. Chapter 23 develops the partners-not-resources response — Stillwater's second move and the chapter the entire book has been building toward — at operational depth. Chapter 24 develops the ecosystem posture that integrates the firm's response with its position in the broader disruptor economy. The reader who finishes Part IV has the response in detail.

The reader who finishes the book — including Part V's worked case and typology — has, by the close, both the diagnostic and the response, ready for application to the reader's own firm. The chapter that closes Part III hands the reader to Part IV.

## Discussion Questions

1. The Stillwater case opens with Maya's recognition in the first instance of Ben Kowalczyk's resignation. The recognition reflects Maya's prior six months of informal monitoring. For your firm's CEO (or for yourself), identify the informal monitoring practices currently in place. What practices would Maya have been conducting that your CEO is not?

2. The Stillwater board's composition includes Lena Schultz, the inflection-specialist director Chapter 16 prescribed. For your firm's board, assess whether any current director provides the equivalent lens. If not, what recruitment posture would secure one?

3. The Stillwater choice is the choice between treating the inflection tactically and treating it strategically. For your firm's current response posture, assess which choice has been made. If the tactical choice has been made, what events would force the choice to be reconsidered?

4. Maya's framing at the March all-hands — explicit acknowledgment that the perks-first move is insufficient — is identified in the chapter as load-bearing. Construct, for your firm, the equivalent framing that the firm's CEO would deliver at an all-hands. What does the framing acknowledge? What does it commit?

5. The chapter identifies executive recognition, executive courage, and executive willingness to advocate against the board as the three limiting factors at each decision point. Maya's case demonstrates each in operation. For your firm's CEO, identify the recent decisions that have tested each of the three. What were the outcomes?

## Key Concepts

- **The Stillwater Choice.** The choice between treating the inflection as a temporary deviation requiring tactical adjustment and treating it as a structural change requiring strategic repositioning.
- **The Prior Monitoring Investment.** Maya's six months of informal monitoring before the first departure. The small investment that enabled the disproportionate value of first-instance recognition.
- **The Inflection-Specialist Director in Practice.** Lena Schultz's role on the Stillwater board. The case-material confirmation that the Chapter 16 prescription is executable.
- **The Two-Phase Response.** Perks-first followed by partners-not-resources. The sequence the Stillwater case demonstrates and that Part IV develops in operational detail.
- **The Truthful Framing.** Maya's explicit acknowledgment that the first phase is insufficient. The framing that the staff recognizes as truthful and consequently is willing to defer judgment on.
