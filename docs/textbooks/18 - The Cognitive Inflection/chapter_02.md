# Chapter 2: Why the Old Lens Fails

The standard graduate business curriculum equips its students with five canonical analytical frameworks. Porter's Five Forces, introduced in 1979 and codified across subsequent decades, gives the student a way to map industry structure and identify the sources of competitive pressure. The Boston Consulting Group growth-share matrix, dating from 1968 and refined since, gives the student a method for allocating capital across a portfolio of business units. The jobs-to-be-done framework, developed by Christensen and his collaborators in the 1990s and 2000s, gives the student a way to understand customer demand at a level deeper than product feature comparison. Blue-ocean strategy, developed by Kim and Mauborgne in the 2000s, gives the student a method for identifying uncontested market space and shaping demand rather than competing for it. The Resource-Based View of the firm, developed by Barney and refined through the 1990s, gives the student a way to assess sustainable competitive advantage in terms of internal capabilities rather than external positioning.

Each of these frameworks is correct on its own terms. Each is taught for excellent reasons. Each will continue to be useful for the analyses for which it was designed. None of them was designed for the analysis the present book demands. The chapter's task is to show, framework by framework, where each produces blindness when applied to cognition-systems disruption, and why the blindness is structural rather than incidental. The chapter does not propose to discard the standard toolkit. It proposes to identify the conditions under which the standard toolkit reads the inflection correctly and the conditions under which it reads it incorrectly, so that the reader can apply the right instrument to the right problem.

Begin with Porter. The Five Forces — bargaining power of suppliers, bargaining power of buyers, threat of new entrants, threat of substitutes, rivalry among existing competitors — assume a particular kind of competitor and a particular kind of competition. The competitor in Porter's framework is a firm. The firm has employees, capital, a cost structure, a sales channel, a brand, and a set of contractual relationships with suppliers and customers. The competition takes the form of moves between firms, each move evaluated against the responses available to the others. Porter's framework is, at its core, a framework for analyzing competition between organizations of comparable type.

The framework does what it was designed to do when the competitors are firms of comparable type. It produces a clean read on the airline industry, the automobile industry, the steel industry, the consumer packaged goods industry, the pharmaceutical industry, the telecommunications industry. The five forces are visible in each case, the threat profiles can be assessed, and the strategic recommendations are actionable. Generations of MBAs have used the framework productively against problems for which it was calibrated.

The framework fails when the competitor is not a firm. A one-person operator with no employees, no capital structure to speak of, no sales channel beyond personal referral, no brand, and no contractual relationships beyond the customer-supplier dyad does not fit the framework's premises. The bargaining-power-of-suppliers analysis does not apply, because the operator's supplier is the AI model, the AI model is offered by a duopoly of providers at commodity prices, and the operator's switching cost between providers is approximately zero. The bargaining-power-of-buyers analysis does not apply, because the operator's buyers are individual enterprises selected one at a time and the operator can simply decline buyers who attempt to extract concessions. The threat-of-substitutes analysis does not apply, because the operator is itself the substitute. The rivalry-among-existing-competitors analysis collapses, because the operator does not perceive the incumbent firms as rivals; the operator perceives the incumbent firms as the market to be displaced, and the operator's strategy is to sell against the incumbent's price, not against the incumbent's products. The threat-of-new-entrants analysis is the only one of the five that produces useful output, and even there the framework returns a misleadingly small number because the framework looks for new firms entering the market and the new entrants are not firms.

The Porter user who applies the framework rigorously to an enterprise software market under cognition-systems conditions will conclude that the industry structure is healthy. The supplier base is concentrated and rational. The buyer base is fragmented and price-insensitive. Substitutes are weak. Rivalry among existing competitors is intense but stable. The threat of new entrants is moderate and absorbable through the standard playbook of pricing, partnership, and acquisition. The Porter user will go home satisfied that the firm's strategic position is defensible. The Porter user is wrong. The framework has produced the wrong answer because the framework's premises do not hold. The competitor is not a firm.

Consider next the BCG growth-share matrix. The matrix sorts a portfolio of business units along two axes — market growth rate and relative market share — and produces four categories of business unit, each with a recommended capital allocation. The framework's premises are that market share is meaningful, that market share can be measured against named competitors, and that the firm's portfolio can be optimized by reallocating capital among the four categories. The framework was designed for the conglomerate era, calibrated against industries whose competitive structure conformed to the assumptions, and applied across the second half of the twentieth century with mixed success.

The framework fails when relative market share cannot be measured. Under cognition-systems disruption, the disruptor population does not have measurable share. The disruptors are not registered as firms in the relevant trade databases. They do not file financial statements that aggregate into industry totals. They do not appear on the analyst-coverage lists that the matrix user consults for share data. Even if the disruptors could be enumerated, their share would not register because the metric the matrix expects — share of named-vendor revenue — is the wrong metric. The disruptors are not capturing share from the named vendors. They are establishing a parallel market with different economics, and the share they capture is the share that flows from buyers who reject the named-vendor market entirely. The BCG user who applies the matrix will conclude that the firm's market share is stable because the firm's share of the named-vendor market is stable. The firm's share of the total market — including the parallel disruptor market — is declining, but the matrix has no way to surface the decline.

Consider next the jobs-to-be-done framework, which is the closest of the canonical five to a useful instrument for the inflection. The framework holds that customers do not buy products; they hire products to do jobs. The job is stable; the product hired to do the job is contingent. The framework's diagnostic question — what job is the customer hiring this product to do — produces insights inaccessible to the feature-comparison frameworks that preceded it. It has a justified reputation among Christensen's intellectual heirs.

The framework's premises are that the job is stable across technology shifts and that the customer's hiring decision is the load-bearing event. Under cognition-systems disruption, the first premise breaks down. The job is not stable across the shift. When the cost of producing custom software falls by an order of magnitude, the customer's job ceases to be "select a vendor whose product approximately fits my use case" and becomes "specify the system I actually want and have it built." The job has reshaped around the new capability. The framework, applied without adjustment, returns the pre-inflection job specification, and the analysis based on the pre-inflection job will misidentify what the customer is hiring for. The jobs-to-be-done user will conclude that the customer is hiring an enterprise software product to do a particular job. The customer, post-inflection, is no longer hiring an enterprise software product. The customer is hiring a one-person operator to build a specific system. The job has changed. The framework caught the change in the customer's behavior but not in the job's specification, because the framework's interview methodology was designed to surface stable jobs rather than to detect their reshaping.

Blue-ocean strategy is the framework most often invoked in response to the inflection. The reasoning is intuitive: cognition-systems capability has opened new market space; the firm should pursue blue ocean rather than red. The reasoning is correct as far as it goes. It fails on the timeline. Blue-ocean strategy assumes that the firm pursuing the new market space has the time to define the space, articulate the value proposition, build the offering, and capture the market before competitors arrive. The framework's case studies — Cirque du Soleil, Yellow Tail, the Nintendo Wii — all describe firms that had multi-year windows during which their blue ocean remained genuinely uncontested. The window is the load-bearing premise. Under phase-transition propagation, the window does not exist. Any blue ocean visible to one strategist is visible to ten thousand strategists in the same quarter, and the ocean is red by the time the offering ships. Blue-ocean strategy is not wrong in its theoretical structure. It is wrong in its assumption about the duration of the strategic window, and the assumption was correct under sigmoid propagation and is incorrect under phase-transition propagation.

The Resource-Based View is the fifth canonical framework and the one that most directly illuminates what the inflection actually does to the firm. The Resource-Based View holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are valuable, rare, inimitable, and non-substitutable. The framework directs the strategist's attention to what the firm has that competitors cannot easily replicate. It produces the moat analysis that underpins much of modern strategic thinking.

Under cognition-systems disruption, the canonical moats become inimitable in the wrong direction. The firm's accumulated codebase, which used to be a moat because reproducing it required many engineer-years, can now be approximated by a competent operator in a fraction of the time. The firm's accumulated customer relationships, which used to be a moat because customer-acquisition cost was high, are weakened when the cost of producing an alternative offering for the same customer falls by an order of magnitude. The firm's accumulated brand, which used to be a moat because brand-building required expensive marketing, retains some defensive power but is weakened when the customer is comparing the brand against an alternative whose price is one-tenth and whose customization profile is dramatically superior. The Resource-Based View user will conclude that the firm has substantial resources. The firm does have substantial resources. The resources have lost much of their inimitability, and the strategist who relies on the framework without auditing each resource against the new inimitability conditions will miss the deterioration.

Each of the five canonical frameworks fails in a slightly different way, and the failures share a common root. The frameworks were calibrated against an industrial substrate in which coordination costs were high, capability propagation was slow, and competitors were firms of comparable type. Cognition-systems disruption collapses each of these conditions. Coordination costs fall by orders of magnitude when AI absorbs the execution work that previously required teams. Capability propagation accelerates from sigmoid to phase transition. The competitors are no longer firms; they are individuals operating with AI-augmented capacity.

Every framework that took these conditions as premises inherits a bug. The bug is not a defect in the framework's logic; the framework's logic is sound. The bug is in the framework's calibration. A correctly-derived theorem with a wrong premise produces a wrong conclusion. The standard MBA toolkit is correctly-derived. Its premises are wrong for the cognition-systems substrate. The conclusions it produces are correspondingly wrong.

This raises the question of what survives. The chapter's claim, made in the introduction and now defended in detail, is that opportunity discovery in McGrath's sense, strategic inflection-point reading in Grove's sense, and disruption-mechanics modeling in Christensen's sense each survive with adjustment. The remaining chapters of Part I take up each in turn. The argument is not that these three frameworks are immune to the substrate shift. It is that they were each constructed to address transition rather than steady-state competition, and the transition-oriented frameworks are closer to the right shape than the steady-state frameworks. The adjustments required to each are tractable. The adjustments required to Porter, BCG, jobs-to-be-done, blue ocean, and the Resource-Based View are not tractable. The frameworks would require reconstruction from first principles, and the reconstructed frameworks would be unrecognizable as the originals.

A constructive note is in order. The chapter has argued that the standard MBA toolkit produces blindness in the face of cognition-systems disruption. The reader who concludes from this argument that the standard toolkit should be discarded has misread the chapter. The standard toolkit remains useful for the analyses for which it was designed, which include a large fraction of the analyses an MBA-trained executive will encounter across a career. The chapter has identified a specific class of problem — cognition-systems disruption — for which the standard toolkit produces wrong answers. The reader's task is to recognize the class of problem when it arises and to apply the right instrument. The standard toolkit is one instrument. The frameworks the remainder of Part I develops are others. The reader who applies all of them, in the right combinations, against the right problems, has the analytical capacity the inflection demands. The reader who applies only the standard toolkit does not.

The diagnostic question the chapter leaves with the reader is the question of substrate. The reader, presented with a strategic problem, should ask first what substrate the problem sits on. If the substrate is industrial — physical goods, capital-intensive production, coordination-cost-bounded competition — the standard toolkit is appropriate and probably sufficient. If the substrate is cognition-systems — software, services, knowledge work, coordination-cost-collapsed competition — the standard toolkit is necessary but not sufficient, and the frameworks of Part I, Part II, and Part III must be brought to bear in addition. The substrate question is not always easy to answer. Many industries are at present hybrid, with industrial-substrate components and cognition-systems-substrate components coexisting in the same firm. The hybrid case is the typical case for the reader of this book. The analytical work for the hybrid firm is to apply the right instrument to the right component, and to update the application as the substrate composition shifts. The shift, in most industries, is from industrial toward cognition-systems. The instruments must shift accordingly.

The chapter closes on a note of intellectual humility that the book intends to maintain throughout. The author does not propose to discard the work of Porter, Hamel, Christensen, Grove, McGrath, Kim and Mauborgne, Barney, or any of the other figures whose frameworks have shaped the discipline. The author proposes to extend that work into a substrate the original authors did not face. The extension is a contribution to the discipline, not a refutation of it. The reader who finishes this book and concludes that the standard toolkit is obsolete has not understood the argument. The reader who finishes this book and concludes that the standard toolkit must be supplemented for a specific class of problem has understood it correctly. The chapter that follows takes up the first of the extensions. McGrath's inflection-point framework is the closest available to the cognition-systems disruption case, and the closest framework deserves the closest examination.

## Discussion Questions

1. Apply Porter's Five Forces to a market in which your firm competes. Identify, for each of the five forces, whether the analysis would be different if the competitors included one-person operators with AI-augmented capacity. Where the analysis differs, identify the framework adjustment that would correct it.

2. The chapter argues that the BCG growth-share matrix fails because relative market share cannot be measured against an unmeasurable disruptor population. Construct an alternative metric that would surface the deterioration the matrix misses. What data sources would you need? What refresh cadence?

3. The chapter holds that the jobs-to-be-done framework misses the inflection because the job itself reshapes when capability collapses cost. For a product category you know well, articulate what the job was pre-inflection, what the job is becoming post-inflection, and which framework adjustments would surface the change at the time of the shift rather than in retrospect.

4. Blue-ocean strategy assumes a strategic window during which the new market space remains uncontested. Under phase-transition propagation, the window collapses. Construct a decision rule that would tell the strategist, before commencing a blue-ocean initiative, whether the window is likely to be sufficient for the initiative to capture the market.

5. The Resource-Based View identifies sustainable competitive advantage in resources that are valuable, rare, inimitable, and non-substitutable. For three resources your firm currently relies on as moats, assess each under post-inflection inimitability conditions. Which retain their moat character? Which do not? What does the firm do about the latter?

## Key Concepts

- **Substrate.** The underlying coordination economics of the competitive environment. Industrial substrates feature high coordination costs and sigmoid capability propagation. Cognition-systems substrates feature collapsed coordination costs and phase-transition propagation.
- **Framework Calibration.** The set of empirical conditions under which a strategic framework produces correct conclusions. Calibration is independent of the framework's logical structure; a correctly-derived framework can produce wrong conclusions when applied outside its calibration range.
- **Inimitability Audit.** The systematic re-examination of a firm's strategic resources against post-inflection conditions, to identify which resources retain moat character and which do not.
