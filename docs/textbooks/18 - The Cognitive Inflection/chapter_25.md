# Chapter 25: The ERP Disruption — A Worked Case

Part V opens with the application of the book's analytical framework to a specific worked case. The case is the disruption of an ERP incumbent by a one-person operator, presented in end-to-end detail from the operator's initial market observation through the incumbent's eventual loss of strategic position. The case is, like the Hartwell and Stillwater cases of Parts III and IV, composed; the firms involved are not specific actual firms, and the specific identifying details are not those of any single firm. The patterns the case dramatizes have been observed across multiple actual sequences whose trajectories conform to the case's structure. The case's instructional value is in the pattern.

The case is presented from two simultaneous perspectives: the disruptor's, in which a single operator executes the assassination pipeline against an incumbent ERP product line, and the incumbent's, in which the incumbent's management observes the disruption unfold without recognizing it in time to respond. The dual perspective is the case's analytical contribution; most cases present either the disruptor's view or the incumbent's view, and the dual presentation surfaces the temporal mismatch between the two sides' awareness that is, in the chapter's analysis, the central feature of cognition-systems disruption.

## The Incumbent's Pre-Disruption Position

The incumbent in the case is a mid-tier ERP vendor — not SAP, not Oracle, not Microsoft, but a competing firm in the same competitive set, with annual revenue of approximately $400 million, approximately 1,200 employees, and approximately 2,500 customers. The firm's product is a multi-tenant SaaS ERP offering targeted at mid-market manufacturers in North America and Europe, with the typical customer being a manufacturing firm of 50 to 500 employees. The firm's revenue is approximately 40 percent license subscription, 50 percent implementation consulting, and 10 percent support and maintenance. The firm has been profitable for seventeen consecutive years and has been growing at approximately 8 percent annually for the past five.

The firm's competitive position, as understood by its management at the start of the case period, is strong. The firm's product compares favorably to the larger incumbents on customization depth (the firm's vertical-specific ERP modules are more tailored than the larger incumbents' generic equivalents), on implementation timeline (the firm's typical implementation runs 12 to 18 months, against the larger incumbents' 18 to 36 months), and on customer satisfaction (the firm's published customer-satisfaction scores are consistently above the industry median). The firm's strategy, as documented in its most recent strategic plan, is to continue serving the mid-market manufacturer segment with steady product improvement, geographic expansion in Europe, and selective vertical expansion in the automotive parts and food processing categories.

The firm's management team includes a CEO who has been in the role for nine years, a CTO who has been in the role for seven years, a CFO who has been in the role for five years, and a head of product who has been in the role for three years. The team is, by all available assessment, competent at the operational demands of running the firm at its current scale. The team's executive performance over the past five years has been consistently in line with the board's expectations. The team's strategic perception of the cognition-systems inflection, as documented in the firm's strategic-planning artifacts, is approximately the perception Davis Hartwell exhibited at the start of the Hartwell case: AI is a meaningful technology, the firm is investing in AI capabilities within its existing product, and the firm's competitive position is not materially threatened by AI-enabled new entrants.

## The Disruptor's Initial Observation

The disruptor in the case is an individual senior practitioner — male, 41 years old at the start of the case period, with 14 years of prior experience at large enterprise software firms including a 6-year stint at one of the incumbent's larger competitors. The practitioner had departed his prior employer 18 months before the start of the case, with the intention of building an independent practice in the mid-market ERP space. The practitioner had spent the intervening 18 months building his initial deployment template, developing his sales methodology, and acquiring his first three pilot customers in the small-manufacturer segment.

The practitioner's initial observation about the incumbent was made during a sales conversation with a 75-employee manufacturer who had been quoted approximately $400,000 by the incumbent for the implementation of the incumbent's ERP product, against an additional $60,000 annual subscription, on an implementation timeline of approximately 14 months. The manufacturer's CFO had declined the incumbent's quote on the grounds of cost and timeline; the manufacturer had been operating on a combination of QuickBooks and Excel for the past several years and had been seeking a replacement that the manufacturer could afford. The practitioner observed, after the conversation, that the incumbent's pricing and timeline were calibrated to a cost structure that the practitioner's own deployment model did not share, and that the manufacturer's segment was consequently structurally available to the practitioner's offering at a price point and timeline the incumbent could not match.

## The Disruptor's Deployment

The practitioner executed his initial deployment against the manufacturer over a 12-day period in the second month of the case period. The deployment followed the assembly-line pipeline the SAS textbook describes: schema design from the manufacturer's data model, database migration generation, type definition, repository pattern, IPC handlers, bridge code, store integration, UI components, and end-to-end integration. The practitioner's working time on the deployment was approximately 70 hours, distributed across the 12 calendar days with breaks for customer-development calls and personal time. The practitioner's AI tooling subscription cost during the deployment was approximately $35, and the cloud-infrastructure costs were under $10.

The deployment included: finance and accounting modules with the manufacturer's specific chart of accounts; inventory management with the manufacturer's specific SKU and warehouse structure; procurement with the manufacturer's specific supplier base; manufacturing-floor management with the manufacturer's specific work centers and routings; quality control with the manufacturer's specific inspection protocols; and a BabyAI integration providing the manufacturer with cross-functional analytics, anomaly detection, and natural-language data access. The deployment included migration of three years of historical data from the manufacturer's prior QuickBooks installation and Excel files.

The practitioner billed the manufacturer $35,000 for the implementation, against the incumbent's quoted $400,000. The practitioner contracted for $1,000 monthly support and incremental customization, against the incumbent's quoted $5,000 monthly subscription. The total first-year cost to the manufacturer was $47,000, against the incumbent's quoted $460,000. The cost ratio was approximately 10:1 in the manufacturer's favor.

## The Incumbent's First Awareness

The incumbent's first awareness of the disruption did not occur during the case's deployment. The manufacturer who chose the practitioner's deployment over the incumbent's was, from the incumbent's perspective, a declined quote among thousands of declined quotes the incumbent's sales organization processed each year. The decline was recorded in the incumbent's CRM under the category "price-sensitive customer, lost on cost" — a category the incumbent's sales analytics treated as essentially noise.

The incumbent's first specific awareness occurred approximately 11 months later, when a second manufacturer — a 110-employee manufacturer in an adjacent vertical — declined the incumbent's quote with explicit reference to "a one-person provider who can do the same work for one-tenth the price." The decline was recorded by the incumbent's account executive with a follow-up note flagging the citation of the disruptor. The note was forwarded to the regional sales director, who forwarded it to the VP of sales, who flagged it in the firm's monthly sales review. The review's discussion of the note lasted approximately five minutes; the VP of sales characterized it as "a fringe alternative that occasionally appears in price-sensitive opportunities." The firm's management did not initiate a structured investigation.

The incumbent's first structured awareness occurred approximately seven months later, when the firm's customer-success team reported that two existing customers had requested early-termination discussions in connection with cheaper alternatives. The early-termination discussions, on follow-up, were both connected to one-person-operator deployments that the customers had piloted in adjacent business units before deciding to consolidate on the alternative. The customer-success team's report was elevated to the head of product and the CFO, who initiated a competitive-intelligence engagement with an external consulting firm.

The competitive-intelligence engagement, completed approximately four months after its initiation, identified approximately 47 one-person and small-partnership ERP alternatives serving the firm's customer segments, with aggregate revenue estimated at approximately $8 million and growing at approximately 200 percent annually. The engagement's strategic recommendation was that the firm should monitor the segment closely, consider acquiring one or more of the leading alternatives, and ensure that the firm's product roadmap addressed the customization-economics axis the alternatives were exploiting. The recommendation was incorporated into the firm's next annual strategic plan as a "monitor and respond" priority.

## The Disruptor's Compound Growth

While the incumbent was working through its initial awareness, the disruptor was compounding. The practitioner's first deployment, completed in month two of the case period, was followed by four additional deployments in the subsequent six months (approximately one every six weeks), then by twelve deployments in the subsequent twelve months (approximately one every four weeks), then by twenty deployments in the subsequent twelve months (approximately one every two and a half weeks). The increasing deployment pace reflected the practitioner's improving template, his accumulating customer references, and his growing word-of-mouth referral network.

At the close of the case's third year, the practitioner had completed 47 deployments. His annual recurring revenue from the deployments was approximately $620,000, against operating costs of approximately $25,000. His annual cash income, after taxes, was approximately $400,000. His customer base spanned three states and four manufacturing verticals. His referral network was producing approximately 60 percent of his new customer acquisition. His operational time was approximately 35 hours per week. His vacation time was approximately five weeks per year. His professional satisfaction, by his own report, was high.

## The Incumbent's Late Awakening

The incumbent's late awakening, in approximately month 36 of the case period, occurred when the firm's CFO produced the firm's annual revenue analysis and identified a structural deceleration in the firm's mid-market segment growth. The deceleration, which had been masked in prior quarters by the firm's continued growth in the enterprise-tier segment, was now visible at annual resolution. The mid-market segment, which had been growing at approximately 12 percent annually for the prior five years, had grown at approximately 4 percent in the most recent year. The CFO's analysis identified, on close examination, that the deceleration was attributable not to declining unit volume (the firm was still adding customers at approximately the prior rate) but to declining average revenue per customer, as the firm's competitive pricing pressure forced the firm to discount more aggressively against the disruptor population.

The CFO's analysis was presented to the board at the firm's Q4 board meeting in month 39 of the case period. The board's response was the strategic-review commission that, in the Hartwell case, would have been the appropriate response. The strategic review, completed approximately five months later, produced the same recommendations Karen Voss's memo had produced in the Hartwell case: substantial restructuring of the firm's product architecture toward determinism-contract single-tenant deployment, substantial restructuring of the firm's compensation toward partnership-based models for senior practitioners, substantial restructuring of the firm's customer relationships toward longer-term retainer arrangements, and substantial restructuring of the firm's relationship with the disruptor population toward the ecosystem posture.

The board's response to the recommendations was substantively different from the Hartwell board's response. The board accepted the recommendations and authorized the restructuring, with a budget of approximately $40 million over three years. The restructuring began in month 45 of the case period.

The restructuring's outcome is, at the close of the case material, partial and ongoing. The firm has retained approximately 65 percent of its pre-disruption mid-market customer base, has captured approximately 30 percent of the high-end of the disruptor-served segment through its restructured product line, and has lost approximately 35 percent of the mid-market position to the disruptor population. The firm's annual revenue at the close of the case material is approximately $340 million, against the pre-disruption peak of approximately $460 million. The firm's profit margin is materially compressed but positive. The firm continues to operate as a viable mid-tier ERP vendor; the firm is materially smaller than it was at the case's start and is occupying a different competitive position than it occupied previously.

## The Financial Scorecard

The case's quantitative scorecard, summarized for the analytical reader, is:

Disruptor's cumulative deployments: 47 across three years.
Disruptor's cumulative revenue: approximately $2.1 million across three years.
Disruptor's cumulative cost: approximately $90,000 across three years.
Disruptor's cumulative profit: approximately $2 million across three years.

Incumbent's revenue decline from peak: approximately $120 million (26 percent of peak).
Incumbent's restructuring investment: approximately $40 million over three years.
Incumbent's mid-market customer loss: approximately 35 percent of the segment.
Incumbent's late-but-substantive response: production of a viable post-inflection position at materially reduced scale.

Customer-side comparison: customer who chose the disruptor over the incumbent paid approximately $47,000 in first-year cost against the incumbent's $460,000 quote. The customer comparison ratio of 10:1 was consistent across the case's deployments.

## Discussion Questions

1. The case dramatizes the temporal mismatch between the disruptor's awareness and the incumbent's. For your firm, estimate the current mismatch — when did the disruptor population begin operating, when did the firm first become aware, when did the firm first take structured action? What is the mismatch's value cost?

2. The incumbent's first awareness, first structured awareness, and late awakening occurred at months 13, 17, and 36 of the case period respectively. For your firm's industry, identify the equivalent timeline. At what month is your firm currently?

3. The incumbent's eventual response — restructuring with a $40 million budget — produced a viable but materially reduced post-inflection position. For your firm, sketch the equivalent restructuring. What is the budget? What is the timeline? What is the projected post-restructuring position?

4. The disruptor's compound growth — from 1 to 47 deployments over three years — reflects the practitioner's improving template, accumulating references, and growing word-of-mouth network. For the disruptor population in your firm's market, estimate the current compound growth rate. What is the trajectory?

5. The customer comparison ratio of 10:1 was consistent across the case's deployments. For your firm's pricing, estimate the equivalent ratio against a credible disruptor alternative. What is the implication for the firm's pricing strategy?

## Key Concepts

- **The Dual-Perspective Case.** The presentation of disruption from both the disruptor's perspective and the incumbent's perspective simultaneously, surfacing the temporal mismatch in awareness.
- **The Temporal Mismatch.** The structural delay between the disruptor's operational presence and the incumbent's awareness of it. The mismatch is the central feature of cognition-systems disruption.
- **The Three Awareness Stages.** First awareness (passive recording in CRM), first structured awareness (initiation of competitive intelligence), late awakening (board-level recognition). The stages typically occur over a 24-to-36-month period.
- **The Customer Comparison Ratio.** The price ratio between the incumbent's quote and the disruptor's quote. Typically 10:1 in the disruptor's favor.
- **The Partial-Response Outcome.** The viable but materially reduced post-inflection position the late-but-substantive incumbent response produces. The position is preferable to dissolution but is materially below the pre-disruption peak.
