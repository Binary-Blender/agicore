# Chapter 11: The Boil, Returned — Reading the Indicators

The chapter closes Part II by operationalizing the boil metaphor into a working instrument. The strategist who has read Chapters 1 through 10 has, by this point, the diagnostic framework, the substrate analysis, the disruptor-vehicle characterization, and the structural-trap inventory. What the strategist does not yet have is a weekly-cadence reading apparatus that converts the analytical framework into an operating discipline. The chapter provides the apparatus. The chapter is, in a sense, the practical complement to Chapter 1's introductory framing: Chapter 1 named the boil; Chapter 11 teaches the strategist to read it.

The instrument has six categories of indicators, each corresponding to a category Chapter 1 sketched and each developed here in operational detail. The categories are capability releases, tooling adoption, economic outliers, labor flows, customer behavior, and infrastructure adoption. The chapter takes each in turn, identifies the specific signals that constitute the category, names the sources from which the signals can be gathered, and proposes the refresh cadence at which the strategist should consult each source. The chapter then provides the convergence test that integrates the six categories into a single weekly read and proposes the trigger thresholds at which the strategist should escalate to the board.

The **capability release category** monitors the frontier-AI providers' product release cadence and the benchmark performance of their successive releases. The relevant signals are: announcements of new foundation-model versions, benchmark performance against industry-standard test suites (HumanEval, SWE-bench, MMLU, and their successors), context-window expansions, latency reductions, and pricing reductions. The sources are: the providers' public release announcements, the major benchmark leaderboards, the analyst reports issued by the small number of research firms covering the AI infrastructure layer, and the technical blogs of the providers' senior engineering staff. The refresh cadence is weekly; the providers' release cadence has accelerated to the point where weekly monitoring is the appropriate resolution. The signal pattern the strategist watches for is a capability jump that exceeds the trailing four-week trend by more than approximately 50%. Such jumps have occurred at irregular intervals over the past three years and each has materially shifted the disruptor's capacity.

The **tooling adoption category** monitors the spread of the tools that operationalize AI capability for professional developers. The relevant signals are: GitHub star and fork counts on the leading agentic-coding tools, package-manager download statistics, employer-job-posting mentions of specific tools, technical-conference talk acceptance rates on specific tool categories, and the appearance of named tools in the technical-blog content of the major engineering firms. The sources are: GitHub directly, npm and PyPI statistics, the LinkedIn and Indeed job-posting data, conference programs (KubeCon, AWS re:Invent, the AI-specific conferences), and the technical-blog feeds of the major firms. The refresh cadence is weekly. The signal pattern is the appearance of a previously-unmentioned tool in three or more of the source categories within a single week, which typically indicates the tool is entering its transition stage.

The **economic outliers category** monitors the financial profiles of one-person and small-team software businesses. The relevant signals are: annual-recurring-revenue-per-employee figures from the indie-SaaS community, exit valuations of one-person firms, fundraising rounds for small-team SaaS companies, customer-count milestones reported by solo founders, and the appearance of new categories in the indie-SaaS revenue distributions. The sources are: IndieHackers, MicroAcquire, the public revenue dashboards a meaningful number of solo founders maintain, the technical-Substack ecosystem, and the X/Twitter-equivalent feeds of the prominent solo founders. The refresh cadence is weekly. The signal pattern is a step-change in the median or upper-quartile of the revenue-per-employee distribution, which typically indicates that the productivity of the operator population has shifted.

The **labor flows category** monitors the migration of senior practitioners from incumbent firms to independent operation. The relevant signals are: LinkedIn profile-update statistics in the senior-engineer cohort, departure announcements from major incumbent firms, indie-SaaS founder-formation rates, the appearance of LLC formations in the relevant categories in the state-business-registry data, and the staff-list movements at the firms the strategist's own firm competes with. The sources are: LinkedIn directly, the technical-press coverage of senior departures, the indie-SaaS community announcements, the state-business-registry feeds (where available), and the strategist's own firm's HR data on staff movements at competitors. The refresh cadence is weekly. The signal pattern is a cluster of senior departures from a single incumbent within a single month, which typically indicates that the incumbent's senior cohort has crossed an internal threshold.

The **customer behavior category** monitors the procurement-side signals from the enterprise buyer base. The relevant signals are: RFP cancellations on active incumbent procurements, requests for disruptor-style alternatives in active RFPs, contract non-renewals at the incumbent firms the strategist tracks, and procurement-officer commentary on the new alternatives. The sources are: the strategist's own firm's sales pipeline data on lost deals (which the firm should be analyzing for cancellation reasons), industry trade press coverage of major contract events, procurement-officer community channels (where accessible), and the strategist's own firm's customer-success interview data. The refresh cadence is monthly at minimum; for the strategist's own firm's data, weekly is appropriate. The signal pattern is a cluster of cancellations or non-renewals citing customization-economics reasons rather than feature reasons. The chapter notes that the firm's existing pipeline data is the strategist's most valuable source in this category and that most firms are not currently analyzing their pipeline data with the right questions in mind.

The **infrastructure adoption category** monitors the spread of determinism-contract architecture and its alternatives. The relevant signals are: open-source contributions to the leading deterministic-substrate projects, customer deployments of contract-bearing systems in compliance-heavy verticals, regulatory-agency commentary on AI-augmented software, and the appearance of contract-style architecture in incumbent product announcements. The sources are: the open-source repositories of the leading projects, the technical-blog coverage of contract-bearing deployments, regulatory publications (FDA, FINRA, OCC, equivalent international bodies), and the press-release feeds of the incumbent firms. The refresh cadence is monthly. The signal pattern is the appearance of contract-architecture vocabulary in two or more of the source categories within a single month, which typically indicates that the architecture is entering the mainstream adoption stage.

The **convergence test** integrates the six categories. The test is a weekly aggregation of the signal counts across the categories, with the convergence event defined as four or more categories returning positive signals within the same week. The convergence event is the strategist's escalation trigger; the strategist who observes a convergence event escalates the reading to the board and presents the response options the firm has prepared. The reasoning behind the four-of-six threshold is empirical: positive signals in fewer than four categories typically reflect category-specific noise rather than substrate-wide phase transition; positive signals in four or more categories typically reflect the underlying substrate's movement. The threshold is not absolute; the strategist's judgment is required to distinguish meaningful convergence from coincidental noise. The four-of-six threshold is a working heuristic.

The chapter offers two worked readings as illustrations of the instrument in operation. The first reading is a fictional ERP incumbent at the close of the prior fiscal year. The second is a fictional CRM incumbent in the present fiscal quarter. Both readings are based on the patterns the chapter has observed across multiple actual firms in the relevant categories; both are presented as illustrative composites.

**Worked reading one: the ERP incumbent at Q4 of Year 0.**

The capability category shows a single positive signal: the release of a new frontier model with measurable benchmark improvement in code-generation tasks relevant to enterprise back-office processes. The tooling category shows a positive signal: an agentic-coding tool optimized for back-office workflow automation has appeared and is being adopted by ERP-implementation consultants at observable rates. The economic outliers category shows two positive signals: a one-person firm selling a manufacturing-vertical ERP alternative has reported $50K MRR, and a small partnership selling a healthcare-vertical ERP alternative has reported $80K MRR. The labor flows category shows two positive signals: a cluster of senior implementation consultants has departed from a major incumbent in the last quarter, and the strategist's own firm has lost three senior architects to independent operation. The customer behavior category shows one positive signal: a multi-million-dollar contract for the incumbent's ERP product has been cancelled mid-deployment in favor of a one-person alternative, with the cancellation reason cited as customization-economics. The infrastructure category shows no positive signals; determinism-contract architecture is still in the latency stage in the ERP segment.

The aggregate count is five of six categories positive. The convergence threshold has been met. The strategist escalates to the board with the diagnosis that the inflection has begun in the ERP segment and the response window has opened. The board's available actions, given the four-to-seven-quarter response window the diagnosis implies, are the actions Parts III and IV will develop. The illustrative timeline: the ERP incumbent that escalates at Q4 of Year 0 has approximately six quarters before the disruption is material to the incumbent's revenue and approximately eight quarters before the disruption is material to the incumbent's market position. The strategist who escalated at Q4 of Year 0 has the response window in front of the firm. The strategist who failed to escalate at Q4 of Year 0 — because the firm was not monitoring the relevant categories or because the strategist did not have the convergence test in place — will escalate at Q2 of Year 1 instead, having lost the first two quarters of the response window to undetected drift.

**Worked reading two: the CRM incumbent at the present fiscal quarter.**

The capability category shows two positive signals: a frontier-model release with improved capacity for sales-pipeline reasoning, and a benchmark improvement in conversational-AI tasks relevant to CRM workflows. The tooling category shows two positive signals: an agentic-coding tool focused on CRM customization has appeared, and an integration framework specifically targeting Salesforce-replacement deployments has been released. The economic outliers category shows three positive signals: a one-person CRM alternative has crossed $200K MRR with eighteen customers, a small partnership has acquired a $1.2M ARR portfolio of mid-market CRM customers, and the leading indie-SaaS index has reported a step-change in the upper-quartile revenue-per-employee figure. The labor flows category shows three positive signals: substantial senior departures from two major CRM incumbents, the formation of named small partnerships from the departed cohort, and the strategist's own firm's loss of senior account executives to independent CRM-replacement firms. The customer behavior category shows two positive signals: two major customer non-renewals citing customization-economics, and a procurement-officer community channel has begun circulating disruptor recommendations. The infrastructure category shows one positive signal: a determinism-contract deployment in financial services has passed SOC 2 audit and the case has been circulated in the analyst community.

The aggregate count is six of six categories positive. The convergence is unambiguous; the inflection in the CRM segment is past the latency stage and in the transition stage. The strategist who escalates at this point is escalating late; the response window has compressed to approximately three to four quarters. The illustrative implication: the CRM incumbent at the present fiscal quarter has months rather than years to begin the response. The response capacity the firm has built in advance — through the diagnostic instrumentation of Part I, the substrate analysis of Part II, and the response readiness of Parts III and IV — determines whether the response begins inside the remaining window or outside it.

The chapter closes Part II by handing the strategist the instrument and the working examples. The strategist who has built the instrument, who is operating it at weekly cadence, and who has the response readiness of Parts III and IV in place will detect inflections in time to respond. The strategist who has not built the instrument will detect inflections only through trailing indicators — customer losses, revenue declines, analyst downgrades — and the trailing indicators print after the response window has closed. The instrument is the difference between the two outcomes.

The instrument the chapter has handed the strategist reads the inflection at the present moment. The next chapter closes Part II by extending the reading across the dimension the present chapter has not addressed: the dimension of time. The disruption's mechanics, the chapter that follows demonstrates, do not stand still while the strategist deliberates. They compound. The strategist who has finished Chapter 12 has the diagnosis in full, including the trajectory the diagnosis is following. The book then proceeds to Part III, in which the strategist's response posture is developed. The boil has been read. The diagnosis will be complete at the close of Part II. The next part is the action.

## Key Concepts

- **The Six Indicator Categories.** Capability releases, tooling adoption, economic outliers, labor flows, customer behavior, infrastructure adoption.
- **The Convergence Test.** The aggregation rule that integrates the six categories into a single weekly read. Four-of-six positive signals within a single week is the escalation trigger.
- **The Latency Reading vs. Transition Reading.** Distinct stages of the inflection that the instrument differentiates. The strategist's response posture differs by stage.
- **The Pipeline Diagnostic.** The firm's own sales-pipeline data as the most valuable source in the customer behavior category. Most firms underuse this data because they are not asking the right questions.
- **Trailing Indicator Failure.** The failure mode of relying on revenue declines, customer losses, and analyst downgrades as detection signals. These print after the response window has closed.
