# Chapter 14: The Hartwell Case

This chapter presents a formal case study in the Harvard tradition. The case is Hartwell Digital, a Boston-based custom enterprise software consultancy founded in 1998 and dissolved, effectively if not legally, by the spring of Year 3 of the inflection. The case is composed; the firm's name and the specific identifying details are not those of any single actual firm. The patterns the case dramatizes have been observed across multiple actual firms whose responses to the inflection have followed the trajectory the chapter describes. The composition permits the chapter to develop the patterns with the analytical clarity the classroom requires, without the source-attribution complications a single-firm case would carry. The chapter's instructional value is in the pattern, not in the firm.

The case is presented in four sections, corresponding to the firm's situation, the precipitating event sequence, the management response, and the outcome. The case is followed by discussion questions, key-concept identification, and a brief instructor's note that the reader can use to lead a graduate-level case-method classroom discussion.

## The Situation

Hartwell Digital was founded in 1998 by Edward Hartwell, a former IBM consultant who had recognized the opportunity to serve the financial-services and life-sciences clients clustered along the Boston-to-Cambridge corridor with custom enterprise software development. The firm grew steadily through the 2000s, weathered the 2008 financial crisis with the loss of approximately a third of its staff, and rebuilt through the 2010s into a stable 220-person consultancy occupying four floors of a Class A tower in the Financial District. The firm's reputation was technical excellence, slow delivery, and high prices; its engagements typically ran into seven figures and lasted twelve to eighteen months.

Edward Hartwell retired in Year minus-5 to a Cape Cod home, transferring the CEO role to his son Davis. Davis Hartwell was forty-eight at the start of the case, a Wharton MBA with prior consulting experience at a Big Four firm, married to a Newton-based real-estate broker named Audrey, with two children in private school in Cambridge. Davis was, by all available assessment, competent at the role's standard demands. He could read a P&L. He could manage a leadership team. He could maintain the client relationships his father had built. He was not, the case will demonstrate, capable of reading what the inflection was doing to the firm's labor market.

The firm's COO at the start of the case was Karen Voss, fifty-one years old, eight years at the firm, recruited from a competing consultancy by Edward Hartwell during the transition planning. Karen was widely regarded as the operational backbone of the firm. She had been responsible for the post-2008 rebuild and for the systems modernization that had carried the firm through the 2010s. She had, the case will demonstrate, the analytical capacity to read the inflection and the institutional position to act on the reading. What she did not have was Davis Hartwell's willingness to take the reading seriously.

The firm's board at the start of the case was chaired by Geoffrey Stansfield, a seventy-two-year-old former Bain partner who had been Edward Hartwell's college roommate and who had served on the board since the firm's founding. The remaining board members were two of the firm's senior client relationships at the executive level — the CFO of a Cambridge biotech and the COO of a Boston insurance carrier — and an outside director recruited from the New England consulting community. The board's composition reflected the firm's historical strategic stability; it did not include any director with significant exposure to the cognition-systems substrate or to the post-inflection labor market.

## The Precipitating Event Sequence

The case opens in October of Year 1 with three resignation events on a single Tuesday. The first was Priya Ramaswamy, a thirty-one-year-old senior engineer with four years at the firm, specializing in healthcare-compliance work. The second was Aliyah Hassan, a twenty-eight-year-old junior developer with two years at the firm, who had given notice the previous Friday. The third was a senior engineer with eight years at the firm whose departure was announced by email at 10:47 AM that Tuesday morning. Davis Hartwell processed each as a discrete event. He did not, on that Tuesday, perceive a pattern.

The firm's HR director, Brian Wexler, reported to Davis that the firm had received nine resignations in the prior six weeks, which Wexler characterized as "above the trailing average but within normal range." The trailing twelve-month average had been four to six resignations per quarter; the prior six weeks alone had matched the entire prior quarter. The framing — "within normal range" — was the first of several framing failures the case will examine. The framing rested on a comparison that no longer applied; the prior averages had been generated under labor-market conditions that the inflection had ended, and using those averages as the reference distribution for the current period was a statistical error of substantial magnitude. The error was not unusual; firms across the industry made the equivalent error in the same period.

Karen Voss attempted, on the afternoon of that Tuesday, to raise the resignation pattern with Davis. She suggested they discuss the trajectory at a working session that week. Davis deferred the conversation to the following week, citing the Stansfield client call at five. The deferral was not malicious; Davis was, on that Tuesday, attending to what he believed were the firm's most pressing demands. The case's instructional point is that the deferral was, in retrospect, the moment at which the response window opened, and the deferral consumed the first week of the window.

The pattern continued through November and December of Year 1. The firm received fourteen resignations in Q4, against the trailing-twelve-month average of eight. The departures were concentrated in the senior tier; of the fourteen, eleven had been with the firm for four or more years. The departing employees, when asked their reasons through the firm's standard exit-interview process, cited compensation in approximately thirty percent of cases, management or culture in approximately twenty percent, growth opportunity in approximately twenty percent, and a category Brian Wexler's HR team classified as "other" in approximately thirty percent. The "other" category was, on closer examination, departures to independent operation. The HR team did not classify departures to independent operation as a distinct category, because the prior averages had not produced enough such departures to warrant a category. The classification system was, in this case as in many across the industry, a reflection of the prior labor market that no longer applied.

The Q4 board meeting, held in mid-December, included a brief HR update from Brian Wexler. Wexler reported the elevated resignation count and characterized it as "tight labor market plus the AI startup phenomenon." Geoffrey Stansfield, chairing, asked Davis whether the firm needed to take action. Davis responded that the labor market would normalize and the firm's standard retention practices were appropriate to the situation. The board moved on. Karen Voss, present at the meeting, did not raise objection; she had attempted to raise the issue privately with Davis and had been deferred, and she judged that raising it publicly without Davis's prior support would have been counterproductive.

The case continues through Year 2 with the pattern intensifying. By the end of Q1, the firm had lost an additional eleven senior employees. By the end of Q2, an additional fourteen. By the end of Q3, an additional sixteen. The cumulative loss across the four quarters covering the start of the case was approximately fifty-five senior practitioners, against the trailing-twelve-month average of twenty. The firm's senior tier had been depleted by approximately a third.

In February of Year 2, Karen Voss completed and delivered to Davis a five-page memo titled "The Talent Migration Problem and What We Should Do About It." The memo identified the pattern as a structural change in the labor market rather than as elevated turnover. The memo proposed five specific responses: equity restructuring toward a principal-partner tier with profit participation, a four-day workweek pilot for engineering, retainer-based pricing replacing the firm's hourly billing model, focus on relationship and embedded-team engagements over commodity development work, and a strategic review of the firm's pricing structure. The proposed responses, in retrospect, were substantively correct; they map closely to the response Parts III and IV of the present book develop. Davis Hartwell read the memo, thanked Karen for her work, and brought the memo to the board for the following week's discussion.

The board reviewed the memo for approximately forty-five minutes. Geoffrey Stansfield's response, after the review, was that the firm had operated on its existing model since 1998 and the model had produced consistent returns through multiple prior cycles. The market, in Stansfield's framing, would normalize. The board's vote, taken in approximately the form of a poll rather than a formal motion, was unanimous against acting on Karen's recommendations. Davis returned to Karen and reported the outcome. Karen accepted the board's decision and continued to operate as COO.

## The Management Response

The Hartwell management's response across the period covered by the case can be characterized in three failures.

The first failure was the failure to recognize the pattern. The firm's quarterly resignation data, presented in the form of departure counts against trailing averages, was structured to dilute the signal the data contained. The pattern of senior-tier concentration was not surfaced by the standard reporting; the standard reporting aggregated departures across tiers and obscured the concentration. The pattern of departures to independent operation was not surfaced by the standard exit-interview classification; the classification used pre-inflection categories and reported the post-inflection phenomenon under "other." Davis Hartwell, reading the standard reports, was looking at numbers that did not show what was happening. The failure to recognize was, in this sense, a failure of the firm's reporting apparatus rather than of Davis's analytical capacity. The firm's reporting apparatus was, however, Davis's responsibility, and the failure to revise the apparatus when its premises had changed was a failure of executive attention.

The second failure was the failure to act on the recognition when Karen Voss provided it. The February memo identified the pattern, characterized it correctly as structural, and proposed substantively correct responses. The memo's reception by Davis was sympathetic, by the board was dismissive, and by the firm as a whole was non-occurrent because the memo did not produce action. The failure to act was, in this sense, a failure of the governance structure rather than of any individual director. The governance structure was, however, the firm's responsibility, and the failure to restructure it when its composition had become inappropriate to the inflection was a failure of executive courage. Davis Hartwell did not advocate for the memo against the board; he accepted the board's vote and reported the outcome to Karen. The acceptance was the load-bearing failure.

The third failure was the failure to read what the failure to act meant. Karen Voss continued as COO for fourteen months after the memo was rejected, during which time she maintained a private spreadsheet of departures, departures' reasons, and the customer relationships the departed employees had taken with them. The spreadsheet, in February of Year 3, ran to forty-six entries. The customer relationships the departed employees had taken with them — or had, in some cases, taken to the small partnerships and independent firms they had founded — totaled approximately a third of the firm's recurring revenue. The pattern was no longer subtle; the firm was, by Q4 of Year 2, in the visible phase of its dissolution. Davis Hartwell continued to characterize the trajectory as "elevated turnover" through Q1 of Year 3. The board continued to accept the characterization through the same period. Karen Voss resigned in February of Year 3 to take a chief operating role at a Cambridge consultancy structured along the lines she had proposed in her memo. The Cambridge firm, in subsequent years, captured a meaningful share of Hartwell's former senior practitioner pool.

## The Outcome

By the spring of Year 3, Hartwell Digital was, in effective terms, dissolved. The firm's headcount had fallen from 220 at the start of the case to approximately 90. Four of its top twenty client engagements had not renewed at the prior contract levels; three of those had been transferred to small partnerships founded by departed Hartwell employees. The firm's revenue had fallen to approximately forty percent of the prior baseline. The firm's operating expense base had not adjusted at the same rate; the firm was approaching unprofitability. The eighth and sixteenth floors of the firm's office space had been vacated. The board had begun, in the spring of Year 3, to ask Davis Hartwell questions he had previously been able to avoid.

The firm was not formally dissolved during the period the case covers. The firm continued to operate, at reduced scale and reduced profitability, through the conclusion of the case material. The forward trajectory, on the visible evidence, was continued contraction. The firm's senior management had not produced a response sufficient to reverse the trajectory. The firm's board had not produced a response sufficient to reverse the trajectory. The firm's available actions in the spring of Year 3 — sale, restructuring, pivot — were available at substantially worse terms than they would have been in the spring of Year 2, when Karen's memo had identified the situation. The cost of the eighteen-month delay was, by the case's accounting, approximately the difference between a strategically positioned mid-market consultancy and a contracting firm with limited options. The cost was, in the going-out-of-business framing of the prior chapter, approximately the difference between a Year-2 sale at a meaningful multiple and a Year-3 sale at a discount.

## Discussion Questions

1. Identify the specific decision points at which Davis Hartwell could have produced a different outcome. For each, articulate the information available to him at the time, the decision he made, the alternative decision available, and the probable consequence of the alternative.

2. Karen Voss is, in the case material, the analytical hero — she identified the pattern, proposed substantively correct responses, and produced a written memo that documented her analysis. The case nonetheless ends with the firm in dissolution. What does the case suggest about the limits of analytical correctness in the absence of executive will?

3. The Hartwell board's composition — composed substantially of long-standing relationships and prior-cycle expertise — is identified in the case as a structural contributor to the failure. Sketch the alternative board composition that would have produced a different outcome. What director profiles would the alternative composition include? What governance changes would accompany the recomposition?

4. The case identifies three failures: recognition, action, and reading-the-failure-to-act. Which of the three was the most decisive? If only one had been corrected, which would have produced the greatest improvement in outcome?

5. The firm's reporting apparatus is identified in the case as having structured the resignation data in a way that diluted the signal. Construct, for your firm, the alternative reporting apparatus that would surface the post-inflection labor-market signals with appropriate resolution. What changes to the HR reporting standards? What changes to the exit-interview classification? What changes to the board-meeting agenda?

## Key Concepts

- **The Reporting Apparatus Failure.** The structural failure mode in which the firm's standard reports use pre-inflection categories and aggregate the data in ways that obscure the post-inflection signal.
- **The Governance Composition Failure.** The structural failure mode in which the firm's board is composed of directors whose expertise reflects the prior cycle's strategic problems and whose composition is inadequate to read the new cycle's problems.
- **The Acceptance Failure.** The executive failure mode in which the CEO, having received correct analytical recommendations, accepts the board's rejection rather than advocating for the recommendations against the board.
- **The Eighteen-Month Cost.** The difference in firm value between Year 2 strategic options and Year 3 strategic options for a firm that has not responded to the inflection during the intervening period.

## Instructor's Note

The case is suitable for a single ninety-minute class session at the second-year MBA level. The instructor should distribute the case material in advance and structure the class around the five discussion questions. The case's strength is the multi-actor structure — Davis, Karen, Stansfield, the board — which permits students to debate the appropriate response from multiple seats. The case is not a morality play; students should be discouraged from treating Davis as a villain. Davis is competent at the role's standard demands and is operating in good faith. The case's instructional point is that competence at the role's standard demands is insufficient in the face of a strategic inflection of this magnitude, and the case demonstrates the specific mechanisms by which insufficiency produces dissolution. The case is the cautionary half of a paired case; the constructive half — the Stillwater case — is developed across Chapters 17, 21, and 22.
