# Chapter 22: Perks First — The Stillwater First Move

The chapter takes up the first of the two Stillwater response moves. The move is the perks-first restructuring of the firm's compensation, benefits, and working-conditions package, intended to slow the departure rate of the firm's senior practitioners while the deeper restructuring of the second move is being designed. The chapter analyzes the move in operations-research detail: what specific components, what costs, what returns, what limitations, and why the move's explicit acknowledgment of its own insufficiency is the load-bearing piece of the framing.

The chapter draws explicitly on the Untied case material for the move's operational specification. The case material has been validated against multiple actual firms whose first-move restructurings followed approximately the Stillwater pattern; the patterns are sufficiently consistent that the case can be treated as representative rather than idiosyncratic. The chapter develops the move at the level of detail required for the strategist to execute approximately the same move at the strategist's own firm.

## The Move's Six Components

The Stillwater first move consisted of six components, announced together at the firm's March all-hands meeting of Year 2 and taking effect across the subsequent sixty to ninety days. The components were designed to address the specific elements of the firm's retention bundle that, under post-inflection labor-market conditions, had become competitively inadequate. The components were not chosen at random; each was selected on analysis of the firm's specific competitive position against the operator-economy outside option.

The **first component** was the compensation restructure. The firm's salary bands across all engineering and customer-facing roles were raised by ten percent above the Twin Cities market median, with the raise applied to existing employees retroactively to the first of the fiscal year. The raise's purpose was to close the gap between Stillwater's compensation and the competing-employer alternatives that some practitioners would consider as transitions. The raise did not close the gap to the independent-operation outside option; the gap to independent operation is materially larger than ten percent, and Stillwater's first move did not attempt to match the operator-economy outside option on compensation alone. The compensation restructure addressed the inter-employer competition, not the employer-vs-independent competition. The annualized cost of the compensation restructure was approximately $1.4 million against the firm's pre-restructure compensation base of $14 million, or approximately ten percent of base compensation in line with the salary-band increase.

The **second component** was the introduction of profit-sharing across all employees, rather than only at the executive tier. The profit-sharing structure allocated approximately ten percent of pre-tax profit to the employee pool, distributed quarterly, with the allocation weighted by tenure and role. The component's purpose was to align the staff's economic interest with the firm's economic performance and to signal that the firm's success would be shared with the staff rather than captured by the executive tier. The signal's effect was substantial; the post-introduction surveys of staff sentiment showed a measurable improvement in the staff's identification with the firm's success. The annualized cost of the profit-sharing component was approximately $700,000 to $1,200,000 depending on the firm's profit performance, against the prior structure in which the same amount would have been captured by the executive tier or retained as undistributed profit.

The **third component** was the four-day workweek pilot for the engineering department, with the pilot to run for six months and be evaluated for continuation or modification at the end of the pilot. The component's purpose was to provide the engineering staff with the flexibility that the independent-operation outside option provides as a structural feature. The pilot's design held the engineering staff's compensation constant while reducing the expected weekly hours by twenty percent, with the expectation that the AI-augmented productivity gains the firm had been investing in would absorb the reduced hours without proportional output loss. The pilot's measured results, at the six-month evaluation, showed that the engineering department's total output declined by approximately five to eight percent against the prior baseline rather than the twenty percent the naive math would have predicted, indicating that the AI-augmented productivity gains absorbed approximately two-thirds of the reduced hours. The cost of the pilot was approximately the value of the five-to-eight-percent output reduction, partially offset by the reduced sick leave, the improved retention, and the recruiting advantage the pilot produced.

The **fourth component** was the introduction of fully-flexible schedules with no questions on remote-vs-office arrangements. The component's purpose was to remove the firm's prior implicit expectation of office-centric work, which had become a meaningful constraint on the firm's appeal relative to the independent-operation outside option. The component's cost was negligible in direct terms; the indirect cost — the loss of in-person collaboration time the prior structure had implicitly produced — was real but small. The component's value was substantial; staff sentiment surveys showed the component to be among the most positively received of the six.

The **fifth component** was the doubling of parental leave from the firm's prior six weeks to twelve weeks, with the leave available equally to all parents regardless of role or gender. The component's purpose was to address the life-stage realities of the firm's senior practitioner population, a meaningful fraction of which was at the family-formation life stage and had been calculating the trade-off between firm employment and independent operation in part on the basis of parental-leave provisions. The component's cost was approximately $150,000 annually, against the value of substantially improved retention in the family-formation cohort.

The **sixth component** was the introduction of a sabbatical entitlement at five years of tenure, providing eligible practitioners with a six-week paid sabbatical with no work expectations during the sabbatical period. The component's purpose was to provide the senior practitioner population with the periodic-reset capacity that independent operation provides as a structural feature, and to signal the firm's investment in the long-term durability of the senior-tier relationships. The component's cost was approximately $200,000 to $300,000 annually, scaling with the tenure distribution of the eligible population.

The six components, taken together, increased the firm's annualized compensation-and-benefits cost by approximately $2.5 million to $3.5 million, against the firm's pre-restructure cost of approximately $14 million. The increase was approximately 18 to 25 percent of base compensation. The increase's funding came from a combination of: the firm's prior profit margin, which absorbed approximately a third of the increase; the AI-augmented productivity gains, which absorbed approximately a third; and the retention-driven reduction in recruiting and training cost, which absorbed approximately a third. The firm's net profit margin contracted by approximately two percentage points in the first year of the restructure, against the alternative scenario in which the restructure had not occurred and the senior practitioner attrition had continued at the pre-restructure rate.

## The Move's Returns

The move's measured returns, at the six-month evaluation point, were:

A senior practitioner attrition rate of approximately six percent annualized, against the pre-restructure rate of approximately twenty-two percent annualized. The reduction in attrition was approximately seventy percent of the gap between the pre-restructure rate and the firm's historical baseline of approximately eight percent. The restructure did not return the attrition rate to the historical baseline; it captured most of the gap.

A staff sentiment improvement of approximately twenty percentage points on the standard engagement-survey instruments, with the improvement concentrated in the components related to the four-day workweek, the flexible scheduling, and the profit-sharing. The compensation increase produced positive sentiment but in a less concentrated way; the other components had been more competitively differentiating.

A measured productivity decline of approximately five to eight percent in the engineering department, against the prior baseline. The decline was the operational cost of the four-day workweek; the decline was smaller than the naive math would have predicted because of the AI-augmented productivity gains the firm had been investing in.

A measurable improvement in the firm's recruiting funnel, with the time-to-fill for senior engineering positions reducing by approximately thirty percent and the offer-acceptance rate for senior positions improving by approximately fifteen percentage points. The improvement was attributable to the restructured package's competitive position in the broader Twin Cities labor market.

## The Move's Limitations

The move's principal limitation, acknowledged in the case material and emphasized in the chapter's analysis, is that the move addressed the inter-employer competition rather than the employer-vs-independent competition. The senior practitioners whose alternative was another employer's offer found Stillwater's restructured package competitive; the senior practitioners whose alternative was independent operation found Stillwater's restructured package improved but still substantially below the independent-operation outside option in economic terms. The move slowed the departure of the latter group but did not stop it.

The chapter notes the specific framing Maya used at the March all-hands meeting: the move would slow the bleeding but would not stop it; the deeper move was coming; the announcement of the deeper move would follow once its design was complete. The framing was, by the case material's account, essential to the staff's reception of the move. The framing acknowledged the move's insufficiency, which the staff would otherwise have detected on their own and would have read as evidence of the firm's failure to comprehend the problem. The framing converted the move from a complete response (which it was not) to a first installment (which it was), with the second installment promised explicitly. The framing's discipline — to acknowledge the move's insufficiency while committing to the second move — is the chapter's load-bearing observation. Firms whose first-move announcements present the first move as a complete response signal, to their staff, that the firm has not comprehended the problem. Firms whose first-move announcements present the move as a partial response signal comprehension and produce the willingness to defer judgment while the second move develops.

## The Move's Place in the Sequence

The chapter has now developed the Stillwater first move in operations-research detail. The move's value is meaningful — it slowed the firm's senior-practitioner attrition by approximately seventy percent of the gap to the historical baseline — but the move's value is sub-strategic in scale. The move purchased the time within which the second move could be designed and announced; the second move is the move that produced the firm's durable post-inflection position. The two moves are not alternatives; they are sequential phases of the same response, and the second phase is incomprehensible without the first having been executed.

The next chapter takes up the second move. The chapter the entire book has been building toward.

## Key Concepts

- **The Stillwater First Move.** The perks-first response. Six components: compensation restructure, profit-sharing, four-day workweek pilot, flexible scheduling, doubled parental leave, sabbatical entitlement.
- **The Inter-Employer vs. Independent Competition Distinction.** The move addressed the former, not the latter. The acknowledgment of the limitation is essential to the move's credibility.
- **The Framing of Insufficiency.** The discipline of presenting the first move as a partial response while committing to the second. The staff's reception of the move depends on the framing.
- **The Three Funding Sources.** Prior profit margin, AI-augmented productivity gains, and retention-driven recruiting cost reduction. The move's funding draws roughly equally from the three.
- **The Move's Sub-Strategic Scale.** The first move purchases time; the second move produces the durable post-inflection position. The two are sequential phases of the same response.
