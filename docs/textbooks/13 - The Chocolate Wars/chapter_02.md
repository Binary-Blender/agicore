# Chapter 2: The Cease-and-Desist

Karim walked into the office at nine forty-two AM Friday with a manila envelope in his right hand and a paper cup of coffee in his left.

He set the coffee on the corner of Jimmy's desk.

He set the envelope on top of the coffee's coaster.

He did not sit down.

He said: *"This is going to take an hour. I cleared the eleven AM."*

Jimmy looked at the envelope. The envelope was the kind of envelope a law firm sent. The return address was on the front upper left in raised black engraving.

The return address read: *Latham & Watkins LLP — Washington, D.C.*

Jimmy said: *"Who."*

Karim said: *"Hershey."*

Jimmy said: *"For what."*

Karim said: *"The Crunch wrapper. They're calling it Krackel trade dress. Fourteen pages. I read it on the drive in. The case is dogshit. Their outside counsel sent it because someone at Hershey told someone at Latham to send it. Which means it's a pressure letter, not a litigation strategy. Which means we make a decision in the next two hours about whether to respond as if it were a litigation strategy or respond as if it were a pressure letter."*

Jimmy picked up the envelope.

He opened it.

He read the fourteen pages.

He read the fourteen pages in eleven minutes. He read them sitting in the chair at his desk, with Karim standing by the window, with the coffee Karim had brought him going cold on the coaster the envelope was now sitting next to.

The letter alleged that the new Feastables "Crunch" wrapper — which had launched in Walmart and Target stores nationwide on the first of February, which had become Feastables' second-bestselling SKU inside six weeks, which had been designed by a small studio in Brooklyn Feastables had commissioned in November and had been color-corrected against twenty-three reference wrappers in Karim's QMS-shared library of every chocolate-bar wrapper that had launched at national retail in North America in the past decade — infringed Hershey's federally registered trade dress in the Krackel wrapper. The letter demanded Feastables stop selling the Crunch product, recall all existing inventory from retail shelves and from distribution warehouses, destroy the recalled inventory under attorney supervision, pay statutory damages of an unspecified amount to be calculated through discovery, pay Hershey's reasonable attorneys' fees, and provide a signed declaration from Feastables' chief executive officer attesting to the company's full compliance with the demands within thirty days of receipt of the letter.

The letter was signed by a partner at Latham & Watkins whose name Jimmy did not recognize.

Jimmy finished reading. He set the letter down. He looked at Karim.

Jimmy said: *"Get Ana. Get Maritza."*

Karim said: *"Already here. Conference room one. They've each read it."*

---

Conference room one was three doors down from the office.

Maritza Pena, Feastables' general counsel, was sitting at the conference table with her copy of the letter open in front of her. Maritza had spent twelve years at Patterson Belknap in New York doing trademark and trade-dress litigation against exactly the kind of letter she was currently looking at, before moving to Greenville in 2023 to take the general counsel job at Feastables on the strength of an offer Ana had personally written.

Ana was at the head of the table with her notebook open and the calendar app on her laptop open behind it.

Jimmy sat down at the table.

Maritza said: *"The case is weak."*

Jimmy said: *"How weak."*

Maritza said: *"The wrappers are not similar under the Polaroid factors. The Crunch wrapper is matte black with foil-stamp lettering. The Krackel wrapper is gloss red with white lettering. The shared element is the word 'crunch' as a product descriptor, which is generic and unprotectable for a textured chocolate bar. They have a registered trade dress that covers a specific color palette and typeface combination Feastables does not use. The likelihood-of-confusion analysis, on any honest reading, comes out for us. The case is not what they sent the letter about. The case is the legal pretext for the actual business goal."*

Jimmy said: *"Which is."*

Maritza said: *"Make us spend two to four million dollars defending it. Tie up your time and Ana's time and my time in discovery for fourteen months. Distract Feastables' marketing team from the second half of the year's product launches. Give Hershey's retailer relations team a story they can tell Walmart and Target's category buyers — *Feastables is under litigation, you should hedge your shelf-space commitments.* That is the play. The letter is the instrument. The letter is not the case."*

Jimmy looked at the letter on the table.

He looked at Maritza.

He looked at Karim.

He looked at Ana.

Ana said: *"Two to four million to defeat in litigation. Six to ten months of distraction across the company even if we win cleanly. The opportunity cost is real. The risk to the Q3 launches is real. The downside of fighting it on Hershey's terms is real."*

Maritza said: *"On their terms. Not on ours."*

Jimmy said: *"What are our terms."*

Maritza said: *"You're the one who knows the terms. The terms are not legal. The terms are media. You have the audience. The audience is the leverage. Hershey doesn't expect you to use the audience because Hershey, in its institutional muscle memory, has never operated in a market where the defendant in a trade-dress letter had three hundred and twelve million subscribers."*

Jimmy looked at the letter again.

He said: *"Give me forty-five minutes."*

He stood up.

He picked the letter up.

He walked back to his office.

He closed the door.

---

He sat at his desk for forty-three minutes.

He did not, in the forty-three minutes, write anything down.

He did not pick up his phone.

He read the letter once more, very slowly, with a pen in his hand making no marks.

He thought, in the forty-three minutes, about the following things in the following order:

The paper mill worker's son and the college loans.

Thea's coat on the hook in the foyer at three forty-seven PM Eastern.

The fact that Hershey's outside counsel had sent the letter on a Thursday morning, which meant it had been signed off by someone at Hershey's general counsel's office on Wednesday afternoon, which meant Hershey's CEO had probably been briefed on the letter on Tuesday or Monday of this week, which meant the letter was the Hershey CEO's response to the Crunch SKU hitting Feastables' Q1 numbers on Monday morning of last week, which meant Hershey was, in operational terms, panicking on a four-day decision cycle.

The fact that Hershey's panicking on a four-day decision cycle was, on every business-school playbook Jimmy had not read, the moment a competitor in Hershey's position made the mistake that ended the fight.

The fact that the mistake Hershey was making was sending the kind of letter you only sent if you didn't understand what audience meant.

The fact that audience meant — in the operational sense Jimmy had spent nine years building — that Jimmy could, at any moment of any day of the week, between approximately ten AM and noon Eastern, turn his phone on, point it at his own face, talk for six minutes, edit the talk down to four, drop the talk on YouTube, and have one hundred million people watch it before the end of the next business day.

Hershey did not have this.

Hershey, in the entire eighty-five-year history of the Hershey Company's communications operation, had never had this.

Hershey did not, on Jimmy's read of the room he had just walked out of, know what it meant that Hershey did not have this.

Jimmy decided in the forty-third minute.

He picked up the phone.

He texted Karim two words: *Bay three.*

Karim's reply came back at eleven sixteen AM. The reply was three words.

The three words were: *Already lit. Three.*

---

Bay three was the bay the studio shot direct-to-camera videos in.

The lights were three softboxes overhead and a key light at four-thirty on Jimmy's right. The chair was the simple black studio chair the audience had been watching Jimmy sit in for nine years. The wall behind the chair was matte black with the Feastables logo at low opacity.

Jimmy walked into bay three at eleven nineteen AM Friday with the cease-and-desist letter in his right hand and a sealed Feastables Crunch bar in his left.

He sat in the chair.

He looked at the camera.

He said, without a script: *"Hey. So this morning at nine forty-two AM I got handed a fourteen-page letter from the Hershey Company's outside counsel at one of the largest law firms in the United States. The letter is a cease-and-desist. The letter alleges that the wrapper of this product I am holding in my left hand — Feastables Crunch, which we launched on February first, and which is now our second-bestselling SKU at every major U.S. retailer — infringes the wrapper trade dress of a Hershey product called Krackel. I am going to show you both wrappers. I am going to read you the relevant claims out of the letter. I am going to tell you what our general counsel, who spent twelve years doing this kind of litigation at one of the biggest law firms in New York, told me about the case. And then I am going to tell you what we are going to do."*

The video was six minutes and four seconds when Jimmy stopped talking.

Karim, in the control booth, said over the intercom: *"That's the take."*

Jimmy said: *"Cut the last beat. The one where I said 'See you in court.' Replace it with the one I'm about to do. Roll."*

Karim said: *"Rolling."*

Jimmy looked at the camera.

He held the physical letter up in his right hand.

He held the physical Crunch bar up in his left.

He said: *"We are not changing the wrapper. We will see them in court. Or, more likely, we will see them on Walmart's shelves."*

Karim said: *"That's the take."*

Jimmy stood up.

He walked out of bay three at eleven thirty-one AM.

He walked to Karim's edit suite.

He sat next to Karim while Karim cut the six minutes and four seconds down to five minutes and forty-one seconds and pulled the closing beat in over the original.

Jimmy approved the cut at twelve fourteen PM Friday.

The video uploaded to the studio's master server at twelve nineteen.

The video was scheduled to drop Saturday at ten AM Eastern.

The video dropped Saturday at ten AM Eastern.

---

The view count at noon Saturday was four million.

The view count at five PM Saturday was eighteen million.

The view count at midnight Saturday was forty-one million.

The view count at noon Sunday was sixty-seven million.

The view count at midnight Sunday was eighty-three million.

The view count at noon Monday was ninety-six million.

The view count at four PM Tuesday was one hundred and four million.

Jimmy's phone, in the same window of time, received approximately seven thousand text messages from people he did not know who had somehow gotten his cell number, twenty-three voicemails from reporters at outlets ranging from *The Wall Street Journal* to *Bloomberg* to *Business Insider* to *The Information,* eleven calls from senior people at five different competing chocolate companies asking, in coded language, whether Feastables would be interested in conversations of various kinds, and one text from a private number that read, in its entirety: *Watching with interest. — L.M.* Jimmy did not, on Wednesday afternoon when Ana finally walked him through the inbound, immediately recognize the initials. Ana told him. Ana said: *"Lila Marsh. The senator. She wants you to know she's watching."*

Jimmy said: *"Marsh. The antitrust subcommittee."*

Ana said: *"The antitrust subcommittee."*

Jimmy said: *"Why is she watching."*

Ana said: *"Because she watched the video, and she knows what Hershey is doing because she's watched dozens of companies the size of Hershey do exactly the same thing to upstarts in regulated categories for the past fourteen years, and she wants you to know that if Hershey escalates from a cease-and-desist into actual litigation, she is going to use the public record of the fight to open hearings on retail-channel-pressure tactics in the consumer-packaged-goods category. She is not telling you this. She is telling you, by way of two initials in a text from a private number, that she is telling you this. The communication is the communication."*

Jimmy looked at the text on his phone.

He looked at Ana.

He said: *"Okay."*

He did not reply to the text.

---

Hershey's outside counsel at Latham & Watkins sent a follow-up communication on Friday afternoon at four eighteen PM Eastern — six days after the cease-and-desist had been delivered, and forty-eight hours after the view count on Jimmy's video had crossed a hundred million.

The follow-up was a single paragraph.

The paragraph withdrew the cease-and-desist.

The paragraph did not contain the word *withdrawn.* The paragraph contained the phrase *electing not to pursue further action at this time,* which was, on Maritza's reading of trade-dress litigation correspondence she had been reading for fourteen years, the phrase Latham used when a corporate client realized they had made a mistake and instructed Latham to extract the firm from the position without using the word that would mean what it meant.

Maritza walked into Jimmy's office at four twenty-seven PM Friday with the follow-up communication in her hand.

She said: *"They folded."*

Jimmy said: *"They folded."*

Maritza said: *"They folded in six days. The shortest cease-and-desist-to-withdrawal cycle I have ever, in fourteen years, seen on a piece of correspondence I was named on."*

Jimmy said: *"Maritza."*

Maritza said: *"Yes."*

Jimmy said: *"This is going to keep happening."*

Maritza said: *"Yes it is."*

Jimmy said: *"It's going to get worse."*

Maritza said: *"Yes it is."*

Jimmy said: *"They're going to get smarter."*

Maritza said: *"Some of them. Not the ones running Hershey. Maybe the ones who come after the ones running Hershey. Probably about three years from now."*

Jimmy said: *"Three years."*

Maritza said: *"Three years."*

Jimmy said: *"Thank you, Maritza."*

Maritza said: *"You are welcome, Jimmy. The video was good. I will tell you, in the privacy of this office on a Friday at four twenty-seven PM, that in twelve years at Patterson Belknap I never once saw a defendant in a trade-dress letter dispose of the letter in six days for the cost of a five-minute video. The legal industry is not, on the read I am giving you right now, ready for what your generation of operator is going to do to it."*

She walked out of the office.

She closed the door.

---

Jimmy sat at his desk.

He looked at the analytics dashboard on the second monitor. The view count was one hundred and eleven million and climbing at approximately two hundred and forty thousand views per hour. The estimated total view count, on the dashboard's seven-day projection model, was going to land between one hundred and forty and one hundred and sixty million by Sunday night.

He looked at the dashboard for ninety seconds.

He turned away from the dashboard.

He looked at the wall calendar.

The wall calendar was a paper calendar, the kind that hung on a nail, with one page per month. The calendar had been there since 2020, when his then-assistant had hung it on the nail and Jimmy had never taken it down. The calendar had been replaced with a new month's page on the first of every month for fifty-one consecutive months. The replacement happened at six AM on the first of the month by a member of the studio's facilities team. Nobody had ever asked Jimmy whether he wanted the calendar replaced. The calendar simply was.

On the March page, in the small white square marked *15* — which was the Friday three weeks earlier, the Friday Thea had walked into bay two at four AM and started the runway — someone had drawn a small red dot.

On the small white square marked *18* — which was today, Friday, the day the cease-and-desist had been delivered and the day the video had been filmed and the day six days later the cease-and-desist had been withdrawn at four eighteen PM — someone had written, in red marker, in the bottom right corner of the square, the four characters: *12/90.*

Jimmy looked at the four characters.

He looked at them for a long beat.

He recognized Thea's handwriting.

He did not know when Thea had been in the office to make the mark. He did not know how many of the other squares between March 15 and today she had also marked. He stood up and looked at the calendar from a closer distance. The square for March 16 had a small red dot in its corner. So did 17. So did every square between then and today. So did the square for tomorrow, Saturday March 19, in advance.

The square for the day at the end of the row that would be ninety days from March 15 had, in the same red marker, in the same small handwriting in the bottom right corner: *90/90.*

Jimmy counted across the calendar.

The square was *Tuesday, June 14.*

Day ninety.

He stood at the wall for forty-three more seconds.

He sat back down at the desk.

He looked at the analytics dashboard. The view count had ticked to one hundred and eleven million seven hundred and twelve thousand.

He looked back at the calendar.

*12 / 90.*

Twelve days down.

Seventy-eight days to go.

He had, in twelve days, won the Hershey cease-and-desist fight.

He had not, in twelve days, found the model.

The cease-and-desist had not bought him back any of the runway.

The runway was the runway.

He picked up his phone.

He opened the note he had created at four sixteen AM on Tuesday in bay two.

He looked at the three words at the top: *MODEL — 90 DAYS.*

He looked at the one sentence on the next line: *I do not know what it is yet.*

He typed, on the third line, two more words.

The two words were: *Hershey folded.*

He saved the note.

He set the phone down.

He looked at the wall calendar one more time.

He said, out loud, to no one, in the office at five eleven PM Friday at the close of a week in which he had publicly humiliated a hundred-and-sixteen-year-old chocolate company in front of one hundred and eleven million people without changing his wrapper or paying a dollar in legal fees:

*"What model."*

The office was silent.

The dashboard ticked.

The calendar was the calendar.
