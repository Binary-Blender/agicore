# Chapter 13: The Third Classroom

Eleanor Whitcomb landed at King Shaka International Airport, fourteen kilometers north of Durban on the eastern coast of South Africa, at six forty-two AM local time on a Tuesday in the second week of October of Year Nine, fifteen weeks after she had walked out of the Trust boardroom in Hummelstown, eleven weeks after the cocoa-supply disruption had publicly confirmed every operational projection she had made in the eleven-minute speech, and four weeks after the Hershey Company's board of directors had announced — in a one-page press release issued at six AM Eastern on a Monday morning in mid-September — the broad outlines of what the press release had termed *a structural cooperation agreement between the Hershey Company and Beast Industries.*

The press release had been thirty-eight hundred words shorter than the four-month internal negotiation that produced it.

It did not name Eleanor.

It did not name the Milton Hershey School Trust except in a boilerplate paragraph identifying the Trust as the company's controlling shareholder.

It did not name the Adelaide Krug piece, the cocoa-supply disruption, the operational hedge differential, or any of the underlying operational reality the cooperation agreement existed to formalize.

In about seven hundred words of carefully constructed CPG-industry standard language, it said the two companies would jointly establish a school-funding program covering both the Milton Hershey School in central Pennsylvania and the Beast Philanthropy school network in southern and east Africa, that the program would be capitalized by a structurally meaningful fraction of both companies' annual chocolate-revenue base, that the program would be administered by a new joint operational entity headquartered in central Pennsylvania, and that the program would, on its first operational milestone, fund the construction of an additional thirty-seven schools across southern and east Africa over the next forty-eight months.

It did not announce who would lead the new joint operational entity.

The announcement of the lead had come in a follow-up release issued by the joint entity's own communications office at six AM Eastern on the Monday morning of the second week of October — exactly four weeks after the first release — naming Eleanor Whitcomb, Class of 1972, who had served on the Trust board for nineteen years through her resignation in May, as the founding chief executive on a five-year operational mandate, and noting that she would, on the schedule of the entity's first ninety days, be making site visits to the Beast Philanthropy school network in southern Africa.

The second release was twenty-four hours before her flight.

The flight had departed Monday evening from JFK via Doha.

Landed Tuesday morning.

Eleanor walked off the plane.

She was, on her own quiet operational practice, alone.

Her assistant — a young woman named Mary Liebowitz who had worked for Eleanor at the Trust for the previous eleven years and who had, on the standard transition arrangement, moved to the joint operational entity on the same schedule — was, on Eleanor's instruction, not on the trip.

Thandi Khoza was at the arrivals gate.

Thandi was holding a small handwritten sign that read, in marker on a piece of construction paper she had cut from the supply closet of the Beast Philanthropy regional office on Monday afternoon: *Welcome to South Africa, Mrs. Whitcomb.*

Eleanor walked through the gate.

She saw the sign.

She walked to Thandi.

"Mrs. Whitcomb."

"Thandi Khoza."

"Welcome."

"Thank you."

"The drive to Underberg is four hours and fifteen minutes. I've got a vehicle. I've brought breakfast for the road. I'm driving you myself. We'll arrive at the eighth school site at about one PM. The school is in session. I'd like, before we sit down to any operational conversation, to walk you through the school as it currently operates. The walk will take about ninety minutes. After it, we'll sit down at the small office that was a converted feed store. We'll spend the rest of Tuesday afternoon and all of Wednesday and all of Thursday on the operational conversation. Friday I'll drive you back. You fly out Friday night. Is the schedule acceptable."

"It is."

"Good."

They walked to the vehicle.

The vehicle was a Toyota Hilux double-cab pickup the regional office had used as its primary site vehicle since Year Seven. One hundred and eighty-three thousand kilometers on the odometer. Two scratches along the left front fender Thandi had not had repaired. A small white sticker on the rear window read: *Beast Philanthropy — Eighth School — KwaZulu-Natal.*

Thandi drove.

Eleanor sat in the passenger seat.

They drove the four hours and fifteen minutes from Durban to Underberg.

The conversation in the truck was, in its first hour, about the road. In its second, about Thandi's fourteen years on commercial-construction projects before Beast Philanthropy. In its third, about Eleanor's nineteen years on the Trust board. In its fourth, about Eleanor's three years at the Milton Hershey School between 1968 and 1972, during which she had been one of the first eleven young women admitted to the school.

The fifteen minutes between the fourth hour and the arrival at the site were silent.

Both women, on a shared discipline they had recognized in each other inside the first ten minutes of the drive, recognized that silence was the right close to the four hours.

The Hilux pulled up to the school site at one oh-six PM.

Thandi killed the engine.

Turned to Eleanor.

"Mrs. Whitcomb."

"Thandi."

"Please call me Thandi."

"Thandi, please call me Eleanor."

"Eleanor."

"Yes."

"On the walk I'd like to end at the library room. The library room is the room I've been advocating for at every program-design review since Year Seven and didn't win until the eighth-school review last February. It's in the southwest corner. I'd like the library room to be the room you're in when we finish the walk, and the room from which the operational conversation begins. Is the request acceptable."

"It is."

"Thank you."

They walked.

---

Sipho Mthembu met them at the entrance.

Sipho had, on Thandi's email to him on Monday morning, prepared the school in the operational mode Beast Philanthropy used for every site visit, which on Thandi's standing instruction since Year Seven was to make no preparations beyond ensuring the bathrooms were clean, the classrooms were in their standard operating state, and the teachers had been told a visitor would be present.

The teachers had not been told who.

The students had not either.

Eleanor walked the corridor.

Past the first classroom. The second. The administrative office. The small kitchen. The bathrooms. The covered outdoor learning area.

She walked into the third classroom.

The classroom was on the south side of the building, with two south-facing windows and one east-facing window and the small built-in storage closet on the back wall Thandi had added on her own design initiative the previous February.

It held, at one fourteen PM, twenty-seven students between the ages of nine and eleven.

The teacher was a woman named Nomvula Dlamini, age thirty-four, who had been teaching at the school since the school opened in March.

The lesson on the chalkboard was a mathematics lesson on long division.

Eleanor walked into the classroom.

The students looked at her.

She walked to the back of the room.

She did not, on her own operational practice from nineteen years of school visits to the Milton Hershey School, sit down at any of the small chairs.

She stood at the back.

She watched.

She watched for about fourteen minutes.

She watched Nomvula teach long division to twenty-seven children in a classroom built by a program funded, in the operational reality of its capital structure, by chocolate sales.

She watched the children answer.

She watched one specific child — a girl about nine years old, third row, second seat from the window — answer the third question Nomvula asked.

The girl answered correctly.

Nomvula said, on her standard classroom-praise: "Yebo, Ayanda. Sharp."

The girl smiled.

Eleanor watched the girl smile.

Eleanor recognized, at one twenty-six PM, that the girl's smile was the same smile Eleanor herself had produced at the same age, in 1963, in a classroom at a public elementary school in Reading, Pennsylvania, at the moment she had answered a long-division question correctly and the teacher had used the praise phrase the teacher had used.

The phrase in Eleanor's classroom in 1963 had been: *Good work, Eleanor.*

The phrase in Nomvula's classroom in October of Year Nine was: *Yebo, Ayanda. Sharp.*

The smile, in both classrooms, was the same smile.

Eleanor turned to Thandi.

Nodded once.

Thandi nodded back.

They walked out of the third classroom.

Walked to the library room.

---

The library room was, on the floor plan of the eighth school, the room Thandi had won in the eighth-school program-design review the previous February.

About forty square meters. One large south-facing window. Three walls of bookshelves Thandi had built into the design. A small round wooden reading table in the center with eight small chairs around it. An aluminum picture frame on the south wall held a single photograph.

The photograph was the photograph Thandi had taken from the south-facing window of the third classroom at eight forty-five AM on the Tuesday morning in March, looking out at the slope of the small valley and the southern Drakensberg foothills.

Eleanor walked to the photograph.

She stood in front of it.

She did not say anything for about forty seconds.

Thandi did not interrupt.

"Thandi."

"Yeah."

"Who took the photograph."

"I did."

"When."

"On a Tuesday morning in March. I was visiting the eighth school for the first site review of the spring. I had decided, sitting in this same room before it was a room — when it was just the rough framing — that I wanted to recommend to Jimmy that we double our school-build commitment across the next forty-eight months. I took the photograph from the third classroom and emailed it to Jimmy that afternoon. The email had no caption. Jimmy approved the expansion in a text reply fifteen minutes after the photograph hit his inbox. The expansion is the thirty-seven schools the press release announced in September. The photograph is on the wall of the library room because the library room is what the photograph paid for."

Eleanor was quiet for a long beat.

"Thandi."

"Yeah."

"I'd like to sit at the reading table for a moment."

"Sit."

Eleanor walked to the reading table.

Sat down in one of the eight small chairs.

The chair was small.

She did not, in the operational discomfort of an adult body in a chair designed for a nine-year-old, adjust her posture.

She sat in the chair.

She looked at the photograph.

She did, at one forty-one PM, the thing she had not done across the nineteen years of her service on the Milton Hershey School Trust board in any operationally observable form in any institutional setting.

She wept.

Quietly.

For about three minutes.

Thandi did not interrupt.

Thandi stood at the doorway and watched the south-facing window, and the slope of the small valley, and the southern Drakensberg foothills about forty kilometers to the west.

She did not, in the three minutes, look at Eleanor.

At one forty-four PM Eleanor took a small handkerchief from her jacket pocket.

Wiped her eyes.

Folded the handkerchief.

Put it back.

Stood up from the small chair.

Walked to the doorway.

"Thandi."

"Yeah."

"Milton Hershey would have liked you."

"Eleanor."

"Yes."

"Thank you."

"Thandi."

"Yeah."

"We're going to build the thirty-seven schools on the operational discipline of Beast Philanthropy and the institutional capacity of the Milton Hershey School Trust. We're going to build them on the schedule the press release announced. We're also, on my operational instruction as chief executive of the joint operational entity effective Monday at nine AM Eastern, going to do one specific thing the press release did not announce. Every school we build across the next forty-eight months is going to have a library room. The library room will be in the southwest corner. It will have one south-facing window. It will have eight small chairs around a small round wooden reading table. It will have a photograph on the south wall. The photograph will be different in every school. The photograph will be taken, on my standing instruction, by you. The photograph will be a photograph you have taken at one specific moment of the construction of each school. The moment is, on what your photograph from this third classroom did in this room across the past four minutes, the moment that distinguishes a school being *built* from a school being *built for.* The thirty-seven library rooms are, on my instruction as of this afternoon, the operational signature of the joint entity. Is the instruction acceptable."

Thandi was quiet for a beat.

"Eleanor."

"Yeah."

"Yes."

"Good."

"Eleanor."

"Yeah."

"Milton Hershey would have liked you too."

Eleanor looked at her.

"Thandi."

"Yeah."

"Thank you."

"Let's walk to the office. There's rooibos."

"I'd like the rooibos."

They walked out.

---

The operational conversation began at two oh-two PM at the small wooden desk in the corner of the converted feed store, with two cups of rooibos, and with the photograph Thandi had taken on the Tuesday morning in March still hanging in the library room of the eighth school three hundred meters away.

It ran across the rest of Tuesday afternoon, all of Wednesday, and all of Thursday.

By Friday morning at six AM local time it had produced the operational specification of the joint entity's first forty-eight months.

Fourteen pages.

Eleanor signed it at the bottom of page fourteen in her own hand, with the date of October of Year Nine, with a small note above the signature.

The note read: *For the schools.*

Thandi drove Eleanor back to Durban Friday afternoon.

Eleanor flew Friday night.

Landed in Philadelphia Saturday afternoon.

Don picked her up at the airport.

The pot roast was at the kitchen table at seven PM Eastern Saturday evening.

The chocolate wars, on the operational reality of the signed specification and the photograph and the small smile of a nine-year-old girl named Ayanda who had answered a long-division question correctly in the third classroom of the eighth school in Underberg, were — on every operational measure Eleanor Whitcomb could apply at her kitchen table in Hummelstown at seven oh-three PM Saturday evening of the second week of October of Year Nine — over.

Chapter 14 is the breakfast in Greenville the following Saturday morning.

The breakfast is pancakes.
