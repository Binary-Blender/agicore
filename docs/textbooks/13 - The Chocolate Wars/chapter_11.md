# Chapter 11: The Trust Meeting

Eleanor Whitcomb walked into the boardroom on the fourth floor of the Hershey Trust Company building in Hummelstown, Pennsylvania, at six oh-six PM Eastern on the last Friday of May of Year Nine, three weeks and two days after she had read the Adelaide Krug piece in the *Wall Street Journal* at her kitchen table at six twenty-two AM on the first Friday of May, with a single black leather folio under her left arm, her reading glasses in her right hand, and the eleven-minute speech she had been composing across the previous three weeks and two days fully assembled in her head and not, on her own quiet operational discipline, written down on any piece of paper that anybody in the building was going to find after the meeting was over.

The boardroom was the boardroom.

The boardroom held the eleven trustees of the Milton Hershey School Trust, the company's CEO Marcus Whittaker, the company's chief financial officer Theresa Park, the company's general counsel Howard Stein, and Eleanor herself.

The agenda for the meeting had three items.

Item one was the Q2 operational review.

Item two was an update on the Feastables competitive situation.

Item three was *new business.*

Eleanor's eleven-minute speech was item three.

Item three was not, on the agenda Howard Stein had circulated on Wednesday morning, attributed to any specific trustee. Item three had been added to the agenda on Wednesday afternoon at Eleanor's own request, in a one-sentence email she had sent to Howard at three eleven PM Wednesday. The email had read: *Howard, please add new business to Friday's agenda as item three. I have a remarks to make to the trustees. I will use eleven minutes. — E.W.*

Howard had not, in the forty-eight hours since the email, asked Eleanor what the remarks were about.

Howard had not asked because Howard had, on his own thirty-one years at the Trust, learned that when Eleanor Whitcomb requested eleven minutes on the agenda, the eleven minutes were not for negotiation in advance.

The meeting started at six PM Eastern.

Item one ran from six oh-one PM to six twenty-eight PM.

Item two ran from six twenty-eight PM to six fifty-one PM.

The Feastables update had been delivered by Theresa Park. The update had included the Walmart-aisle-video performance metrics (two hundred and forty-one million views by the previous Tuesday), the Q1 category-share data (Feastables at 11.9%, up from 11.3% in the previous quarter and from 9.4% the quarter before that), the projected Q2 category-share forecast (between 13.1% and 13.7% on the current trajectory), and a brief mention of the Adelaide Krug *WSJ* piece, which Theresa had described as *the piece that, on our outside-communications counsel's read, has materially raised the bar on what the public market is going to expect from us in the Q3 earnings call.*

Theresa had finished item two at six fifty-one PM.

Howard Stein, who chaired the meeting on his standard rotating-chair schedule, had said: "Item three. New business. Eleanor."

Eleanor stood up.

She did not, on her own operational practice in the boardroom, walk to the front of the room. She stood at her seat, on the long side of the table, four seats down from the head of the table where Howard sat, with the eleven trustees on both sides of the table, with Marcus and Theresa at the foot, with the boardroom's south windows behind her, with the late spring evening light coming in across her left shoulder and falling on the polished walnut surface of the boardroom table that the Milton Hershey School Trust had commissioned in 1947 from a furniture shop in Lancaster, Pennsylvania.

She set her reading glasses on the table.

She did not pick up the folio.

She did not, in the eleven minutes that followed, refer to a single note.

She said: "Trustees."

She paused.

She said: "I am Eleanor Whitcomb. I am, by every operational measure of this Trust, one of you. I have been on this board for nineteen years. I have served on this board's investment committee for eleven of those years. I have served as the investment committee's chair for the past four years. I am, by my own count, the seventh-longest-serving trustee in the current composition of the board. I am, by the same count, the only trustee currently serving who is also a graduate of the Milton Hershey School. I graduated from the school in the class of 1972. I was, in that year, one of the first eleven young women admitted to the school in the school's then-fifty-three-year history. I am going to speak to you tonight, in eleven minutes, as both a trustee and as a graduate. I am going to speak to you because, on my own honest accounting of the past three weeks, the time for the speech I am about to deliver has arrived, and the eleven minutes I am about to use are the eleven minutes I am, on the dignity of the work this Trust does, required to use."

She paused.

She picked up the reading glasses.

She did not put them on.

She held them.

She said: "Three weeks and two days ago, on the first Friday of this month, I read at my kitchen table a piece by a reporter named Adelaide Krug in the *Wall Street Journal*. The piece was twenty-three hundred words. The piece closed with a paragraph the trustees of this board will, by now, have read at least once. The closing paragraph identified, in operational terms more precise than any piece of trade press I have read across the past nineteen years of my service on this board, the structural reality of the competitive position the Hershey Company is now in. The structural reality is that a man named Jimmy Donaldson, operating out of a studio in Greenville, North Carolina, has, by means of a direct operational relationship with three hundred and fifty million people, built a chocolate brand that is, in the current category-share trajectory, going to overtake the Hershey Company in the U.S. confectionery market sometime in the next decade. The piece called this *the eating of the institutional relationship by the direct relationship.* The piece is correct. The piece will, in operational terms, be correct again every quarter for the next ten years. The Krug piece is, on my honest read, the piece that ended the operational era of the Hershey Company that I have served on this board for nineteen years of."

Several trustees shifted in their chairs.

Eleanor did not look at them.

She said: "I do not, on the substance of the Krug piece, fault Mr. Whittaker. Mr. Whittaker has, in his thirty-two months as CEO, made every operational decision a competent CEO of a hundred-and-thirty-two-year-old CPG incumbent would make. The decisions are not the issue. The decisions are not why the Hershey Company is in the operational position it is in. The Hershey Company is in this position because the operating model on which a hundred-and-thirty-two-year-old CPG incumbent runs is, in the operational reality of the year Year Nine, no longer the operating model on which the U.S. confectionery market is going to be won. The operating model that is going to win the U.S. confectionery market is the operating model Mr. Donaldson has, by accident and instinct across the past nine years, been building. I have, across the past three weeks, read every piece of public-record material on Mr. Donaldson's operating model I have been able to obtain. The material includes seventeen long-form interviews, four academic case studies, a Bloomberg-Businessweek profile from January of Year Eight, and Mr. Donaldson's own approximately eight hundred and forty published YouTube videos, of which I have, on the operational discipline I have applied to this question, watched two hundred and eleven across the past nineteen days. I have, in the watching, identified the operational substance of what Mr. Donaldson is doing. The substance is, on my honest read, the operational substance of the Toyota Production System adapted to the medium of long-form digital entertainment and from there to consumer-packaged goods. Mr. Donaldson is, by every operational measure I can apply, a Toyota Production System practitioner who has never, in his own published interviews, used the term *Toyota Production System.* He is doing it by ear. He is doing it correctly. He is going to keep doing it. The Hershey Company is not, on the operating model the Hershey Company currently runs, in any operational position to compete with him on the substance of what he is doing."

She paused.

She set the reading glasses down.

She said: "Trustees. I am going to make three observations and one recommendation. The three observations are operational. The recommendation is structural. I am going to ask, after the recommendation, for the board to take a vote on the recommendation tonight. The vote is going to require a simple majority. I am, on the standard order-of-business of this Trust, entitled to ask for the vote. Howard, I will hand you the formal motion in writing at the close of my remarks. The motion will be on the table at six fifty-eight PM Eastern."

Howard Stein nodded once.

Eleanor said: "Observation one. The Hershey Company is, on the operational reality of the next ten years, going to lose market share to Feastables every quarter. The loss is going to be between forty and seventy basis points per quarter on the current trajectory. The compounded loss across forty quarters is, on the standard CPG-industry compounding model, between fourteen and twenty-three percentage points of category share. The fourteen-to-twenty-three percentage points are, in the current trajectory, the difference between the Hershey Company holding category-leader position in U.S. chocolate at year forty and the Hershey Company being the number-three or number-four player by the same year. The trajectory is the trajectory. The trajectory will not, on the operating model we currently run, change. The trajectory is the operational reality. We are going to lose. We are going to keep losing. The losing is the operational signature of an institutional relationship being eaten by a direct relationship. We have, in this board's deliberations across the past four quarters, not yet honestly named the operational reality. I am, in this minute, naming it. I am asking the board to absorb the naming."

She paused.

She said: "Observation two. The Hershey Company is not, on the operational substance of what makes the Hershey Company the Hershey Company, primarily a chocolate company. The Hershey Company is the operational vehicle by which the Milton Hershey School Trust funds the Milton Hershey School. The school is, by the operational charter of this Trust dated 1909, the reason the Hershey Company exists. The school educates, at present, two thousand one hundred and fourteen children from families of significant economic need. The school's operating endowment, of which this Trust is the principal manager, is approximately twenty billion dollars. The endowment generates approximately one billion dollars per year of operational capital, of which the school consumes approximately three hundred million dollars per year. The remaining capital is, on the standard reinvestment model the Trust has used since 1947, returned to the endowment. The Hershey Company's annual cash flow to the Trust is, in the current operational model, approximately one hundred and ninety million dollars per year. The cash flow is, by the Trust's investment-committee accounting which I myself have chaired for four years, between seven and twelve percent of the Trust's annual operating budget. The Hershey Company is, in the operational reality of the Trust, a single-digit-percentage contributor to the school's operational capital. The Hershey Company is not, on the operational substance of how the Trust funds the school, the operating system of the school. The Hershey Company is, in operational terms, one of a portfolio of holdings. The holdings are diversified. The school does not, on the operational reality I am naming, depend on the Hershey Company in the way the board's institutional reflex has, across the past several decades, treated the dependency."

She paused.

She said: "Observation three. Mr. Donaldson's operating model, by his own public-record disclosure across the past nine years of running Beast Philanthropy, allocates a structurally significant fraction of Feastables' operating revenue to a school-building program in southern and east Africa. The program has, on the public record as of last Friday, opened eight schools and is, on the announcement Mr. Donaldson's wife made at the eighth-school opening on Tuesday of this week, scheduled to open between forty and fifty additional schools across the next thirty months. The program is, in operational substance, doing precisely the work the Milton Hershey School Trust does in central Pennsylvania, in a different geography, on a different scale, with a different funding model. The funding model is: chocolate sales pay for schools for children of significant economic need. The funding model is, in operational substance, the funding model Milton Hershey established in 1909. Mr. Donaldson did not, on any public-record evidence I have been able to obtain, study the Milton Hershey funding model before he built the Beast Philanthropy funding model. He built it independently. He built it from the same operational logic Milton Hershey built it from in 1909. The two funding models are, on operational substance, the same funding model. The competitor we are losing to is, in operational substance, the institutional reincarnation of the founder of the company this Trust is the operating successor of. We are losing to ourselves. We are losing to a version of ourselves that has, in operational substance, remembered why the work exists. We have, in operational substance, forgotten."

The boardroom was silent.

Several trustees were no longer making eye contact with Eleanor.

Eleanor did not look at the trustees.

She said: "Recommendation. The recommendation is structural. The recommendation is in two parts."

She paused.

She said: "Part one. I am recommending that the Trust, in its capacity as the controlling shareholder of the Hershey Company, instruct the Hershey Company's board of directors to open, within ninety days, a formal exploratory process around a structural partnership with Beast Industries — the operational holding entity Mr. Donaldson has, by his own disclosure on the December Year Eight Bloomberg-Businessweek profile, established as the parent of Feastables, MrBeast Studios, Beast Games, and Beast Philanthropy. The partnership is not, on this recommendation, an acquisition. The partnership is not, on this recommendation, a merger. The partnership is, on this recommendation, an operational integration along the funding-model axis I have just named. The integration would route a structurally meaningful fraction of the combined chocolate-revenue base of both companies into a unified school-funding program covering both the Milton Hershey School in central Pennsylvania and the Beast Philanthropy school network in southern and east Africa. The integration would, in operational terms, recombine the two halves of the operating substance Milton Hershey established in 1909 and that Mr. Donaldson has, by accident and instinct, rebuilt independently across the past nine years. The integration would, on every operational measure I can apply, preserve the school in central Pennsylvania, extend the operational reach of the Trust's charter to a geography the Trust's 1909 charter did not contemplate but does not exclude, and recover for the Hershey Company a competitive position in the U.S. confectionery market that the operating model of the past several decades has, on the operational evidence of the past three years, structurally surrendered."

Eleanor paused.

She said: "Part two. I am recommending that, conditional on the board's approval of part one, the Trust accept my resignation, effective immediately, from my seat on this board, and that my resignation be communicated to Mr. Donaldson and to the Hershey Company's board of directors as the operational signal that the Trust is, in its serious institutional capacity, prepared to open the conversation part one calls for."

The boardroom was silent.

Several trustees looked at each other.

Marcus Whittaker, at the foot of the table, did not move.

Eleanor said: "I am resigning because, on my honest accounting of how this board has functioned across the past four years, the institutional reflex of this board is going to be to absorb the recommendation, study it, defer it across two or three quarterly cycles, and ultimately decline it on grounds the institutional reflex will, in the moment of declining, find sufficient. The reflex is not going to be overcome by my continued presence on this board. The reflex is going to be overcome only by the operational signal of a trustee — the only currently serving trustee who is herself a graduate of the school the Trust exists to fund — resigning her seat tonight on the strength of the recommendation she has just made. The resignation is the signal. The signal is the operational instrument the recommendation requires. The signal is, in operational substance, the thing I owe the school. The school is the school. The school made me. I am, in the eleven minutes I have just used, making the school the gift the school is owed."

She set the reading glasses on top of the leather folio.

She said: "Trustees. I will leave the formal motion with Howard. I will not be present for the vote. I will not, after I walk out of this room in approximately fourteen seconds, return to this building in any operational capacity. My personal effects will be collected by my husband Donald on Monday morning. I am, on the operational record, no longer an officer or fiduciary of this Trust. Howard. The motion is on the table. The motion is yours to call. Thank you, trustees, for nineteen years."

She picked up the leather folio.

She did not pick up the reading glasses.

She walked from her seat to the door of the boardroom.

She opened the door.

She walked out of the boardroom.

She did not look back.

The door closed behind her at six fifty-seven PM Eastern.

The boardroom was silent for approximately forty seconds.

Howard Stein cleared his throat.

He said, on his standard chairing-rotation cadence and on the standard procedural language he had used in approximately fourteen hundred prior board meetings across his thirty-one years: "Trustees. We have a motion on the table. The motion is in writing. I am going to read the motion into the record. We will then, on the standard order of business, take questions, and we will take a vote at the close of questions. The vote will be by show of hands. Eleanor has, on the operational record, vacated her seat. The motion will be voted on by the remaining ten trustees."

He picked up the folio Eleanor had left at her seat.

He opened it.

The motion was on a single sheet of paper inside the folio.

He read the motion into the record.

The motion was the motion Eleanor had just spoken.

The motion ran to approximately four hundred words.

Howard finished reading the motion at seven oh-two PM Eastern.

He set the motion on the table.

He said: "Trustees. Questions."

The boardroom was silent.

A trustee at the far end of the table — a man named Phillip Lyon, age sixty-eight, who had served on the board for fourteen years — said: "Howard."

Howard said: "Phillip."

Phillip said: "I would like to ask one question before we vote."

Howard said: "Ask."

Phillip said: "Howard. Has Eleanor, on the operational substance of what she just said, been right about every substantive question she has brought to this board across the past nineteen years."

Howard was quiet for a beat.

He said: "Phillip."

Phillip said: "Yes."

Howard said: "Yes. She has."

Phillip said: "Has she been right about the question of whether to authorize the 2018 expansion of the Milton Hershey School's vocational-training program against the recommendation of the then-CEO who is no longer with the company."

Howard said: "Yes."

Phillip said: "Has she been right about the question of whether to vote against the proposed 2020 sale of the Trust's holding in the chocolate-distribution subsidiary that the then-CFO recommended we sell."

Howard said: "Yes."

Phillip said: "Has she been right about the question of whether to authorize the school's 2023 expansion of its admissions criteria to include children of currently incarcerated parents over the objections of three members of this board who are not in the room tonight because they declined to seek reappointment after the 2024 vote."

Howard said: "Yes."

Phillip said: "Howard."

Howard said: "Yes."

Phillip said: "I am voting in favor of the motion."

Howard said: "Phillip."

Phillip said: "Yes."

Howard said: "Thank you."

He turned to the rest of the trustees.

He said: "Trustees. Any further questions."

The boardroom was silent.

Howard said: "I will call the vote. All in favor of the motion as read into the record, raise your hand."

Nine trustees raised their hands.

One trustee — a woman at the middle of the table, age fifty-seven, who had served on the board for six years — kept her hand down. She did not, on the operational record, say anything.

Howard said: "The motion carries. Nine in favor. One opposed. The motion is adopted as Trust resolution number two thousand eleven. The resolution is, on the standard order of business, going to be communicated to the Hershey Company's board of directors at the standard Monday-morning communication cycle of the Trust office, which is nine AM Eastern Monday. The resolution is going to be communicated to Mr. Donaldson, by the operational mechanism the resolution itself prescribes — namely, Mrs. Whitcomb's resignation as the operational signal — by Mrs. Whitcomb herself, on the timeline she herself chooses. We are adjourned."

The meeting closed at seven nine PM Eastern.

The boardroom emptied across the next eleven minutes.

The last person to leave the room was Marcus Whittaker.

He sat at the foot of the boardroom table for the eleven minutes.

He did not, in the eleven minutes, speak to any other trustee.

At seven twenty PM Eastern, he stood up.

He walked out of the boardroom.

He walked to his office on the third floor.

He closed the door.

He sat at his desk.

He picked up the phone.

He called his wife.

He said: "Helen."

Helen said: "Marcus."

He said: "I am going to be home in forty-five minutes."

She said: "All right."

He said: "I am going to need to talk to you about something tonight."

She said: "What is it."

He said: "Helen."

She said: "Yes."

He said: "I am not going to be the CEO who saves the Hershey Company. The Hershey Company has, this evening, opened the operational process that is going to save it. The process is not me. The process is going to require a CEO who is not me. I am, on my honest accounting after the trust meeting that closed twelve minutes ago, going to need to write a letter to the board of directors tomorrow morning. I will write the letter at the desk in the study. I will write it across the morning. The letter is going to say what the letter needs to say. I am going to call you in the morning when the letter is done. We are going to talk this weekend about what comes next for us. I am not, on the operational reality of what just happened in the boardroom, sad. I am, on my own honest internal accounting, the closest to relieved I have been in thirty-two months."

Helen was quiet for a beat.

She said: "Marcus."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "Come home."

He said: "I am coming home."

He hung up.

He walked out of the office.

He drove home.

Eleanor Whitcomb arrived at her kitchen in Hummelstown at seven forty-one PM Eastern Friday evening.

Donald was at the kitchen table reading the *Times* sports section.

She set the leather folio on the kitchen counter.

She walked to the table.

She sat down across from Donald.

She said: "Don."

Don said: "Yes."

She said: "I did the thing I have been thinking about for the past three months."

Don said: "All right."

She said: "I resigned."

Don said: "All right."

She said: "The board voted nine to one in favor of the motion. The motion is going to send the Trust into a structural partnership conversation with Beast Industries. The conversation is going to be the operational instrument that, on my honest read of the next ten years, saves the Hershey Company by recombining it with the half of itself that has, in operational substance, been building independently in southern North Carolina for nine years."

Don said: "Eleanor."

She said: "Yes."

Don said: "Are you all right."

She said: "Don."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "I am better than I have been in nineteen years."

Don said: "Eleanor."

She said: "Yes."

He said: "Dinner is in twelve minutes. I made the pot roast."

She said: "Don."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "Thank you."

He said: "You are welcome."

She walked to the cupboard.

She took down two wine glasses.

She poured.

She handed one to Don.

She sat back down at the table.

The Friday-evening light came in across the kitchen window.

The Krug piece was, in operational reality, no longer the closing paragraph of the chapter.

The chapter, on the operational substance of what had just happened in the boardroom on the fourth floor of the Trust building, had been re-closed.

The new closing paragraph was, on Eleanor Whitcomb's honest accounting at her kitchen table at seven forty-three PM Eastern Friday evening, the paragraph she had just delivered into the boardroom across eleven minutes.

The paragraph was the paragraph.

The conversation between the Trust and Beast Industries was going to open on Monday morning at nine AM Eastern.

The conversation was going to be Eleanor's last operational act on behalf of the school the school had made her on behalf of.

The conversation was the conversation.

The chocolate wars, by the close of the Friday evening, had — on the operational reality of what had just happened in the Hershey Trust boardroom — already, in a sense the public would not understand for another seven months, ended.

The next chapter is going to be the test of whether that sentence is true.
