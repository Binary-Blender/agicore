# Epilogue: From the Future

> *Excerpted from* Industrial Reorganization in the Post-Frontier Era: A Casebook, *edited by Linnea Mbeki and Theo Ortega-Reyes, AI WIN-WIN Institute Press, 2057 edition. Chapter Four: "The Chocolate Wars." Lightly condensed.*

The period historians of the post-frontier era have come to call **the Chocolate Wars** runs, on the conventional dating, from the predawn hours of a Tuesday in March of Year Nine — the morning Jimmy Donaldson opened an unfamiliar GitHub repository on his phone in his Greenville studio — through the announcement of the Tony's–Feastables–Hershey acquisition in November of Year Twelve. The period is approximately three years and eight months in length. The period is, on the consensus of the discipline, the canonical case study in what is now called *substrate displacement*: the process by which a category's incumbent operating model is rendered structurally non-competitive not by a better product, not by a cheaper input, not by a more disciplined marketing organization, but by the public availability of an open-source substrate that compresses the incumbent's institutional knowledge into declarative code an upstart founder can deploy from a laptop in an afternoon.

The substrate, in this case, was Agicore. The upstart was the operating partnership that took the field in October of Year Nine when Jimmy Donaldson flew commercial to Amsterdam to ask the Tony's Chocolonely leadership a question and came home with a deal: Tony's would make the bar; Jimmy would walk the shop floor, run the Kaizens, route the school-funding ledger, and stop trying to be a chocolatier. The combined operation went to retail under a joint mark — the Tony's wrapper carried the Feastables co-brand and the Beast Philanthropy school-funding ledger; the Feastables shelf space became Tony's shelf space; the Walmart planograms, the Target endcaps, the convenience-store distribution stayed where they had been. The incumbent the partnership displaced was the Hershey Company.

---

## The market chronology

The category-share trajectory across the period is the load-bearing chart in every undergraduate textbook on the era.

By the close of Year Eight — the calendar quarter immediately preceding the events of the Wars — the Hershey Company held approximately fifty-one percent of the U.S. chocolate-bar category by dollar value. Feastables held seven. Tony's Chocolonely held two. The remaining forty points were distributed across Mars, Mondelez, and a long tail of smaller players.

By the close of Year Nine — twelve months into the Accelerando deployment Jimmy and Ana Vasquez led across the empire's then-thirteen LLCs — Hershey had ceded eight points and Feastables had taken seven. The trajectory was, in the language of the discipline's standard model, *linear in the early years and exponential in the late.*

By the close of Year Ten — two quarters into the operating-structure rollout the partnership had stood up following the October Year Nine Amsterdam meeting — the gap had closed to thirty-six against twenty under the joint mark. By the close of Year Eleven, twenty-two against thirty-three — the year of the cross. By the close of Year Twelve at the moment of the acquisition announcement, fourteen against forty-five. The category-share inversion took thirty-six consecutive quarters of competitive pressure against an incumbent that had held majority share for ninety-one consecutive years. The inversion is, on the discipline's flat-line projection of historical incumbent half-lives across the consumer-packaged-goods sector, approximately seven decades faster than the precedent.

## The acquisition

The announcement of the Tony's–Feastables–Hershey acquisition was made on the morning of Tuesday, November fourth, Year Twelve. The acquirer of record was the Amsterdam-domiciled joint operating entity that Tony's Chocolonely and Beast Industries had stood up across the two quarters following the October-fourteenth meeting at the Westerdoksdijk office in Year Ten; the entity carried, on the deal documentation filed with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission, the name *Tony's & Beast Operating Company B.V.* The deal closed on March third, Year Thirteen, after a four-month regulatory window during which the Federal Trade Commission's antitrust division — chaired by then-Commissioner Bharati Kapoor under the Antitrust Subcommittee's revised framework that Senator Lila Marsh's office had shaped in the post-frontier reforms of Year Eleven — declined to challenge the deal on the grounds that the acquirer's open-source operating substrate was, in operational fact, available to every prospective competitor in the category at the cost of a single `git clone`, and that the deal therefore could not produce the structural anti-competitive effect the Sherman Act's modern interpretation required for enforcement.

The total deal value was fifty-four-point-three billion dollars in stock and cash. The per-share premium offered to Hershey shareholders represented approximately ninety-one percent of the share price at which the Hershey Company had traded at the open of the Chocolate Wars in March of Year Nine. The deal documentation listed Jimmy Donaldson, in his capacity as a director of the Amsterdam-domiciled operating entity, under the line item *Director — Operations and Philanthropy.* The deal documentation listed Tony, in his capacity as the operating CEO of the chocolate side of the partnership, under the line item *Director — Chocolate.* The deal documentation listed Eleanor Whitcomb, in her capacity as the inaugural chair of the entity's board since the first business Monday of April of Year Ten, under the line item *Chair of the Board.*

The Milton Hershey School Trust accepted the offer. The trust's chair, Harold Brennan, signed the resolution in the trust's main boardroom on East Chocolate Avenue at three forty-seven PM Eastern on Tuesday, November fourth. Eleanor Whitcomb walked into the boardroom at three fifty-one PM — four minutes after Harold's signature, in her capacity as the chair of the acquiring entity's board — and countersigned the buyer's side of the deal documentation at three fifty-three. The chair she was offered was the third chair from the head on the south side of the table. The chair was the same chair Eleanor had occupied at every Q1, Q2, Q3, and Q4 trust meeting between her appointment in 2012 and her resignation in July of Year Nine. She did not, on her own subsequent recollection, comment on the chair. She did not, on her own subsequent recollection, speak during the meeting. Harold did, after the countersigning, extend his hand across the table. Eleanor shook it. The handshake was four seconds. She returned to her car in the trust's parking lot at four twenty-three PM Eastern. She stopped, on the way to the car, at the twelve-foot bronze statue of Milton Hershey holding a small bag of chocolate kisses that the trust had commissioned in 2004. She looked up at the statue for, on her own subsequent recollection, somewhere between thirty seconds and two minutes. She said, out loud, to the statue, the one sentence she had not been able to say to it on her walk to her car on the afternoon of her resignation in July of Year Nine: *"Milton. The schools are funding the schools."*

She drove home to Hummelstown.

She poured herself one bourbon at six oh-three PM Eastern.

She slept seven hours.

## The structural inversion

The chocolate company that had, for one hundred and twenty-eight consecutive years, funded the Milton Hershey School Trust was now owned by the partnership whose joint-marked bar funded the Beast Philanthropy schools — the bar Tony's Chocolonely made on the cocoa supply chain Tony's Chocolonely had been disclosing the farm names of for twenty-two years, sold across the retail footprint Feastables had built in nine. By the date of the acquisition close — March third, Year Thirteen — Beast Philanthropy had built and was operating approximately four thousand two hundred schools across KwaZulu-Natal, the Eastern Cape, Gauteng, Limpopo, the Free State, Mpumalanga, the Northwest, Northern Cape, and Western Cape provinces of South Africa, plus first-wave deployments in Mozambique, Lesotho, Eswatini, Zimbabwe, Botswana, Namibia, Tanzania, Kenya, Uganda, and Rwanda. The next thirty thousand schools were capitalized on the ledger and scheduled for ribbon-cutting cadence of approximately fifteen new operating schools per day across the following six years.

The Milton Hershey School in Hershey, Pennsylvania, continued to operate under the trust's residual charter on the campus Milton Hershey had endowed in 1909. The campus's enrollment as of Year Thirteen was approximately two thousand one hundred students. The Beast Philanthropy school network's enrollment as of the same date was approximately one million eight hundred and thirty thousand students.

The structural inversion is, on the discipline's conventional treatment, the load-bearing image of the entire Chocolate Wars period: *the candy bar that fed two thousand children for a century was acquired by the joint-marked candy bar that feeds two million — made by the chocolatiers who had always been the right chocolatiers, sold by the operator who had finally stopped trying to be one.*

## The substrate

By the close of the Chocolate Wars, the agicore-main repository on GitHub showed approximately seven hundred and forty-two thousand contributors, approximately two-point-three million `.agi` specifications running production workloads on the project's small public registry, and an estimated eight trillion dollars of enterprise-software market capitalization displaced from incumbent vendors across the categories the substrate had reached. SAP, Oracle, Salesforce, Workday, ServiceNow, Epic, Cerner, and the broader category of nine-figure enterprise-software incumbents had, by the close of Year Twelve, each entered the phase of contraction the discipline now categorizes as *substrate-displaced.* The phase has, on every observed precedent, no recovery exit.

The project's primary author — known throughout the period only by the initials *C.B.* and a residential address in a town of approximately three thousand seven hundred people in southern Missouri — declined, across the duration of the Wars and the decade that followed, every invitation to speak publicly about the project, to monetize the project beyond the modest residual revenue from a single Shopify storefront, or to accept the equity, the honorary degrees, the major-prize nominations, and the at least four documented Forbes-cover offers the platform's adoption surfaced for him. He continued, on the public commit log, to commit at a rate of approximately fourteen commits per week from the kitchen of the small house in Missouri until his quiet retirement from public commits in Year Twenty-Three, by which time the project had a contributor base in excess of two million and the maintainer's role had become, in operational terms, that of a retired-but-still-respected village elder consulted on the rare load-bearing architectural decision and otherwise left to his garden, his weekly meatloaf at the town's diner, and the small monthly Sponsor note that read, every month since the first one, the same five words: *For the work. — C.W.*

## What the period is known for

The Chocolate Wars are taught, in the standard MBA curriculum of the post-frontier era, for four reasons.

**One.** They are the first case study in which an incumbent of the magnitude of the Hershey Company was overtaken in a category it had held for nearly a century, by a competitor whose operating advantage was *not proprietary.* Hershey could have, at any moment in the four years of the Wars, cloned the open-source substrate. Hershey did not. Hershey could not. The substrate required a manufacturer's mind to operationalize, and the Hershey Company had, on the trust's own subsequent honest review, hired no manufacturer to its CEO chair in the fifteen years preceding the Wars. The Wars are taught, on this beat, as the canonical case of *organizational-antibody collapse* — the failure mode in which an incumbent recognizes the threat too late, not because the threat is hidden but because the incumbent's hiring practices have, across decades, structurally eliminated the personnel category capable of recognizing it.

**Two.** They are the first case study in which a founder transitioned from operational bottleneck to deterministic-substrate operator within a single calendar year. The standard curriculum's depiction of the transition — Jimmy Donaldson's calendar moving from eighty hours a week at the open of Year Nine to thirty hours a week by the close of Year Ten and to twenty-two hours a week by the close of Year Twelve, with two children, the first uninterrupted seven-week sabbatical of his adult life, and the resumption of a wife who had her own interior-design practice and her own studio above the garage of the Greenville house across the same span — is treated as the canonical proof that operational compression at the founder layer is, in the post-frontier era, an architectural problem rather than a discipline problem. The Wars are taught, on this beat, as the case in which *the operating model freed the operator.*

**Three.** They are the first case study in which a publicly-traded incumbent's strategic-review committee, in its own internal write-up, used the formulation *"we are not, on our honest assessment, competing against a company. We are competing against a file format."* The phrase, leaked to the financial press in the third quarter of Year Eleven by a junior associate at Hershey's outside counsel whose subsequent career the trust did not pursue, became the period's epitaph for the brand-and-distribution era of consumer-packaged-goods incumbency. The Wars are taught, on this beat, as the case that named the substrate-displacement model in the words of the displaced.

**Four.** They are the first case study in which philanthropic infrastructure — Beast Philanthropy's school network across Southern Africa, funded deterministically by a fractional disbursement of every Feastables bar sold across the empire — became operationally indistinguishable from the operating company itself. By the close of the Wars, the company's revenue line and the network's school-day count were the same dashboard. The chocolate was the means. The schools were the end. The means and the end ran on the same `.agi` file. The Wars are taught, on this beat, as the case in which *the work that funded the work* became architecturally one piece of work.

## The mirror

The four reasons above are the conventional curriculum. The reason the Wars are *read* — by every founder, every CFO, every board chair, every executive of every industry that has ever opened the casebook — is the fifth reason, the one that does not appear in the formal curriculum because it does not have to:

**Hershey's was every company.**

The Hershey Company was, at the open of the Wars in March of Year Nine, a one-hundred-and-twenty-eight-year-old organization with global brand recognition, blue-chip institutional shareholders, approximately eleven billion dollars in annual revenue, a defensible distribution moat across approximately ninety thousand retail accounts, a workforce of eighteen thousand, an executive bench every member of which had been hired from the same dozen MBA programs the rest of the Fortune 500 had been hiring from for forty years, and a board of trustees whose institutional memory of category dominance extended unbroken back to the second administration of Theodore Roosevelt. The Hershey Company was, on every measurable dimension of incumbent strength, the kind of company every public-market analyst would have, in March of Year Nine, rated structurally durable across any reasonable forward window. The Hershey Company was structurally durable.

The Hershey Company was also, by November of Year Twelve, acquired at approximately ninety-one percent of its Year Nine share price by a competitor that had not existed as a chocolate brand in Year Six.

The lesson the period encodes is, on the consensus of the discipline, the lesson every company in every category needs to learn about artificial intelligence in the post-frontier era. The lesson is not a chocolate-industry lesson. The lesson is not a consumer-packaged-goods lesson. The lesson is not a single-category lesson. The lesson, in its general form, is this:

> *The existence of an open-source substrate that compresses your category's institutional knowledge into declarative code is not a vendor threat. It is not a startup threat. It is not a competitor threat. It is an organizational-design threat, and the standard hiring practices of your industry have, across the previous four decades, structurally rendered you incapable of recognizing it in time.*

Every company that reads the Wars should see itself in Hershey's. The reader who finishes the casebook and concludes that the lesson does not apply to their organization — because their category is different, because their relationships are deeper, because their compliance moat is structural, because their data is proprietary, because their customers are stickier, because their brand is iconic, because their regulatory protection is durable, because their margins justify their pricing, because their workforce is loyal, because their geography is defensible, because their network effects are intact, because *something* — is the reader the discipline is most interested in, because that reader is, on the standard flat-line projection from the precedent, somewhere between three and eleven years from being the protagonist of the next casebook.

The Wars are read for the mirror.

The mirror is the lesson.

The lesson is the lesson.

The next casebook is always being written.

## The maintainer's quiet remark

On the morning of November fourth, Year Twelve, approximately three hours after the Feastables–Hershey acquisition was announced on the financial wire, the maintainer of the agicore project — at his kitchen table in southern Missouri, on his second cup of coffee, on the parser for an unrelated declaration whose codegen would ship in the project's next quarterly minor-version release — opened a fresh tab on his laptop. He navigated to the most recent issue thread on the agicore-main repository. He wrote a six-word comment on the thread.

The comment, archived on the public log, read in its entirety:

> *The work continues. The work continues. — C.B.*

He posted the comment at eight forty-seven AM Central.

He closed the tab.

He went back to the parser.

The comment is, on the consensus of the discipline, the closing remark of the Chocolate Wars period. It is also the opening remark of the era that followed.

The work, on every honest read of the four decades since, has continued.

The work continues.
