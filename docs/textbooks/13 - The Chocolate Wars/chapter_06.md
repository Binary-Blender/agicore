# Chapter 6: The Walmart Aisle Video

Jimmy was at his desk in the office at the Greenville studio at three forty-eight PM Eastern on a Monday in early May of Year Nine, three weeks after the Bentonville meeting, eighteen days before the Andon Cord chapter is going to be about, and at day fifty-eight of the ninety-day clock Lerato had started in bay number two in March, when he opened the small private file on his laptop in which he had, across the previous six years of running Feastables, kept a list of video ideas that he was not, at the time of having the idea, going to make immediately but that he was, on his own quiet sense of the operational timing of each idea, going to make at the right moment.

The file was titled, in the standard internal-documentation taxonomy Karim had set up in 2019, *Slow Burn.*

The file had, as of the morning of the Monday in question, approximately three hundred and forty entries.

Entry number two hundred and eleven had been added to the file on the morning of January fourteenth of Year Nine, three months and twelve days before the Monday in question.

Entry number two hundred and eleven was, in its original form, a single sentence.

The single sentence was: *Walk through the Walmart chocolate aisle on camera and say, on the record, exactly what the shelf allocation looks like.*

Jimmy had added the entry on a Saturday morning in January after walking through the Walmart in Greenville on a routine grocery run with Lerato. He had, on the walk through the chocolate aisle, counted Feastables' facings against Hershey's facings against Mars facings against Mondelez facings against the regional smaller-player facings. He had done the math in his head. He had not, in the months since, made the video.

He had not made the video because, on his own quiet assessment of the operational timing, the video had not yet had the right context.

The right context had, by the morning of the Monday in question, arrived.

The right context was the Bentonville meeting, the SKU allocation Henry Calloway had committed to, the Hershey-pressure campaign that Walmart had quietly chosen not to publicly call out, and the cumulative effect of those three things on the underlying chocolate-aisle facing allocation across approximately seven thousand four hundred and twenty Walmart stores nationwide.

The video, on the right context, was the right video.

Jimmy opened the file.

He read entry number two hundred and eleven.

He read entry number two hundred and twelve, which had been added on the Tuesday after the Bentonville meeting and which was: *Companion video to #211, filmed three weeks after #211 drops, in the same Walmart aisle, with the same camera, narrated by the same voice — but with the new SKU allocation in place. The math from #211 will have changed. The change is the second video.*

He read the two entries together.

He nodded once.

He picked up his phone.

He called Karim.

He said: "Karim. Charter to Northwest Arkansas Regional tomorrow morning. Single-day trip. I am going to film a video in the Walmart on Pleasant Crossing Boulevard in Rogers. The video is short. The video is the chocolate aisle. I am going alone. I am taking the small camera. I am back tomorrow night."

Karim said: "Jimmy."

Jimmy said: "Yes."

Karim said: "The Walmart aisle video. From entry two-eleven."

Jimmy said: "Entry two-eleven."

Karim said: "I have been waiting for you to call about that entry for four months."

Jimmy said: "I know you have."

Karim said: "I will have the charter at six AM Eastern."

Jimmy said: "Six AM."

Karim hung up.

---

The charter landed at Northwest Arkansas Regional at eight thirty-six AM Central Tuesday morning.

Jimmy was on board.

He was alone except for the two pilots and the small flight attendant.

He drove the rental Suburban himself.

He drove the seventeen minutes from the airport to the Walmart on Pleasant Crossing Boulevard in Rogers, Arkansas.

He parked in the visitor lot.

He walked into the Walmart at nine twelve AM Central in a baseball cap, a charcoal hoodie, dark sunglasses he took off at the entrance, and a small DSLR camera he had bought himself in 2019 and that he had used to film, by Karim's quiet count, approximately fourteen of the most-viewed videos in the MrBeast YouTube archive.

The camera was the camera.

The Walmart was the Walmart.

The chocolate aisle was the chocolate aisle.

Jimmy walked the aisle once, without filming, to confirm the facing allocation against the operational data Karim had pulled the previous evening from the third-party retail-tracking service Feastables subscribed to.

The data and the aisle matched.

Hershey's had fifty-one percent of the chocolate-bar facings.

Feastables had fourteen percent.

Mars had nineteen percent.

Mondelez had eleven percent.

The remainder of five percent was distributed across regional and specialty players.

Jimmy walked the aisle a second time, filming.

He filmed for approximately seven minutes.

He narrated in the standard handheld walking-narration register he had developed across nine years of YouTube production.

He counted the facings.

He named them.

He compared them.

He did, in the closing thirty seconds of the seven minutes, the math.

He closed the video at the end of the aisle, on a single shot of himself looking at the camera, with the chocolate aisle visible in the soft-focus background, with no music, with no edit.

He said: "The reason Hershey's has fifty-one percent of this aisle and we have fourteen is that they have been here longer. The reason we have fourteen and not three is that the people who walk past these shelves are voting. The voting is going to keep going. We will see how this aisle looks in a year."

He hit stop.

He walked out of the Walmart at nine forty-three AM Central.

He drove the rental Suburban back to the airport.

The charter lifted at eleven fourteen AM Central.

He was back in Greenville by three twenty-two PM Eastern.

He walked into the editing bay of bay number two at three forty-eight PM.

He edited the video himself across the next two hours.

He titled the video, in the small text field of the YouTube uploader, *The Walmart Chocolate Aisle.*

He thumbnailed it himself.

The thumbnail was Jimmy in the chocolate aisle, with Hershey's products visible on his left and Feastables products visible on his right, with his right index finger pointing at the math written in chalk-marker on a small sign he had brought to the aisle in the morning and held up for the closing shot — though the chalk-marker math was not in the actual video and was, by Karim's eventual decision, a thumbnail-only prop the audience would never know was a thumbnail-only prop.

The thumbnail was the thumbnail.

The video was scheduled to drop on Tuesday morning at ten AM Eastern of the following week.

Karim, on the Wednesday morning after the filming, asked Jimmy if he wanted to push the drop up to that Saturday at ten.

Jimmy said no.

Jimmy said: "The video drops Tuesday. The cease-and-desist video dropped Saturday. The Walmart-aisle video drops Tuesday. The two videos are, by my own quiet read of the audience-attention rhythm I have been operating under since 2017, the two videos that need to be a week apart in the public-attention cycle. The Saturday drop is for the news cycle. The Tuesday drop is for the analyst cycle. The Walmart-aisle video is the analyst cycle. The analysts read it on Tuesday morning. The financial-Twitter quote-tweets start by Tuesday afternoon. The Bloomberg piece runs Wednesday afternoon. The Wall Street Journal piece runs Friday morning. The trajectory is the trajectory. The Tuesday is the Tuesday."

Karim said: "Tuesday."

The video dropped Tuesday at ten AM Eastern.

---

It hit ten million views in three hours.

It hit fifty million views in eighteen hours.

It hit one hundred million views on Wednesday at four eleven PM Eastern.

The closing line of the video — *"The reason Hershey's has fifty-one percent of this aisle and we have fourteen is that they have been here longer. The reason we have fourteen and not three is that the people who walk past these shelves are voting. The voting is going to keep going. We will see how this aisle looks in a year."* — became, within forty-eight hours of the drop, the top quote on financial Twitter.

The quote was, by Wednesday afternoon, on the Bloomberg terminal's news-pane filter that filtered for *most-cited single statements by individual executives in the consumer-packaged-goods sector in the past seventy-two hours.*

The quote was, by Thursday morning, in two separate analyst notes published by senior research analysts covering the U.S. chocolate-bar category — one from a Morgan Stanley analyst Jimmy had not previously been aware of, and one from a Bank of America analyst Jimmy had been aware of for three years because the analyst had written a 2022 note about Feastables' projected market-share trajectory that had, in retrospect, been more accurate than Feastables' own internal forecasts.

The quote was, by Friday morning at six AM Eastern, the headline of the *Wall Street Journal*'s front-page business section, in a piece by a reporter named Adelaide Krug who had been covering the Carrick-Lattice partnership for the previous year and who had, in February of Year Nine, started covering Feastables on her own initiative because Feastables had been, in her own quiet read of the U.S. CPG industry, the second-most-operationally-interesting company in the country across the previous twelve months.

The Krug piece was titled: *The Voting Is Going to Keep Going: How a YouTube Aisle-Walk Re-Framed the Chocolate Industry in 72 Hours.*

The piece was twenty-three hundred words.

The piece walked through the cease-and-desist video, the cease-and-desist withdrawal, the Hershey-pressure campaign at Walmart that Krug had been able to triangulate from three independent industry sources, the Q3 planogram review that was scheduled for July, and the underlying competitive dynamics between Feastables and Hershey's across the previous three years.

The piece closed with a paragraph that became, in the long arc of the chocolate-industry trade press's coverage of the year, the single most-quoted paragraph of any chocolate-industry piece across the previous two decades.

The paragraph was:

> *Jimmy Donaldson is, by every measurable indicator of competitive dynamics in the U.S. chocolate-bar category, doing to Hershey's what no upstart has been able to do to a hundred-and-thirty-two-year-old CPG incumbent in the modern era of consumer packaged goods. The doing is not the result of capital, of distribution leverage, of celebrity-endorsement networks, or of any of the standard mechanisms by which upstart CPG brands have historically gained market share. The doing is the result of one thing: Donaldson has, in operational terms, a direct relationship with three hundred and fifty million people, and the Hershey Company has an institutional relationship with approximately one hundred and twenty retailers. The direct relationship is eating the institutional relationship. The eating is going to continue across the next decade. The Hershey Company has, on the public-record evidence of the past nine weeks, not yet figured out that the eating is happening. The Hershey Company is going to figure it out. The figuring out is going to be expensive.*

Marcus Whittaker read the Krug piece on Friday morning at his desk in the Hershey Trust Company building.

He did not, across the four hours after reading the piece, respond to it publicly.

Yvette Park read the piece at her desk on the third floor of the same building.

She read it three times.

She did not, across the four hours, respond to it publicly either.

Eleanor Whitcomb read the piece at her kitchen table in Hummelstown at six twenty-two AM Eastern Friday morning, with her cup of tea, with Don across the table reading the *New York Times* sports section.

She read the piece across twelve minutes.

She set the *Wall Street Journal* down on the kitchen table.

She looked at Don.

She said: "Don."

Don said: "Yes."

She said: "I am going to need to think about something for the next three months."

Don said: "All right."

She said: "I am not going to tell you what it is until I have figured it out."

Don said: "All right."

She said: "Thank you."

He said: "You are welcome."

She picked the tea back up.

She drank it.

She walked to her study.

She closed the door.

She did not, in the next three hours, come out of the study.

---

Jimmy read the Krug piece at six oh-eight AM Eastern Friday morning, at the kitchen counter of the house in Greenville, with Lerato across the counter on her second cup of coffee.

He read the piece.

He set the laptop down.

He looked at Lerato.

He said: "Lerato."

She said: "Yes."

He said: "Adelaide Krug just wrote the closing paragraph of the chapter we are in."

Lerato said: "Read it to me."

He read it to her.

Lerato listened.

When he finished, she said: "Jimmy."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "The piece is the piece. The piece is going to do, across the next ninety days, what no piece in the chocolate-industry trade press has been able to do across the previous twenty years. The piece is going to force the Hershey Company to publicly respond. The public response is going to be the inflection point."

He said: "I know it is."

She said: "Are you ready for the inflection point."

He said: "Lerato."

She said: "Yes."

He said: "I am not ready."

She said: "Why not."

He said: "I do not yet have the architecture."

She said: "Jimmy."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "Find it."

He said: "I am going to find it."

She said: "When."

He said: "Lerato."

She said: "Yes."

He said: "I do not know when."

She said: "Find it this week, Jimmy. The piece changes everything. The week is the week. The architecture is the architecture. You are not, on my own honest read of the next thirty days, going to be able to hold the empire together through the public response without the architecture. Find it."

He looked at her.

He said: "I will find it this week."

She said: "Good."

She picked up her coffee.

She finished it.

She walked into the bedroom.

She got dressed for the firm.

She left the house at seven oh-eight AM.

Jimmy was, at the kitchen counter, alone.

He opened his laptop again.

He looked at his open browser tabs.

He had, by his honest count, fourteen browser tabs open across various small-business-AI tooling websites, three enterprise-CRM platform demos he had been evaluating for the previous eleven weeks, and two academic papers on systems-engineering methodologies that Ana Vasquez had emailed him in March that he had not yet read.

He closed all fourteen tabs.

He opened a new tab.

He typed, into the search bar, three words.

The three words were: *systems-authoring architecture.*

He hit enter.

The first result was a Wikipedia stub about a category of software engineering methodologies he had not previously encountered.

The second result was a research paper from MIT's CSAIL group on the history of domain-specific languages.

The third result was a Hacker News thread from approximately three weeks earlier.

The Hacker News thread was titled: *Show HN: Agicore — a deterministic systems-authoring platform that compiles a DSL into Tauri apps.*

Jimmy looked at the thread for approximately seven seconds.

He did not, in the seven seconds, click on the thread.

He bookmarked the thread for later.

He closed the tab.

He walked into the bedroom.

He got dressed for the studio.

He drove to the studio.

He worked through the rest of Friday, all of Saturday, and all of Sunday on the response strategy to the Krug piece, in coordination with Ana Vasquez, Priya Sharma, Karim Brock, and the senior brand-team leads.

He did not, across the weekend, open the bookmark.

He did not, across the weekend, find the architecture.

He went to bed at eleven PM Sunday night.

He was up at five-thirty AM Monday morning.

He drove to the studio.

He worked through Monday.

He worked through Monday night.

By Tuesday at two AM, he was in his office at the studio, alone, with Karim having gone home twenty minutes earlier, with the studio silent for the first time in two weeks, with the small DSLR camera he had used for the Walmart-aisle video on the corner of the desk, and with a Hacker News tab open on his phone that he had refreshed approximately seventeen times in the previous hour because he had been, on his own quiet sense of the situation, looking for something specific and had not yet recognized what.

The bookmark from Friday morning was, at two AM Tuesday, still in his bookmarks bar.

He clicked the bookmark.

The Hacker News thread loaded.

The top comment of the thread linked to the project's GitHub repository.

The project was the project.

The project was called Agicore.

The project's repository was the repository.

Jimmy clicked the link to the repository.

The README loaded.

The README opened with a single sentence in bold.

The sentence was: ***Agicore — A deterministic systems-authoring platform for AI-native organizations.***

Jimmy started reading.

The next chapter is the chapter.

The architecture, by the close of the next chapter, is going to be the architecture.
