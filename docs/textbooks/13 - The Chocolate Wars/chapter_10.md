# Chapter 10: Beast Games

The empire.app deployment ran on Monday morning at nine eleven AM Eastern, on the dedicated Tauri-host server Karim had stood up across the previous weekend on the studio's own infrastructure, in a small data closet on the second floor of the Greenville facility that had been, before that morning, the room the studio's IT team called *the Closet Where We Keep the Thing Nobody Has Asked About Yet*.

The deployment took one minute and forty-one seconds.

The empire.app — fourteen hundred and seventeen lines of .agi, compiled to a 47.3-megabyte Tauri application with a deterministic hash that matched the one Christopher had read off the terminal in his workshop in Ava, Missouri, on Sunday evening at six oh-four PM Central — came up on the host at nine twelve PM AM Eastern, listened on its assigned port, and began, on its first runtime cycle, the work of being the operating system of the empire.

Karim watched the deployment from his laptop in the doorway of the second-floor closet.

Jimmy watched the deployment from his desk in his office, on a small browser window that showed the deployment dashboard the empire.app's runtime exposed by default.

The deployment finished.

The dashboard showed sixty-three active constraint boundaries, four hundred and eleven scheduled operational checks, eleven distinct sub-system pipelines, and one banner across the top of the screen that the runtime's default UI had auto-generated from the metadata in the .agi specification.

The banner read: *Empire — operational specification version 1.0.0 — deterministic hash a4f2c81d — operator: J. Donaldson — cord-holder schedule: see system/cord-holders.*

Jimmy looked at the banner.

Karim, on the phone from the second-floor closet, said: "Jimmy."

Jimmy said: "Karim."

Karim said: "It's up."

Jimmy said: "It's up."

Karim said: "What do we do first."

Jimmy said: "Beast Games."

Karim said: "Beast Games."

Jimmy said: "Beast Games has, on the operational accounting of the past eighteen months, produced approximately four operational failures that have cost between two and seven million dollars each. The failures were, on Christopher Bender's framing at four eleven PM Saturday in the workshop, the operational signature of a production-line system without a clear cord holder. The empire.app, on the specification we wrote, identifies the Beast Games line, identifies the seventeen distinct constraint boundaries that line operates under, and assigns the cord to a single human — to be named within four months, per the cord-transfer schedule — who is going to be the operational holder of the Beast Games Andon Cord. Until that human is hired, the cord is, in operational terms, mine. The cord is mine for the next twelve to sixteen weeks. The cord is, in the runtime of the empire.app, going to surface every constraint violation across the Beast Games line to my dashboard in real time, and is, on the runtime's default behavior, going to halt the line on any unresolved violation until I clear it. The cord is going to do, in the runtime, exactly what I have been doing in my head for the past eighteen months — except the runtime is going to do it without me having to remember to do it. The runtime is going to do it on every constraint, every time, on the deterministic guarantees Christopher built into the compiler. The runtime is going to do it correctly. I am going to be, in operational reality, less of the cord than I have been in the past eighteen months, not more, because the cord is now in the runtime and the runtime is more reliable than my own attention. The cord is the runtime. The runtime is the cord. Karim. Beast Games starts production on the new season's casting cycle on Friday. We are going to run the Friday casting cycle through the empire.app. We will find out, on Friday, whether the architecture is the architecture."

Karim said: "Friday."

Jimmy said: "Friday."

Karim said: "I will brief the Beast Games operations team this afternoon."

Jimmy said: "Good."

Karim hung up.

Jimmy looked at the dashboard.

He clicked on the *Beast Games* sub-system.

The sub-system view loaded.

The view showed the seventeen constraint boundaries the .agi specification had encoded for Beast Games. The boundaries were, in the order the .agi file had emitted them: contestant medical clearance, contestant background verification, contestant insurance coverage, talent agency contract compliance, set construction safety, on-site medical staff ratio, on-site security staff ratio, broadcast clearance for filming locations, sponsor compliance for product placement, prize disbursement legal compliance, child-labor compliance for any contestant under eighteen, food service compliance for on-set catering, transportation insurance for contestant transport, set construction permit compliance, broadcast standards compliance for filmed content, post-production legal review for filmed content, and a seventeenth boundary that the .agi specification had emitted under the label *operational integrity — show is the show.*

The seventeenth boundary was, on Christopher Bender's quiet articulation at three forty PM Sunday afternoon, the boundary that distinguished the Beast Games line from a generic large-scale entertainment-production line. The seventeenth boundary was the boundary that required the runtime to verify, on every operational decision, that the decision was consistent with the operational identity of the show as Jimmy had defined it in the .agi file. The seventeenth boundary was, in operational terms, the boundary that asked: *would Jimmy, on his own honest accounting, recognize the show this decision is producing as the show he is trying to produce*. The boundary was a generative-AI-mediated boundary that ran every operational decision through a build-time-compiled adversarial prompt that Christopher had designed specifically for the empire.app and that, in the empire.app's deployment, would surface to Jimmy's dashboard any decision the boundary's logic could not, in the prompt's evaluation, confidently classify as consistent.

The seventeenth boundary was the one Jimmy had spent the longest of the three days in Ava arguing with Christopher about.

The argument had not been about whether the boundary should exist.

The argument had been about whether the boundary was, in operational terms, a cord Jimmy could hand off.

Christopher had said: no.

Jimmy had said: yes.

Christopher had said: no, because the show is the show, and the show, on its operational reality, is your show — the cord on the show's identity is not a cord any other human can pull on your behalf until the show has, in its operational evolution, become a show that is not, in any operationally meaningful sense, your show.

Jimmy had said: I want the cord to be transferable.

Christopher had said: it can be transferable. The transfer is going to be the operational milestone that signals the end of the founder phase of the empire. The transfer is not going to be in this version of the specification.

Jimmy had said: when.

Christopher had said: I do not know when. The when is, in operational terms, on you. The .agi specification will have a hook for the transfer when you tell me you are ready to write the transfer logic. Until you tell me, the hook is empty.

Jimmy had said: the hook is empty.

Christopher had typed the empty hook into the specification.

The empty hook was, on Monday at nine fourteen AM Eastern, the only TODO marker in the empire.app's runtime.

Jimmy looked at the empty hook.

He clicked away from the *Beast Games* sub-system.

He clicked into the *Beast Philanthropy* sub-system.

The Beast Philanthropy sub-system showed nine constraint boundaries, all of them currently assigned to the cord-holder Lerato. The dashboard's cord-holder field, for every boundary in the Beast Philanthropy sub-system, read: *L. Booysen.*

Jimmy looked at the field.

He read it nine times.

He, on his own honest internal accounting at nine sixteen AM Eastern Monday morning, recognized that the field reading *L. Booysen* on every Beast Philanthropy constraint was, in operational reality, the resolution of the conversation that had started in bay number two on the second of March.

He picked up his phone.

He called Lerato.

It was nine seventeen AM in Greenville.

It was three seventeen PM in Underberg.

She answered on the second ring.

She said: "Jimmy."

He said: "Lerato."

She said: "Is it up."

He said: "It's up."

She said: "What does it look like."

He said: "Your name is on every Beast Philanthropy constraint. Nine constraints. Nine instances of *L. Booysen.* The runtime is going to send you operational notifications when constraints fire. The runtime is going to escalate to me only if you cannot, within the configured response window, clear the notification. The response window is, on the default Christopher and I set on Sunday, seventy-two hours. I am the escalation. I am not the operator. You are the operator."

She was quiet for a beat.

She said: "Jimmy."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "Thank you."

He said: "Lerato."

She said: "Yes."

He said: "Day seventy-two."

She said: "Day seventy-two."

He said: "Eighteen days to spare."

She said: "Jimmy."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "Come home for dinner."

He said: "I will come home for dinner."

She said: "I love you."

He said: "I love you."

She hung up.

Jimmy set the phone down.

He looked at the dashboard.

He worked through the day.

The Beast Games Friday casting cycle ran on the empire.app starting at eleven AM Eastern Friday.

The cycle was, on the standard operating model of Beast Games' new-season casting, a single-day operational push that processed approximately fourteen hundred applicants through six distinct review stages — initial-screen video review, application-form verification, background-check submission, medical-clearance submission, agent-contract negotiation triage, and final scheduling for on-site interview the following week. The cycle had, across the past four seasons of Beast Games, produced approximately two operational failures per cycle on average, primarily in the agent-contract triage and background-check stages, where applicants who appeared qualified at the initial-screen stage were discovered, between forty-eight and ninety-six hours later, to have constraints that should have, in operational terms, screened them out at the initial-screen stage.

The two-failures-per-cycle had, on Karim's quiet accounting, cost the production approximately three hundred thousand dollars per failure on average across the past four seasons.

The Friday casting cycle on the empire.app ran the same fourteen hundred applicants through the same six stages.

The empire.app halted the line eleven times during the cycle.

The eleven halts were, on Karim's review of the runtime logs at four PM Friday afternoon, eleven applicants who, at the initial-screen stage, the runtime's seventeen-boundary check identified as having one or more downstream constraint violations that would, in the operational pattern of the past four seasons, have surfaced as failures forty-eight to ninety-six hours later.

The runtime did not, on the eleven halts, reject the applicants.

The runtime, on the configured behavior of the seventeen-boundary check, halted the line, surfaced the specific constraint that would have triggered the downstream failure, and routed the applicant to a manual-review queue that the Beast Games casting team could process across the next four hours with full visibility into the specific constraint that had triggered the halt.

The casting team processed the eleven halts across the next three hours and forty minutes.

Eight of the eleven halts resulted in the applicant being cleared on the basis of additional documentation the casting team requested and received during the manual-review window.

Three of the eleven halts resulted in the applicant being declined on the basis of the constraint the runtime had surfaced.

The three declines were, on the operational accounting of the past four seasons, the three operational failures that would have, in the absence of the runtime, surfaced as production-stage failures across the following ninety-six hours.

The three declines were, on the operational accounting of cost-per-failure across the past four seasons, approximately nine hundred thousand dollars of avoided downstream cost.

The eight clears were, on the operational accounting of casting cycles that had previously been disrupted by manual-review queues that the casting team did not have the operational visibility to clear quickly, approximately one hundred and forty hours of casting-team labor that the runtime's transparency on the constraint had, in operational reality, eliminated.

The Friday casting cycle finished at six twelve PM Eastern.

The cycle had processed fourteen hundred and three applicants. The cycle had cleared one thousand three hundred and eighty-eight to the on-site interview stage. The cycle had declined three on the runtime's surfaced constraints. The cycle had escalated zero to Jimmy's dashboard.

Jimmy had, in the seven hours and twelve minutes of the cycle, received zero notifications from the empire.app.

He had, in the same seven hours and twelve minutes, filmed two cold-opens for the personal channel, recorded the audio for a podcast appearance scheduled for the following week, taken Lerato on a forty-five-minute lunch in downtown Greenville, and read approximately sixty pages of a book on the history of the Erlanger Toyota plant that Karim had handed him on Wednesday morning with the note *read this on the plane to Missouri but don't tell Bender I gave it to you.*

He had not, across the same seven hours and twelve minutes, been the cord on Beast Games.

The cord had been the runtime.

The runtime had been the cord.
