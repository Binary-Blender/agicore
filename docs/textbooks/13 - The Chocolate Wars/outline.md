# The Chocolate Wars: A Future Case Study

**Author:** Christopher Bender
**Publisher:** AI WIN-WIN Institute
**Volume:** 13 (Textbook Shelf, companion to *Carry* and *Forward*)
**Genre:** Business thriller / fast-twitch operator novel — YouTube-coded register, Gen-X-Builder cadence, *Liar's Poker* / *Moneyball* lineage filtered through a creator-economy operator
**Length target:** ~50,000 words, 14 chapters, 4 acts
**Register:** Close third, past tense, **kinetic**. Short sentences. Counters and dashboards in the prose. Jimmy thinks in subscriber counts, sell-through rates, and seven-day rolling averages. The book has the cadence of a Mr. Beast video — fast cuts, big stakes, audacious flexes, the dollar amount on the screen. Where *Forward* was a chess game, *The Chocolate Wars* is a speedrun.

> **Revision note (May 2026 — second pass):** This outline reflects the second-draft plan after a full reframe around four load-bearing additions. (1) The Agicore adoption is now a **four-phase enterprise transformation** the book stages as four Mr. Beast–style challenge arcs: TAO training rollout → Accelerando enterprise-stack swap → SKILLDOC judgment encoding → SPC-driven autonomy loosening. (4) The Christopher character is single, loves chocolate; Jimmy's icebreaker on the Missouri visit is a literal pallet of Feastables Hazelnut Cups. Plot beats from the first draft (cease-and-desist, Walmart aisle video, KwaZulu-Natal trip, trust meeting, supply-chain crisis, Sunday pancakes) survive but are now staged inside the four-phase transformation arc. **One operating note for the rewrite:** the book is not about the maintainer. The book is about **the architecture and what Jimmy does with it.** Christopher is the entry point and an occasional sounding board across Act 3; he is not the hero, the wizard, or a figure the prose admires. Cole's quarterly Hazelnut Cup shipment to Christopher and Jimmy's pallet on the first visit are the only two moments Christopher is materially on stage. Everywhere else he is async on a Slack channel, on the other end of a phone call, or off-page entirely while Ana, Karim, and Thandi do the actual deployment work. The book does not write him a fan letter. The book treats him the way a serious case study treats the engineer who shipped the tool: name, brief role, get out of the way.

## Concept

This is the book where the most-subscribed YouTuber on the planet — a man with a hundred-and-fifty-employee studio in Greenville, North Carolina, thirteen separate LLCs nobody but his chief of staff can keep track of, a chocolate brand eating Hershey's shelf space for three years, a wife of eleven months he married eighteen months after they met, and a philanthropy operation that has, by the start of the book, built eight schools in KwaZulu-Natal with two more under construction — discovers an open-source project called Agicore at two AM on a Tuesday in March of Year Nine, reads the README in ninety-one minutes, recognizes the operating philosophy he has been running on instinct for nine years, and proceeds, over the next twelve months, to **rebuild the entire empire on top of it.**

The rebuild is the book.

It happens in four moves.

**Move one — TAO.** Jimmy puts every one of his four hundred and ten employees through a thirty-day AI-augmentation curriculum. Stop Being the Bottleneck. Mastering AI Prompts. Strategic AI. Field-manual EPUBs on a Kindle on every desk by week two. By day thirty, the company's per-employee throughput is up four hundred percent. Karim Brock's production team is shipping videos in seven days that used to take eighteen. Ana Vasquez's chief-of-staff office is processing in a Wednesday morning what used to take her week. Nobody in the building can believe what just happened. Jimmy puts up an internal counter on a wall in the studio. The counter shows hours saved across the company that week. The counter ticks up while people walk past it.

**Move two — Accelerando.** Ana and Karim, across a six-week build sprint with Christopher on a shared Slack channel for compiler questions and the occasional weekend call, replace the empire's SaaS patchwork — the seven CRMs, the four ERPs, the legal-hold tool, the LMS, the QMS spreadsheet Karim has been maintaining by hand since 2019, the Asana boards Ana stopped trusting in 2023 — with eighteen `.agi` files. Each file compiles to a deterministic Tauri or web service. The eighteen apps talk to each other via Interchange packets. The thirteen LLCs become **one operating system.** Feastables sells a bar → Interchange packet → Beast Philanthropy ledger → school-day funded → public dashboard updates → counter ticks. Beast Games scopes a season's contestant pool → packet → Beast Philanthropy cross-references the regions schools are serving → contestant outreach concentrates where it does the most leverage. The OIE intelligence layer watches all of it and, on its own scheduled batch runs, surfaces three opportunities a week to Jimmy and Ana that no human had time to see: *the Feastables Hazelnut Cup buyer base is forty-three percent more likely to donate to school-funding campaigns than the milk-chocolate buyer base — recommend separating philanthropy CTA at SKU level.* The empire stops being thirteen LLCs Ana coordinates by paying attention. The empire becomes one specification.

**Move three — SKILLDOCS.** Jimmy starts dictating his judgment into signed skill documents. Thumbnail-taste SKILLDOC, signed by JimmyDonaldson, governance-locked. Contestant-treatment SKILLDOC, signed jointly by Jimmy and Karim, all-actions audit, clearance-required. Charity-scoping SKILLDOC, signed by Jimmy and Thandi, governance-locked, propagates into every Beast Philanthropy site-selection decision automatically. Wrapper-aesthetic SKILLDOC — *what makes a Feastables wrapper feel like a Feastables wrapper* — dictated to a recording app across an hour by Jimmy alone on a Saturday afternoon, edited into seventeen hundred words of declarative prose by Ana, signed, deployed. The AI now proposes work — thumbnails, contestant rosters, school sites, wrapper revisions, marketing copy, charitable disbursement schedules — and the proposals come back already wearing Jimmy's taste. Jimmy reviews. Jimmy approves. Jimmy does not, on any of the work, type. He sees. He approves. He moves on. The shop runs at his taste without his hands.

**Move four — SPC.** Across ninety days of the Andon Loop running every proposal through tier verification, regression sandbox, optional shadow window, and ordered approval chain, the system accumulates the statistical evidence of its own reliability. The tier-one auto-deploys (thumbnail copy revisions, minor wrapper artwork tweaks, individual contestant-meal substitutions) hit a four-nines success rate inside six weeks. The tier-two reviewed-then-deployed (new product SKUs, contestant-elimination protocols, school site-selection commitments) hit three-nines. The tier-three governance items (acquisitions, public-stance statements, supply-chain contracts above a threshold) stay at full Jimmy-or-board approval. The approval thresholds loosen when the regression data justifies it — a tier moves from human-required to auto-deploy when its sandbox-pass rate has held above the threshold for thirty consecutive days, full stop, no exceptions. Inside ninety days the leash is loose. Inside one hundred and eighty days Jimmy's calendar drops from eighty hours a week to thirty. Inside two hundred and seventy days Karim's calendar drops from seventy hours to twenty-five. Inside three hundred and sixty-five days Ana takes her first uninterrupted two-week vacation in four years. The system runs the empire. The humans set the taste. The taste is in the SKILLDOCs. The SKILLDOCs are signed. The signatures are on the ledger. The ledger is the truth.

By the close of the book Beast Philanthropy has built **five hundred operating schools.** The counter on the wall of the studio reads five hundred. Jimmy is in the kitchen of his house in Greenville making pancakes on a Tuesday morning — not a Sunday — because the four-phase rebuild has given him Tuesdays back. Thea is at the kitchen table; their first child is in the bassinet, brief presence, not load-bearing. The Hershey Company has, by the same close, lost approximately thirty-one points of category share, replaced its CEO, replaced two of three of its president-level executives, and convened a strategic-review committee whose internal write-up concedes — in a sentence Eleanor Whitcomb's resignation-day successor on the trust will not, in his lifetime, forget — that Hershey's is *"not, on our honest assessment, competing against a company. We are competing against a file format."*

This is the book.

This is *Empire Strikes Back* in the YouTube register. *Forward* was the chess game. *The Chocolate Wars* is the prizefight.

## What the book is about, in the AI WIN-WIN tradition

The principle, named once and used as the load-bearing argument of the entire book, is:

**The chocolate is the means. The schools are the end.**

And, paired with it:

**The empire is the means. The life is the end.**

Both are versions of the same argument: *the work is not the point.* The work is the substrate that makes the point possible. AI is what makes the substrate cheaper. AI is what lets the substrate become **one substrate** rather than thirteen LLCs holding hands by force of Jimmy's attention. The point, in the AI era, is the same point it has always been — feeding the people who need to be fed, schooling the children who need to be schooled, building the life the founder actually wants to live.

The book argues this through Jimmy. Jimmy is, by his own honest admission to Thea in the opening chapter, working too much. He is missing the funeral of her father. He is missing the birth of his nephew. He cannot stop because the empire requires him to be in every operational decision and there is, today, no architecture that lets him delegate the way he needs to delegate. He finds Agicore. The architecture exists. He runs the four-phase rebuild. The empire becomes deterministic. Jimmy gets his time back. The time goes to Thea, to a family on the way, and to the schools — five hundred of them by the close, with the next five hundred already capitalized.

The book lands on the same closer as *Forward,* at a different scale and in a different register. In *Forward* it landed as the wisdom of a sixty-five-year-old founder at the end of his career. In *The Chocolate Wars* it lands as the lesson a twenty-six-year-old founder learns before he wastes the rest of his life on it.

## Why business-systems ownership matters in this book

Jimmy Donaldson has, by every public-record observation his real-world counterpart has given across five years of interviews, been running a creator-era empire on top of a brand-era enterprise-software stack. Seven CRMs, four ERPs, three project-management tools, a legal-hold system nobody deployed, an LMS nobody completed, a QMS spreadsheet Karim maintains by hand, a customer-service inbox routing to Karim's phone. By the start of the book the empire pays approximately forty-three million dollars a year for SaaS that does not, at any layer, talk to itself. The constraints that should live in the tools live in three humans — Jimmy, Ana, and Karim — remembering everything. The forty-three million dollars is decoration around the three humans.

This is the load-bearing operational beat of the book.

When Jimmy finds the Agicore README in Chapter 7, the click moment is not *wow, new tech.* The click moment is **recognition** — that every system the empire pays rent on can be replaced by a text file the empire owns, and that the constraints in the three humans' heads can be moved into signed declarative code. He calls Ana Vasquez, his chief of staff, at two forty-seven AM. Ana — who came over from Disney's content-studio operations in 2023 specifically to bring institutional rigor to the empire, who has been writing fourteen-hour Monday operating briefs across Saturday and Sunday for two years to hold thirteen LLCs in one legible picture, who has been telling Jimmy every Monday for thirteen months that the coordination overhead is going to break her before it breaks the empire — reads the README in eleven minutes of silent scrolling on the phone line and walks Jimmy through it section by section. She maps each Accelerando `.agi` file onto a specific existing SaaS contract. She lands the cost-ownership argument: the README is not rent, the README is a compiler; AI is paid once at compile time and never runs again per user request; forty-three million in rent goes to approximately zero. She points him to the SKILLDOC architecture and Jimmy recognizes it as **Tyler Conklin** — the thumbnail designer who has been, across four years of Sunday afternoons, an effective proxy for Jimmy's taste at the scale of one human. The recognition is not that Tyler is the bottleneck; Tyler ships every thumbnail at Jimmy's standard. The recognition is that there is one Tyler, and that Tyler — competent, ambitious, ready for higher-leverage work since Year Seven — has been blocked into being a propagation mechanism instead of being unlocked into running the studio's design language. SKILLDOCS do at the scale of the entire studio what four years with Tyler did in one human, on a Saturday's dictation, and free Tyler into the work that has been waiting for him. She points him to the SPC section and Jimmy recognizes the trust-earning curve: the system holds its sandbox-pass rate above the threshold for thirty consecutive days, the tier auto-deploys, the reins loosen on a curve the regression data justifies, he is not the bottleneck in eighteen months because the system by then has earned the loosened bar. She points him to the "Replace your company's entire ERP" archetype in the README's "What's Worth Building With This" section. Hershey runs SAP since 2008. One-point-one-billion-dollar deployment. Eight months and six million in consulting to change a chart-of-accounts revision. The archetype, applied to Hershey, is the kill shot.

The architecture is, in operational terms, the substrate that lets every dollar of forty-three million in rent become zero, every Sunday afternoon with Tyler become a Saturday's dictation, every operational decision on Jimmy's desk become a tier-verified proposal the system auto-deploys when the data justifies it. He doesn't *adopt* it. He **recognizes** it as the substrate Ana has been describing for two years without the vocabulary, Tyler has been receiving for four years without the file format, and Hershey's incumbent stack has been physically incapable of being.

The chapter's quietest beat — and on Ana's read the load-bearing one — is the agicore-studio sub-directory. The studio is a full visual IDE for the `.agi` DSL: Make.com-shaped node canvas paired with a CodeMirror text editor, two-way text-to-canvas binding, per-node debugger with breakpoint and step semantics, context-aware DSL autocomplete, integrated test runner, project-wide search, multi-window, git-aware explorer, crash recovery, opt-in telemetry, auto-update via GitHub Releases. The repo ships it with a file called `ESTIMATE.md` that runs a rigorous IFPUG function-point count cross-checked against COCOMO II — the two methods independent, the two methods agreeing within two percent — and lands at fifty-two person-months / eight-FTE team / seven-to-eight calendar months / six-hundred-and-sixty-five-thousand-dollar loaded cost as the credible old-world price. The directory's commit log shows the actual build was sixty-three commits across two calendar days, in two clean session clusters totaling approximately twelve hours of focused work, by one contributor. Ana, who has only used the phrase *paradigm shift* once before in her career — to Bob Iger in 2024 about generative video, where she was wrong on the timeline by two years — uses it for the second time on the phone with Jimmy at three twenty-six AM. The arithmetic is not a productivity gain. The arithmetic is approximately five-to-seven-hundred times what an eight-person team produces at 2026 market velocity. Three orders of magnitude. The studio is not a tool the project shipped alongside its substrate. The studio is the *proof of the substrate.* One person, with the right `.agi` compiler in his hands, ships what an eight-person team in seven months ships, in twelve hours, at the same quality bar. The arithmetic generalizes. The arithmetic is the thesis.

The book quietly names what Jimmy has been doing on instinct for nine years. The name is **Cognition Systems Engineering** — the discipline of designing, operating, and improving systems that coordinate humans, AI models, workflows, validation subsystems, semantic transformations, and organizational processes into stable, scalable information production systems. The discipline has, as of Year Nine, its own graduate-level textbook on Christopher's reference shelf (Volume 1A in the AI WIN-WIN Institute textbook series, *Cognition Systems Engineering: Theory, Architecture, and Practice* by Christopher Bender — the book is, in operational fact, Christopher's textbook, written across the same eighteen months he was writing the Agicore compiler; Jimmy has not read it and will not, on his current trajectory, ever sit down with it, because Jimmy reads the world by running it). Jimmy has not, in nine years, heard the phrase. He has been a CSE practitioner since he was eighteen — every thumbnail-iteration cycle is a cognitive QC gate, every contestant-treatment protocol is a semantic contract, every Sunday afternoon with Tyler is a cognition-allocation handoff, every wall calendar with a runway-counter in red marker is a control chart. Jimmy has been running, on instinct and without the vocabulary, what the CSE textbook formalizes as the eight principles of cognitive production. The README hands him the vocabulary and the Agicore reference implementation in the same document. The vocabulary makes the substrate legible. The substrate makes the practice transferable — out of Jimmy's head, into signed files, propagated across the entire studio without a single additional Sunday afternoon. The book treats this as a one-line observation rather than a thesis: *Jimmy has a gift for Cognition Systems Engineering. He has been doing it without the name. The name is now in his head. The textbook is on Christopher's shelf. The substrate is now on Jimmy's laptop. The next twelve months are the proof of what happens when the gift meets the substrate the discipline formalizes.*

This gives the book its third quiet beat: *Jimmy out-operates Hershey's because he owns his compiler.* Hershey stopped being able to materially change its business rules approximately the year it finished its SAP deployment, and has, on every honest assessment from anyone who knows the inside of that building, been running on the constraints baked into a 2008 implementation ever since. Jimmy is, in operational terms, the first creator-era founder to own the substrate his operating company runs on — and the architecture is open source, which means the substrate is not, on any honest read, a moat against the next creator-era founder either. The substrate is the leverage. The leverage is the gap. The gap is closing on every incumbent in the category and on no creator-era organization that picks up the file format.

## Chapter-by-chapter

### Act 1: The Empire (Ch 1-4, ~13,000 words)

**Chapter 1: Greenville, 4 AM Tuesday** (Jimmy POV, ~3,300 words)
Cold open. Four AM Tuesday morning in March of Year Nine. Greenville studio, bay number two. Jimmy is on hour fifty-seven of a video drop. Thea walks in at four oh-eight. She does not raise her voice. She tells him, in seven sentences across ninety seconds, that her father's funeral was three weeks ago and that her brother's first child was born last Tuesday and that he has missed both, and that she is, in operational terms, going to be making a decision about the marriage inside ninety days. She is not threatening to leave. She is telling him the runway. The runway is the runway. Jimmy listens. Does not interrupt. Does not promise anything he cannot deliver. Says, at the end: *"Thea. Ninety days. I am going to come back to you with a different operating model inside ninety days. The model is going to be the model. The funding mechanism is the load-bearing operational beat — every Feastables bar sold across the past year deterministically deposits a fraction of its margin into the Beast Philanthropy ledger, the ledger is hash-chain-public on a dashboard at beastphilanthropy.org/ledger, the dashboard ticks in real time as bars sell, and the ledger's school-day count converts at a published rate into operating-school-day disbursements. Across Year Ten the substrate funded four hundred and ninety-two new schools without Jimmy personally approving a single site beyond the ones that fell above the SKILLDOC-encoded thresholds. Thandi approved them. Her in-country team scoped them. The OIE intelligence layer recommended their geographic distribution. The substrate disbursed them. The schools opened. The fifth-hundredth opens this Saturday. The five-hundred-and-first opens the following Tuesday. The five-hundred-and-second the Tuesday after that. The schools are now opening at a cadence of approximately one every two days and the cadence is, on Thandi's projection, going to accelerate. Saturday morning ribbon-cutting. Jimmy at the podium for ninety seconds — he does not give a speech, he hands the microphone to Thandi after thirty seconds and the program's head teacher and the local mayor speak for the remaining twenty-three minutes. Eleanor Whitcomb is in the back row. Eleanor flew commercial from Philadelphia to Johannesburg on a Thursday night. Drove out to the new site Friday afternoon. Stayed at a small bed-and-breakfast in the village. Walked into the ceremony Saturday morning. Jimmy notices her at the close of the ceremony. Walks over. *"Eleanor."* *"Jimmy."* *"You flew here."* *"I flew here. I wanted to see one. I wanted to see the five-hundredth one."* The conversation is brief — three minutes, not six. Eleanor tells Jimmy what she came to tell him: that the trust has, on the strength of her resignation letter and the eight months that followed, opened a structural-review process; that the chair of the trust read the Accelerando documentation in November; that Marcus is on a quiet runway out by spring; that the trust does not yet have a frame for what comes next but is, for the first time in fifteen years, *looking for* the frame. She does not lecture Jimmy. She does not call him an heir to anything. She tells him what she observed, that is what she came to do, and she lets the schools and the substrate make whatever argument they are going to make on their own. Her closing line, before she walks back to her car: *"Jimmy. The schools are the schools. The trust is going to figure out the schools or it is not. That is not on you. You did your part. Thandi is doing hers. Five hundred is a number. The number is going to keep going up. That is enough."* Eleanor walks back to her car. Drives back to the village. Flies back to Philadelphia. Chapter closes on Jimmy and Thea on the bench in the third classroom of the eighth school — the bench Thandi sat on alone in Chapter 3, eighteen months earlier, when she was thinking about the cap. The cap is broken. The bench is in a building that is now one of five hundred. Thea puts her hand on her stomach. Jimmy puts his hand on hers. They sit on the bench for a long minute. Thea: *"Jimmy."* Jimmy: *"Thea."* Thea: *"This was the day."* Jimmy: *"This was the day."*

**Chapter 14: Pancakes on a Tuesday** (Jimmy POV, ~5,000 words)
Six months later. **A Tuesday morning** in February of Year Eleven. Jimmy is in the kitchen of the house in Greenville. Thea is at the kitchen table. Their first child — born ten weeks earlier, brief presence in the bassinet by the window, not the subject of the chapter — is asleep. Jimmy is making pancakes. He has been making pancakes on Tuesday mornings since the third week of the baby's life, when Thea told him she wanted one weekday morning per week in which Jimmy did not start work until ten. > *Thea. The chocolate is the means. The schools are the end. The empire is the means. ## The epilogue — *From the Future*

After the main fourteen chapters and the post-credits scene, the book closes with an **Epilogue** framed as a future-history excerpt — a "lightly condensed" reading from *Industrial Reorganization in the Post-Frontier Era: A Casebook*, edited by Linnea Mbeki and Theo Ortega-Reyes, AI WIN-WIN Institute Press, 2057 edition, Chapter Four: "The Chocolate Wars." The voice is dry-academic in Christopher's series cadence — declarative, numerical, fragment-friendly — with a quiet undercurrent of the textbook's own awareness that it is describing a turning point. The structure:

- **The market chronology** — category-share trajectory across the Wars, the cross at Year Eleven, the inversion approximately seven decades faster than the precedent.
- **The acquisition** — Feastables–Hershey deal announced November fourth, Year Twelve. Total deal value fifty-four-point-three billion dollars in stock and cash, ninety-one-percent premium to Hershey's Year Nine share price. The FTC declines to challenge on the grounds that the acquirer's substrate is open source and available to every prospective competitor at the cost of a `git clone`. Harold Brennan signs the resolution at three forty-seven PM Eastern. Eleanor Whitcomb is in the room. She stops at the Milton Hershey statue on the way to her car. She does not, on her own subsequent recollection, say anything to the statue.
- **The structural inversion** — by acquisition close, four thousand two hundred Beast Philanthropy schools operating across ten African nations with 1.83M students enrolled, against the Milton Hershey School's residual 2,100. *The candy bar that fed two thousand children for a century became, under one substrate, the candy bar that feeds two million.*
- **The substrate** — by the close of the Wars, agicore-main shows ~742K contributors, ~2.3M production `.agi` specifications, ~$8T of enterprise-software market cap displaced from SAP / Oracle / Salesforce / Workday / ServiceNow / Epic / Cerner across categories. The maintainer continues to commit fourteen times a week from his kitchen and accepts none of the Forbes-cover offers.
- **What the period is known for** — the conventional four-reason curriculum: organizational-antibody collapse, the operating model freeing the operator, *"we are competing against a file format"* as the leaked epitaph, philanthropic infrastructure operationally indistinguishable from the operating company.
- **The mirror** — the load-bearing universal lesson: **Hershey's was every company.** The lesson is not chocolate-specific. The lesson is not CPG-specific. The lesson is: *the existence of an open-source substrate that compresses your category's institutional knowledge into declarative code is not a vendor threat, not a startup threat, not a competitor threat — it is an organizational-design threat that the standard hiring practices of your industry have, across the previous four decades, structurally rendered you incapable of recognizing in time.* Every company that reads the Wars should see itself in Hershey's. The reader who concludes the lesson does not apply to their organization — because their category is different, their relationships deeper, their compliance moat structural, *something* — is the reader the discipline is most interested in, because that reader is, on the standard projection, three to eleven years from being the protagonist of the next casebook. The Wars are read for the mirror. The mirror is the lesson.
- **The maintainer's quiet remark** — three hours after the acquisition announcement, Christopher posts a six-word comment on an unrelated GitHub issue: *"The work continues. The work continues. — C.B."* The comment is, on the discipline's consensus, the closing remark of the Chocolate Wars period and the opening remark of the era that followed.

The epilogue file lives at `epilogue.md` and is wired into `build_epub.py` between the post-credits scene and the EPUB's terminal artifacts.

## Register guide for the rewrite

The first draft of this book read like a slower, slightly more kinetic *Forward.* The rewrite is correcting in the other direction. Concretely:

- **Short sentences.** Jab, jab, cross. Subordinate clauses get cut to commas or full stops.
- **Counters and dashboards in the prose.** Jimmy thinks in numbers. The numbers are on screens. The screens are in the book.
- **YouTube cadence.** Cold open, escalating stakes, audacious flex, payoff number on screen, next chapter starts a beat ahead of where the last one ended.
- **Tech surface visible.** SKILLDOC, Andon Loop, OIE, Interchange packets, `.agi` snippets — all named on-page. Where useful, short code excerpts appear as block quotes, exactly as the existing Ch 7 quotes the README. Readers who want to look up the repo can. Readers who do not, skim.
- **Showman beats.** Jimmy films videos. Jimmy bets on outcomes on camera. Jimmy walks the camera through the chocolate aisle, the studio with the live counter, the fifth-hundredth school's ribbon cutting. The chapters that culminate in a video drop, end on the view count.
- **Thandi gets the dignity beats.** The KwaZulu-Natal chapters are not about Jimmy parachuting in. They are about Thandi running the program. Jimmy is the substrate's owner; Thandi is the operator who scales it from eight to five hundred. The book honors that. Her POV in Ch 3 and the ribbon-cutting in Ch 13 are her chapters.
