# The Chocolate Wars: A Future Case Study

**Author:** Christopher Bender
**Publisher:** AI WIN-WIN Institute
**Volume:** 13 (Textbook Shelf, companion to *Carry* and *Forward*)
**Genre:** Business thriller / fast-twitch operator novel (Lewis / Stone / *Liar's Poker* lineage, with a YouTube-coded register where the previous two used a hedge-fund register)
**Length target:** ~50,000 words, 14 chapters, 4 acts
**Register:** Close third, past tense, kinetic. Faster cadence than *Carry* or *Forward*. The protagonist runs on different operational metabolism than Cole Westerlund did.

> **Revision note (May 2026):** This outline reflects the first-draft plan. The published chapters supersede it where they conflict. Two substantive revisions to the cast: (1) Jimmy's wife was renamed and reshaped — she is now **Thea**, American, no Cape Town / KwaZulu-Natal background, supportive Greenville spouse rather than program director; (2) **Thandi Khoza** runs Beast Philanthropy on the ground (she was already in the original outline as a supporting character; the revision elevates her to the operational lead and gives her the Ch 3 POV and the Ch 13 visit with Eleanor that the first draft assigned to Lerato Booysen). The book's themes, plot beats, and closing arguments are unchanged.

## Concept

This is the book where the most-subscribed YouTuber on the planet, a man with a hundred-and-fifty-employee studio in Greenville, North Carolina, a chocolate brand that has been eating Hershey's shelf space for three years, a wife he met eighteen months earlier and married eleven months ago, and a philanthropy operation that is, by the start of the book, building its eighth school in KwaZulu-Natal — discovers an open-source project called Agicore and realizes, across approximately ninety-one minutes of reading the README in his studio at two AM on a Tuesday in March of Year Nine, that the operating philosophy he has been using on instinct for nine years is the operating philosophy somebody named Christopher Bender has been formalizing into infrastructure in a small house on the north side of a small town in Missouri.

Jimmy Donaldson does not know who Christopher Bender is.

Christopher Bender knows who Jimmy Donaldson is, because everybody knows who Jimmy Donaldson is.

This is the book where they find each other.

It is also the book where the Hershey Company — a hundred-and-sixteen-year-old chocolate manufacturer founded in 1894 in central Pennsylvania by a man named Milton Hershey who, in 1909, donated all of his chocolate-company stock to a trust whose sole beneficiary was a school for underprivileged children — discovers, across thirty months in real time, that the legitimate heir of its founder's operating philosophy is not the financial-services-trained CEO who currently runs the company. The legitimate heir is, in operational terms, a twenty-six-year-old in Greenville who has never read a Milton Hershey biography, has never visited the Hershey factory, has never been to Hershey, Pennsylvania, and is, by the cold accounting of his own published 2024 internal-operations talk at his studio, running the most disciplined small-batch chocolate manufacturing operation in the United States.

This is *Empire Strikes Back* in the YouTube register. *Forward* was the chess game. *The Chocolate Wars* is the prizefight.

## What the book is about, in the AI WIN-WIN tradition

This book is, in the imprint's tradition, an extended demonstration of the operating principle the imprint exists to teach.

The principle, named once and used as the load-bearing argument of the entire book, is:

**The chocolate is the means. The schools are the end.**

And, paired with it:

**The empire is the means. The life is the end.**

Both are versions of the same argument: *the work is not the point.* The work is the substrate that makes the point possible. The point, in the AI era, is the same point it has always been — feeding the people who need to be fed, schooling the children who need to be schooled, building the life the founder actually wants to live. AI is what makes the substrate cheaper. AI is not the point.

The book argues this through Jimmy. Jimmy is, by his own honest admission to his wife in the opening chapter, working too much. He is missing the wedding of her brother. He is missing the funeral of her cousin. He is, in operational terms, the most overworked CEO in the United States. He cannot stop working because the empire requires him to be in every operational decision and there is, today, no architecture that lets him delegate the way he needs to delegate. He finds Agicore. The architecture exists. The empire becomes deterministic. Jimmy gets his time back. The time goes to the wife, to the family that is on the way by the close of the book, and to the schools.

The book lands on a closing image of Jimmy in the kitchen in Greenville on a Sunday morning, making pancakes for a child Lerato is pregnant with. The closing argument is the closing argument. *The work is the work. The work is what AI is for. The work is what we are for. The work is the answer.*

This is the same closer as *Forward.* It lands at a different scale and in a different register. In *Forward* it landed as the wisdom of a sixty-five-year-old founder at the end of his career. In *The Chocolate Wars* it lands as the lesson a twenty-six-year-old founder learns before he wastes the rest of his life on it.

## Why the Toyota Production System matters in this book

Jimmy Donaldson has, by every public-record observation his real-world counterpart has given in five years of interviews, internalized the operating principles of the Toyota Production System without ever having read a page about Toyota. He talks about thumbnail iteration the way a Toyota line worker talks about cycle-time reduction. He talks about cutting decisions the way Lean talks about eliminating waste. He talks about feedback loops the way the TPS Andon Cord — the line worker's right to stop the line — talks about feedback loops. He has never named TPS in any public interview. He has never cited Toyota. He has, however, in operational terms, been running a TPS-coded video-and-product manufacturing operation for nine consecutive years.

This is the load-bearing character beat of the book.

When Jimmy finds Agicore in Chapter 7, the click moment is not "wow, new tech." The click moment is *recognition.* The Agicore README contains the Andon Loop section — Agicore's named application of the Andon Cord principle to AI-proposed system modifications. Jimmy reads the section. He laughs out loud at his desk in Greenville at two AM. He calls his head of production. He says, in a sentence the head of production will remember for the rest of his career: *"I think I have been a Toyota guy this whole time and didn't know it."*

The Agicore architecture is, in operational terms, the formalization of what Jimmy has been doing for nine years on instinct. He doesn't adopt it. He recognizes it. The recognition is the moment the book pivots from *Jimmy versus Hershey's* into *Jimmy versus the version of Jimmy who has been doing all of this without infrastructure.* The second version of Jimmy is the bigger fight. Agicore is the equipment that ends it.

This also explains, in the book's frame, why Jimmy has been so interested in manufacturing across the previous three years. Real-world Jimmy has, in approximately fourteen public statements between 2022 and 2025, expressed deep and unusual interest in manufacturing as a discipline. The book's frame for this interest: manufacturing is the highest expression of the TPS-coded operational thinking Jimmy has been practicing on YouTube. YouTube is manufacturing thumbnails at scale. Feastables is manufacturing chocolate bars at scale. Beast Games is manufacturing experiences at scale. Jimmy has been a manufacturer the whole time. He just thought he was a content creator.

Which gives the book a third quiet beat: *Jimmy out-manufactures Hershey's because he understands manufacturing better than Hershey's does.* Hershey's stopped being a manufacturer in operational posture two decades ago. The financial-services-trained executives who have run the company since the mid-2000s have managed it as a brand-and-distribution business. Jimmy is, in operational terms, the kind of manufacturer Milton Hershey was in 1909. The book demonstrates, across fourteen chapters, that the legitimate heir of Milton Hershey's operating philosophy is not the trust that owns the company. The legitimate heir is a twenty-six-year-old in North Carolina who has never set foot in Pennsylvania.

The closing image of the book reinforces this. The last we see of Eleanor Whitcomb, the Milton Hershey School Trust board member who is the book's institutional foil, is her standing at the original Milton Hershey grave in Hershey Cemetery on a Saturday morning in November, having sent Jimmy an email at two AM that morning that read, in its entirety: *"You won. Use it well."* Eleanor understands what has happened. The trust does not yet understand. Eleanor resigns from the trust the following Monday.

## Chapter-by-chapter

### Act 1: The Empire (Ch 1-4, ~13,000 words)

**Chapter 1: Greenville** (Jimmy POV, ~3,400 words)
Four AM Tuesday morning in March of Year Nine, in the Greenville, North Carolina, studio Jimmy bought in 2021 and has been expanding for the previous four years. Jimmy is at the editing bay of bay number two, on his fifty-seventh consecutive hour of work on a video that is supposed to drop on Saturday morning at ten AM Eastern. Lerato walks into the bay at four oh-eight AM in her coat, having driven to the studio from the house in Greenville they bought after the wedding. She does not, on her honest read of him in the editing bay, ask him to come home. She tells him, in seven sentences, that her brother's wedding was last Saturday, that her cousin's funeral was three weeks earlier, that he has missed both, and that she is, in operational terms, going to be making a decision about whether the marriage works at the current pace inside the next ninety days. She is not threatening to leave. She is telling him the runway. The runway is the runway. Jimmy listens. He does not interrupt. He does not promise anything he cannot deliver. He says, at the end of the seven sentences: *"Lerato. Ninety days. I am going to come back to you with a different operating model inside ninety days. The model is going to be the model. If the model does not work, I will be the one who leaves the empire, not you."* She kisses the top of his head. She walks out of the bay. The bay clock reads four eleven AM.

**Chapter 2: The Cease-and-Desist** (Jimmy POV, ~3,200 words)
Friday morning of the same week. Karim Brock walks into Jimmy's office at the studio with a manila envelope. The envelope contains a cease-and-desist letter from the Hershey Company's outside counsel at Latham & Watkins. The letter is fourteen pages. The letter alleges Feastables' new "Crunch" wrapper design infringes on Hershey's Krackel wrapper trade dress. The letter demands Feastables stop selling the Crunch product, recall existing inventory, and pay damages. Jimmy reads the letter. He hands it to Ana Vasquez, who reads it. He hands it to his general counsel, who reads it. The general counsel says, in operational terms, that the case is weak but is going to cost between two and four million dollars to defeat in litigation. Jimmy thinks about it for forty-three minutes. He does not respond through counsel. He films a video. The video is six minutes long. The video walks the audience through the letter, the wrapper-design specifics, the Hershey Company's history of trade-dress-litigation in the chocolate space, and Jimmy's response. The video closes on Jimmy holding the actual physical letter, looking at the camera, and saying: *"We are not changing the wrapper. We will see them in court. Or, more likely, we will see them on Walmart's shelves."* The video drops Saturday at ten AM Eastern. It hits one hundred million views by Tuesday. Hershey's withdraws the cease-and-desist on the following Friday afternoon.

**Chapter 3: KwaZulu-Natal** (Lerato POV, ~3,300 words)
Two weeks after the cease-and-desist. Lerato is at the Beast Philanthropy operations site in Underberg, KwaZulu-Natal, where the eighth school is being built. Jimmy is supposed to be on the trip with her. He is not on the trip. He sent her an apology video on Sunday night. She is on the trip alone. The chapter is her — the school, the children, the on-the-ground operations, the small staff who run the program in South Africa, her own family connection to the region, the funeral of her cousin three months earlier that Jimmy missed. The school is going to open in May. The third classroom is going to be the third classroom. She sits in it on a Tuesday morning, alone, on a small chair built for a six-year-old, and she thinks about whether the marriage is going to be a marriage. She does not, at the close of the chapter, have an answer. She writes Jimmy an email at two AM South African time that contains a photograph she took that morning of the third classroom, with no caption. She sends it. She closes the laptop.

**Chapter 4: Hershey, Pennsylvania** (Eleanor Whitcomb POV, ~3,400 words)
The Milton Hershey School Trust's quarterly meeting. Eleven board members. The chapter is from Eleanor's POV. Marcus Whittaker, CEO of the Hershey Company, presents the Q1 numbers. The numbers show Feastables has taken approximately eleven percent of the chocolate-bar category by dollar value across the previous twelve months — up from approximately seven percent in Q1 of Year Eight and approximately three percent in Q1 of Year Seven. Marcus walks the board through the response plan — the cease-and-desist (already withdrawn after the YouTube video, which Marcus does not show the board), the Walmart-pressure campaign Yvette Park is running, the new product launches Hershey's has scheduled for Q3, and the marketing-spend increase the brand team has requested. Eleanor listens. Eleanor, on her own honest read of the meeting, is the only person in the room who recognizes that the response plan is the Stupid Company™ playbook applied to a fight the Stupid Company™ playbook does not know how to win. She does not, at this meeting, say so out loud. She makes a note in the small leather notebook she has carried to every trust meeting for fourteen years. The note is: *"Marcus is not going to be the CEO who saves the company. The trust does not yet know."*

### Act 2: The Wars Begin (Ch 5-8, ~13,000 words)

**Chapter 5: Bentonville** (Jimmy POV, ~3,300 words)
A Wednesday in April. Walmart's senior vice president of edible-grocery category management, a woman named Carla Hernandez who has been at Walmart for nineteen years, calls Jimmy on a secure line at eight AM Pacific. Carla has been a friend of Jimmy's since Feastables' first national-distribution deal in 2022. Carla tells Jimmy that the Hershey Company has, across the previous nine weeks, been running a multi-pronged retailer-relationship campaign — calls from Marcus Whittaker to Walmart's CEO, calls from Yvette Park to Carla's boss, a series of co-marketing deals Hershey's is offering Walmart at terms Walmart has not been offered before — explicitly to position Walmart to reduce Feastables' shelf allocation in the next planogram review in Q3. Carla is calling to tell Jimmy because she does not, on her own read of the situation, want to be the executive who has to tell him after the planogram has changed. Jimmy thanks her. He flies to Bentonville the next day on a private charter. He meets with Carla at her office. He meets with Carla's boss. He meets, on the third meeting of the trip, with Walmart's chief merchant. The meeting runs ninety minutes. The chief merchant tells Jimmy, in plain language, that Walmart will not, under any circumstance, give Feastables less shelf space than its sell-through justifies. The Hershey pressure campaign has, on the chief merchant's read, been an attempt to manipulate Walmart that Walmart is not going to tolerate. Jimmy walks out of Bentonville with the shelf allocation expanded by a category SKU.

**Chapter 6: The Walmart Aisle Video** (Jimmy POV, ~3,000 words)
Three weeks after the Bentonville trip. Jimmy, who has been thinking about how to land the Hershey-pressure-campaign-failure beat with the public, decides to film a video in a Walmart chocolate aisle. He flies to a Walmart in Rogers, Arkansas, on a Monday afternoon. He shoots, in the aisle, a five-minute video in which he walks the camera past every Feastables product and every Hershey product on the shelf, narrates the relative shelf allocation (Feastables has approximately fourteen percent of the chocolate-bar facings; Hershey's has approximately fifty-one percent; the rest is distributed across Mars, Mondelez, and smaller players), and closes with a line that becomes, within forty-eight hours, the top quote on financial Twitter: *"The reason Hershey's has fifty-one percent of this aisle and we have fourteen is that they have been here longer. The reason we have fourteen and not three is that the people who walk past these shelves are voting. The voting is going to keep going. We will see how this aisle looks in a year."* The video drops Tuesday at ten AM. It hits two hundred and forty million views by Friday.

**Chapter 7: The Andon Cord** (Jimmy POV, ~3,500 words)
Two AM on a Tuesday in early May. Jimmy is in his office at the Greenville studio. He has not slept more than four hours in any of the previous nine nights. The empire's operational fragmentation has, across the previous six weeks of the Hershey fight, become the binding constraint on every decision Jimmy is trying to make. He cannot, in operational terms, move fast enough because the empire requires him to hold the coordination across thirteen LLCs and four hundred and ten employees in his head, and his head is not, on his own honest read of himself, holding the coordination cleanly any more.

He is at his desk reading a Hacker News thread on his phone.

A thread links to an open-source project he has not, in three years of building Feastables and four years of running Beast Games, encountered. The project is called Agicore. He clicks the link. He reads the README. The README opens with a single sentence in bold: *Agicore — A deterministic systems-authoring platform for AI-native organizations.* He keeps reading. The Andon Loop section appears on screen seven minutes into his read. He reads the section three times. He laughs out loud at his desk at two seventeen AM.

He calls Karim Brock. Karim is at his apartment in Greenville. It is two seventeen AM. Karim picks up on the third ring.

He says: *"Karim."*

Karim says: *"Jimmy. It is two seventeen AM."*

Jimmy says: *"Karim. I think I have been a Toyota guy this whole time and didn't know it."*

Karim is quiet for a beat.

Karim says: *"Jimmy."*

Jimmy says: *"Yes."*

Karim says: *"I have been waiting for you to find this out for six years. Welcome."*

Jimmy says: *"Karim."*

Karim says: *"Yes."*

Jimmy says: *"There is an open-source project. The project is the project. I am going to submit an issue."*

Karim says: *"Submit the issue. Then go to bed."*

Jimmy submits the issue. He does not go to bed.

**Chapter 8: The Issue** (Jimmy POV + brief Bender beat, ~3,200 words)
The chapter is the GitHub issue Jimmy submits at two forty-six AM Eastern, with the open-source repository's URL at github.com/Binary-Blender/agicore. The issue is titled: *I think we have been doing the same thing.* The body is eleven sentences. The body opens with: *Jimmy Donaldson. I have been running a manufacturing operation on YouTube and on the chocolate aisle for nine years. I have been doing it without knowing I was doing it. I read your README tonight. I recognize the Andon Loop. I have been doing the Andon Loop without knowing it had a name.* The issue closes with: *I would like to talk. I would like to learn what your project does that my organization can use. I would also like to talk about what my organization does that your project can use. We have, in operational terms, been doing the same work in different rooms. Let's see if we should be in the same room. — Jimmy.*

The issue posts as #58 on the repository.

Bender, in his back room in Missouri, sees the GitHub notification at one fifty-three AM Central. He reads the issue. He reads it three times. He looks at the timestamp. He sits back. He smiles.

He thinks: *Hello, Jimmy. I have been waiting for you too.*

He types four words into the comment box.

He types: *Jimmy. Come visit. — C.B.*

He clicks Comment.

The comment posts at one fifty-six AM Central.

It is, by Bender's quiet count, the second time in twenty-two months that he has answered an issue from someone he has not, in his own life, expected to be operating in his orbit. The first time was Cole, in May of Year Nine. The second time is Jimmy, in May of Year Ten.

The pattern is the pattern.

The work continues.

### Act 3: The Unification (Ch 9-12, ~13,000 words)

**Chapter 9: The Specification** (Jimmy POV with brief Karim Brock POV, ~3,300 words)
Jimmy flies to Missouri. He spends three days with Bender in the small house. The three days are the three days Karim's working title for the trip, in the studio Slack he is not telling anyone he has, is *Jimmy's Lost Weekend.* The three days are the days Jimmy and Bender write together the first draft of the MrBeast empire's `.agi` specification — the deterministic operational substrate that will, across the next ninety days, replace the operational fragmentation Jimmy has been holding in his head for nine years.

The chapter is not the technical detail. The chapter is the conversation across the three days. Bender, at his kitchen table. Jimmy, in the chair across from him. The two of them mapping out the empire's operational dependencies on a whiteboard Bender bought at the small-town hardware store on Friday afternoon when it became clear Jimmy was going to be in Missouri longer than the original itinerary had allowed for.

Karim's brief POV is the call he takes from Ana Vasquez on Saturday afternoon. Ana asks where Jimmy is. Karim tells her, in operational terms, that Jimmy is *on a research trip he did not pre-clear with you, and that he is going to come back from the trip a different operator than the one who left.* Ana is quiet for a beat. Ana says: *"Karim."* Karim says: *"Yes."* Ana says: *"I have been waiting for this trip for two years."* Karim says: *"I know you have."*

By the end of the chapter Jimmy is on a plane back to Greenville with a draft `.agi` file on his encrypted laptop that runs to approximately three thousand seven hundred lines and that specifies, in deterministic operational terms, the inventory rules, production scheduling, charitable disbursements, brand-extension licensing, contestant-treatment protocols, and the school-funding pipeline that automatically converts every Feastables bar sold to a verifiable number of school-days funded for the Beast Philanthropy operation in KwaZulu-Natal. The file is the file. The work begins on Monday.

**Chapter 10: Beast Games** (Jimmy POV, ~3,400 words)
The first major application of the new operational substrate. Beast Games — the Amazon-distributed reality competition that had, in real-world public reporting across 2024 and 2025, generated a series of operational difficulties involving contestant treatment, production logistics, and prize-disbursement transparency — is, by the start of *The Chocolate Wars,* in pre-production for its third season.

Jimmy commits, across the chapter, to rewriting the production playbook for Season Three on top of the Agicore substrate. Every contestant has a deterministic treatment-protocol envelope generated from the `.agi` file. Every meal, lodging assignment, and medical-coverage decision is automated and logged on the hash-chained ledger. Every prize-pool disbursement is, by the end of the season, traceable from the chocolate-bar revenue line through the production-budget allocation to the contestant's bank account on a public-facing accountability dashboard.

The Season Three production is, on Karim's later operational read, approximately forty percent more capital-efficient than Season Two, with zero contestant-treatment incidents across the production window. The press write-up of the season, by a *Hollywood Reporter* writer named Adelaide Krug (yes, that Adelaide Krug — she covers the entertainment industry on the side now, having become the most-read corporate-strategy reporter in the country across the previous three years), titles the piece: *Beast Games Grows Up.*

**Chapter 11: The Trust Meeting** (Eleanor Whitcomb POV, ~3,200 words)
The Q2 Milton Hershey School Trust meeting. Marcus Whittaker presents. Feastables has, in the quarter following the Bentonville trip and the Walmart aisle video, taken an additional four points of category share. The Q2 numbers are worse than the Q1 numbers by every metric the trust tracks. Marcus walks the board through a new, larger response plan. The response plan involves three acquisitions of smaller candy companies, an internal restructuring of Hershey's R&D organization, and a planned twenty-percent increase in trade-marketing spend across the back half of the year.

Eleanor, at this meeting, speaks.

She speaks for eleven minutes.

She walks the board through the structural analysis she has been quietly building across the previous fourteen years on the trust. The analysis is that Hershey's has, in operational posture, stopped being a manufacturer and has become a distribution-and-brand-management company. The analysis is that the company's competitive moat — its eighty-five years of retailer relationships — is a moat that has, in operational terms, been irrelevant since the rise of direct-to-consumer audience-owning brands in approximately 2018. The analysis is that the trust has, across the previous fifteen years, hired CEOs from financial-services and consumer-packaged-goods backgrounds rather than from manufacturing backgrounds, and that the cumulative effect of those hires has been to systematically erode the operational posture Milton Hershey built the company on in 1894.

The trust does not respond well to the eleven minutes.

Marcus interrupts twice. The trust chair asks Eleanor to defer the conversation to the strategic-review subcommittee. Eleanor declines.

She closes the eleven minutes by saying: *"The Hershey Company has, in operational terms, a legitimate heir of its founder's philosophy currently operating in Greenville, North Carolina. The heir is twenty-six years old. The heir is not running the Hershey Company. The reason the heir is not running the Hershey Company is that this board has, across the previous fifteen years, hired CEOs whose operating philosophies are structurally incompatible with the operating philosophy that built this company. I have, on the strength of the analysis I have just walked you through, prepared a resignation letter. I am going to submit the letter at the end of the meeting. I am also going to submit, to the trust's general counsel, a recommendation that the trust open conversations with the heir about whether there is a partnership arrangement that could, in operational terms, return the Hershey Company to its founding philosophy."*

The trust does not, at the meeting, vote on the recommendation.

Eleanor submits the resignation letter.

She walks out.

She goes home.

She emails Jimmy at eleven oh-eight PM that night. The email's subject line is *From a trust board member who has just resigned.* The body is one paragraph. The paragraph is the same paragraph the trust did not let her finish in the meeting. Eleanor sends it. She goes to bed.

**Chapter 12: The Test** (Jimmy POV, ~3,200 words)
The crisis. Mid-Year-Ten. A Feastables supply-chain disruption — a labor stoppage at a major cocoa processor in West Africa — threatens to take Feastables offline for between three and six weeks. The Agicore substrate is now running the Feastables operation. The substrate's response, on its own deterministic rules, is to surface the stoppage to Jimmy at six AM Pacific the morning the news breaks, route an alternative-supplier identification to the supply-chain ops team, calculate the inventory-burn rate against the projected shortage window, and propose three alternative procurement paths each costing between fourteen and twenty-two percent above the baseline cocoa price.

Jimmy, in his office in Greenville, reviews the surfaced alternatives.

He chooses the third option — direct procurement from a cooperative of West African cocoa farmers Lerato's network has been working with for the previous three years. The cooperative pays its farmers approximately three times the market rate for cocoa and has been, by every honest accounting Lerato has been able to assemble across two years of due diligence, the cleanest cocoa supply chain in West Africa. The cooperative has not, until this morning, had the scale to supply Feastables. The disruption is the disruption. The disruption is also the opportunity. Jimmy signs the five-year supply contract at three PM Pacific. The cooperative's revenue, on the contract, is going to grow by approximately seventy times across the next eighteen months.

The chapter is the day of the crisis. Jimmy on the phone with the cooperative's lead organizer (a woman named Adwoa Mensah, based in Kumasi, Ghana). Jimmy on the phone with Lerato (who is, at the moment of the call, in KwaZulu-Natal at the eighth school's opening preparations). Jimmy on the phone with Karim, with Ana, with the Feastables supply-chain ops team, with two of his lawyers, with a Cargill executive he is, on the strength of the new procurement arrangement, going to publicly call out in a video the following week.

The day ends with Jimmy at his desk at midnight Eastern, having taken thirty-one calls across the eighteen hours, having signed two contracts and one open letter, and having committed Feastables to a supply-chain transformation that — by Lerato's own honest read on the closing phone call of the day at eleven forty-eight PM Eastern — is going to be the most consequential operational decision Jimmy has made since founding the company.

The day ends with Jimmy on the phone with Lerato.

Lerato says: *"Jimmy."*

Jimmy says: *"Lerato."*

Lerato says: *"I am pregnant."*

The chapter ends here.

### Act 4: The Reframe (Ch 13-14, ~9,000 words)

**Chapter 13: The Third Classroom** (Jimmy POV, ~4,400 words)
Six weeks after the supply-chain crisis. Jimmy is in KwaZulu-Natal for the opening of the eighth school. Lerato is on the trip with him. She is fourteen weeks pregnant. The trip is the first long international trip the two of them have done together since the wedding eleven months earlier.

The school opens on a Saturday morning. The third classroom has the bench Lerato sat on in Chapter 3, alone, when she was deciding whether the marriage was going to be a marriage. The bench is, on this Saturday morning, occupied by a small girl named Nomvula who is six years old and who is, on her own quiet read of the morning, more excited about being in the third classroom than any other adult or child in the building. Jimmy sits next to Nomvula. Lerato sits next to Jimmy. The three of them watch the school's first-day ceremony from the bench.

Eleanor Whitcomb is at the ceremony.

Jimmy did not, in advance, know Eleanor was going to be at the ceremony.

Eleanor came on her own. She flew commercial from Philadelphia to Johannesburg on a Thursday night. She drove to Underberg on Friday afternoon. She stayed at a small bed-and-breakfast in town. She walked into the school on Saturday morning. She is, in the back row of the ceremony, with no name tag and no announcement, observing the morning.

Jimmy notices her at the close of the ceremony.

He walks over.

He says: *"Eleanor."*

She says: *"Jimmy."*

He says: *"You flew here."*

She says: *"I flew here. I wanted to see one."*

He says: *"What did you think."*

She says: *"Jimmy. I went to the Milton Hershey School in 1965 because my mother could not afford to raise me. The school was the school. The school is, today, sixty years later, the school it was for me. The school changed my life. The school in this building changed nineteen lives this morning. The school that is going to be in the next building will change another nineteen. The school after that will change another nineteen. Milton Hershey would have liked you. I want you to know that. I also want you to know that the trust does not, today, like you, and that the trust is wrong, and that the trust is going to be wrong for a few more years before the trust catches up. I came here because, in the operational sense, I am the only person on the trust who has read what you are doing carefully enough to recognize it. Milton Hershey's school in Pennsylvania is going to keep operating. Your schools in KwaZulu-Natal are going to keep operating. Both of them are going to be operating because the chocolate revenue is the chocolate revenue. Both of them are going to be funding the same kind of work. I came here to tell you, in person, that I have, on my own honest assessment of the previous fifteen years on the trust, decided you are the legitimate heir of Milton Hershey's operating philosophy. I am sixty-six years old. I have read this man's biography seven times. I know the philosophy. The philosophy is in you. The philosophy is not in the trust. The trust is going to figure this out across the next decade. By the time the trust figures it out, you are going to have built something that the trust is going to have to come to and ask for partnership terms on. I am telling you now because I want you to be ready when they come."*

Jimmy listens.

He says: *"Eleanor."*

She says: *"Yes."*

He says: *"Thank you."*

She says: *"You are welcome."*

She walks back to her car.

She drives back to Underberg.

She flies back to Philadelphia.

She begins, on her own, the consulting work she will spend the rest of her professional life on — advising trusts and foundations on the structural alignment between their operating philosophy and their named missions. She becomes, in the AI-WIN-WIN-imprint frame, the senior advisor of a small consulting practice the imprint will publish a textbook by inside two years.

The chapter closes with Jimmy and Lerato on the bench in the third classroom, after the ceremony has ended and the children have left for the day, watching the late-afternoon light through the small windows. Lerato puts her hand on her stomach. Jimmy puts his hand on hers. They sit on the bench for a long minute. Lerato says: *"Jimmy."* Jimmy says: *"Lerato."* Lerato says: *"This was the day."* Jimmy says: *"This was the day."*

**Chapter 14: Pancakes** (Jimmy POV, ~4,400 words)
Six months later. A Sunday morning in February of Year Eleven. Jimmy is in the kitchen of the house in Greenville. Lerato is at the kitchen table. The child Lerato was pregnant with at the close of Chapter 12 was born nine weeks earlier. The child is in the bassinet by the window. The child's name is Kgosi, which is, in Sotho, a name that means *king,* and which Lerato's mother chose on the morning of the second day of Kgosi's life when Lerato had not yet picked a name and was about to default to something English.

Jimmy is making pancakes.

He has been making pancakes on Sunday mornings since the third week of Kgosi's life, when Lerato told him on a Saturday night that she wanted, for the first Sunday of every month for the rest of their lives, a Sunday in which Jimmy did not work. Jimmy agreed. The pancakes are the new tradition. The pancakes are the work he does, in the kitchen, on Sundays.

The chapter is the Sunday morning. The kitchen. The pancakes. Lerato. Kgosi. The Sound of Greenville on a winter morning. The Carrick Cloud-deck weight of *what comes next* that is, by the start of February of Year Eleven, no longer on Jimmy's shoulders because Agicore has been running the empire for the previous nine months and is, on Karim's last operational report, running it at one hundred and forty-three percent of the throughput it ran at in the previous year with seventeen percent fewer hours of senior-staff time committed to operational coordination.

Jimmy has, in operational terms, his life back.

The chapter closes on the speech.

Jimmy says it across the kitchen counter to Lerato while she is feeding Kgosi.

He says:

> *Lerato. The chocolate is the means. The schools are the end. The empire is the means. This — what we are doing right now, in this kitchen, on a Sunday morning, with Kgosi in the bassinet and the pancakes on the griddle and the rest of the world somewhere else — this is the end. The work is the work. The work is what AI is for. The work is what we are for. The work is what makes this possible. The work is not the answer. This is the answer. The work is the substrate that lets the answer be the answer. I have been doing the work for nine years to get to this Sunday morning. I am going to do the work for thirty more years. The work is going to keep being the work. The Sunday mornings are going to keep being the answer. I am sorry I missed the wedding. I am sorry I missed the funeral. I am here now. I am going to be here for the rest of it.*

Lerato does not respond.

She does not need to.

She places her hand on his.

The pancakes are the pancakes.

Kgosi is the answer.

The work continues on Monday.

The book ends here.

## The post-credits scene

Yes, there is one.

A small town in Missouri. Christopher Bender at his desk. Late at night.

He has, by the morning of the second Sunday of February of Year Eleven, exactly two GitHub issues open from people he had not previously expected to be operating in his orbit.

Issue #43 is from Cole Westerlund, posted twenty-two months earlier.

Issue #58 is from Jimmy Donaldson, posted ten months earlier.

The repository has, in the time since Jimmy submitted his issue, grown from one thousand and forty-seven GitHub stars to approximately one hundred and forty-eight thousand. The contributor count has grown from nineteen to approximately two thousand four hundred. The number of `.agi` specifications running production workloads in the world has, on the small public registry the project maintains, crossed approximately seventeen thousand. The post-frontier-AI era is operating, in approximately thirty percent of the consequential business contexts it has begun to reach, on top of the Agicore substrate.

A new GitHub-notification email arrives in Bender's personal inbox at eleven fifty-three PM Central on the Sunday night Jimmy is making pancakes.

The subject line is: *Re: Funding inquiry — from someone you have probably also been expecting.*

Bender reads the email.

He sits back in the chair.

He smiles.

He thinks the line he has, in his own quiet count, been waiting to think for approximately eleven months.

He thinks: *Hello. We have been expecting you too.*

The third person has shown up.

The third person's name is, on the email's signature line, a name Bender has been reading in the business press for the previous five years.

The third person is not in this book.

The third person is the protagonist of the next book.

The book ends with Bender's reply.

The reply is: *Welcome to the room. We have work to do. — C.B.*
