# Chapter 3: KwaZulu-Natal

Lerato Booysen was at the Beast Philanthropy operations site in Underberg, KwaZulu-Natal, on a Tuesday morning two weeks after the cease-and-desist video, in the small site office that had been a converted feed store before Beast Philanthropy bought the building in Year Seven, with a cup of rooibos at her elbow and a small leather notebook open in front of her on the desk that had, by her own three-year accounting of the notebooks she had filled since the start of her work with Beast Philanthropy, become the thirty-first one.

Jimmy was not on the trip.

Jimmy was supposed to be on the trip.

Jimmy had, on Sunday evening, sent her an apology video he had filmed on his phone in the kitchen of the house in Greenville at eleven PM Eastern. The video was forty-two seconds long. The video was Jimmy looking at the camera. The video was Jimmy saying, in seven sentences, that he was not going to be on the trip because the cold-open of the response video to a separate Hershey's pre-positioning move had landed in his lap on Sunday afternoon and that he was, on his honest read of the next thirty hours, the only person in the company who could land it. The video closed with Jimmy saying: *"Lerato. I am sorry. I am going to be at the eighth school opening in May. I am going to be at every school opening for the rest of my life. The May opening is the May opening. I will be there. Tell the team I am sorry."*

Lerato had received the video at six twelve PM South Africa time on Sunday.

She had watched it once.

She had not, in the forty-six hours since, watched it a second time.

She had flown commercial from Greenville to Johannesburg via Atlanta on Sunday night.

She had driven the four hours from Johannesburg to Underberg in a rental SUV she had picked up at OR Tambo on Monday morning.

She had arrived at the site at three twenty-two PM Monday.

She had spent the rest of Monday with the on-the-ground team — Sipho Mthembu, the Beast Philanthropy operations director for KwaZulu-Natal, who had been with the program since the second school in Year Eight; Thandi Khoza, the construction site manager for the eighth school, who had managed three of the previous seven school builds; and the small administrative team of four staff who handled the day-to-day operations of the regional office.

The site office was the office.

The work was the work.

Lerato had been here, by her own count from the small leather notebook, forty-three times in the previous three years.

This was the first trip she had taken without Jimmy.

She had thought, on the flight from Atlanta to Johannesburg, about whether the trip was the right trip to take alone.

She had decided, by the time the plane crossed the Equator, that the trip was.

The trip was the trip.

The work was the work.

The notebook was the notebook.

---

She drove to the school site at seven forty AM Tuesday.

The site was three kilometers east of Underberg, on a small road that ran past two farms before it reached the property the program had bought from the local municipality in Year Seven for thirty thousand rand. The school was approximately seventy percent complete on the morning Lerato arrived. The structure was a single-story building of approximately eight hundred square meters with four classrooms, an administrative office, a small kitchen, two bathrooms, a covered outdoor learning area, and a small library room that Lerato had personally requested in the program-design review in Year Eight on the basis that the previous seven schools had not had dedicated library rooms and that the request had been pushed through against the standard cost-optimization template Sipho had been operating under.

The library room was the library room.

The library room was the room Lerato wanted in every school.

The library room was, on the on-the-ground team's quiet read of the program-design conversation, the room Lerato had been advocating for since the first school in Year Seven and had, until the eighth-school review in Year Eight, been declined on the standard cost-optimization grounds.

She had won the eighth-school library room.

She would, by her own quiet plan she had not yet articulated to Jimmy, win every subsequent school's library room.

The library room was the library room.

The work was the work.

She walked the site.

Thandi Khoza walked with her.

Thandi was forty-three years old, born in Pietermaritzburg, a structural engineer by training, who had spent fourteen years on commercial-construction projects across South Africa before joining Beast Philanthropy in Year Seven for, in Thandi's own description on her first day, *the work I have, across the previous fourteen years, been waiting to be paid to do.*

Thandi walked Lerato through the third classroom.

The third classroom was the classroom on the south side of the building, with two south-facing windows and one east-facing window and a small built-in storage closet on the back wall that Thandi had, on her own design initiative in February, added to the original architectural plans because she had observed across the previous seven school builds that the teachers in each school had ended up improvising storage solutions for classroom supplies that, in operational terms, would have been cheaper and more durable to design in from the start.

The storage closet was the storage closet.

The storage closet was, by Lerato's own honest read of the work Thandi did across every site, the kind of small operational decision that distinguished the Beast Philanthropy school-building program from approximately ninety-three percent of the other school-building programs Lerato had visited across the previous six years of her own documentary work in southern and east Africa.

The Beast Philanthropy program was the program.

The work was the work.

The schools were the schools.

Lerato sat down on the small chair built for a six-year-old in the third classroom at eight oh-six AM Tuesday morning.

She did not, in the moment of sitting down, intend to sit on the chair for any particular length of time.

She sat on the chair.

She looked out the south-facing window.

The window looked at the slope of the small valley the school was built in. The slope was dry winter grass. The horizon was the foothills of the southern Drakensberg, approximately forty kilometers to the west.

She thought about Jimmy.

She thought about the marriage.

She thought about the conversation in bay number two two weeks earlier.

She thought about whether the ninety days were going to be the ninety days she had told him they were going to be.

She thought, in the small classroom in Underberg on the slope of the small valley, for approximately forty minutes.

Thandi did not, in the forty minutes, interrupt her.

Thandi understood, from her own three years of having been the one Lerato sat in classrooms with, that Lerato sat in classrooms when Lerato was deciding things.

Thandi waited outside the classroom in the corridor.

She watched the building's other workers continue the work.

She did not, in the forty minutes, mention to anyone on the site that Lerato was in the third classroom.

The site continued.

The work continued.

---

At eight forty-six AM Lerato stood up from the chair.

She walked to the doorway of the third classroom.

She said: "Thandi."

Thandi said: "Lerato."

Lerato said: "I have been deciding something. The deciding is done. I am going to tell you the deciding in two sentences because the deciding is going to be in the next quarterly operations report, and you and Sipho need to know it now."

Thandi said: "Tell me."

Lerato said: "Beast Philanthropy is going to commit, by the end of Year Eleven, to building between forty and fifty additional schools in southern and east Africa, on top of the eight already operating and the two currently under construction. The additional schools are going to be funded by a combination of the existing Feastables-revenue allocation, a new Lerato-led private-foundation arrangement I am going to put fifteen million dollars of my own money into in Q2, and a partnership with two regional African development organizations I have been speaking with for the previous nine months that are, on the strength of the partnership terms, going to add approximately eleven million dollars of additional capital per year across the next four years. The total program is going to be the largest school-building program of any single source of capital in southern and east Africa in the previous twenty years."

Thandi was quiet for a beat.

She said: "Lerato."

Lerato said: "Yes."

Thandi said: "Does Jimmy know."

Lerato said: "Jimmy will know on Saturday."

Thandi said: "All right."

Lerato said: "Thandi."

Thandi said: "Yes."

Lerato said: "I am going to need you to be the operations director for the expanded program. The role is going to be different from your current role. The role is going to be the lead operations role for the full forty-to-fifty-school build-out. I am going to ask you on Saturday after I have told Jimmy. I am telling you now because I want you to have the weekend to think about it."

Thandi was quiet for a longer beat.

She said: "Lerato."

Lerato said: "Yes."

Thandi said: "I do not need the weekend."

Lerato said: "Tell me anyway."

Thandi said: "Yes. I will take the role. I have been waiting for the role for two years. I am going to take it on the terms you offer because the terms you offer are going to be the right terms. The work is the work. The schools are the schools. The library rooms are the library rooms. I am going to build all of them."

Lerato smiled for the first time in three days.

She said: "Thandi."

Thandi said: "Yes."

Lerato said: "Thank you."

Thandi said: "You are welcome."

Lerato walked back to the rental SUV.

She drove to the small bed-and-breakfast in Underberg where she was staying.

She sat at the small desk by the window of the room.

She opened her laptop.

She wrote an email to Jimmy.

The email had no subject line.

The body of the email was a single photograph she had taken from the third classroom of the eighth school at eight forty-five AM that morning, looking out the south-facing window at the slope of the small valley and the southern Drakensberg foothills forty kilometers away.

The photograph had no caption.

She attached the photograph.

She clicked send.

The email arrived in Jimmy's inbox at six oh-eight AM Eastern Tuesday morning.

Jimmy was at the editing bay of bay number two in the Greenville studio when the email arrived.

He saw the email come in.

He clicked it.

He saw the photograph.

He looked at the photograph for one minute and forty seconds.

He did not, in the one minute and forty seconds, type a response.

He closed the email.

He went back to the editing bay.

He did not, across the rest of the day, send Lerato a response.

He thought about the photograph for the rest of the day.

He thought about the ninety days.

He thought about the operating model.

He thought about the architecture.

The architecture, by the close of the day, was still not the architecture.

---

Lerato flew back to Greenville on Saturday morning.

She arrived at the house at six forty PM Eastern Saturday.

Jimmy was at the house.

He had cooked dinner.

He had cooked the only thing he had reliably been able to cook across the previous nine years of his life, which was a roast chicken with potatoes and carrots that Lerato had taught him to make in the third month of their dating.

The chicken was on the kitchen counter.

The potatoes were on the kitchen counter.

The carrots were on the kitchen counter.

Lerato walked into the kitchen.

She set her bag down.

She looked at the kitchen.

She said: "Jimmy."

He said: "Lerato."

She said: "The chicken."

He said: "The chicken."

She said: "Did you make the chicken."

He said: "I made the chicken."

She said: "Jimmy."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "I have something to tell you about the trip."

He said: "Tell me."

She told him about the forty-to-fifty-school expansion plan.

She told him about the fifteen-million-dollar Lerato-led private-foundation arrangement.

She told him about Thandi taking the lead operations role.

She told him about the library rooms.

Jimmy listened.

When she finished, he said: "Lerato."

She said: "Yes."

He said: "The schools are the schools."

She said: "The schools are the schools."

He said: "I am going to make the empire fund the schools at the rate the schools are going to need to be funded at. I am also going to find the operating architecture I have been failing to find for the previous three weeks of the ninety-day window. I am going to find it because, on the photograph you sent me from the third classroom on Tuesday, I figured out the thing I had been missing about why I have not been able to find the architecture."

She said: "Tell me."

He said: "Lerato. I have been looking for the architecture in the operational software industry. I have been looking at enterprise CRM platforms, ERP systems, the standard tooling that the CPG industry runs on. The architecture is not there. The architecture I need is, on my own honest read of the photograph, going to be the architecture of a small system somebody has been building specifically for the problem I have. I have not yet found it. I am going to find it across the next seventy-three days. I am telling you, on this Saturday night in our kitchen with the chicken you have been waiting for me to learn to cook for the previous three years, that I am going to find it. The photograph was the photograph. The photograph was the message. The message was: the schools are real. The schools deserve the architecture I am about to build for them. I am going to build it."

She did not say anything for a beat.

She walked across the kitchen to him.

She placed her hand, palm flat, on his chest.

She said: "Jimmy."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "Eat the chicken with me."

He said: "I am eating the chicken with you."

They ate the chicken.

The chicken was the chicken.

The potatoes were the potatoes.

The carrots were the carrots.

The dinner was the first dinner Jimmy had cooked for Lerato in their kitchen in the eleven months of their marriage.

The dinner was the first dinner.

The dinner would not, in the future arc of their marriage, be the last.

The ninety-day clock, by Lerato's quiet count on the small notepad on the kitchen counter, was at day twenty-three and counting.

Jimmy did not, that night, find the architecture.

He found it forty-five days later, at two AM on a Tuesday in early May, in his office at the Greenville studio, after Karim Brock had walked out at one twenty AM, after the studio had been silent for forty minutes, after a Hacker News thread on his phone had linked to an open-source project he had not previously encountered.

The project was the project.

The architecture was the architecture.

The night in question was the night the Andon Cord chapter is going to be about.

For now, in Chapter 3, the Saturday-night dinner is the Saturday-night dinner.

The schools are the schools.

The library rooms are the library rooms.

The marriage is, on Lerato's quiet count, going to make it through the ninety days.

The architecture is, on Lerato's quiet count, going to be found.

The work is the work.

The work continues.
