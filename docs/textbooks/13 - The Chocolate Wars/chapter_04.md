# Chapter 4: Hershey, Pennsylvania

Eleanor Whitcomb walked into the boardroom of the Milton Hershey School Trust at the Hershey Trust Company building on East Chocolate Avenue at seven fifty-four AM Eastern on a Wednesday in mid-April.

She was wearing the charcoal-gray suit.

She had worn the charcoal-gray suit to every quarterly trust meeting for the previous fourteen years.

She was carrying the small leather notebook.

She had carried the small leather notebook to every quarterly trust meeting for the previous fourteen years.

She was wearing, on the lapel of the charcoal-gray suit, the Milton Hershey School class-of-1972 alumni pin she had worn on her left lapel every working day of her professional life since the morning of her own graduation from the school in May of 1972, the morning the girl who had been Eleanor Margaret Whitfield of Hummelstown, Pennsylvania, the daughter of a single mother who had worked the cafeteria line at the Hershey factory across forty-one years and had not been able, in any of those forty-one years, to afford to raise her own child at home, had walked across the stage in the school's auditorium with a one-way bus ticket to Philadelphia in her pocket and the pin in her hand.

The pin was small.

The pin was a small enameled-bronze rendering of the school's coat of arms.

The pin had cost the school approximately two dollars and seventy-three cents in 1972.

Eleanor had never, in fifty-four years, worn a different pin on the lapel of any suit she had owned.

The eight other board members who had arrived before her were already seated.

The chair of the trust — a seventy-three-year-old retired federal judge from Harrisburg named Harold Brennan, in his eleventh year on the board and his sixth as chair — looked up from his agenda packet at the head of the long mahogany table and said, in the dry small-voice register he used at the start of every meeting: *"Eleanor. Good morning."*

Eleanor said: *"Harold. Good morning."*

She sat down in her usual seat — the third chair from the head of the table on the south side, the seat she had taken at her first trust meeting in May of 2012 and had not moved from in the fifty-five quarterly meetings since.

She opened the leather notebook.

She uncapped the small black fountain pen she carried in the inside breast pocket of the suit.

She wrote, at the top of the first blank page of the notebook, in the small precise hand she had been writing in for fifty years:

*Q1 — Y9 — April*

She drew a horizontal line under the date.

She waited.

---

Marcus Whittaker walked into the boardroom at seven fifty-eight AM.

Marcus was fifty-two.

Marcus was wearing the navy two-button suit Eleanor had, across the previous four years, watched him wear to every trust meeting he had given a presentation in. The suit had been tailored on Savile Row during the year Marcus had spent at McKinsey London after the year at Goldman New York and before the four years at Kraft Chicago that had positioned him for the Hershey CEO seat at the start of Year Six. Marcus had told the trust, at the dinner the trust had hosted in Marcus's honor the night before his hiring vote in February of Year Six, that the suit was the suit he had worn to the interview at Goldman in 1996 and that the suit was, on the strength of having been tailored well in 1996 and having been re-tailored to his size every eighteen months since, the only suit he ever wore to a meeting that mattered.

Eleanor had, in the four years since the dinner, noted the suit at every meeting.

Eleanor had noted, in the small leather notebook on the morning of the dinner, in 2010, the following sentence: *Marcus wears the suit to signal continuity. The continuity is McKinsey continuity. The continuity is not Hershey continuity. The trust has, on the strength of two interview rounds and a Savile Row suit, hired a McKinsey CEO. We will see what this means.*

Across four years she had seen what it meant.

Across four years she had seen what it meant in the marketing-spend decisions, in the trade-marketing reallocations, in the brand-extension strategy, in the three small acquisitions Marcus had executed in Year Seven that had, in operational terms, been the kind of acquisitions a McKinsey-trained CEO acquired and were not, in operational terms, the kind of acquisitions a chocolate manufacturer needed.

She had not, in four years, said so out loud at a trust meeting.

She had, in four years, written approximately four hundred and seventy pages of small-fountain-pen observations across the leather notebook and the eleven leather notebooks that preceded it on the small shelf in the office of her home in Hummelstown.

She watched Marcus walk to the south end of the boardroom.

Marcus set his laptop on the small podium.

Marcus connected the laptop to the boardroom's display via the HDMI cable that lived in a small box on the podium.

The boardroom's wall-mounted seventy-five-inch display lit up with the first slide of the Q1 presentation.

The first slide was titled: *Q1 Year Nine — Hershey Company Strategic Review.*

The second slide was titled: *Category Share — Trailing Twelve Months.*

The second slide showed a chart.

The chart showed the chocolate-bar category by dollar value across the trailing twelve months.

The Hershey Company's share of the category at the start of the trailing twelve months was approximately fifty-three percent.

The Hershey Company's share of the category at the end of the trailing twelve months was approximately forty-six percent.

The slide's caption, in twelve-point text under the chart, read: *Category share down 7 points YoY. Driven primarily by Feastables (now 11% category share, up from 7% prior-year, 3% two-year prior).*

The room was quiet.

Marcus said, in the same dry McKinsey register he had used to open every presentation Eleanor had watched him give in four years: *"Good morning, board. I want to walk you through our Q1 numbers and the response strategy we have built across the past six weeks. Some of what you are about to see is going to be difficult. I want to assure the board that the response strategy is comprehensive and that we are confident in our ability to recover the lost share across the back half of this fiscal year and the entirety of the next."*

Eleanor wrote, in the leather notebook:

*Marcus opening register: McKinsey reassurance. The reassurance does not match the slide.*

She did not look up from the notebook.

---

The presentation ran forty-eight minutes.

Marcus walked the board through, in order:

The category-share decline (slide two, charted).

The Feastables product-line expansion across the trailing twelve months — three SKUs at start of period, eleven SKUs at end of period, including the Crunch SKU that had launched in February (slide three, with photographs of the eleven wrappers).

The Feastables retail-velocity data Hershey's category analytics team had compiled from the syndicated panel sources (slide four, table).

The Hershey Company's response strategy. (Slides five through fourteen.)

The response strategy had, on Marcus's framing, four pillars.

Pillar one was the cease-and-desist letter the Hershey Company's outside counsel at Latham & Watkins had sent to Feastables on Thursday morning of the third week of March. Marcus walked the board through the letter's claims — the Crunch wrapper, the Krackel trade-dress allegation, the demand for product recall — at the level of detail Marcus had been briefed on by his general counsel on the morning the letter had been signed. Marcus did not, in his walk-through, mention the YouTube video Jimmy had filmed in response to the letter. Marcus did not mention the video's view count, which had, by the morning of this meeting, crossed one hundred and seventy-eight million. Marcus did not mention the follow-up communication from Latham & Watkins that had withdrawn the cease-and-desist at four eighteen PM Eastern on the previous Friday afternoon. Marcus did not mention the withdrawal at all.

Eleanor wrote, in the leather notebook:

*Marcus is presenting the letter and not the withdrawal. The withdrawal happened five days ago. The board, on the available evidence of the room, has not been told.*

Pillar two was a multi-pronged retailer-relationship campaign Yvette Park's category team had been running across the previous nine weeks. The campaign involved direct calls from Marcus to Walmart and Target and Kroger's CEOs, direct calls from Yvette to her counterparts at the same retailers, and a series of co-marketing and trade-spend offers Hershey was placing on the table at terms that were, on Marcus's framing, *aggressive and category-leading.* The goal of the campaign, on Marcus's framing, was to *protect Hershey's shelf-allocation across the Q3 planogram review cycle and reverse the share-loss trend through retail-channel discipline.*

Eleanor wrote, in the leather notebook:

*Pillar two: pressure Walmart. Walmart will not tolerate the pressure. The pressure is the kind of pressure Hershey applied successfully in the 1990s. Walmart in Year Nine is not Walmart in 1995. Marcus does not, on the framing of this slide, appear to know this.*

Pillar three was a Q3 product-launch slate — six new Hershey SKUs scheduled for August, October, and November of Year Nine, with combined trade-marketing support of approximately one hundred and forty-three million dollars. The SKUs included two new flavors of an existing Hershey master brand, a co-branded line extension with a small premium-chocolate house Hershey had acquired in Year Seven, and three SKUs in a new sub-brand Marcus's brand team had named *Hershey's Reserve.* The Reserve sub-brand was, on Marcus's framing, positioned as Hershey's *answer to the millennial-and-Gen-Z premium-craft segment that Feastables has been winning in by default.*

Eleanor wrote, in the leather notebook:

*Pillar three: launch six new SKUs. The new SKUs are the new SKUs Hershey's product-innovation team has been incubating for thirty-six months. Hershey is going to ship in Q3 of Year Nine product the company has been developing since Q3 of Year Six. Feastables ships a new SKU at retail in approximately six weeks from concept. Hershey's R&D cycle, on the public reporting of the past three years, is fourteen to eighteen months. Feastables is operating on a six-week cycle. Hershey is bringing thirty-six-month-old product to a fight Feastables is running at six-week tempo. The Reserve sub-brand is going to lose. Marcus does not appear to know this.*

Pillar four was a twenty-percent increase in trade-marketing spend across the back half of the fiscal year, funded by a reduction in R&D spend the board's investment-policy subcommittee — of which Eleanor was a member — had approved in the prior quarter under the framing of *reallocating capital to high-conviction near-term initiatives.* The shift was, on Marcus's framing, *consistent with the company's strategic priorities and reflective of the competitive intensity of the current operating environment.*

Eleanor wrote, in the leather notebook:

*Pillar four: cut R&D, pour the savings into trade marketing. We are funding our short-term defense by mortgaging our ability to invent the products we will need to defend ourselves with three years from now. The investment-policy subcommittee approved this in January on a vote of four to one. I was the one. The subcommittee did not, on the meeting's published minutes, record my objection. I am, on this morning's reading of the room, the only person in the building who is going to remember that I objected.*

---

Marcus finished the presentation at eight forty-seven AM.

Marcus thanked the board.

Marcus asked for questions.

The trust chair — Harold Brennan, the retired federal judge — asked three questions across approximately seven minutes. The questions were the kind of questions a retired federal judge from Harrisburg in his sixth year as chair of the Milton Hershey School Trust asked at the close of a strategic-review presentation by a McKinsey-trained CEO he had personally hired four years earlier. The questions were the kind of questions whose answers were known in advance to both parties.

The other board members asked two questions between them.

Eleanor did not, in the question-and-answer period, ask a question.

She wrote, in the leather notebook:

*I do not ask a question because the question I want to ask is not a question the room is, this morning, prepared to hear. The room has heard a presentation. The room has accepted the presentation. The room will, on Harold's motion at the close of the meeting, ratify the response strategy and authorize the trade-marketing-spend increase. The vote will be eight to nothing with three abstentions. I will be one of the abstentions. The minutes will record the abstention without the reason for the abstention. The reason for the abstention will be in this notebook on the shelf in Hummelstown.*

Harold called for the motion at nine oh-three AM.

The motion passed eight to zero with three abstentions.

Eleanor was one of the abstentions.

The minutes recorded the abstention without the reason for the abstention.

Harold adjourned the meeting at nine oh-eight AM.

---

Eleanor walked out of the boardroom at nine eleven AM.

She walked down the long carpeted hallway past the framed photographs of the previous CEOs of the Hershey Company on the east wall and the framed photographs of the previous chairs of the trust on the west wall. The CEO wall had nine photographs. The trust wall had eleven. The current CEO's photograph would be added to the CEO wall the year Marcus left the company. The current chair's photograph was already on the trust wall because the trust placed each chair's photograph on the wall at the start of the chair's tenure, not the end.

Eleanor walked past the photographs.

She walked through the building's main lobby.

She walked through the glass doors at the front of the lobby.

She walked across the small plaza in front of the building toward the parking lot.

The bronze statue of Milton Hershey was in the center of the plaza.

The statue had been commissioned by the trust in 2004 on the strength of a unanimous board vote. The sculptor had been a Pennsylvania-born artist who had done seven of the eight bronze statues of historical figures on display in Harrisburg's capitol complex. The statue had cost the trust approximately one point two million dollars including the bronze casting, the marble pedestal, the landscape-architecture work around the plaza, and the dedication ceremony. The dedication ceremony had been attended by the governor.

The statue was twelve feet tall.

The statue depicted Milton Hershey as a man of approximately sixty years of age, in a three-piece wool suit of the 1909 vintage, standing on the marble pedestal with his left hand at his side and his right hand extended slightly outward at waist level, holding a small bronze rendering of a paper bag of Hershey's Kisses.

The bronze bag of Kisses was, on the artist's representation, the bag of Kisses Milton Hershey had personally walked across the field from his factory to the school in 1909 and handed to the first student enrolled in the Hershey Industrial School — a four-year-old boy from Lancaster named Nelson Wagner, whose father had died in a quarry accident in 1907 and whose mother had been unable to afford to raise him on the wages she earned as a seamstress in Reading.

Eleanor stopped at the statue.

She looked up at it.

The morning was cold. The plaza was empty except for Eleanor and the statue.

She looked at the statue for forty-three seconds.

She did not, at the end of the forty-three seconds, write anything in the notebook.

She said, out loud, to the statue, in the empty plaza at nine fourteen AM Eastern on a Wednesday morning in April of Year Nine:

*"Milton."*

The statue was silent.

She said: *"Milton. You would not recognize this."*

The statue was silent.

She stood at the statue for another minute.

She walked the rest of the way to her car.

She got into her car.

She sat in the car for two more minutes without starting it.

She opened the leather notebook on the passenger seat.

She wrote, on the page she had opened at seven fifty-four AM in the boardroom:

*Marcus is not going to be the CEO who saves the company.*

*The trust does not yet know.*

She closed the notebook.

She put the notebook on the passenger seat.

She started the car.

She drove the four point two miles back to the small house in Hummelstown her late husband had bought in 1995 with the proceeds of the down-payment loan Eleanor had taken from the bank she had spent twenty-two years working at in Philadelphia.

She arrived at the house at nine thirty-one AM.

She walked into the small office at the back of the house.

She walked to the shelf above the desk.

She placed the leather notebook on the shelf next to the eleven other leather notebooks that had preceded it across the previous fourteen years.

The shelf now held twelve leather notebooks.

She walked out of the office.

She walked to the kitchen.

She put a kettle on the stove.

She made a pot of tea.

She drank the tea at the small kitchen table by the window that looked out on the small back garden her husband had planted in 1996 and she had maintained, alone, since his death in 2009.

She did not, in the rest of the day, do any further work on the trust's business.

The Q1 numbers were the Q1 numbers.

The Q2 meeting was in July.

She had ninety-four days to think about what she was going to do at the Q2 meeting.

She had a feeling, she did not yet have a frame for, that by the Q2 meeting the room she had just walked out of would be a different room.

She did not yet know why.

She drank the tea.

The kettle whistled, the second time she made the second pot, at one fourteen PM.

The afternoon light came through the kitchen window onto the kitchen table.

The bronze statue of Milton Hershey, four point two miles away in the plaza on East Chocolate Avenue, stood with the small bronze bag of Kisses extended at waist level in the empty plaza.

The plaza was empty until five oh-eight PM, when a small group of fifth-graders from a school across the river walked through the plaza on a class field trip and one of the fifth-graders — a small girl named Imani who lived with her grandmother in the small town twelve miles to the west — stopped at the statue, looked up at it, looked at the small bronze bag of Kisses, and said, to her grandmother:

*"Grandma. Why is he holding the chocolate."*

Her grandmother said: *"He gave it to a little boy. A long time ago."*

Imani said: *"Why."*

Her grandmother said: *"Because the little boy did not have any."*

Imani looked at the statue.

She did not, at five oh-eight PM Eastern on a Wednesday afternoon in April of Year Nine, have a follow-up question.

She walked on to the rest of the field trip with her grandmother.

The statue stood.

The plaza emptied.

The bronze bag of Kisses was the bag of Kisses.

Milton was Milton.

The trust did not yet know.
