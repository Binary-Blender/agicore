# Chapter 4: Hershey, Pennsylvania

Eleanor Whitcomb walked into the boardroom of the Milton Hershey School Trust at the Hershey Trust Company building on East Chocolate Avenue in Hershey, Pennsylvania, at seven fifty-four AM Eastern on a Wednesday in mid-April of Year Nine, in the charcoal-gray suit she had been wearing to quarterly trust meetings for the previous fourteen years, with the small leather notebook she had been carrying to every trust meeting in the same fourteen years in her right hand, with the Hershey-school class-of-1972 alumni pin she had worn on her lapel since the morning of her own graduation in May of 1972 in her left.

The pin was small.

She had worn it to every quarterly meeting for fifty-three years.

The other trustees, on her quiet three-year observation of the seating chart and the body language and the small interpersonal patterns of the eleven-member board, did not particularly notice it anymore.

This morning, the pin was going to take on an operational significance the rest of the trust did not yet understand.

She took her seat.

Her seat was the third chair on the south side of the table, between Robert Fenstermacher, who had been on the trust for nineteen years and who was the chair of the strategic-review subcommittee, and Catherine Mei-Ling Park, who had been on the trust for six years and who was, in Eleanor's own honest read of the board, the second-most-honest board member after Eleanor herself.

The other eight members filed in.

The trust chair, an elderly Pennsylvania business figure named Howard Pendleton who had been on the trust for twenty-two years and who had been the chair for the previous nine, called the meeting to order at eight AM.

Marcus Whittaker, the chief executive officer of the Hershey Company, walked in at eight oh-three.

Marcus was fifty-two.

Marcus was in his fourth year as CEO.

Marcus had spent twelve years at Goldman Sachs followed by nine at McKinsey followed by seven at Kraft followed by his current tenure at Hershey's. He had, across thirty-two years of professional life, never worked in a manufacturing role. He had managed manufacturing organizations through the lens of brand, distribution, and balance-sheet optimization. He was, in Eleanor's quiet thirteen-year observation of the chief executive officers the trust had selected, the third consecutive CEO whose professional formation had been outside of operating-floor manufacturing.

The pattern had begun in 2006, when Marcus's predecessor's predecessor had been hired from a Mars confectionery executive role. Continued in 2014, when Marcus's predecessor had been hired from PepsiCo. Continued in 2021, when Marcus himself had been hired from the Kraft CFO chair.

By Eleanor's count from the small leather notebook, the pattern was fifteen years deep at the start of this meeting.

The trust did not, on its own published self-assessment across the same fifteen years, recognize the pattern as a pattern.

The trust thought of itself as a body that had been selecting CEOs on the standard CPG-industry best-practice template.

The standard CPG-industry best-practice template was, on Eleanor's own honest read, the template that had produced the operational decline she had been documenting in the small leather notebook across the same fifteen years.

Marcus sat down.

Marcus opened the presentation.

The presentation was forty-three slides, titled *Q1 Year Nine Strategic Review.*

Marcus walked the board through it across the next one hour and forty-one minutes.

The headline was on slide eleven: *Feastables has, in Q1 of Year Nine, captured approximately eleven point three percent of the U.S. chocolate-bar category by dollar value, up from approximately seven point one percent in Q1 of Year Eight and approximately three point four percent in Q1 of Year Seven.*

The chart on slide twelve showed the trajectory.

It was a vertical line.

In the operational history of the U.S. CPG industry, no category-share movement on that slope had a comparable precedent across the previous forty years.

Marcus walked the board through the response plan across slides thirteen through thirty-eight.

The response plan had four pillars.

The first pillar was the retailer-relationship pressure campaign Yvette Park was running. The pillar was on slide fourteen. The pillar described the multi-pronged operational program through which Hershey's was attempting to position major U.S. retailers — primarily Walmart, but also Target, Kroger, Costco, and the regional chains — to reduce Feastables' shelf allocation in the Q3 planogram review. The pillar acknowledged, in a small footnote on slide sixteen, that the Walmart engagement had not, in the previous nine weeks, produced the operational result the pillar had been designed to produce.

The second pillar was the product-launch acceleration. The pillar was on slides eighteen through twenty-two. The pillar described a series of new product launches Hershey's had scheduled for Q3 of Year Nine, each of which was designed to compete in the specific subcategory positions where Feastables had been gaining the most rapid share. The pillar's projected revenue impact was approximately a hundred and forty million dollars across the back half of Year Nine.

The third pillar was the marketing-spend increase. The pillar was on slide twenty-three. The pillar described a forty-seven-percent increase in trade-marketing spend across Q2 through Q4 of Year Nine, focused on the chocolate-bar category and supported by an additional eighteen-percent increase in brand-marketing spend across the same window.

The fourth pillar was the legal-and-regulatory campaign. The pillar was on slide twenty-four. The pillar described the cease-and-desist letter that had been sent to Feastables on the trade-dress claim. The pillar did not mention, on slide twenty-four or anywhere else in the presentation, that the cease-and-desist had been withdrawn approximately ten days earlier following the publication of a six-minute YouTube video that had hit one hundred and forty-two million views across the three weeks following its release. The pillar's omission of the withdrawal was, by Eleanor's own honest read of the presentation, the single most important thing Marcus did not say across the one hour and forty-one minutes of his time at the podium.

Marcus closed the presentation.

He sat down.

Howard Pendleton, the trust chair, looked around the table.

Howard said: "Questions."

Three board members asked questions.

Robert Fenstermacher asked a question about the planogram-review timing.

Marcus answered.

Catherine Mei-Ling Park asked a question about the marketing-spend ROI projections.

Marcus answered.

A third board member, a Pennsylvania business-figure named Dennis Eichelberger who had been on the trust for eleven years, asked a question about the legal-and-regulatory pillar's expected costs.

Marcus answered.

Marcus did not, in any of the three answers, mention the cease-and-desist withdrawal.

The three board members did not, in any of their follow-up questions, ask about the cease-and-desist withdrawal.

Eleanor did not, in the question period, speak.

She had not, in the question period of any quarterly trust meeting across the previous fourteen years, spoken first.

She made a note in the small leather notebook.

The note was three lines.

The first line was: *Marcus did not mention the withdrawal.*

The second line was: *No board member asked about the withdrawal.*

The third line was: *The trust has, by the operational evidence of the previous one hour and forty-eight minutes, lost the capacity to recognize a structurally consequential omission in its own CEO's strategic-review presentation.*

She closed the notebook.

She did not, in the rest of the meeting, take any more notes.

She did not, in the rest of the meeting, speak.

---

The meeting adjourned at eleven thirty-four AM.

The board members filed out.

Eleanor walked, alone, to the small kitchenette at the back of the boardroom floor where the trust had, since approximately 2009, kept a small Keurig coffee machine for the board members' between-meeting use.

She made a coffee.

She drank it.

She walked back to her office on the fourth floor of the Hershey Trust Company building.

Her office was an office she had occupied for eleven years, since her promotion in Year Minus Four from the trust's investment-policy advisory committee to a full board seat. The office had a single window that looked east toward the original Milton Hershey factory, the small chocolate-shaped streetlights of East Chocolate Avenue visible four floors below, and the larger Hershey Park footprint approximately a mile beyond.

She sat down at her desk.

She opened the small leather notebook.

She read through the notes from the meeting.

She read through the notes from the previous three trust meetings.

She read through the notes from a single trust meeting from approximately eleven years earlier, in November of the year Catherine Mei-Ling Park had joined the trust, at which Marcus's predecessor's predecessor had presented a Q3 strategic review that had, in retrospect, been the meeting at which the trust had first failed to register a structurally consequential omission in its own CEO's presentation.

The pattern had begun in November of approximately eleven years earlier and had continued, with increasing severity, across every quarterly meeting since.

Eleanor closed the notebook.

She opened a new file on her desktop.

She titled the file: *Note to self — what the trust does not see.*

She typed for approximately ninety-three minutes.

What she typed was, in operational form, the structural analysis she had been quietly building across the previous fourteen years.

The analysis was that the Hershey Company had, in operational posture, stopped being a manufacturer in approximately 2006 and had become a brand-and-distribution management company.

The analysis was that the company's competitive moat — the eighty-five years of retailer relationships Hershey's had built — was a moat that had, in operational terms, been irrelevant since the rise of direct-to-consumer audience-owning brands in approximately 2018.

The analysis was that the trust had, across the previous fifteen years, hired chief executive officers from financial-services and consumer-packaged-goods backgrounds rather than from manufacturing backgrounds, and that the cumulative effect of those hires had been to systematically erode the operational posture Milton Hershey had built the company on in 1894.

The analysis was that the trust did not, today, recognize the analysis as the analysis, and that the trust was, on Eleanor's quiet projection across the previous three years, going to require approximately four more years of quarterly meetings of the same kind that had just concluded before the trust began to recognize the pattern.

The four years were, in operational terms, the years across which the Hershey Company was going to lose the operational ground it had been losing at the current trajectory.

The four years were, by Eleanor's own honest projection on the last page of the analysis, going to end with the Hershey Company at approximately forty-one percent of its current market capitalization, with Feastables at approximately thirty-eight percent of the U.S. chocolate-bar category, and with the trust facing a structural choice between (a) continuing the current trajectory, which would, by Year Thirteen, produce a Hershey Company with insufficient revenue to fund the Milton Hershey School at its current operating level, or (b) entering into a partnership arrangement with the company that had, by Year Thirteen, become the legitimate operational heir of Milton Hershey's manufacturing philosophy.

The company that would, by Year Thirteen, be the legitimate operational heir was Feastables.

The founder of Feastables was Jimmy Donaldson.

Jimmy Donaldson was twenty-six years old.

Jimmy Donaldson lived in Greenville, North Carolina.

Jimmy Donaldson had never, in any public record Eleanor had been able to assemble across her own private research on him across the previous nine months, set foot in Hershey, Pennsylvania.

Eleanor saved the file.

She made the note on the last page of the small leather notebook.

The note was a single line.

The line was: *Marcus is not going to be the CEO who saves the company. The trust does not yet know.*

She closed the notebook.

She put the notebook in her bag.

She walked to the window.

She looked east at the original Milton Hershey factory.

The factory had been built in 1903 on the same plot of land Milton Hershey had bought from a local dairy farmer in 1894 when he had moved his confectionery operation from Lancaster to the small farming community he was, across the next thirty years, going to turn into a company town.

It was, by Eleanor's quiet observation across the previous fourteen years, the factory the company had been failing to be a factory in for the previous two decades.

She turned from the window.

She picked up her bag.

She walked out of the office.

She walked the four flights down to the lobby.

She walked out the front door of the Hershey Trust Company building onto East Chocolate Avenue.

The small chocolate-shaped streetlights were on the lampposts, where they had been since approximately 1963.

Eleanor walked the eleven blocks to her car.

She drove home.

She arrived home at three twelve PM.

She put the small leather notebook on the desk in her study.

She did not, that evening, mention the trust meeting to her husband, a retired Penn State agriculture professor named Don Whitcomb whom she had been married to for forty-seven years and who knew, on his own quiet read of his wife's body language at dinner, that something specific had happened at the meeting that morning.

Don did not, at dinner, ask.

He had not, in forty-seven years of marriage, asked about trust meetings.

He had learned, across the same forty-seven years, that Eleanor would tell him when she was ready to tell him.

She would, in approximately three months, be ready.

She would tell him on a Saturday afternoon in July, on the porch of the small house they had bought in Hummelstown in 1998, when she would, in operational terms, have completed the structural analysis that this morning's notebook entry had begun.

For now, on the evening of the third Wednesday of April of Year Nine, the notebook was on the desk in the study, and the pin was on the lapel of the charcoal-gray suit hanging in the closet.

Eleanor did not, in any of the four hundred and twenty-seven trust meetings she had attended across her fourteen years on the trust, expect that the work she had been doing — quietly, alone, in the small leather notebooks, in the small Pennsylvania town she had been born in — was going to matter.

It was going to matter.

She did not yet know it.

She would, in approximately fourteen months, walk into a school opening in Underberg, KwaZulu-Natal, and find out.

For now, on the Wednesday evening in question, she went to bed at ten oh-eight PM.

She slept for nine hours.

She was at her desk at seven the next morning.

The work continued.
