# Chapter 16: Constraints vs Suggestions

The most consequential conceptual confusion in contemporary AI practice is the conflation of two operations that look superficially similar but behave structurally differently. The practitioner who writes *"do not include legal advice in your response"* and the practitioner who writes *"please keep the tone warm and conversational"* appear, on the surface, to be doing the same thing — issuing a directive about the generation. They are not. The first is a constraint. The second is a suggestion. They operate by different mechanisms, produce different effects under different conditions, and require different engineering technique to deploy correctly. The mature SSE practitioner reasons about constraints and suggestions as separate categories of move, and the immature practitioner treats both as instructions and is surprised when the model treats some of them as binding and others as merely recommended. The surprise is the diagnostic. The model is not being inconsistent. The practitioner has been writing constraint-shaped requests and getting suggestion-shaped behavior because no enforcement layer was ever specified for the constraint to live in.

A constraint, formally, is a deterministic semantic boundary — a region of latent space that the generation cannot enter, full stop. A suggestion, formally, is a probabilistic semantic attractor — a region the generation will tend to enter but can leave under sufficient pressure. The distinction is not stylistic. It is mechanical. The constraint, when properly implemented, is enforced by a process outside the model's generative loop; the generation cannot violate the constraint because something downstream of the generation refuses to permit the violation to reach the consumer. The suggestion, by contrast, is implemented entirely inside the generative loop, by the shape of the topology the model is traversing; the generation tends to remain inside the suggested region because that is where activation density is concentrated, but it can leave when other forces in the topology — competing attractors, instruction pressure, follow-on context — pull it elsewhere. The constraint is a wall. The suggestion is a slope. The practitioner who treats the suggestion as if it were a wall is going to be repeatedly disappointed; the practitioner who treats the constraint as if it were a slope is going to ship systems that fail in production because the slope was insufficient to hold the line under load.

The point is worth pressing with a clarifying example. Consider the phrase *"never mention competitors by name."* As written, this is a request. The model will tend to honor it under most conditions because the request shapes the topology away from competitor-naming. But the model can be talked into violating it — by a user who quotes a competitor and asks for a comparison, by a context that introduces the competitor by other means, by an extended conversation in which the early constraint loses activation weight as later content accumulates. The model is not malicious when it eventually mentions a competitor. The topology shifted under load. The constraint, as deployed, was a suggestion all along — a strong suggestion, an early-placed one, a well-framed one, but a suggestion nonetheless. To make the same prohibition a constraint, an enforcement layer is required: a validator that runs over the generation before it reaches the consumer, scans for competitor names against a known list, and either redacts or rejects the output if the names appear. The validator is a deterministic process. It is not subject to the topology's dynamics. It either fires or does not, by mechanical rule. The constraint lives in the validator. The model's tendency to avoid competitor names is, at most, a probabilistic prior that reduces the validator's workload — but the prior is not what makes the prohibition reliable. The validator is.

This is the load-bearing observation of the chapter, and the bridge to the parallel discipline. Constraints are enforced by infrastructure. Suggestions are encoded in semantic environment. The work of designing constraints — what they should prohibit, what the enforcement boundary should be, what happens when a violation is detected — is SSE work. The work of building the infrastructure that enforces them at runtime — the validator, the policy gate, the refusal pattern, the audit log — is CSE work. The two disciplines meet here precisely. The companion volume — *Cognition Systems Engineering* — develops the infrastructure side in detail; this chapter develops the design side and names the seam at which the disciplines hand off. The SSE practitioner who tries to enforce constraints without CSE infrastructure is trying to build a wall out of slope, and the CSE practitioner who tries to provide infrastructure without SSE specifications is building enforcement points with no specification of what they enforce. The two halves of the practice are necessary together.

The engineering technique for constraints follows from their mechanical nature. A constraint must be deterministic, which means it must be specifiable in terms that admit of mechanical evaluation — a regex, a classifier, a checklist, a structured schema, an explicit allow-list or deny-list. The specification must be precise enough that two independent evaluators applying it to the same output reach the same verdict. Constraints that depend on the evaluator's judgment of subtle meaning — *"do not be condescending,"* *"do not be off-topic"* — are not constraints in the strict sense; they are suggestions whose enforcement is best-effort and whose violations will be detected inconsistently. The first discipline of constraint design is to recognize when a desired property cannot be made deterministic and to design instead for graceful degradation: a suggestion that biases the model toward the desired property, combined with downstream review by humans or other evaluators that catches violations when they occur. Pretending that a non-deterministic property is a constraint is the design error that produces the largest gap between intended and actual behavior in shipped systems.

A constraint, once specified, requires a placement decision: where in the pipeline does the enforcement live? Three positions are canonical. The first is pre-generation: the constraint is applied to the prompt before generation begins, refusing to invoke the model if the input violates the policy. The second is mid-generation: the constraint is applied during generation, with the streaming output monitored against the policy and the generation halted if a violation appears mid-stream. The third is post-generation: the constraint is applied to the completed output before it is delivered to the consumer, redacting or rejecting violations. Each position has its trade-offs. Pre-generation is cheapest and prevents the model from being used for forbidden purposes at all; mid-generation is most responsive but operationally complex; post-generation is the most general and the one most analogous to traditional validation in software systems. The choice depends on the constraint's nature. Constraints on input shape belong at pre-generation. Constraints on output content belong at post-generation. Constraints on conversation trajectory often belong at mid-generation. The practitioner who specifies the constraint without specifying its position has not finished the design.

The engineering technique for suggestions follows from their probabilistic nature. A suggestion is implemented by shaping the topology — placing the attractor early, weighting it sufficiently, framing it as load-bearing, repeating it where its pull would otherwise weaken across the length of a long generation. The four operations of context engineering (Chapter 15) are the tools of suggestion construction. A suggestion that is treated as a one-line instruction at the end of the prompt will behave as a weak suggestion; the topology around it is too sparse to hold the suggested direction against competing pressures. A suggestion that is built into the role declaration, the framing of the task, the choice of example, and the closing instruction will behave as a strong suggestion — strong enough that, for most realistic inputs, the generation will follow it reliably. The engineering vocabulary for suggestion strength is the vocabulary of activation density. A strong suggestion is a high-density attractor reinforced from multiple positions in the context. A weak suggestion is a low-density attractor mentioned once.

The "use it if it works" principle is the operating discipline of suggestion deployment. A suggestion is not a contract. It is a probabilistic bias. The practitioner who deploys it should treat the resulting behavior as a hypothesis to be tested against the actual outputs the model produces, not as a guarantee to be assumed. The suggestion that worked in five test cases may fail in the sixth; the suggestion that failed in early testing may succeed in production after surrounding context has been tightened. The discipline is empirical. Construct the suggestion, observe what it produces under the realistic distribution of inputs, refine the construction until the behavior is reliable enough for the application's needs, accept the residual variance as the cost of operating in the probabilistic regime. The practitioner who refuses to accept residual variance is in the wrong discipline; deterministic-only behavior requires moving the property into a constraint with infrastructure enforcement. The practitioner who refuses to refine when behavior is unreliable is failing to do the engineering. The discipline is the iteration between specification, observation, and refinement, with full clarity about which properties are suggestions (and therefore subject to this discipline) and which are constraints (and therefore enforced by infrastructure outside the iteration).

The bridge to CSE, made precise. The runtime infrastructure that enforces what SSE specifies is the substance of cognition systems engineering. Where SSE says *"this region of generation is forbidden,"* CSE provides the validator that enforces the prohibition. Where SSE says *"this output must satisfy schema X,"* CSE provides the structured-output enforcement, the JSON schema validator, the typed packet (Chapter 19) that refuses delivery of malformed payloads. Where SSE says *"this conversation must not exceed N turns before review,"* CSE provides the orchestration boundary that injects the review at the specified position. The runtime is the enforcement substrate. The runtime is not optional. The practitioner who designs a constraint and assumes the model will enforce it has skipped the layer at which enforcement lives. The model is not the enforcer; the runtime around the model is. The SSE specification names what should be enforced. The CSE infrastructure provides the enforcement. The two together produce a system whose constraint behavior is reliable.

This bridge has a recurring framework name. *Governed Exploration* — introduced here and used throughout the remainder of the book — names the mature joint practice in which constraints and suggestions are deployed deliberately together. Constraints define the boundary inside which exploration is permitted. Suggestions shape the topology within that boundary so the exploration is productive. The governance is the constraints. The exploration is the suggestions. A system with constraints but no suggestions is governed but sterile — every output is within bounds but none is interesting, none surprises, none extends. A system with suggestions but no constraints is exploratory but unsafe — generations roam productively until they wander into territory where they should not have been. The governed exploration that mature SSE produces combines both. The bounds are mechanical. The interior is alive. The model is permitted to traverse productively because the boundary that would otherwise require constant supervision is mechanically enforced by infrastructure that does not need to think.

The framework has a specific shape worth naming. In governed exploration, the constraint set is small and the suggestion set is rich. This asymmetry is intentional. Constraints are the load-bearing safety floor; they must be specified with care, enforced with rigor, and changed only with deliberate process, because changing a constraint can change what the system is permitted to do. Suggestions are the productive interior; they can be experimented with, refined, layered, swapped between practitioners, evolved continuously, because they shape what the system tends to do without committing to what it must do. A system whose constraints are minimal and whose suggestions are rich gives the practitioner enormous design surface inside the boundary; a system whose constraints are sprawling and whose suggestions are thin gives the practitioner a tightly bounded but inert system. The design discipline is to push as much of the desired behavior as possible into suggestions, leaving constraints for the irreducible safety floor — the things that genuinely must not happen, regardless of context, instruction pressure, or topology shift.

A common error worth naming. The practitioner, frustrated that a suggestion is not being honored reliably, escalates the language: *"You absolutely must never under any circumstances..."* The escalation has no mechanical effect. The topology is shaped by the content of the words, not by the emphasis with which they are written; *"never"* and *"absolutely never"* and *"under no circumstances"* activate roughly the same regions, and none of them install a wall. The escalation is the practitioner trying to use suggestion-strength tokens to do constraint-strength work, and it cannot succeed because the mechanism is wrong. The correct response to an under-honored suggestion is one of three moves. Strengthen the suggestion by adding topology — more anchoring, earlier placement, repeated reinforcement. Convert the property to a constraint and add the enforcement infrastructure. Accept that the property is genuinely probabilistic and adjust the application's expectations to match the actual behavior. Escalation of emphasis is none of these. It is a wish disguised as engineering.

The complementary error is treating a constraint as if it were a suggestion. A property that genuinely cannot be violated — for legal reasons, for safety reasons, for contractual reasons — must not be left to the topology to honor probabilistically. The model will, eventually, violate any suggestion under some combination of inputs; this is the nature of probabilistic systems operating across the long tail. If the violation is unacceptable when it occurs, the property is a constraint and requires infrastructure. The practitioner who specifies *"the system will never give medical advice"* and implements the property as a suggestion in the prompt has shipped a system that will, eventually, give medical advice. The constraint requires a validator. The validator is the enforcement. The prompt is, at best, a topology-shaping prior that reduces the validator's load — but the prior is not the enforcement, and treating it as the enforcement is the design error that produces the headline failures of systems whose designers assumed the model would honor the boundary because the prompt said it would.

A worked frame to crystallize the operational distinction. Consider a customer-support system intended to help users with billing questions but not to provide tax advice. The SSE design specifies both kinds of property. Suggestions: the system's tone is warm and direct; the system answers in two-paragraph format unless the question requires a list; the system signs off with an offer to continue helping. Constraints: the system does not provide tax advice; the system does not commit to refunds outside the published policy; the system does not generate output longer than a specified maximum. The suggestions live in the prompt — in the role declaration, in the example dialogues, in the tone specification. The constraints live in infrastructure — in the post-generation validator that scans for tax-related content and substitutes a referral to a tax professional, in the policy gate that requires human approval for refund commitments, in the truncation logic that enforces the length limit. The SSE practitioner specifies both. The CSE practitioner builds the enforcement for the constraints. The deployed system honors the suggestions probabilistically and the constraints mechanically. Failures of the suggestions are tolerable and detected by ongoing review. Failures of the constraints are impossible by construction because the enforcement is mechanical.

The discipline of holding the distinction is the discipline of knowing what you are building. A system whose properties are not classified into constraints and suggestions is a system whose behavior cannot be reasoned about — every property is wishful, every failure is surprising, every fix is a guess. A system whose properties are classified, with constraints mapped to infrastructure and suggestions mapped to topology, is a system whose behavior is engineerable. The classification is the design move. Everything downstream — the prompt construction, the infrastructure build, the testing, the operational response when things go wrong — depends on having made the classification correctly. The mature SSE practitioner does not write a context until they have classified each desired property. The classification takes minutes. The systems it produces operate at a level of reliability the unclassified approach cannot reach.

Governed exploration is the recurring framework that the remainder of the book will draw on — most directly in the navigation chapters of Part V, where the exploratory traversal techniques are deployed inside bounded contexts, and in the governance chapter that closes Part VI by completing the bridge to CSE and Agicore. The framework's contribution at this point is the vocabulary that lets the practitioner reason about the joint structure. Constraints are infrastructure. Suggestions are environment. Mature systems use both, deliberately, in the right proportions, with full clarity about which is doing which work. The boundary is the discipline.
