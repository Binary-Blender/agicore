# Chapter 4: The Linguistic Lineage — Saussure to Lakoff

The previous chapter grounded Semantic Systems Engineering in Shannon's information theory, establishing the formal mathematical anchor the discipline requires to be tractable to engineering reasoning. This chapter establishes the conceptual lineage. The mathematical foundation is necessary but not sufficient. A discipline that engineers meaning needs a theory of meaning, and the theory of meaning that SSE inherits is not new. It was developed across the twentieth century by linguists, philosophers, and semioticians who were addressing exactly the questions SSE now addresses computationally, with one limitation that has now been removed: they lacked a substrate sufficient to engineer their theories against. Language models, treated as the probability geometries they are, supply that substrate. The discipline's most important conceptual claim is that the theory was already in place; what was missing was the formal object against which the theory could be operationalized. This chapter walks the lineage and names the inheritance precisely.

The first of the major ancestors is Ferdinand de Saussure, whose *Course in General Linguistics* — assembled posthumously in 1916 from his students' lecture notes — introduced what is now called the structural turn in linguistics. Saussure's central claim was that meaning is structural rather than referential. A word does not mean what it means by pointing at an object in the world; it means what it means by its relations to other words. The sign, in Saussure's formulation, has two faces: the *signifier* (the acoustic or written form) and the *signified* (the conceptual content). The relationship between them is arbitrary in the sense that there is no necessary connection between any particular form and any particular content — *tree* and *arbre* and *Baum* refer to the same conceptual category despite sharing no phonetic structure. What gives a sign its meaning is its position within a system of contrasts. *Tree* means what it does because it is not *shrub*, not *bush*, not *vine*, not *grass*. The meaning lives in the differential structure of the system, not in the sign considered in isolation.

This claim is the conceptual ancestor of the entire vector-space treatment of language. When a contemporary language model represents a token as a vector in a high-dimensional space, what it has learned is the position of that token relative to every other token — a positional encoding within a differential system. The vector does not point at anything in the world; it occupies a location whose meaning is constituted by its distances and directions relative to other vectors. This is Saussure's claim, made literal by the geometry of learned embeddings. The famous embedding-arithmetic results — king − man + woman ≈ queen, Mikolov et al. (2013) — are a quantitative demonstration of structural semantics: the relational positions in the geometry encode the conceptual relations the words actually carry. Saussure did not anticipate vector spaces, and the geometry of contemporary language models is far richer than anything early structural linguistics could have predicted, but the conceptual move is the same. Meaning is structural. Position in a relational system determines content. The structure is the engineering object. SSE inherits this directly.

Saussure also introduced a distinction between *langue* and *parole* — the underlying system of language and the particular utterances produced from it. *Langue* is the structural system, available to every speaker, in which the meaning relations live. *Parole* is the individual speech event, the particular trajectory through the system that a speaker produces in a particular moment. The distinction maps cleanly onto the engineering object SSE acts upon. The latent space of a trained language model is *langue* — the system of structured relations that the model has learned. A generation produced from a particular prompt is *parole* — a particular trajectory through that system. The SSE practitioner is not engineering *langue*; that work was done at training time and is fixed by the time the practitioner arrives. The SSE practitioner is engineering the conditions under which *parole* unfolds — the surrounding context that biases the trajectory the system will trace through its own learned structure. The Saussurean distinction is operative throughout the book. It is helpful, when the abstraction layers become hard to keep straight, to return to *langue* and *parole* and recognize which is being discussed.

The second major ancestor is Charles Sanders Peirce, whose triadic semiotics — developed across his writings from the 1860s through his death in 1914 — supplemented Saussure's dyadic sign with a third term, the *interpretant*. Peirce's claim was that a sign relation requires not two terms but three: the sign itself, the object the sign represents, and the *interpretant* — the effect or response or further sign that the sign produces in a sufficiently competent interpreting system. The interpretant is not the interpreter; it is the structured response that the sign generates within whatever system is processing it. A traffic light is a sign. The instruction to stop or proceed is the object. The driver's actual deceleration or acceleration — including the perceptual processing, the motor preparation, the secondary signs that propagate downstream — is the interpretant. The semiotic process is the production of interpretants from signs, and the production is not a passive reception but an active, structured generation of further meaning.

Peirce's triadic structure maps almost directly onto the architecture of a contemporary transformer. A token presented to the model is a sign. The object it refers to is whatever conceptual category the model has learned to associate with the token (positions in latent space, regions of activation, clusters of co-occurrence statistics). The interpretant is the generation the model produces when the sign enters its context — the structured response that propagates through the model's attention layers and emerges as further tokens. The model is, in Peirce's sense, an interpretant-producing system. Each token in a generation is the interpretant of the previous tokens in the context, and the generation continues by the recursive production of further interpretants. This is not a metaphor. It is the semiotic structure that Peirce articulated, made computational by the transformer architecture. The SSE practitioner who shapes the context is shaping the conditions under which the model will produce interpretants. The practitioner's intervention is at the sign layer; the interpretants are what the model generates in response. Peirce's framework gives the formal vocabulary for this dynamic, and SSE inherits the vocabulary as well as the framework.

The third major ancestor is Ludwig Wittgenstein, specifically the later Wittgenstein of the *Philosophical Investigations* (1953), which broke with the picture theory of meaning he had earlier defended in the *Tractatus*. The Wittgenstein who matters for SSE is the one who proposed that meaning is constituted by use within forms of life. Words do not mean what they mean by pointing at fixed conceptual content; they mean what they mean within the *language game* in which they are used. A word's content is its role in the practices, the situations, the surrounding behaviors of the community that uses it. Different language games — the language game of giving orders, the language game of telling jokes, the language game of solving equations — recruit the same words to different functions, and the meaning of the word in each game is constituted by its role in that game. Wittgenstein's image of *family resemblance* captures the structure: the various uses of a word do not share a common essential property but are linked by overlapping similarities, the way different members of a family share various features without any single feature being common to all.

For SSE, the Wittgensteinian framework is the conceptual ancestor of context engineering. The practitioner who establishes a context — *"You are a senior systems architect explaining this to a board of directors"* — is establishing a language game. The model's subsequent generation is governed by the game-structure that has been set. The same word, used in the context of one language game, will be inflected toward a particular meaning; used in the context of another, it will be inflected differently. *Architecture* means one thing in the language game of building design and another in the language game of software systems and a third in the language game of cognitive science. The model has learned all of these language games from its training corpus and maintains the apparatus to deploy any of them. The SSE practitioner's job is to specify which language game the generation will play. The Wittgensteinian frame names this work precisely. The work the practitioner does when they construct a context is the work of establishing the form of life within which the generation will unfold.

Family resemblance is also operative in the practitioner's reasoning about attractors and regions. A *semantic region* — the framework that will be developed in Part II — is not a class defined by a necessary and sufficient set of properties. It is a region of latent space populated by concepts that share overlapping features without any single feature being common to all. The Stoic-philosophy region includes Marcus Aurelius and Epictetus and Seneca and the contemporary thinkers who have inherited the tradition; it does not have a single defining property; it is held together by the kind of family resemblance Wittgenstein described. The practitioner who activates the Stoic region by naming any single member of the family activates the whole region by resemblance — the standard activation behavior of contemporary language models is in fact the technical realization of Wittgensteinian family resemblance. This is not coincidence. Wittgenstein was describing the structure of natural-language meaning. Language models, trained on natural-language corpora, learn structures isomorphic to what they were trained on. The Wittgensteinian framework predicted the activation behavior the engineering practice now exploits.

The fourth ancestor is Noam Chomsky, whose generative grammar — first articulated in *Syntactic Structures* (1957) and developed across the following decades — proposed that language is structured generation. The speaker of a language does not retrieve sentences from memory; they generate sentences from a finite grammar that produces an infinite range of possible utterances. The grammar is, in Chomsky's framework, an internalized formal system, and the speaker's competence is mastery of that system. The vast diversity of actual utterances is the surface manifestation of a structured generative system whose underlying rules are far more compact than the surface they produce.

Chomsky's framework is the conceptual ancestor of the entire contemporary language model architecture. A language model is, structurally, a generative system. It does not retrieve outputs from a stored corpus; it produces outputs by structured generation from learned parameters that compactly encode the generative regularities of the training corpus. The compactness is striking: a model with billions of parameters has learned regularities that span vastly more than billions of possible utterances. The compression is exactly the kind that Chomsky's framework predicted: a finite underlying structure that generates an unbounded range of surface forms. The relationship between Chomsky's generative grammar and contemporary language models is not direct — Chomsky's specific theoretical claims about innate universal grammar are contested, and the architecture of transformers does not directly implement any Chomskyan grammar. But the conceptual move — language as structured generation from compact underlying systems — is shared. Without Chomsky's framework, the formal apparatus to think of language as a generative system would have been substantially weaker, and the conceptual readiness to interpret language model behavior as generation rather than retrieval would have been correspondingly weaker. SSE inherits the conceptual frame even when it does not inherit the specific theoretical apparatus.

The fifth major ancestor, and in some ways the most directly operative for SSE practice, is the conceptual metaphor tradition of George Lakoff and Mark Johnson, whose *Metaphors We Live By* (1980) made the strong claim that conceptual metaphor is not decoration but the cognitive substrate itself. Lakoff and Johnson's argument is that abstract concepts are systematically understood in terms of more concrete source domains, and the metaphorical mappings are not poetic flourishes but the structural organization of thought. *Argument is war* — we attack positions, defend claims, retreat from indefensible ground, gain or lose ground in a debate. *Time is money* — we spend it, save it, waste it, invest it, borrow it. *Understanding is grasping* — we hold ideas, get a grip on them, drop them when we lose them. The metaphors are not optional. They structure how the concept is reasoned about, what inferences are licensed, what consequences feel natural.

For SSE, Lakoff and Johnson's framework is operationally central. The activation behavior of language models is largely the activation of conceptual metaphor structures. When a practitioner anchors *"think like a senior architect"*, they are not just selecting a role; they are activating a metaphor cluster — architecture as the source domain, with its structures of foundation, load-bearing element, blueprint, materials, construction sequence — that subsequently organizes the generation. The model treats the conceptual task through the architecture metaphor, with the inferences that metaphor licenses, in the same way a human reasoner would. Conceptual metaphor is the substrate. The practitioner who recognizes this can deploy metaphors deliberately as semantic-environment construction. A well-chosen metaphor produces generation that is coherent because the metaphor organizes the inferential structure consistently. A poorly chosen metaphor — or a clash of metaphors — produces incoherent generation because the inferential structures are pulling in different directions. The conceptual-metaphor literature gives SSE the formal account of why metaphor selection matters and what to do about it.

The continuity claim made by this chapter, taken as a whole, is that Semantic Systems Engineering is the computational rendering of a century of structural-semantics work. Saussure's structural turn established that meaning is differential. Peirce's triadic semiotics established that signs produce interpretants in structured ways. Wittgenstein established that meaning is constituted by use within language games and held by family resemblance. Chomsky established that language is structured generation. Lakoff and Johnson established that conceptual metaphor is the cognitive substrate. None of these claims is original to SSE. All of them were available, fully articulated, before the first transformer was trained. What language models supply is the substrate against which the claims can finally be engineered. The discipline of SSE is what becomes possible when a hundred years of theory finds a sufficient engineering object.

This continuity is not a humble move. It is the opposite. A discipline that emerges from a hundred years of theoretical preparation has a foundation that a discipline emerging from contemporary practice alone could not achieve. The young engineering disciplines that struggle most in their early decades are those that arrive without inherited theoretical resources — that have to build their formal vocabulary as they go, with no body of prior work to draw from. SSE is not in that position. It inherits Saussurean structural semantics, Peircean semiotics, Wittgensteinian use theory, Chomskyan generative grammar, and conceptual metaphor theory, alongside the information-theoretic foundation developed in the previous chapter. The combined inheritance is substantial. Practitioners who internalize it carry tools that no purely-practical prompt-engineering tradition could have given them.

Umberto Eco, whose *A Theory of Semiotics* (1976) synthesizes the structural and Peircean traditions, will return in the Part III treatments of compression and archetypal activation. Roland Barthes, whose *Mythologies* (1957) analyzes cultural compression structures, will return in Chapter 11. Each will be picked up where it most directly informs the practitioner's work. The figure-by-figure treatment in this chapter is the foundation; the later chapters will deploy the lineage where it lands.

One final observation belongs in this chapter, because it sets up Part II directly. The structural-linguistic tradition, across all its variants, shares a commitment that has not been emphasized so far but which becomes operative immediately when the substrate opens. The commitment is that the structure is real. Saussure's relational system is not a heuristic for thinking about meaning; he treated it as the actual constitution of meaning. Peirce's triadic process is not a metaphor for cognition; he treated it as the formal structure of all sign processing. Wittgenstein's language games are not approximate descriptions; they are what use of language actually is. Chomsky's generative grammar is not a model that loosely fits the data; he treated it as the actual cognitive structure underlying linguistic competence. Lakoff and Johnson's conceptual metaphors are not poetic embellishment; they are the actual substrate of abstract thought. The tradition takes structure as ontologically serious. SSE inherits this commitment. The latent space of a language model is not a useful abstraction for talking about model behavior. It is the actual geometry within which the model's representations live. Engineering against it is engineering against a real object, not modeling a metaphor. The chapter that opens Part II makes this commitment formal, and the rest of the book develops what becomes possible when the commitment is taken seriously.

Umberto Eco, working in the structural-Peircean synthesis, made an observation in *A Theory of Semiotics* (1976) that deserves preview here because it bears directly on the SSE practitioner's work. Eco distinguished between the *code* — the system of established conventions that govern sign interpretation in a given community — and the *text* — the particular utterance that deploys the code. Reading a text is the work of recovering, from the surface utterance, the codes the utterance is operating against. Some texts deploy a single dominant code; some deploy several codes in interaction; some deliberately subvert their codes to produce effects that would be unavailable within any single code. The SSE practitioner is, in Eco's vocabulary, a constructor of texts that deploy specific codes deliberately. The model is, in the same vocabulary, a system that has internalized an enormous range of codes from training and that will deploy whichever codes the surrounding context activates. The practitioner's leverage is not at the level of telling the model what to say. It is at the level of activating which codes the model brings to the saying. This Eco framing will reappear in Chapter 11 when the book treats compression operators, and again in Chapter 16 when it treats the protocol structure of human politeness. The reader who wants a single short volume that synthesizes the structural and Peircean traditions will find Eco's the most accessible entry point.

It is worth a paragraph on what this chapter has deliberately not done. It has not treated the cognitive-linguistic tradition (Langacker, Talmy, the broader contemporary cognitive-linguistics literature) in detail, despite the relevance of that tradition's frame-semantics apparatus to attractor theory. It has not treated the formal semantics tradition (Montague grammar and its descendants) at all, despite its sophistication, because the formal-semantics treatment of meaning as set-theoretic denotation does not translate naturally to the geometric substrate SSE operates upon. It has not treated the post-structuralist developments (Derrida, late Barthes, the deconstructive tradition), which are genuinely interesting and which surface specific problems about meaning closure that SSE will eventually have to address, but which are not yet operative in the discipline's current practice. The lineage walked in this chapter is the lineage that is currently load-bearing. Other ancestors will be acknowledged when the discipline reaches the point where their contributions become directly operative. The current book covers the load-bearing inheritance, and the inheritance suffices for the architecture Part II opens.

A practitioner reading this chapter for its operational implications, rather than for its scholarly genealogy, should leave with a small number of working principles drawn from the lineage. From Saussure: meaning is positional, so the construction of context is the construction of a position from which the generation will speak. From Peirce: every element of context produces interpretants downstream, and the practitioner is engineering the conditions under which a chain of interpretants will unfold. From Wittgenstein: every construction establishes a language game, and the game-rules are the deep determinants of what the generation can and cannot do — the visible instructions are surface. From Chomsky: language is generation, not retrieval, so the practitioner's leverage is at the level of the generative system's biases rather than at the level of selecting outputs from a stored repertoire. From Lakoff and Johnson: conceptual metaphor is structural, so the practitioner who selects a metaphor is selecting an inferential architecture and the metaphor's internal logic will propagate through the generation whether the practitioner intended it to or not. These five principles are operational distillations of the chapter's theoretical content. They are not the full content; the full content is the architecture the principles assume. But the principles are usable today, and the practitioner who carries them into their construction work will find that their constructions become more deliberate immediately.
