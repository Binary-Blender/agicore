# Chapter 22: Semantic Anti-Patterns

The discipline of medicine made a decisive turn in the nineteenth century when it began naming pathologies precisely. Before the naming, the practitioner confronted illness as an undifferentiated category — a patient was sick, the symptoms were what they were, and treatment was an art rather than a method. After the naming, the practitioner confronted specific named conditions, each with its diagnostic criteria, its etiology, its prognosis, and its evidence-based treatment. The naming did not invent the conditions; the conditions had always existed. The naming made them recognizable and made the practitioner's response repeatable. The same move is what this chapter undertakes for the failure modes of latent-space navigation. The failures are not new; practitioners have been encountering them since the first interactions with generative language models. What has been missing is the disciplined taxonomy that makes each failure recognizable, names its topology, distinguishes it from adjacent failures, and provides the remediation that fits its specific structure. The chapter functions as a reference: the chapter the practitioner returns to after a generation has gone wrong, to find the named anti-pattern that matches the symptoms and the corresponding fix.

The first move the taxonomy must make is to dismiss the common framing that treats all generation failures as a single category called *hallucination*. The framing is too coarse to be useful. Generation can fail in qualitatively different ways, with qualitatively different topologies, requiring qualitatively different responses. Treating them as a single category is the diagnostic equivalent of treating all fevers as a single disease. The discipline of SSE requires that the practitioner can distinguish, in a given failure, what specifically has gone wrong at the topology level, because the remediation that fixes one failure mode will not fix another. The taxonomy this chapter develops has six named anti-patterns: hallucination as fabrication-of-plausible-structure-where-none-exists; drift as traversal-without-anchor; mode collapse as over-attraction; coherence collapse as topology breach; specification loss as failure-to-preserve-constraints; persona drift as gradual-loss-of-identity-anchor. The six are not exhaustive; senior practitioners will encounter additional failure modes in their specific working domains. The six are foundational, in the sense that they recur across domains and that recognizing them is the minimum diagnostic competence the discipline expects of a serious practitioner.

*Hallucination* is the failure mode that gets the most published attention, often at the cost of being least understood. The topology-level diagnosis is precise: hallucination occurs when the model traverses to a region of latent space that has surface structure — plausible word patterns, syntactically coherent prose, locally consistent reasoning — but no corresponding referential anchoring. The model is generating well-formed structure where the structure does not correspond to any actual external object. The generation looks like the kind of thing a true statement about the world would look like, but the underlying region of latent space is structured by surface coherence rather than by reference. Hallucination is not random output; it is the specific failure of generating fabricated structure that mimics the surface form of real structure. The classic worked example is a citation to a paper that does not exist — the citation has author names, a plausible title, a plausible journal, a plausible year, and is structurally indistinguishable from a real citation, but no such paper has ever been published. The model has traversed to a region where the topology supports the surface form of citations without anchoring to the actual referential structure that distinguishes real citations from fabricated ones.

The diagnostic for hallucination is independence verification. The practitioner who suspects hallucination must verify the suspect claim against a source independent of the model. If the claim is factual, it can be checked against authoritative sources. If the claim is a citation, the citation can be searched. If the claim is a quotation, the quotation can be looked up. The verification is non-negotiable; the model's own confidence in the claim is not informative, because the topology that produces hallucination produces it with the same surface confidence that genuinely anchored generation produces. The remediation has two components. The first is structural: the practitioner uses queries and prompts that bias traversal toward regions with strong referential anchoring — concrete, specific, well-documented domains rather than abstract or speculative ones. The second is procedural: the practitioner builds verification into their working process, treating any claim that will be load-bearing as requiring external confirmation before it is committed to anywhere consequential. Hallucination cannot be eliminated by clever prompting; it can only be detected and contained by disciplined process. The practitioner who depends on the model to be reliable about facts has misunderstood the medium.

*Drift* is the failure mode most often misdiagnosed as hallucination, and the misdiagnosis matters because the remediation is different. Drift occurs when traversal proceeds through latent space without sufficient anchoring to keep the generation in the region the practitioner intended. The topology-level diagnosis: the generation begins in the intended region, but the surrounding context does not provide enough attractor density to hold traversal in place, and the generation gradually slides toward adjacent regions, then toward regions adjacent to those, until it is operating in territory the practitioner did not specify and would not have chosen. The result reads as plausible prose — drift, unlike hallucination, does not produce fabricated referential structure — but the prose is no longer answering the practitioner's question. It has wandered.

The diagnostic for drift is comparison against intent. The practitioner who suspects drift should re-read their original prompt and compare it to the generation's actual content. If the generation begins on target and gradually moves off, the failure is drift. If the generation begins off target, the failure is something else — likely an attractor problem at the prompt-construction level. The remediation for drift is anchor reinforcement. The practitioner adds attractors to the context that hold the traversal in the intended region — identity anchors (Chapter 13), explicit regional names, well-formed compression operators (Chapter 11), recurring framing language that re-asserts the region across the length of the generation. The principle is that long generations require more anchor density than short generations, because the traversal has more opportunities to slide; a prompt that produces clean two-paragraph output may produce drifted six-paragraph output, and the remediation is to add anchors proportional to the generation length the practitioner is asking for. The cartographic practice (Chapter 21) provides the inventory of well-formed anchors the practitioner can draw on; the practitioner without a map is reduced to improvising anchors at composition time, which produces inconsistent results.

*Mode collapse* is the opposite failure of drift. Where drift is insufficient attraction, mode collapse is over-attraction. The topology-level diagnosis: a single attractor in the topology has become so strongly weighted that traversal pins to its center and refuses to explore the rest of the region the practitioner specified. The generation is internally coherent — possibly more coherent than the practitioner wanted — but it has collapsed to a stereotype, a cliché, a single canonical example, when the practitioner wanted breadth or variation or freshness. Mode collapse is recognizable by its predictability: the practitioner runs the prompt twice and gets nearly identical output; the practitioner asks for five examples and gets five minor variants of the same example; the practitioner asks for a creative treatment and gets the most canonical possible treatment. The model has not failed by going wrong; it has failed by going right too narrowly.

The diagnostic for mode collapse is range testing. The practitioner asks the model to produce multiple results — five examples, three approaches, four variations — and observes the variance. If the variance is high, the topology is producing exploration; if the variance is low, traversal has collapsed to a single attractor. The remediation involves three moves. The first is temperature adjustment, when available — increasing the sampling temperature widens the distribution and reduces collapse, though at the cost of some coherence. The second is attractor diversification at the prompt level — the practitioner explicitly names multiple attractors and asks the generation to engage them, distributing activation rather than concentrating it. The third is constraint introduction — the practitioner adds `WHERE` predicates (Chapter 20) that exclude the canonical attractor and force traversal into adjacent regions. The third move is the most powerful, and the most often overlooked. A practitioner who asks for unconventional leadership archetypes and gets the inevitable Steve Jobs reference can add `WHERE archetype != 'Jobs'` to the underlying query specification — translated into prose as *avoid the canonical Silicon Valley founder archetype; surface positions that the canonical reading would miss* — and the traversal will be forced into the productive non-canonical territory.

*Coherence collapse* is the most structurally serious of the six anti-patterns, because it indicates that the topology the practitioner constructed contained an incoherence that the traversal has now exposed. The topology-level diagnosis: the prompt specified attractors that lie in incoherent regions of latent space — regions that the topology of the training corpus does not connect smoothly — and the model's traversal, attempting to honor all the attractors, has produced output that oscillates incoherently between them or produces structure that cannot exist consistently. The generation reads as broken: paragraphs that contradict each other, internal logic that fails, claims that are mutually exclusive. The failure is not a fabrication failure (hallucination) or an attraction failure (drift, mode collapse). It is a topology failure at the prompt-construction step: the practitioner asked for something whose specification cannot be satisfied because the specified region does not exist in the topology as a coherent zone.

The diagnostic for coherence collapse is structural inspection of the prompt. The practitioner asks: what regions did I activate, and are those regions coherent with one another in the topology I am working in? A prompt that asks for *a fierce warrior who is also a gentle pacifist* is asking for an incoherent topology; the two attractors lie in regions that do not connect smoothly. A prompt that asks for *a senior architect explaining this to a child while maintaining full technical rigor* is asking for incoherence; the two register requirements are not jointly satisfiable. The remediation is prompt-level redesign. The practitioner identifies the conflicting attractors and either chooses one or restructures the prompt so the apparent conflict resolves into a sequence — *first explain technically, then translate for a child* — that the topology can navigate. Coherence collapse cannot be remediated by anchor reinforcement or temperature adjustment; the failure is upstream of those parameters, in the construction of the semantic environment itself. The practitioner must redesign the environment.

*Specification loss* is the failure mode that emerges in long generations, multi-turn interactions, and contexts where the practitioner's original constraints are far in the past relative to the model's current position in the conversation. The topology-level diagnosis: the constraints the practitioner specified at the prompt's beginning are no longer active in the topology that surrounds the current position. The model is still generating, but it has lost track of the prompt's original constraint structure, and the generation has begun to violate constraints the practitioner believed were in force. A practitioner who specified *use only data from the document I provided* in a long generation may find, halfway through, that the model is freely inventing data; a practitioner who specified *write at an eighth-grade reading level* may find that the register has drifted upward; a practitioner who specified *do not name specific competitors* may find a competitor named on page four when they specified the constraint on page one.

The diagnostic is constraint audit. The practitioner reviews the generation against the original constraints, looking for violations. The remediation has two components. The first is structural: the practitioner re-asserts the constraints periodically across the generation length, treating constraints not as one-time declarations but as ongoing context that must be refreshed. The second is procedural: the practitioner builds constraint-checking into their working process for any generation long enough that specification loss is plausible, treating the audit as a non-optional review step. Long-form generation in particular is unreliable about constraint preservation; the practitioner who depends on a constraint to hold over thousands of tokens of generation without periodic reinforcement is depending on a property the medium does not guarantee.

*Persona drift* is the specific specification-loss failure mode that affects identity anchors in conversational contexts. The topology-level diagnosis: the identity anchor that was active at the beginning of the conversation has gradually faded as the conversation has accumulated, and the generation has slid toward the model's default identity rather than the practitioner-specified one. A practitioner who opened the conversation by establishing the model as *a senior systems architect* may find, fifty turns later, that the responses have lost the architectural register and reverted to a generic assistant voice. The identity anchor is still in the context window, but its activation has been diluted by the accumulation of subsequent content, and the topology around the current position no longer reflects the anchored identity strongly.

The diagnostic for persona drift is register inspection. The practitioner reviews recent generations against the established persona and looks for drift in vocabulary, cadence, default associations, and reasoning style. The remediation is anchor refresh. The practitioner periodically re-asserts the identity anchor in subsequent turns — *as the senior architect I established earlier*, or simply *staying in the architectural register* — to bring the anchor's activation back into the current topology. The refresh does not require a long restatement; a phrase is usually sufficient. The discipline is in remembering to do it before drift has become severe enough to require restart. Practitioners with cartographic notebooks can include, in their session records, the cadence at which they refreshed the anchor — twenty turns? thirty? — and develop empirical sense for how often refresh is needed in particular contexts.

The six anti-patterns are the working taxonomy. They cover most of the failures the practitioner will encounter in serious work. Several principles tie them together at the meta-level and deserve explicit statement.

The first principle is that *failure modes have topologies*. Each of the six is a specific structural failure at the latent-space level, not a generic *the model got it wrong*. The discipline of SSE requires the practitioner to think about failures topologically: where did traversal go, why did it go there, what would have held it in place, what anchor was missing, what attractor was over-weighted, what region was specified incoherently. The topological framing makes failures diagnosable in a way that the generic *hallucination* framing does not. A practitioner who can name a failure as drift rather than hallucination is two steps closer to the remediation than a practitioner who has only the one category to use.

The second principle is that *remediation matches the failure mode*. Fixing drift with more constraints is wrong; the drift failure is anchor density, not constraint specification. Fixing mode collapse with more anchors is wrong; the collapse failure is over-attraction, and adding anchors will worsen it. Fixing hallucination with anchors is wrong; the hallucination failure is referential, not topological, and anchor density will produce more confident fabrication, not less. The discipline of matching remediation to failure mode is the discipline that converts the practitioner from someone who tries random fixes to someone who applies the specific fix that the specific failure requires. The taxonomy makes the matching possible.

The third principle is that *cartography enables diagnosis*. The practitioner with a working map (Chapter 21) can recognize most of the failures faster than the practitioner without one. Drift is recognizable as the generation leaving the mapped region. Mode collapse is recognizable as the generation pinning to a single mapped attractor. Coherence collapse is recognizable as the generation crossing mapped coherence boundaries. The map is the diagnostic baseline. The practitioner without a map can still recognize failures, but more slowly and less reliably, because they have no working representation of what success would have looked like.

The fourth principle is that *some failures are upstream of others*. Coherence collapse is upstream of drift, mode collapse, and specification loss — if the prompt is incoherently specified, no amount of downstream remediation will produce coherent generation. The practitioner who is encountering repeated failures of the downstream kind should consider whether the upstream specification is itself coherent. Coherence-collapse failures are the most expensive to diagnose because they require the practitioner to re-examine their own design rather than the model's behavior, and the temptation is always to blame the model first. The discipline is to consider the topology the practitioner constructed before considering the traversal the model produced. Most failures are downstream; a meaningful minority are upstream; the practitioner who confuses the two will iterate ineffectively on the wrong level of the problem.

The fifth principle is that *failure modes interact*. Drift and persona drift often appear together, because the same context-window dynamics that dilute identity anchors dilute regional anchors. Mode collapse and specification loss can appear together, because both can be triggered by long generations where the model's traversal has lost track of the originating constraints. Hallucination can appear in combination with any of the others, because referential anchoring is independent of the topological mechanics that produce drift, collapse, and breach. The practitioner who has identified one anti-pattern in a generation should check for the others before concluding the diagnosis is complete. The named taxonomy supports this kind of multi-failure analysis precisely because each named failure has its own diagnostic, and applying all six diagnostics is the work of a few minutes rather than a substantial investigation.

The chapter functions, as the outline names it, as a reference. The practitioner who has just produced a failed generation should return to this chapter, work through the six diagnostics in order, identify which anti-pattern matches the symptoms, and apply the corresponding remediation. The order matters: coherence-collapse first, because if the upstream specification is incoherent no downstream fix will help; drift and mode collapse next, because they are the most common; specification loss and persona drift next, because they are length-dependent and recognizable by audit; hallucination last, because it requires independent verification and is the most resistant to topology-level remediation. The discipline of working through the diagnostics in order is the discipline that converts ad hoc troubleshooting into systematic practice. The taxonomy is the apparatus.

A practical observation about the use of the taxonomy in working environments deserves note. Practitioners who adopt the named taxonomy often find that their communication with colleagues about failed generations becomes substantially more productive. A team in which one practitioner can say *this looks like drift around turn forty, not hallucination — we need anchor refresh, not verification* and have the rest of the team understand the diagnosis at the same resolution operates more efficiently than a team that has only the generic vocabulary of *the model got it wrong* to work with. The naming, in addition to its diagnostic value for the individual practitioner, has a coordination value for the team. The conversation about failures becomes precise enough to converge on remediation rather than circling in ambiguity. This is, again, the same kind of value that nineteenth-century medicine gained when its practitioners began naming pathologies precisely: the diagnostic conversation between physicians became tractable in a way it had not been before. The semantic-anti-pattern taxonomy provides the analogous tractability for the SSE practitioner community, and the value of adoption increases with the number of practitioners using the same vocabulary.

A second practical observation concerns the relationship between failure-mode literacy and what gets shipped. Generation failures that the practitioner does not recognize do not get fixed; they get shipped. A long-form document produced by a practitioner who cannot recognize drift will be shipped with drifted passages still in it; a multi-turn assistant deployment built by a team that cannot recognize persona drift will exhibit persona drift in production. The taxonomy, in this sense, is not only a diagnostic instrument; it is a quality-floor instrument. The practitioner who has internalized the taxonomy is constitutionally less likely to ship work that exhibits the named failures, because each failure is now a thing they will notice during review rather than a thing they will overlook. The cartographic practice of Chapter 21 supports the same quality-floor function on the constructive side; the failure-mode taxonomy of the present chapter supports it on the corrective side. Together, the two practices constitute most of the practical discipline that distinguishes professional SSE work from amateur SSE work.

The closing observation returns to the chapter's opening claim. The naming of pathologies in nineteenth-century medicine did not eliminate disease; it converted disease from an undifferentiated mystery into a tractable problem space that medicine could subsequently work on with method. The naming of semantic anti-patterns will not eliminate generation failures; the medium is probabilistic and the failures are intrinsic to the topology. What the naming will do is convert the failures from undifferentiated mystery — *the model got it wrong* — into a tractable problem space that the discipline can work on with method. The practitioner who has the taxonomy has, in their possession, the diagnostic apparatus that distinguishes serious practice from improvisation. The taxonomy is the inheritance the chapter offers. The work of applying it, refining it, extending it in the practitioner's own domain, is the work the rest of the practitioner's career consists of.
