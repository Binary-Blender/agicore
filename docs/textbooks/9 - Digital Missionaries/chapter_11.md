# Chapter 11: Andon Cords and Moral Escalation

I want to begin this chapter with two scenes that took place a long way from each other and have, I think, more in common than the distance between them would suggest.

The first scene is a Toyota assembly plant in Japan in the early 1960s. A line worker is fitting a component into a chassis as the line moves past his station. He notices something wrong — the fit is not right, the tolerance is off by a fraction, the part will pass the immediate inspection but will, somewhere downstream, cause a problem he cannot fully predict. Above his station, suspended from the rafters where every worker on the line can see it, is a cord. The worker reaches up and pulls the cord. A light comes on. A bell sounds. The line stops. The supervisor and the team gather at the station. They look at the part. They look at the fit. They figure out what is wrong, and they correct it, and they restart the line. The worker who pulled the cord is not reprimanded. He is, by the explicit design of the system, *thanked*. Pulling the cord is what the system wanted him to do. The cord exists for exactly this reason, and the act of pulling it is treated by the institution as a virtue, not a failure.

The second scene is a small wooden booth in a parish church in any of the centuries the Catholic Church has been holding it. A penitent kneels on one side of the screen. A priest sits on the other. The penitent has come to confess a sin that has been on his conscience — perhaps a serious sin, perhaps a small one, perhaps a habit he has been working against for years and has fallen into again. He names the sin. The priest, who is bound by absolute confidentiality and trained by long practice in the careful work of the cure of souls, asks the questions that need asking, hears what needs to be heard, and offers counsel where counsel is asked for. Then, if the penitent is sincere, the priest pronounces the words of absolution. The penitent leaves the confessional and goes back into his life, having stopped — for the duration of the confession — the long line of his ordinary existence in order to correct a defect, and having done so, by the explicit design of the sacrament, *without catastrophe*. The act of going to confession is not a sign that the penitent has failed beyond hope. It is the routine maintenance procedure of a serious moral life. The Church does not want its faithful to confess less often. It wants them to confess when there is something to confess, with the same matter-of-fact frequency that a Toyota plant wants its workers to pull the Andon cord whenever they see a defect.

I want to be careful here, because the parallel I am about to draw is a structural one, and it is easy to mistake the structural observation for a theological claim. The sacrament of reconciliation is what the Church teaches it is. It is not a manufacturing-control mechanism. It is not a cognitive ritual whose value can be exhausted by analyzing what it does to the person who participates in it. The Catechism is clear about what the sacrament is, on the Church's authority, and the Church's teaching is the truth that governs in this domain, not my engineer's eye. What I am about to say about the *structural pattern* of confession-and-absolution as a low-friction escalation mechanism is one observation among others — one true level of analysis among many. The sacramental reality is what it is. The structural observation does not contain it, replace it, or exhaust it. I am making the structural observation because the parallel to the engineering tradition is striking and useful for thinking about AI governance, not because I am proposing a reductive account of what happens at the screen of the confessional. The Church will continue to teach what it teaches. I am sitting here, in the engineer's chair, noticing that the institutional pattern of confession-and-absolution and the institutional pattern of the Andon cord are the same pattern, that the pattern was discovered by both traditions independently, and that the modern question of AI alignment needs the pattern at a third level the earlier traditions could not have anticipated.

Taiichi Ohno, the engineer most responsible for the Toyota Production System as it crystallized in the postwar decades, did not invent the Andon cord ex nihilo. He inherited a long Japanese craft tradition of attention to defect at the source, and he refined it, under the pressure of postwar reconstruction, into a manufacturing philosophy he called *jidoka*. The word is hard to translate. It is sometimes rendered as "automation with a human touch," which captures part of it. What jidoka named, in practice, was the principle that machines and processes should be designed to *stop themselves* when a defect occurred, and that the workers operating them should be empowered to stop the line whenever they noticed something the machines had missed. The Andon cord was the physical instantiation of the jidoka principle on the assembly line. The kaizen circle — the small group of workers who met regularly to discuss what they had seen on the line and how the process could be improved — was the social instantiation. The jidoka principle, the Andon cord, and the kaizen circle together formed an integrated system for *surfacing problems early, making correction routine, and treating the act of stopping the line as a virtue rather than a failure*. I have just used the same sentence I used at the beginning of this chapter, and I have used it deliberately, because the sentence applies to both traditions. The structural pattern is the same.

I have read enough of Ohno and his followers — the systematized account in James Womack and Daniel Jones and Daniel Roos's *The Machine That Changed the World* is the best one-volume introduction — to be confident in the claim that the Toyota system was, at the level Ohno designed it for, profoundly humane. The system was designed around the assumption that the line workers knew things the engineers did not, that defects accumulated invisibly when there was no mechanism for early surfacing, that the cost of stopping the line briefly to correct a defect was almost always less than the cost of letting the defect propagate. The system trusted the worker. The worker, in turn, was trained to trust the system. The cord existed because the institution believed the cord was a good idea. Pulling it was an act of fidelity to the institution's purpose, not a betrayal of it.

What I want to argue is that the Catholic Church discovered, in the sacrament of reconciliation and in the broader spiritual disciplines that surround it, the same pattern at a different level. The sacrament of reconciliation, at the level of structure, is an institutionalized low-friction escalation mechanism for moral defects. The penitent, who is the line worker of his own soul, has noticed something wrong. The cord is available. The cord can be pulled with very low friction — the parish church is open, the priest is available, the confessional is private. The pulling is routine. The institution wants the pulling. The act of going to confession is not, in the Church's teaching, a sign of catastrophic failure; it is the normal maintenance procedure of a serious moral life. The priest, who is the supervisor in this analogy, is bound by absolute confidentiality and trained in the cure of souls. Together they examine the defect, they figure out what is wrong, and they correct it. The penitent goes back into life. The line restarts.

The associated practices reinforce the pattern. The *examen of conscience*, the Ignatian end-of-day review in which the practitioner sits quietly with the day and looks for the places where he fell short and the places where he received grace, is a daily personal version of the same cord-pulling. The *spiritual director*, the priest or religious or trained layperson with whom a serious practitioner meets regularly to discuss the long arc of his interior life, is the kaizen circle at the level of personal formation. The *retreat*, the periodic withdrawal from ordinary life for a few days of concentrated examination, is the scheduled-maintenance procedure that the daily and weekly versions of the cord cannot accomplish on their own. The whole apparatus, taken together, is an integrated system for the *early surfacing, routine correction, and accumulating wisdom* of moral life. It was developed across centuries. It works.

I should note, because the comparison would be misleading without the note, that the Catholic tradition did not develop these practices in isolation. The Jewish tradition has its own deep apparatus of moral self-examination, particularly the practices around the High Holy Days — the long arc of preparation leading into Yom Kippur, with the Days of Awe in between, structured to produce in the practitioner exactly the kind of accumulating moral seriousness the Catholic disciplines aim at. The Islamic tradition has its own disciplines of *muhasaba*, the daily self-accounting. The Protestant traditions have their own forms of prayer, examination, and accountability, often less sacramentally mediated but structurally similar in many cases. The Buddhist traditions have their own contemplative practices that, while operating within a very different metaphysical framework, address some of the same questions about how the practitioner can become aware of his own habitual patterns and correct them. The pattern I am describing — the routine, low-friction, institutionalized escalation of moral problems before they become catastrophic — is a pattern that several major civilizational traditions have discovered, each in their own idiom. The Catholic version is the one I know best and the one whose institutional structure is most explicit, which is why I use it as the worked example. The pattern is not Catholic; it is human, and the Catholic tradition has named it carefully.

I want now to turn to the AI question, because the structural pattern I have been describing is one I have spent the last several years thinking about in the engineering context, and I have come to believe that we are at a moment where the pattern needs to be deliberately built into the new systems.

Modern AI systems, of the kind I work with daily, are probabilistic. They generate their responses by sampling from distributions over possible next tokens, conditioned on the input and on whatever context the system has been given. The probabilistic nature is what makes them powerful — they can respond to inputs they were not specifically trained for, they can recombine ideas in genuinely useful ways, they can find solutions in regions of the response space that a rule-based system would never reach. The probabilistic nature is also what makes them risky. They can be confidently wrong. They can produce outputs that look reasonable but are subtly defective in ways that propagate downstream. They can, when faced with an ethically uncertain situation, guess — and the guess can be wrong in ways that, multiplied across millions of users and many domains, cause real harm.

The trilogy this book is part of has, in its prior volumes, developed the engineering response to this problem in some detail. *Agicore*, the volume that articulates the deterministic-boundary architecture for systems built on probabilistic models, makes the case that the model's outputs need to be funneled through a deterministic layer that can catch defects before they propagate. *Cognition Systems Engineering*, the volume that develops the architecture in technical detail, names what I have come to call the *Andon loop* — the cryptographically-audited mutation gate that surfaces an ethically uncertain decision to a human reviewer rather than letting the system guess, the human-in-the-loop checkpoint that pauses the line when the model encounters a case it has not seen, the explainable refusal that surfaces the uncertainty as a feature rather than hiding it as a failure. The architecture is a direct application of the jidoka principle to AI systems. The model is the line worker. The deterministic gate is the cord. The human reviewer is the supervisor who comes when the cord is pulled. The audit log is the kaizen record that accumulates wisdom across many pulls.

What I want to claim in this chapter, beyond what the prior volumes claimed, is that the architecture has a Catholic ancestor as well as a Toyota one. The cord-pulling pattern was, before it was an engineering principle, a moral-formation principle. The Catholic tradition discovered it centuries before the Japanese manufacturing tradition rediscovered it for the assembly line. Recognizing the older ancestor helps us understand *why* the architecture matters in a way that the engineering ancestor by itself does not fully convey. The reason the cord works, in the Catholic tradition, is that it expresses a particular theological anthropology — a view of the human person as fallen, capable of grace, and best served by an institutional architecture that makes the routine correction of small defects easy and the catastrophic accumulation of unconfessed ones rare. The cord works on the assembly line because the same anthropology, in a thinner form, is true on the assembly line — the line worker is fallible, capable of insight, and best served by an institutional architecture that makes the routine correction of small defects easy and the catastrophic accumulation of unflagged ones rare. The cord works for the AI system because the same pattern, in still a third form, is true at the level of probabilistic systems operating at scale — the model is fallible in its own way, capable of useful contribution within the right constraints, and best served by an architecture that surfaces its uncertainties early and routinely rather than letting them accumulate into catastrophic failure.

I do not want to push the parallel further than it will bear. The AI model is not a soul. It does not confess in the sense the penitent confesses. There is no question of grace at the engineering level, although the question of grace remains everywhere it ever was, in the practitioners who build and operate these systems and in the users whose lives the systems touch. The structural parallel is what I am claiming. The theological reality of the sacrament is not in the parallel; it is in the sacrament, where the Church teaches it is. The parallel helps me, as an engineer, understand why the architecture matters. It is not a reduction of the sacrament to its function. The sacrament does what the Church teaches it does. The architecture, separately and in its own domain, does what the architecture does. The pattern is what both inherit.

There is a practical implication of this recognition that I want to name explicitly before closing the chapter. The implication is that the engineering of AI Andon loops is *morally serious work*. It is not merely a question of technical correctness. The architecture we are building is structurally analogous to one of the deepest moral-formation patterns the Catholic tradition has developed, and the structural analogy carries with it certain obligations. The cord must be easy to pull. The reviewer must be competent, available, and trustworthy. The pulling must be treated as a virtue, not a failure — the engineer who builds a system that flags many uncertain cases is not building a defective system; he is building a serious one, just as the worker who pulls the cord often is not a defective worker but a serious one, and just as the penitent who confesses often is not a defective Christian but a serious one. The audit trail must be honest. The wisdom that accumulates across many pulls must be allowed to refine the system over time. The institution that operates the system must want the cord pulled. If any of these properties is missing — if the cord is hard to pull, or the reviewer is incompetent, or the pulling is punished, or the audit is dishonest, or the wisdom does not accumulate, or the institution wants the cord left alone — the architecture fails. The Catholic tradition learned all of these properties the hard way, across centuries of attempted reform when the sacrament had become rote or punitive or evaded. The Toyota tradition learned them too, in the postwar decades when the system was being built and the wrong incentives could easily have killed it. The AI tradition is going to have to learn them as well, and the learning will be faster if we recognize that the pattern has been worked on before.

I want to close on a slightly more personal note. I have been a regular penitent in the Catholic tradition for most of my adult life. I have also been an engineer who builds systems for serious organizations for most of my adult life. The two practices are not, in my own experience, separate. The discipline of routinely surfacing moral and operational defects before they accumulate is the same discipline in both domains, exercised in different idioms and with different theological assumptions. When I sit down to design an Andon loop for an AI system, what I bring to the design is, in significant part, what the confessional taught me about the architecture of routine correction. When I go to confession, what I bring with me is, in significant part, the operational seriousness about defect surfacing that my engineering work has taught me. The traditions inform each other in my practice in ways I could not separate even if I wanted to. The book you are reading is, in this respect, a record of one practitioner's attempt to think clearly about the convergence. I do not believe the convergence is accidental. I believe it is what happens when several serious traditions, working on different problems across long periods of time, discover the same underlying pattern about how creatures like us can live well with our own fallibility. The pattern is one of the most important things any tradition has ever found. It is worth carrying forward into whatever comes next.

The next chapter takes up a related question. If the Andon loop is one of the patterns we need to bring forward, the other is the question of *deterministic boundaries* — the structural constraints that make probabilistic systems safely powerful. The Catholic moral tradition has thought hard about this question too, in its theology of the virtues and its account of how freedom is enabled rather than constrained by good habits. That is the conversation the next chapter takes up.
