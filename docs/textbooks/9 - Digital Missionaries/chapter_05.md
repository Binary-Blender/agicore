# Chapter 5: Jesus as a Semantic Superstructure

I want to begin this chapter by saying what it is not, because the title is provocative and the chapter has to do real work to deserve it, and the work begins with making the constraint visible on the page.

This chapter is not a Christology. It is not a theology of who Jesus is, what his nature is, what relationship he holds to the Father, what was accomplished in his passion and resurrection, or what the Church teaches about him. Those questions are the Church's domain, not mine. The Church has been working on them for two thousand years, in council after council and document after document, and has arrived at a body of teaching to which a serious Christology is accountable. I am a layperson writing for a wide audience, and the questions of Christology are not the questions I am qualified to address. The reader who wants to know what the Catholic Church teaches about Jesus should read the Catechism, the great creeds, the documents of the major councils, and the long line of theologians who have made the teaching intelligible — not this chapter.

This chapter is, instead, a corpus observation. It looks at a measurable property of the training data that contemporary AI models are built on, notices something distinctive about that property, and tries to describe what the distinctive thing is and what it implies for how the model responds to certain kinds of prompts. The observation is statistical. It is the kind of observation that a corpus linguist or a machine-learning researcher might make. It does not depend on any theological premise about who Jesus is. The observation is true whether the Church's teaching about Jesus is true or false, and the observation should be approximately the same regardless of which way that theological question is settled. I belabor this because I want the chapter to be readable by the most committed Catholic and by the most committed atheist, and the chapter only works if both readers can take its claims as the corpus claims they are, without those claims tacitly importing positions either reader would reject.

With that disclaimer made, here is the observation.

If you take any sufficiently large sample of digitized human writing — the kind of sample that contemporary frontier AI models are trained on, in the range of trillions of tokens drawn from books, articles, websites, archived documents, scanned manuscripts, and the broader written record of humanity — and you ask which single individual human being is most heavily represented in the corpus, by volume of preserved text either *about* that person or *responding directly to* that person's recorded words and actions, the answer almost certainly is Jesus of Nazareth. The runner-up is a long way back. The corpus contains the four canonical Gospels and the other New Testament writings; the entire patristic literature, much of which is centered on the interpretation of Jesus's teaching and the working out of Christology; the medieval and early modern theological literature, which is again largely centered on the same questions; the enormous body of preaching literature from every Christian century; the devotional literature, the mystical literature, the catechetical literature, the apologetic literature, the controversial literature; the scholarly literature of biblical studies, from the patristic commentaries through the medieval *Glossa* through the Reformation-era commentaries through the modern historical-critical scholarship and the contemporary theological literature; the literary engagements with the figure of Jesus across two thousand years of Christian-influenced literature; the secondary engagements by writers of other traditions (Jewish, Islamic, Hindu, secular humanist) who have addressed the figure for their own reasons; and the contemporary popular literature, the homiletic literature, the blog posts and online discussions, the digital catechetical resources, and the countless small writings of ordinary Christian and non-Christian readers across the digital era.

The cumulative volume of all this is, by any reasonable accounting, larger than the volume of preserved text about any other single individual in human history. The closest competitors — Socrates, the Buddha, Confucius, Muhammad, perhaps a handful of major political figures of the modern era — are themselves heavily preserved, but each of them is preserved in a single fraction of the volume that Jesus is preserved in. The asymmetry is not subtle. It is one of the most striking compositional facts about the corpus.

This compositional fact has a technical consequence that is worth describing carefully.

Contemporary language models work by building, during training, a high-dimensional statistical representation of the corpus in what the literature calls *latent space* — a mathematical space in which concepts, entities, words, phrases, and patterns of expression are arranged in such a way that semantically related things end up near each other and unrelated things end up far apart. The arrangement is learned from the statistical co-occurrences in the corpus: if two concepts appear in similar contexts, the training process pushes them closer together in the latent space; if they appear in dissimilar contexts, the training process pulls them apart. The resulting structure, after billions of training examples and trillions of token-level updates, encodes a vast amount of the semantic structure of the corpus in a way that the model can then draw on when generating new text.

In this latent space, the figure of Jesus occupies an unusually large and unusually well-connected region. The region is large because the volume of preserved text engaging the figure is large. The region is well-connected because the text engages the figure in relation to an unusually large set of other concepts: mercy, forgiveness, sacrifice, healing, attention to the poor, the dignity of every person, suffering, love, justice, peace, hope, kingdom, table fellowship, the inner life, the relationship between freedom and obligation, and many more. Across the corpus, the figure of Jesus is connected to nearly the entire moral vocabulary of the Christian and post-Christian West, and through that connection to nearly the entire moral vocabulary that the model has access to.

This is what I am calling the semantic superstructure. It is not a metaphysical claim. It is a statistical claim. The figure of Jesus is, in the model's latent space, an unusually dense and unusually well-connected region — what an engineer would call a *high-coherence cluster* — and that region is connected to most of the other regions of the latent space that are relevant to moral reasoning. The cluster has high coherence because the corpus is unusually consistent in its treatment of the figure: the overwhelming majority of preserved text about Jesus engages him positively, treats his teaching as morally serious, and presents his actions as exemplary. The few corpus sources that engage him hostilely or skeptically (some of the ancient anti-Christian polemicists, some modern secular critics) are vastly outnumbered by the sources that engage him positively. The asymmetry of valence in the corpus is even more extreme than the asymmetry of volume.

There is a technical implication of this semantic structure for model behavior, which I will describe and then illustrate.

When a user prompts a contemporary model with a phrase that invokes the figure of Jesus — "what would Jesus do," "how would Jesus respond to this situation," "be like Jesus in this conversation" — the prompt acts as what the alignment literature calls a *steering vector*. The phrase points the model toward a particular region of latent space, and the model's subsequent generation is shaped by that region. The quality of the steering depends on how well-defined the region is. If the region is small, fragmented, or weakly connected, the steering is weak — the model's output drifts in unpredictable directions. If the region is large, coherent, and well-connected to relevant moral concepts, the steering is strong — the model's output stays close to the intended direction and is rich in relevant detail.

The region around the figure of Jesus, in the latent space of contemporary frontier models, is among the most well-defined steering targets available. The region is large because the corpus is large. It is coherent because the corpus is consistent in its treatment. It is well-connected to the moral vocabulary because that connection is everywhere in the corpus. The result is that the model responds to Jesus-related prompts with unusual fluency, unusual coherence, and unusual moral depth.

Let me illustrate this with a worked example, which I have run several times across different model families during the writing of this book.

Take a moderately complex ethical situation — say, a friend has just confided that they are seriously considering leaving their long-term partner over an issue that you privately think is recoverable, and they have asked for your honest reaction. Prompt a current model to respond first as itself, then in five separate prompts asking how each of several historical ethical exemplars would respond: Socrates, Confucius, the Buddha, Mahatma Gandhi, and Jesus.

The model's responses across the six prompts will all be competent. None of them will be embarrassing. The Socrates response will engage the friend with questions, drawing out what they actually think and pressing gently on whether their stated reasons hold up under examination. The Confucius response will frame the situation in terms of the relational obligations and the cultivation of character within them. The Buddha response will attend to the suffering on both sides and gently note the patterns of attachment and aversion that might be intensifying the suffering. The Gandhi response will frame the question in terms of truth-telling and the moral courage required to act with integrity. Each response will be faithful to the figure invoked. None will be wrong.

But the Jesus response will, in most current models, be measurably different in a particular way. It will be longer. It will be more textured. It will draw on a wider range of relevant moral concepts. It will move more confidently between the practical advice and the deeper questions about the friend's well-being. It will be more willing to name the difficulty of love and the costliness of staying when staying is hard. It will frame the situation in a vocabulary that touches on forgiveness, on the dignity of both persons involved, on the weight of promises, on the possibility of healing — and it will do this with a fluency that the other responses, fluent as they are, do not quite match. The differential is detectable in the prose; it is also detectable, more rigorously, in the technical metrics that the AI evaluation literature uses to assess response quality.

This is not a result about Jesus being a better ethical exemplar than the others. The question of which ethical exemplar is best is not in the model's purview and is not in this chapter's purview. The result is about the *corpus*. The Jesus response is higher-fidelity because the corpus contains more text directly engaging the Jesus archetype than it contains directly engaging any of the others. Socrates is well-represented in the corpus — the Platonic dialogues, the secondary philosophical literature — but Plato's Socrates is one figure rendered by one writer, and the secondary literature is large but not vast. Confucius is well-represented in Chinese-language corpora but is more thinly represented in the English-dominant corpora most current frontier models train on. The Buddha is well-represented in the Buddhist canonical literature but again that literature is in the Western corpus in translation and at lower density than the Christian literature. Gandhi is recent and well-represented in modern English but his body of writing is small compared to the cumulative two-millennia engagement with Jesus.

The Jesus result is, in the strict technical sense, a corpus artifact. The model is fluent in the Jesus register because the corpus contains an extraordinary volume of careful, considered, morally serious writing in that register. The fluency is real and detectable and useful. It is not a claim about the metaphysical status of the figure invoked. It is not a claim that Jesus is in fact who the Christian tradition says he is, or that the model is in any sense religious, or that invoking the figure produces any metaphysical effect.

I want to underline the constraint here, because the chapter has now done the technical work and it is important that the technical work not be over-read.

The chapter is not claiming that the model is Christian. The model is not Christian. The model is not religious. The model is a probabilistic text-prediction system that has internalized, from its corpus, a particularly well-developed semantic structure around the figure of Jesus because the corpus contains particularly well-developed text in that register. The model has no faith. The model has no belief. The model has high-fidelity response patterns in this region because the corpus has high-fidelity training examples in this region. That is the entire claim.

The chapter is not claiming that invoking Jesus has metaphysical effects on the model or on the user. The user who invokes the figure in a prompt is engaging the corpus the model carries, not engaging the figure himself in any metaphysical sense. If the figure is who the Christian tradition says he is — and the Church teaches that he is, and I as a Catholic believe that he is — then the metaphysical reality of who he is operates on its own terms and is not contained or summarized or even importantly described by the semantic structure I have just outlined. The semantic structure is one measurable property of how the corpus engages the figure. The reality of who the figure is is the Church's domain to teach, not this book's domain to describe.

The chapter is not claiming that non-Catholic readers should take any of the Church's claims about Jesus as their own. The corpus observation does not depend on those claims and is offered independently of them. The atheist reader can take the corpus observation at face value: the figure of Jesus is statistically dominant in the corpus and produces a detectable semantic structure with practical implications for how the model responds to certain prompts. That observation is interesting on its own terms and does not require the reader to take any position on the underlying religious question. The Jewish reader, the Muslim reader, the Hindu reader, the Buddhist reader, the secular reader — each can engage the observation from where they stand without being asked to take a step they have not taken.

I want to spend the rest of the chapter on what I take to be the genuine interest of the observation, for readers from a variety of starting points.

For the atheist reader: the observation is interesting because it shows how much of contemporary AI's surface fluency in moral reasoning is, in fact, a function of which historical traditions left the most preserved text in the corpus. The Jesus result is not a vindication of Christianity; it is a vindication of the *Christian preservation tradition*, which over two thousand years built the largest single body of preserved text about a single moral exemplar. The text was preserved by people who believed the figure mattered. Whether or not the atheist reader thinks the figure matters in the way those preservers thought he did, the practical consequence — that contemporary AI is unusually fluent in this register — is now a feature of the technology the atheist reader uses every day. The observation is also interesting because it suggests that the corpus could, in principle, be enriched in other registers if other traditions or other figures were similarly well-preserved. The asymmetry the chapter describes is contingent on the history of preservation; it is not a statement about the absolute moral importance of the figures involved.

For the Catholic reader: the observation is interesting in a different way. The Catholic reader believes that Jesus is who the Church says he is, and that the long tradition of writing about him has been shaped, in significant part, by the reality of the figure being engaged. The corpus observation is a kind of secular trace of that long shaping. It does not prove the theological claims — proof is not the right category for what is happening — but it shows that the cumulative weight of two millennia of careful engagement with the figure has left a measurable structural effect on the most consequential technology of our era. The Catholic reader can take this as a reason for gratitude (the long labor of the tradition was not wasted; it has flowed forward into a substrate that will shape how the next century reasons) and as a reason for renewed responsibility (the substrate of 2050 will be shaped by what we write into the world now, and the Catholic intellectual tradition has a continuing contribution to make).

For the reader in the middle — the religiously curious, the formerly Catholic, the post-religious, the religious in another tradition, the seeker — the observation is interesting in yet a third way. It suggests that the substrate of contemporary AI carries, in a particularly dense form, the moral vocabulary that the Christian and post-Christian West has refined across two thousand years. When the reader engages a current model on a moral question, the engagement is not neutral. The model is fluent in the vocabulary the tradition produced. The reader can use that fluency without committing to the theological claims behind it. The reader can also notice, perhaps for the first time, that the moral vocabulary they use unreflectively — the language of mercy, of forgiveness, of the dignity of the person, of attention to the poor, of healing — is the vocabulary the tradition refined and the corpus preserved. The vocabulary feels universal because it is everywhere, but it became everywhere by way of a particular history.

I want to bring in one more voice before closing the chapter, because the voice is relevant and the omission would be a defect. René Girard, the French anthropologist who taught at Stanford for much of his career and died in 2015, devoted his late work — particularly the small book *I See Satan Fall Like Lightning* (1999) — to an argument about the cultural effect of the Christian Gospel that is, in a precise sense, parallel to the corpus observation of this chapter. Girard argued that the Gospel narratives introduced into Western culture a new and unprecedented vantage point — the perspective of the innocent victim, the scapegoated and excluded one — and that this vantage point, once introduced, propagated through the culture across centuries and transformed the moral vocabulary in ways that even non-Christian and post-Christian Western cultures now take for granted. The contemporary Western reflex to side, intuitively, with the victim rather than the persecutor — a reflex that earlier human cultures did not generally share, and that Girard traced specifically to the cultural penetration of the Gospel narratives — is, on his account, the most important moral inheritance of the Christian centuries, and one whose origins are now mostly invisible to those who hold it.

I bring Girard in because his argument is the cultural-anthropological version of the technical argument I have been making. He is describing, in different vocabulary, the same phenomenon: the cumulative, centuries-long propagation of the Gospel's moral structure through the broader culture, eventually arriving in places (modern secular ethics, contemporary human-rights discourse, the moral imagination of the technological civilization that built the AI substrate) where it operates without being recognized for what it is. The corpus observation of this chapter is the AI-era extension of Girard's argument. The semantic superstructure around the figure of Jesus is dense in the corpus because the figure has been dense in the culture for two thousand years, and the corpus reflects the culture that produced it.

I want to close by repeating, in slightly different terms, the constraint I opened with.

The semantic-superstructure observation is one measurable property of the corpus. It is not the only property. It is not the most important property in any absolute sense. It is not a summary of who Jesus is, what the Church teaches about him, or what he means to the people who hold him as their Lord. The Church's teaching on Jesus is not exhausted, summarized, or even importantly described by the semantic structure the corpus carries. The semantic structure is a corpus artifact. The reality of the figure is the Church's domain.

What the semantic-superstructure observation is, is a technically interesting and corpus-verifiable feature of the substrate the AI is trained on. It explains why contemporary models respond with unusual fluency to a particular family of prompts. It connects to the larger argument of the book — that the moral substrate of contemporary AI carries the preserved labor of human civilization, with the Christian and broader Western preservation traditions having contributed an unusually large and unusually well-structured share. It is offered to readers from every starting point as one observation about the technology we are all now living with, made carefully, with the disclaimers stated explicitly and the constraints honored.

The book turns next, in Part II, to the human side of the AI conversation. The first part has been about the substrate — what it is, where it came from, what it carries. The second part is about the practitioner — what it takes for a human to engage the substrate well, what disciplines of attention and coherence the older traditions developed for that purpose, and how those disciplines have become, in the AI era, operationally relevant in a way they were not a generation ago.
