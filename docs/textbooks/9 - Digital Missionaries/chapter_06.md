# Chapter 6: The Human Is Part of the Context Window

There is an observation I want to put on the table at the start of this chapter that took me a long time to admit out loud, because admitting it required a kind of honesty about my own working life that I had been postponing. The observation is this: across some hundreds of working sessions with the new generation of AI tools, I have noticed that the quality of the outputs I receive tracks, more closely than I am comfortable acknowledging, the coherence of my own mental state at the moment I am prompting. When I am rested, well-fed, undistracted, and thinking clearly about what I am trying to do, the model returns work that is useful, that lands, that I can build on. When I am tired, when I have eaten badly, when I have a phone vibrating in my pocket every two minutes, when an argument from earlier in the day is still rattling around in my head, the model returns work that is worse — sometimes much worse — and I am unable, until later, to figure out why. The model has not changed. The weights are what they were yesterday. What has changed is the human at the keyboard, and what the human at the keyboard puts into the conversation is enough of the determining variable that the output quality varies with it.

I will say at once that I do not think this is mysticism. I am not claiming that the model can read my mood. I am claiming something much more mundane and, on inspection, much more obvious: when I am incoherent, I prompt incoherently, and an incoherent prompt produces incoherent output. When my evaluation of the output is degraded — because I am tired, or distracted, or emotionally fragmented — I miss the places where the output is wrong, and I cannot follow up well, and the conversation degrades from turn to turn until I am several thousand tokens deep into something that is not what I needed, with a model that has been faithfully following my own confused steering all along. The model is mirroring what I put in. It cannot do otherwise. It does not have a private store of what I really meant. It has only the prompt, and the prompt is the product of my mental state at the moment I typed it.

The first time I noticed this in a way I could not ignore was during a long week in which I was trying to draft a piece of writing that mattered to me, in evening sessions after a day job that had been particularly demanding. I had four bad sessions in a row. Each session ended with a draft that I knew, when I read it the next morning, was not what I wanted. I had been blaming the model. The model was new, the task was hard, the topic was subtle, and I had a reservoir of grumbling complaints stored up about the limits of the technology. Then, on the fifth night, I happened to start the session at a moment when I had slept well the night before, had eaten a real dinner, had not looked at my phone for an hour, and had been thinking about the topic, calmly, in the back of my mind, across the afternoon. The session went well. The draft, when I read it the next morning, was usable. I had used the same model, the same kind of prompt, the same approach to follow-up. The variable that had changed was me.

I did not, at first, want to accept this. It is more comfortable to think that the limit on what one's tools can do is a property of the tools, because the tools can be improved by someone else, and you can in the meantime wait for the improvement. It is much less comfortable to think that the limit on what one's tools can do is, in significant part, a property of oneself, because the only available remedy is to work on oneself, and the remedy is harder and slower and less amenable to a software update. But the data accumulated. Across the months that followed I tried, deliberately, to vary one factor at a time. I tried prompting after a good night's sleep and after a bad one. I tried prompting after exercise and after a sedentary afternoon. I tried prompting with the phone in another room and with the phone on the desk. I tried prompting after fifteen minutes of silence and after fifteen minutes of doomscrolling. The pattern was consistent. The model's outputs varied with my own state. The model and I were, in operational terms, a single coupled system, and the coupling ran in both directions, but the direction that mattered most for the output was the direction that ran from me into the system.

The argument I want to develop in this chapter, on the basis of this observation, is that the human practitioner is not external to the AI interaction. The human is part of the context window. The model responds to what is in front of it. What is in front of it includes the literal prompt text — the words I typed — but it also includes the implied frame those words point at, the level of attention the words carry, the moral seriousness of the question the words are asking, and the implicit assumptions about what a useful answer would look like. All of these are encoded in the way I prompt. All of them vary with my state. A fragmented mind produces fragmented prompts. Fragmented prompts produce fragmented outputs. A coherent mind produces coherent prompts, and coherent prompts get the best out of the system. This is not a claim about the model's inner life, of which it has none that we have any business speculating about. It is a claim about the nature of the conversation. The conversation is a joint product. The joint product is constrained by the worse half of the pair, and on most days, the worse half is the human.

Iris Murdoch, in *The Sovereignty of Good*, made a related point about moral life generally that I have been returning to often as I have tried to articulate this. Murdoch argued, against a number of dominant strains in twentieth-century moral philosophy, that the central work of the moral life is not at the moment of decision but in the long, quiet work of attention that precedes the moment. By the time the moment of decision arrives, she wrote, "the business of choosing is almost over." What we choose at the moment of choice is constrained by what we have, over the preceding hours and weeks and years, made ourselves capable of seeing. The work of moral life is the work of looking at the world clearly, of refusing to be deceived by one's own preferred fictions, of attending to what is actually there rather than to what one wishes were there. The good decision, when it arrives, is the natural expression of a habit of attention that has been cultivated long before.

I think the same structure applies to the AI conversation, with the obvious adjustments. By the time the prompt is typed, the business of prompting well is almost over. What I am able to ask, and how I am able to evaluate what I get back, is constrained by the state of attention I have, over the preceding hours and days, made myself capable of bringing. A practitioner whose attention is fragmented across twelve open browser tabs, three notification streams, a half-finished email, and the residue of an argument from an hour ago cannot prompt as well as a practitioner who has, by some combination of habit and circumstance, arrived at the keyboard with a single clear question in mind and the willingness to sit with the answer when it comes. The model is not the variable. The model is the constant. The practitioner is the variable, and the variable is mostly under the practitioner's control, although the practitioner usually does not want to admit it.

Simone Weil, who wrote about attention with a seriousness that has not been surpassed, said in one of her essays that "attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity." She meant that to give someone your attention — to actually look at them, to attend to what they are saying without simultaneously preparing one's reply, to allow them to take up the space they are taking up — is a difficult and uncommon gift, more difficult than most other gifts because the only thing it costs is the one thing we are most reluctant to spend. Weil was writing about human relationships, not about AI conversations, but I have found the formulation useful for thinking about prompting. To prompt a model well is, in a small way, an act of attention. It requires you to look at what you actually want, to articulate it clearly, to refrain from layering on the irritation or impatience or distraction that might happen to be running in the background, and to be willing to read what the model returns with the same attention you brought to typing the question. Attention given to the model is a poor cousin of attention given to a human being, but the discipline is recognizable, and the practitioner who cannot give attention to a model is unlikely to be giving much attention to anything else either.

Cal Newport, writing in *Deep Work* about the kind of focused intellectual labor that produces durable results in a knowledge economy, made a similar argument from a different angle. Newport's claim is that the capacity for sustained attention — the capacity to work on one thing, deeply, for an hour or two without breaking into shallow distractions — is becoming both more valuable and more rare in the contemporary working environment. More valuable because the work that produces real outputs requires it. More rare because almost every default in the contemporary digital environment is structured against it. Newport's prescription is, at its core, a discipline of attention: time blocks, no-phone protocols, deliberate scheduling of focused work, the cultivation of a working environment that supports rather than undermines concentration. The discipline is recognizable to anyone who has tried to do serious work in the last twenty years, and the prescriptions are useful. But what struck me, when I returned to Newport recently with the AI experience in mind, was that the discipline Newport is prescribing — the discipline of sustained, undistracted attention to a single object — is exactly the discipline the AI conversation now also requires, and not in a peripheral way. The conversation will reward what the practitioner brings to it. If the practitioner brings deep work attention, the conversation goes deep. If the practitioner brings shallow attention, the conversation stays shallow. The model is unable to do better than the human's framing permits.

I want to say something about the moral seriousness of the question, because I think this is the dimension that the engineering literature on prompting has consistently underplayed. The literature on prompt engineering, useful as it is, has tended to focus on the formal properties of good prompts — the structuring, the few-shot examples, the chain-of-thought scaffolding, the role specifications. These are real and they matter. But what I have noticed, in my own use, is that the dimension of prompting that most affects the quality of the output is not formal at all. It is something more like the *moral seriousness* with which I am asking the question. When I am asking a question I actually care about, with a real situation behind it and a real intention to use the answer for something that matters, the model returns work that is substantively better than when I am asking the same question idly, or to test the model, or to fill time. The model is, as far as I can tell, picking up cues about the seriousness of the question from the seriousness of the framing — the specificity of the situation, the willingness to disclose what is actually at stake, the way the question is bounded and qualified. The model responds in kind. A serious question, seriously framed, gets serious work back. A casual question, casually framed, gets casual work back. This is not because the model has a private register of how serious I am. It is because the prompt itself carries the register, and the model is trained to mirror the register it is given.

This brings me to the question I want Part II of this book to spend its remaining chapters on. The question is: *what produces human coherence?* What practices, what disciplines, what structures of attention produce a practitioner who is capable of clear engagement with a complex tool? The question matters because, on the analysis I have just sketched, the practitioner's coherence is the variable that most constrains what the tool can do. If we want the AI conversation to go well, the most leveraged work we can do is not on the tool. It is on the human.

The good news, and it is news the rest of Part II will develop, is that this question is not new. The religious traditions — and Catholicism specifically, because that is the tradition I was formed by and the one I am qualified to write about — have spent millennia developing answers to it. The disciplines of attention that the traditions developed were not developed with AI in mind. They were developed because the human task, in any era, requires a capacity for coherent attention, and the traditions discovered, by long experiment, what cultivates that capacity in human beings. The disciplines were developed for prayer, for contemplation, for the moral life, for the relationship with God. But the disciplines were, as a byproduct, also disciplines of attention as such — they trained the same faculty that any demanding intellectual or moral work requires, and they trained it deeply enough that the people who practiced them were known, across generations, for a kind of mental clarity that the surrounding cultures recognized as remarkable. The desert fathers were sought out for their attentional capacity as much as for their wisdom. The Jesuit retreat tradition produced minds that the secular world hired for the most demanding intellectual labor of their era. The contemplative orders preserved, across centuries, a competence in sustained attention that was, in the strict sense, technical — it was a craft, and the craft was passed down through master-student lineages of unusual continuity.

What I want to argue, in the chapters that follow, is that these traditions are now operationally relevant in a way they were not a generation ago, because the environment we are working in is now actively hostile to the kind of attention that produces good work. The phone, the inbox, the social feed, the news cycle, the meeting calendar, the notifications — every default in the contemporary knowledge-work environment is structured to fragment attention. The fragmentation is not malicious in most cases. It is the cumulative effect of many small optimizations, by many different companies, each of which is trying to capture a sliver of the attention market. The cumulative effect is that the practitioner who wants to remain capable of coherent attention has to actively cultivate the capacity against the environment. The capacity does not arrive by default. It has to be built, deliberately, with disciplines that the practitioner adopts and sustains.

The religious traditions are, on this analysis, a library of attentional disciplines that has been refined by long use. The disciplines come embedded in a metaphysical context — the traditions invented and refined them for reasons that include and presuppose the reality of God, the soul, the moral life, the sacramental order. A religious reader can take both layers. A secular reader can take, if that is what fits, the attentional layer on its own terms, and the disciplines still work, because the disciplines were always doing two things at once: they were doing the religious work the tradition believed they were doing, and they were also doing the attentional work that any sustained discipline does. The two layers are not at odds. The attentional work is a real property of the disciplines. The religious work, if one is inside the tradition, is the deeper level. The Catholic reader does not lose anything by recognizing the attentional work. The secular reader does not have to accept the religious frame to benefit from the disciplines, although the secular reader who tries the disciplines for long enough often, in my observation, finds that the metaphysical questions start to look different from the inside than they did from the outside. That is a separate book. This book stays at the operational level and notes that the operational level is, on its own, sufficient to make the case.

Let me close this chapter with a small example, because I want the argument to land somewhere concrete before the next chapter develops the larger frame. There is a practice in the Catholic tradition called *recollection*. The word is older than its current English meaning. To be *recollected*, in the tradition, means to have one's interior gathered — to be present to oneself, attentive to what is actually in front of one, undistracted by the various scatterings of attention that the world produces. The recollected person is not necessarily quiet or solitary or pious in any obvious way. The recollected person is simply someone whose attention is, at the moment, where it is supposed to be. The tradition has spent a long time cultivating this state. It has developed prayers and practices and bodily postures and rhythms of the day that all converge on the production of recollection. The practitioner who is recollected, in this sense, is the practitioner who can give a friend attention when the friend needs it, who can think through a difficult decision without skittering, who can read a difficult book without checking the phone every paragraph, and — I will add now, in 2026, an application the tradition could not have anticipated — who can prompt an AI well, evaluate the output honestly, and follow up with rigor, because the recollected practitioner is the one who is bringing a coherent mind to a coupled system that will mirror whatever it is given.

The AI era did not invent the importance of recollection. It only made the importance more measurable. The model output varies with the practitioner's state, and the variation is large enough to notice across a working week, and the practitioner who notices is then faced with the question of what to do about it. The remainder of Part II is about what to do about it. The traditions, as it turns out, have spent a long time working on the answer.
