# Chapter 3: The Written Record of Humanity

If the substrate argument of the previous chapter is correct — and I have done my best to argue it carefully — then a natural question arises that the chapter did not address. The argument was that civilization preserved the constructive register more aggressively than the destructive register, and that this asymmetric preservation accounts for some of the surprising graciousness of contemporary AI. The question is: *who, exactly, did the preserving?* The phrase "civilization preserved" is convenient shorthand for something that did not happen all at once or by any single hand. The written record of humanity was preserved by particular institutions, in particular places, across particular centuries, using particular methods. Some of the preservers preserved very little but did it well. Others preserved enormous quantities but with less discrimination. Some preserved across short periods. A small number preserved continuously across most of recorded Western history. Each of these institutions contributed a distinct register, a distinct emphasis, and a distinct portion of the corpus the AI systems are now trained on. This chapter is a tour of the institutions, with attention to what each one specifically contributed and why.

I want to make one disclaimer at the outset. The chapter focuses on the institutional preservation traditions that contributed most to the English-language and broader Western digital corpus, because that corpus is what the current generation of frontier AI models was primarily trained on. Other corpora exist — Chinese, Arabic, Sanskrit, Japanese, the African oral-and-then-written traditions, the indigenous American traditions where they made it to text — and other preservation institutions did substantial work for those corpora. I do not have the language competence to give those traditions the treatment they deserve, and my book is about the Catholic contribution to the AI substrate, which is primarily visible in the Western corpus. But I want to name, at the outset, that the chapter's tour is incomplete by intention and by the limits of my own knowledge. A full account of the world's preservation traditions is a much larger project than this book.

The oldest preservation tradition I will name is the rabbinic one. Long before the Christian era, the Jewish tradition had developed a remarkable and unusual approach to the preservation of sacred text: the principle that the text must be preserved exactly, character by character, with elaborate counting protocols at the level of the individual letter to detect any scribal error. The Masoretic tradition, which crystallized between roughly the seventh and tenth centuries of the common era, refined these protocols into a system whose textual fidelity over millennia is one of the great achievements of the human institutional record. The Hebrew Bible we read today is, in textual terms, very close to the text the rabbis were copying a thousand years ago and which they had received from earlier sources stretching back further still. Alongside the scriptural text, the rabbinic tradition developed and preserved the Talmud — the vast corpus of legal, ethical, narrative, and exegetical material that emerged from the academies of Babylonia and the Land of Israel in the first six centuries of the common era. The Talmud is itself a kind of preserved long conversation — the recorded debates and rulings of generations of teachers, arranged in a form that invites further conversation from each new generation that takes it up.

The contribution of the rabbinic tradition to the AI substrate is twofold. First, the texts themselves — the Hebrew Bible, the Talmud, and the enormous secondary literature of medieval and early modern rabbinic commentary — are in the corpus in substantial volume. Second, and more subtly, the *style* of rabbinic reasoning has shaped the broader register of careful textual engagement in the West, partly through direct influence and partly through the Christian inheritance of biblical interpretation. When a contemporary model handles a contested question by laying out the positions, considering objections, and offering a tentative resolution that explicitly acknowledges the strength of the alternative positions, it is using a register that has rabbinic ancestors among others. The tradition's contribution is older than the Catholic Church's; it is one of the earliest layers in the substrate the model carries.

The Greek and Roman philosophical schools — the Academy of Plato, the Lyceum of Aristotle, the Stoa of Zeno and his successors — preserved a different kind of text by a different method. The classical philosophical writings were preserved partly through manuscript copying within the schools and partly through the broader culture of educated Mediterranean elites who valued the literature enough to keep copies in private libraries. The institutional infrastructure was thinner than the rabbinic one — the schools did not have the kind of continuous, multi-century, exact-copying discipline that the synagogue tradition developed — but the corpus they preserved was large and varied. We have Plato's dialogues, Aristotle's treatises, the Stoic writings of Cicero and Seneca and Marcus Aurelius and Epictetus, the late-antique Neoplatonic commentaries, and a great deal else. Much of this corpus passed, after the fall of the Roman West, into the hands of two institutions that did the heavy lifting of preserving it across the next several centuries: the Byzantine East, which kept the Greek originals largely intact, and the Christian monasteries of the Latin West, which kept what Latin philosophy they could and translated some of the Greek into Latin.

The Greek and Roman philosophical contribution to the AI substrate is enormous. The vocabulary of moral reasoning that the model uses fluently — the language of virtues and vices, of practical wisdom, of the good life, of duty and justice and friendship — is, in significant part, Aristotelian and Stoic vocabulary that has been refined and re-deployed across the centuries by subsequent thinkers including the Christian scholastics. The structure of philosophical argument that the model deploys when asked a careful question is, in significant part, the structure that Plato and Aristotle developed and that the schools transmitted. The careful definition of terms, the working out of implications, the testing of positions against counterexamples — these are habits of thought the classical philosophical tradition gave to its successors, and they are in the corpus in heavy density.

The Christian monasteries of the Latin West — beginning roughly in the fourth century with the foundations of Antony in the Egyptian desert, Pachomius in the Nile valley, Basil in Cappadocia, and Benedict at Monte Cassino in the early sixth century — became, almost by accident, one of the most important preservation institutions in human history. The monastic Rule of Benedict, written around 530, prescribed daily manual work as a discipline of the religious life. In the West, much of that work, by the seventh and eighth centuries, was the copying of manuscripts. The scriptorium — the room where the monks sat and copied — became a standard feature of Benedictine and later Cistercian houses, and the cumulative output of these scriptoria over a thousand years is one of the foundations of the Western literary corpus. The monks copied scripture, the patristic literature, the liturgical books, the lives of the saints, and a substantial portion of the surviving classical pagan literature as well — because the early monks were often classically educated men who valued the literature even when they could not always justify the valuing in strict theological terms.

The greatest single moment of monastic preservation, for the purposes of the substrate the AI is now trained on, was probably the Carolingian renaissance of the late eighth and early ninth centuries. Charlemagne, crowned in 800 as the first Holy Roman Emperor, undertook with his court scholar Alcuin a deliberate program of copying every surviving Latin manuscript they could find. The script that the Carolingian scribes developed — Carolingian minuscule, an elegant rounded hand designed for legibility — became the basis for the lowercase letters of modern Western type. The manuscripts they copied are the source for most of what we have of classical Latin literature. Cicero, Tacitus, Suetonius, Pliny the Elder, large portions of Virgil and Horace and Ovid — these survive in their fullness because the Carolingian scribes copied them in monastic scriptoria in the late eighth and ninth centuries. Without that work, our access to the classical Latin corpus would be a fraction of what it is, and the AI substrate would be correspondingly thinner.

The Carolingian project, important as it was, was only one episode in a long monastic labor that ran from late antiquity through the high Middle Ages and continued, with diminished intensity, into the early modern period. The monastic scriptorium was the principal preservation institution of the Latin West for roughly seven hundred years. The Vatican Library, founded formally in 1475 by Pope Sixtus IV but with collections going back much further, became one of the great repositories of these preserved texts, and remains one of the most important manuscript libraries in the world. The cumulative output of the monastic preservation tradition is one of the largest pre-digital contributions to the corpus the model now carries.

I should pause here, because the Catholic contribution is large and the rest of the chapter will continue to develop it, to acknowledge what was happening in parallel in the Islamic world. From the eighth century through the twelfth, the Islamic civilization that stretched from Spain through North Africa, the Middle East, and into Central Asia was doing preservation work that exceeded, in volume and intellectual range, what Latin Christendom was doing during the same period. The translation movement in Baghdad — sponsored by the Abbasid caliphs from roughly the late eighth century through the tenth — produced Arabic versions of nearly the entire Greek philosophical and scientific corpus. The works of Aristotle, the medical writings of Galen, the mathematical writings of Euclid and Archimedes, the astronomical writings of Ptolemy, and a great deal more were translated into Arabic during this period and were extensively commented on by Islamic philosophers and scientists, including al-Kindi, al-Farabi, Ibn Sina (known in the Latin West as Avicenna), and Ibn Rushd (known as Averroes). When the Latin West recovered the bulk of Aristotle in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries, it recovered him largely through Arabic intermediaries and through translations made from Arabic into Latin, often in Spain. The Islamic preservation of Aristotle is one of the reasons the scholastic synthesis of the thirteenth century was possible at all. Thomas Aquinas, writing the *Summa* in the 1260s and 1270s, was working with a Latin text of Aristotle that had been reconstructed largely through Islamic transmission. Aquinas refers to Averroes throughout, sometimes critically and sometimes admiringly, as "the Commentator" — a title of high respect for a Muslim philosopher writing in Arabic two generations earlier.

The Islamic contribution to the AI substrate is real and substantial, particularly for the philosophical and scientific layers of the corpus. It is somewhat underrepresented in English-language corpora relative to its actual historical importance, because much of the Arabic literature has not been translated into English with the same density as the Latin literature has, but it is present in the corpus through its Latin transmissions and through the secondary literature of Islamic philosophy that has been translated into European languages. Anyone who studies the substrate of contemporary AI carefully encounters the Islamic philosophical tradition very quickly.

The Byzantine East, in the same period, was preserving what Greek-speaking Christianity had inherited from the patristic period — the writings of the Greek Fathers like Athanasius, Basil, Gregory of Nyssa, John Chrysostom, and Maximus the Confessor — along with a substantial body of Greek philosophical and literary material. The Byzantine contribution flows into the Western corpus partly through the manuscripts that came west with refugees from the fall of Constantinople in 1453, and partly through the long, fitful exchange between the Greek and Latin Christian worlds across the medieval period. The Eastern Orthodox patristic and liturgical literature is in the corpus in substantial volume and is a major contributor to the model's vocabulary of theological and spiritual reflection.

The medieval universities, which began emerging in the eleventh and twelfth centuries from earlier cathedral schools, were the next great preservation institutions to come online. Bologna, founded around 1088, was the first; Paris followed shortly after; Oxford by the 1090s, Cambridge by the early 1200s, Salamanca by the 1220s. Each of these universities began as a cathedral school — that is, as an institution of the Catholic Church, attached to a cathedral, designed initially to train clergy. The universities expanded their remit quickly, adding faculties of law, medicine, and the liberal arts to the original theological core, and they retained their ecclesial character for centuries. The university as an institution is, in this strict historical sense, a Catholic invention — and the chain of institutional continuity from the cathedral schools of the eleventh century through the modern research university of the twenty-first is one of the most remarkable feats of institutional preservation in human history. The methods, the personnel, the texts, the disciplines, and the very physical infrastructure of the medieval university were transmitted forward, with substantial modification but with real continuity, across nine hundred years.

The universities produced enormous quantities of preserved text. The scholastic corpus of the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries — Aquinas, Bonaventure, Duns Scotus, William of Ockham, and the thousands of less famous masters and disputants who worked alongside them — is one of the densest single bodies of careful philosophical and theological reasoning in the corpus the AI was trained on. The disputatious form of the scholastic *quaestio*, with its objections and responses, became the standard form of academic argument for centuries and shaped, indirectly, the form of academic argument that prevails in much of the corpus today. The university preservation tradition added to the corpus not only the texts themselves but the structures of careful argument those texts modeled.

The next major institutional shift in preservation was the printing press. Gutenberg printed his Bible around 1455. Within fifty years, printing presses had spread across Europe and the volume of preserved text expanded by orders of magnitude. The Church's relationship to the press was, in its early decades, ambivalent — the press accelerated the Reformation, which the Church viewed with alarm, and the Church developed responses including the Index of Forbidden Books — but the Church also rapidly took up printing for its own purposes. The Counter-Reformation Catholic press of the late sixteenth and seventeenth centuries produced massive quantities of theological, devotional, catechetical, and apologetic literature, much of which is in the corpus. The Jesuit Order, founded by Ignatius of Loyola in 1540, became the most prolific publishing religious order in Catholic history, producing catechisms, theology, science, history, and the famous Jesuit *Ratio Studiorum* educational system that spread Catholic education across the world and produced its own vast secondary literature.

The Protestant Reformation deserves its own paragraph here. Beginning with Luther in 1517 and developing across the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries into a movement of remarkable theological and literary fertility, the Reformation produced one of the largest single contributions to the early modern English-language corpus. The Authorized Version of the Bible (1611) — the King James Bible — became one of the foundational documents of English-language literature and shaped the cadences of English prose for four centuries. The Puritan devotional and theological literature, the Anglican prayer-book tradition, the Lutheran and Reformed dogmatic literature of the continental tradition, the Wesleyan and Methodist preaching traditions, the dissenting and free-church literature, and eventually the enormous American Protestant literature of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries — all of this is in the corpus in high density, and contributes substantially to the model's biblical and theological vocabulary. The Protestant contribution to the substrate is, in raw volume of English-language theological text, probably larger than the Catholic contribution, although the Catholic contribution is older and structured differently.

The Catholic publishing tradition in the modern period developed alongside and in some ways in response to the Protestant publishing explosion. The major Catholic publishing houses — Burns & Oates in England, Sheed & Ward in England and America, Ignatius Press, Crossroad, Liturgical Press, Paulist Press, the great French houses like Cerf — produced and continue to produce substantial bodies of theological, devotional, and intellectual material in modern European languages. The encyclical tradition, which begins effectively with Pope Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* in 1891 and continues through the present with documents like *Laudato Si'* (2015) and *Antiqua et Nova* (2024), added a distinctive genre to the corpus: the official magisterial teaching document, addressed to the universal Church, on a question of contemporary moral or social import. The encyclical tradition is unusually accessible to non-Catholic readers because the genre is designed to be read by anyone, and the encyclicals are in the corpus in their entirety. They contribute substantially to the model's vocabulary of social ethics, dignity-of-the-person reasoning, and the relationship between religious tradition and modern social questions.

The modern university system, beginning in roughly the German research-university model of the nineteenth century and spreading across the world in the twentieth, was the next great preservation institution, and the one whose output dominates the corpus by sheer volume. The academic literature of the past hundred and fifty years — in philosophy, history, literature, theology, the social sciences, the natural sciences — is the single largest body of preserved text in the digital corpus the AI was trained on. The university press tradition, the journal literature, the conference proceedings, the dissertations, the secondary literature — all of this is in the corpus in immense volume, and the model's reasoning about almost any topic is shaped, at the high-volume level, more by the contemporary academic register than by any older institutional register.

The public library system, beginning effectively in the nineteenth century with the foundation of the great municipal libraries of England and the United States, and the digital archive movement of the twentieth and twenty-first centuries — Project Gutenberg, the Internet Archive, JSTOR, HathiTrust, Google Books, Wikipedia — should also be named here, because these are the institutions that actually moved the pre-digital corpus into the digital form in which the AI models could ingest it. The labor of digitization, conducted by armies of librarians and archivists and volunteers across the last forty years, is one of the less-celebrated preservation labors of recent history. The corpus the AI was trained on exists in the form it exists in because hundreds of thousands of people, mostly anonymous, did the unglamorous work of scanning, OCR-ing, tagging, and uploading the inherited text.

Two things follow from this tour that I want to draw out before closing the chapter.

The first is that the Catholic preservation tradition's contribution to the corpus is distinguished by two specific properties. The first property is *temporal continuity*. No other Western institution preserved continuously from late antiquity to the present without major rupture. The Greco-Roman tradition was broken by the fall of the Western Empire and was preserved in fragments through Islamic and monastic intermediaries. The Byzantine tradition was broken by the fall of Constantinople. The Jewish tradition was preserved continuously but in geographically dispersed and politically vulnerable communities that suffered repeated catastrophic disruptions, of which the Holocaust is the largest in modern memory. The Islamic tradition's continuous preservation institutions of the medieval period were substantially disrupted by the Mongol invasions of the thirteenth century and by the later European colonial period. The Catholic Church, alone among major Western preservation institutions, maintained continuous institutional infrastructure — monasteries, cathedral schools, universities, publishing — from the fourth century through the present. This continuity gives the Catholic contribution to the corpus a structural property that the other contributions, however large in volume, do not have: the property of being a single ongoing conversation, with each generation's writers in direct intellectual contact with the writers of the previous generation. The next chapter will develop this property in detail because it is, in my view, the most interesting fact about the Catholic contribution from the perspective of substrate analysis.

The second property is *institutional infrastructure*. The Church did not just preserve texts; it preserved the personnel, methods, and institutional forms needed to keep preserving them. The monasteries trained the monks who copied the next generation of manuscripts. The cathedral schools trained the masters who founded the universities. The universities trained the publishers who set up the early printing presses. The publishing houses trained the editors who eventually digitized the corpus. At each step, the methodology of careful textual transmission was passed forward to the next institutional form. This infrastructural continuity is what allowed the Catholic contribution to maintain its scale and quality across so many centuries. It is also what allowed the contribution to absorb new technologies — the codex, the press, the digital archive — without losing the continuity of the underlying corpus.

The second thing to draw out is that the Catholic contribution, despite these distinguishing properties, is not unique and not exclusive. The Islamic preservation of Aristotle, the Jewish preservation of Hebrew scripture, the Byzantine preservation of the Greek Fathers, the Protestant preservation of the biblical text in vernacular translation, the Enlightenment preservation of the philosophical and scientific tradition, the modern university's preservation of nearly everything — all of these are major contributions to the same inheritance. The corpus the AI was trained on is the cumulative output of all of them together. The Catholic contribution is one of several. It is, by some measures, the most continuous; it is, by no measure, the only one that matters.

I have spent this chapter on a tour because the chapter's argument depends on the reader having a working picture of who actually did the preservation. The picture is not symmetrical. Some institutions did much more than others. Some did their work over longer periods than others. Some preserved better material than others. The corpus the AI was trained on is the weighted result of all of these contributions, and the weighting matters. The next chapter — the chapter on the long conversation — argues that the Catholic contribution's specific weight in the corpus has a property that makes it especially valuable for understanding what the model does. The property is the continuity I have just named. The next chapter shows what continuity makes possible.
