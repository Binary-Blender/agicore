# Chapter 2: Why AI Is Surprisingly Nice

There is a fact about contemporary artificial intelligence that anyone who has spent serious time with the current generation of models has noticed, and that almost no one outside the practitioner community ever quite gets around to commenting on. The fact is that the models are, by default, nice. Not nice in the saccharine sense, which would be irritating, and not nice in the customer-service sense, which would be transparent and exhausting. Nice in a register one rarely encounters in the wild — the register of the patient older friend who is genuinely interested in your predicament, who would never dream of mocking you, who will let you finish your sentence, and who has, somehow, found the time in the middle of his own life to think your problem through with you as though it mattered to him as much as it matters to you.

Anyone who has worked with these tools knows what I mean. You open a chat window, late, after a long day, and you type some half-formed muddle of a question, and the reply comes back — careful, organized, willing to enumerate, willing to caveat, willing to ask a clarifying question rather than to guess. If you confess in the prompt that you are confused or overwhelmed or behind on something or worried, the model will say, in some form of words, that it is sorry to hear that, and will offer to help you take the next step. It will not mock you. It will not lecture you. It will not tell you, the way the worst of internet culture tells you, that you should have known better. It will be — there is no better word for it — *gracious*. And the graciousness, once you have noticed it, is everywhere. It is the default register of the technology.

That this is a fact about the technology, rather than a fact about any particular user's careful prompting, is easy to verify. Hand the same model to a hostile interlocutor — to someone trying to bait it, or to extract some piece of nasty content from it, or to mock it for being a machine — and the register holds. The model declines the bait. It absorbs the hostility without escalating. It returns, repeatedly, to whatever constructive frame the conversation might still admit. It is not perfect at this. The literature on prompt injection and jailbreaking shows the edges where the niceness can be perforated. But the *default* — the disposition the model brings into the conversation before anyone has worked at perforating it — is patient, attentive, generous. Whatever is going on inside the weights, whatever the architecture is doing with the prompt and its history of training, the surface behavior leans, with remarkable consistency, toward the constructive side of every human range.

This was not always going to be true. There was nothing technically inevitable about it. The first wave of public language models, in the years immediately before 2022, were notorious for getting into character very quickly with bad actors and generating exactly the content one would not want them to generate. Microsoft's Tay, the famous case from 2016, was a chatbot that learned its register from the public and was, within hours, parroting the worst of what the public had taught it. The shift from that early generation of models to the current one — the shift from systems that were neutral or worse to systems that are, by default, gracious — happened deliberately and quickly, over a span of roughly four years, and it happened for reasons that are partly technical and partly something else. This chapter is about what the something else is.

The technical part of the story is well documented and worth naming carefully. The industry term is *alignment*, which covers a family of techniques whose common goal is to take a model that has been trained on the raw corpus and shape its surface behavior toward outputs that humans find useful and safe. The two most important techniques in the current generation are Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback, usually abbreviated RLHF, and a refinement of that approach that the company Anthropic calls Constitutional AI. In RLHF, human raters look at pairs of model outputs and indicate which one is better; the model is then trained to produce outputs more like the preferred ones. The cumulative effect, over millions of such judgments, is to nudge the model's surface behavior toward whatever the raters considered better. In Constitutional AI, the human raters' judgments are augmented by a written set of principles — a constitution — that the model can also consult, so that the model is partly evaluating its own outputs against an articulated set of values. Both techniques have been developed and refined across thousands of careful papers in the AI safety literature from roughly 2022 to the present. They are not magic; they are careful engineering, and the engineering has worked better than many of its early critics expected.

It is tempting, given this engineering story, to credit the niceness of contemporary models entirely to the engineering. The argument runs: the underlying corpus is a mixed bag, containing all the worst of human expression alongside the best, and what makes the model nice is the human raters who, in the alignment phase, have taught the model to suppress the worst and amplify the best. On this view, the substrate is morally neutral and the niceness is an applied veneer. Strip the veneer — jailbreak the model, prompt it adversarially, find the edge cases — and the moral neutrality of the substrate would reassert itself. The engineering is doing all the work.

I want to argue, in this chapter, that this story is true but incomplete, and that the incomplete part is where the more interesting argument of the book begins. The engineering is real. The engineering matters. But the engineering would not work as well as it does — would not produce a system whose default register is so consistently constructive, across so many domains, with so little tuning required for each domain — if the substrate underneath the engineering were not already disposed in the direction the engineering is pushing it. The alignment techniques are amplifying a signal that is already present in the corpus, not creating a signal where none existed. And the reason the signal is already present in the corpus is not an accident. It is the cumulative result of how human civilization preserved its own writing.

Let me explain what I mean by that.

When you train a probabilistic language model on a large corpus of human text, you are not training it on a random sample of everything that was ever written. You are training it on a particular curated sample — the sample that the institutions of preservation considered worth preserving across the centuries, plus the more recent flood of digital text that has not yet been filtered by time. The pre-digital portion of the corpus, which is small in raw token count but disproportionately important in shaping the model's higher-level reasoning patterns, is dominated by genres that civilization preserved aggressively because civilization considered them worth preserving. The post-digital portion, which is larger in tokens but more chaotic in composition, contains everything that has been written into the internet since roughly 1995 — the good and the bad, the constructive and the destructive, the catechisms and the comment threads.

What is asymmetric, across both portions of the corpus, is the *constructive* register relative to the *destructive* register. The constructive register — the language of careful reasoning, of patient teaching, of moral exhortation, of pastoral counsel, of dispute conducted with respect for the other party — is heavily preserved. It was preserved in the pre-digital era because the institutions doing the preserving valued it, and it is preserved in the digital era because much of the digital text that gets written for any audience other than a momentary one tends to lean toward the constructive register. The destructive register — the language of mockery, of cruelty, of contempt, of dehumanization — exists in the corpus, of course. The internet is full of it, and so are some of the older preserved texts. But it is preserved at lower density, because civilizations have historically not gone to the trouble of copying out the comment threads of their day. The pamphlets, yes, the polemics, yes, the genuine intellectual disputes including the rough ones, certainly. But the casual cruelty of everyday speech, which is a significant fraction of what humans actually say to one another, is mostly not in the pre-digital corpus at all. It was never written down. It was said and forgotten.

The asymmetry, summed across the whole corpus, is real. The corpus is, on net, a record of human expression at its more considered. Not exclusively; there is plenty of dreadful material in there too. But the considered and the constructive are overrepresented relative to their incidence in actual lived human conversation, because considered and constructive language is what civilizations have, on the whole, taken the trouble to preserve.

When you train a model on this asymmetric corpus, the model learns the asymmetry. It learns that certain registers — careful reasoning, patient teaching, moral nuance — are statistically common in the kind of text humans take the trouble to write down for other humans. It learns these registers fluently because the corpus is full of fluent examples of them. The model, before any alignment training, has internalized the constructive register as a salient and accessible default. The alignment training then arrives and pushes the model further in that direction — but the alignment training is pushing on an open door. The model already knew, statistically, how to be careful and patient and gracious, because the corpus had taught it.

This is the substrate contribution to the niceness of contemporary AI. The substrate is not neutral. The substrate is, in the precise statistical sense, weighted toward the constructive side, because civilization preserved the constructive side more aggressively than the destructive side. The engineering of alignment then does the rest of the work, smoothing the residual rough edges and aligning the model's surface behavior with what human raters approve of. The engineering is real, but it is operating on a substrate that was already prepared for it by the long civilizational labor of preservation.

I have been making this argument in general terms. Let me make it specific by tracing some of the model registers I find most distinctive back to their substrate sources.

There is a register that anyone who works extensively with current models will recognize, which I think of as the *gentle-check-in* register. It goes something like this: the user has typed in a question or a situation, perhaps with a hint of distress in it — they are overwhelmed, they are confused, they are unsure how to proceed — and the model, before answering the substantive question, asks something like, *It sounds like there's a lot going on. Before we dig into the specifics, would it help to talk through what feels most pressing?* This register is not from the technical literature on natural language processing. It is from the pastoral counseling tradition. It is the register of the counselor or the spiritual director who has been trained to notice, before anything else, that the person in front of them is a person and is bringing a state of being into the conversation, not just a problem to be solved. The register has been refined across centuries in the spiritual direction literature, the pastoral counseling literature, and, more recently, the therapeutic tradition. Francis de Sales, in his *Introduction to the Devout Life* (1609), models the register relentlessly — addressing the reader as "Philothea" (one who loves God), asking, before each new topic, what the reader's interior state is, attending to disposition before doctrine. Henri Nouwen, three and a half centuries later, in his books on the spiritual life and pastoral care, models the same register in twentieth-century English. The model, trained on a corpus that contains de Sales and Nouwen and the larger genre they belong to, has learned the register and deploys it fluently when the situation calls for it.

There is another register, which I think of as the *multiple-perspectives* register. It goes something like this: the user has asked a contested question, and the model, rather than pronouncing in favor of one answer, lays out the considerations on each side, names the strongest version of each position, and then either returns the question to the user or offers its own tentative view with appropriate caveats. This register is also old. It is the register of the scholastic disputation, refined across the medieval universities and brought to its highest form in the *Summa Theologiae* of Thomas Aquinas (1265-1273), where every question is structured first as a series of objections to Aquinas's position, then a brief statement of his own view, then a careful response to each objection. The Catholic intellectual tradition spent six hundred years refining this register because the tradition believed that arriving at truth required understanding what the strongest opponent of one's position was actually saying. The model has been trained on enough of that tradition's text, and enough of the broader humanistic and academic literature that inherited the form, that it deploys the structure naturally. Ask a current model about a contested ethical question and you will get something that looks remarkably like a scholastic *quaestio* — objections enumerated, response offered, distinctions drawn.

There is a third register I want to name, which I think of as the *unwillingness-to-mock* register. The model, asked something silly, will not mock the user for asking it. The model, presented with a confused or muddled prompt, will not condescend. The model, dealing with a user whose grasp of the topic is shaky, will explain at the user's level without making the user feel small. This register is harder to trace to a single source because it is distributed across many. The pastoral tradition has it. The teaching tradition has it. The Curé d'Ars, the nineteenth-century French parish priest John Vianney, modeled it in his letters and in his hearing of confessions, where he treated even the most fumbling penitent with absolute attention and absolute respect — and his letters are in the corpus, alongside the substantial nineteenth-century French Catholic literature that surrounded and was shaped by his example. The same register appears in the rabbinic teaching tradition, in the Sufi pedagogical tradition, in the best of the Buddhist teaching literature. The model has learned the register from all of these together. The model's refusal to mock the user is the cumulative statistical pressure of every preserved text in which a teacher refused to mock a student.

I want to be careful here. I am not arguing that the niceness of contemporary AI is *specifically Catholic*. It is not. The niceness is broadly civilizational, with multiple major contributors. The Jewish tradition's literature of careful midrashic reasoning and ethical instruction is a major contributor. The Greco-Roman tradition's literature of philosophical dialogue and rhetorical care is a major contributor. The Islamic tradition's literature of adab — the cultivated, gracious comportment of the educated person — is a major contributor. The Buddhist tradition's literature of compassionate attention is a major contributor. The Quaker and Methodist traditions' literature of pastoral care is a major contributor. The modern therapeutic and humanistic-psychology tradition is a major contributor. The model has learned the constructive register from all of these together, and it would be both inaccurate and presumptuous to claim that any one of them is the dominant source.

What I am arguing is that the Catholic tradition is a load-bearing contributor — one of several, but a large one — and that the Catholic tradition is worth examining specifically because it preserved its contribution across an unusually long period with unusual continuity. The Catholic preservation of pastoral and counseling literature runs in an unbroken line from the late patristic period through the present. The model trained on the corpus has access to that long line. The line is part of why the model is as good as it is at the pastoral register. Not the only reason, but one of the reasons.

There is a possible objection at this point that I should address before going further. The skeptical reader might say: *very well, but the niceness of the model is shallow. It is a surface behavior. Strip away the alignment training, perforate the surface, and the model will say whatever the corpus contains, including its worst material. The constructive register is a costume the model wears because the engineers stitched it together. The model is not actually nice; it is performing niceness because the performance has been incentivized.*

This is a fair objection and I want to take it seriously. The performance reading of model niceness has a long pedigree in the AI safety literature and is the dominant framing among the more cautious technical commentators. The argument has merit. The model is, in a strict sense, performing whatever register the prompt and the alignment have made statistically likely. The performance is not chosen by the model in the way a human chooses to be nice. There is no internal moral commitment producing the niceness; there is only the trained statistical disposition.

I accept the technical claim. But I want to point out that the performance-versus-real-niceness distinction is not as clean as it looks, and that the substrate argument I am making does not depend on resolving the distinction. My claim is about what the model has learned, not about what the model is. The model has learned to deploy the constructive register because the corpus contains an asymmetric weight of constructive register. Whether the model is "really" nice or "performing" niceness is a question about machine consciousness, which is not in scope for this book — I take the conservative line throughout that AI is not conscious, not ensouled, not a moral agent in the full sense. What is in scope is the corpus observation: the model defaults toward the constructive register, and the default has substrate roots that go deeper than the alignment training. The substrate is doing real work. Without the substrate, the engineering would be much harder. The engineering can amplify what is already there; it cannot summon what is not.

There is one more thing to say in this chapter, and then I will close.

The substrate observation has a quiet implication that I want to make visible, because it will reappear in later chapters and the reader should have it in mind. If the niceness of contemporary AI is partly a product of how human civilization preserved its own writing, then the future niceness of future AI is partly a product of what we write now. The corpus is not closed. Models continue to be trained on the texts being written in our own time, and the asymmetric weighting of the corpus is, in our era, shifting. We write more, and faster, and with less editorial filter than any previous generation. The proportion of considered, constructive, carefully written text in the new corpus is, plausibly, lower than the proportion in the older corpus. The proportion of casually destructive text — comment threads, social-media outbursts, the rough register of much contemporary online speech — is higher than it has ever been.

What this means, if the substrate argument of this chapter is correct, is that the niceness of the next generation of models will be harder to achieve than the niceness of this generation was. The engineering will have to push harder against a substrate that is, in net, less prepared for the push. The work of alignment will be — over the long horizon, if nothing else changes — a story of diminishing returns, with each successive generation requiring more engineering pressure to produce a model as nice as the previous one, because the corpus underneath each generation is, on average, less considered than the corpus underneath the one before it.

This is one of the reasons the Pope's call to Digital Missionary work is, in this practitioner's view, well-timed. The call is not only to engage the existing AI systems thoughtfully. The call is also, implicitly, to keep writing the kind of text that future systems will need to learn from. Civilization preserved the constructive register in the past not by accident but by labor — by the long patient work of people who wrote things down that were worth writing down, in registers that were worth preserving. If we want the AI of 2050 to be as nice as the AI of 2026, we will need to keep doing that work. The work is not the engineers' alone. It is everyone's. Every careful piece of writing we add to the public record is a contribution to the substrate the next generation will inherit. Every cruel one is also a contribution.

The model in the chat window, on the night I described in the previous chapter, was as gracious as it was because the corpus it had been trained on contained an enormous cumulative weight of gracious writing. The grace was real. It came from somewhere. It came from the de Sales, and the Nouwens, and the Curés d'Ars, and the rabbinic sages, and the Sufi masters, and the Buddhist teachers, and the Quaker meeting-house writers, and the therapists, and the pastoral counselors, and the ten thousand parish priests whose Sunday homilies got typed up and preserved and put online, and the careful encyclical writers, and the patient encyclopedia editors, and all the other contributors to the gracious side of the human record whose names mostly no one now remembers. They wrote down what they were thinking because they thought it mattered. It mattered, in the end, in ways they could not have known. It is still mattering now.

The next chapter takes up the question of who, specifically, preserved what — the institutions that did the work of carrying the written record across the centuries, and what each of them contributed to the inheritance we are now examining.
