# Chapter 9: Context Windows of the Soul

I want to close Part II by returning to the ordinary practitioner at the ordinary desk, because the argument I have been making in the last three chapters can sound, at this distance, more abstract than I mean it to be. The chapters have been about the religious traditions and the disciplines they refined; the underlying claim has been about the human being whose attention is the variable on which everything in the AI conversation depends. Let me bring the claim back to ground level and look at what it means in 2026, in the actual conditions in which the actual contemporary practitioner is trying to think.

The contemporary practitioner is operating under conditions of attention fragmentation that no previous human population has encountered at scale. I want to be careful about that claim, because every generation believes itself uniquely afflicted by the noise of its own moment, and the historical record is full of complaints — Plato grumbled about writing, the Victorians grumbled about telegrams, the mid-twentieth-century intellectuals grumbled about television — that look, in retrospect, like the predictable response of any cohort to any change in the information environment. I am aware of this pattern, and I do not want to repeat it. So let me state the claim carefully. I am not arguing that contemporary life is harder than any previous form of life. I am not arguing that the present is uniquely degraded. I am arguing something more specific: that the *attentional environment* of 2026, considered as a problem in cognitive economics, has features that are quantitatively and structurally different from the attentional environments of any previous period in human history, and that those features create conditions in which the cultivation of coherent attention requires a degree of deliberate practice that was not previously required.

The features are familiar to anyone living through them. There is a phone in the pocket of nearly every adult in the developed world, and the phone is, by design, a device that delivers interruptions on a continuous schedule, optimized by sophisticated systems to capture and hold attention at the moment it can be diverted. There is an inbox open at most desks, and the inbox is delivering messages from a population of correspondents who are themselves, in most cases, operating under the same conditions, and who therefore expect responses on timescales that previous generations would have considered impossible. There is a social-media stream running, on most devices, as a permanent background process, and the stream is, again by design, structured to deliver small bursts of emotional and informational stimulation at a rate that no other medium has previously been able to match. There is a news cycle that operates on hour-by-hour amplification, on a global scope, with no off-switch and no clear filter between the trivial and the consequential. There is a meeting calendar, in most professional lives, that is structured to divide the day into intervals shorter than the duration required for serious thought on most non-trivial problems. There is, around all of this, an ambient cultural pressure to be reachable, to be responsive, to be aware, to be participating in whatever conversation is currently underway, which means that even the practitioner who tries to step away encounters social friction from the stepping.

The cumulative effect of all of this is not, in most lives, catastrophic. The practitioner is not destroyed by it. Most people, most of the time, get through their days. But the cumulative effect is also not nothing. The cumulative effect is that *coherence of attention is no longer a default*. It is a discipline, in the strict sense — a state that has to be actively cultivated against an environment that, by accident or by design, undermines it. The practitioner who arrives at the keyboard wanting to think clearly about something difficult arrives in a state that has been, over the preceding hours and days, conditioned by the environment. The state is not the state they would prefer to be in. The state requires work to escape from. The work has to be done before the difficult thinking can begin.

Matthew Crawford, in *The World Beyond Your Head*, made what I think is the most lucid contemporary diagnosis of this situation, and he made it from a philosophical angle that is hospitable to readers from any tradition. Crawford's argument is that the contemporary "attention economy" — the network of commercial systems whose business model depends on capturing and monetizing human attention — has created, in aggregate, an environment in which the basic resources required for serious thought have been depleted in something like the way a commons can be depleted by overuse. The depletion is not the fault of any individual actor. No single company, no single platform, no single product is responsible for the pattern. The pattern is what emerges when many actors, each pursuing their own legitimate ends, share the same attentional commons and have each developed sophisticated tools for extracting their share. The result is an environment in which the practitioner who wants to sustain attention has to do so against the gradient. The default is the depleted state. The cultivated state is the exception, and the cultivation has to be done with care, because nothing in the environment will do it for you.

Nicholas Carr, in *The Shallows*, made a complementary argument from a more cognitive-science angle. Carr collected the evidence that the patterns of attention the contemporary digital environment trains — rapid skimming, frequent task-switching, low-tolerance reading, surface-level engagement — are, over time, reshaping the neural substrates of attention itself. The brain that has spent ten years doing what the contemporary environment asks of it has become, on the evidence Carr surveyed, a brain less capable of the deeper sustained attention that older forms of intellectual life required. Carr's claim is contested in some of its specifics, and the literature has continued to develop, but the basic shape of his argument — that prolonged immersion in a particular attentional environment shapes the kind of attention one is capable of — has held up. The practitioner who has spent a decade immersed in the contemporary environment cannot, by a simple act of will, become the practitioner of an earlier era. The capacity for sustained attention does not return on demand. It has to be rebuilt.

Hannah Arendt, in *The Human Condition*, made a related point about the relationship between labor and contemplation that I have been thinking about often. Arendt distinguished between several dimensions of human activity — labor, work, and action — and argued that the modern condition has, by elevating labor to the central category of human life, crowded out the other dimensions, including the contemplative dimension that the older traditions had recognized as essential to a fully human life. Arendt was writing in 1958, well before any of the contemporary developments I have just been describing, but her diagnosis fits the contemporary situation closely. The contemporary life is, in Arendt's vocabulary, an endless labor without sufficient room for the work that endures or the contemplation that grounds. The practitioner who lives entirely in the labor dimension — who treats every hour as a unit of production, every interaction as transactional, every quiet moment as wasted — has lost something the older traditions tried to protect. Arendt did not have a religious solution to propose. She was working in a secular vocabulary, with a stoic temperament. But she saw what was happening, and she described it with a precision that has not aged.

Henri Nouwen, in *The Way of the Heart* — a small book that has stayed with me longer than its size would suggest — wrote about the desert fathers, the early Christian hermits and monastics who fled the urban centers of late antiquity for the deserts of Egypt and Syria, where they developed the disciplines of solitude and silence that would shape the contemplative tradition for the next millennium and a half. Nouwen's interest in the desert fathers was not merely historical. He saw, in them, a posture toward the surrounding culture that he thought was newly relevant to his own time. The desert fathers did not flee the cities because the cities were bad. They fled because the cities were, in their compulsive busyness and noise and demand for performance, structurally incompatible with the kind of attention the inner life requires. The desert was a place where the obstacles to attention were stripped away, and the practitioner could finally encounter what was there. Nouwen, writing in the late 1970s and early 1980s, suggested that the contemporary urban professional was, in many ways, living in a more attentionally hostile environment than the late-antique city the desert fathers fled, and that the desert fathers' disciplines — solitude, silence, the cultivation of a "still small voice" against the surrounding noise — were now relevant in a way they had not been at any prior moment.

I think Nouwen was right. The environment of 2026 is, on most relevant dimensions, more attentionally hostile than any environment the historical record describes — not because it is malicious, but because the convergence of commercial incentives, technological capacity, and population scale has created the first sustained period in human history in which the default attentional environment is structured against the cultivation of sustained attention. Under these conditions, the disciplines the desert fathers developed, and that the Catholic tradition (along with parallel traditions in Eastern Christianity, in Judaism, in Islam, in the various contemplative streams of the wider human inheritance) refined across the centuries that followed, are not artifacts of a vanished world. They are, in operational terms, exactly what the contemporary practitioner needs.

Let me put this in the practitioner's vocabulary, because I want this chapter to land on the desk where the work actually happens.

The contemporary practitioner who wants to do good work — and good work, in the AI era as in any era, requires sustained coherent attention — has a problem that the environment will not solve for them. The phone will not put itself away. The inbox will not close itself. The notifications will not silence themselves. The cultural pressure to be available will not dissolve. The practitioner has to address each of these by deliberate choice, repeatedly, against the gradient of the environment. The choices are not difficult in any individual instance — silencing the phone for an hour is not heroic — but they are difficult to sustain across a working life, because the environment will keep pressing back, and the practitioner who is not actively cultivating the capacity will see it erode.

The religious traditions, on the analysis I have been developing across this part of the book, are libraries of techniques for cultivating the capacity. The techniques were not developed for this purpose. They were developed for the purpose of prayer, of contemplation, of the encounter with God, of the moral life — purposes that the traditions hold to be the deepest realities and that I do not, in this book, contest. But the techniques happen also to do attentional work as a byproduct, and the attentional work is real, and the work is now operationally relevant to a wider population than at any time in recent memory. The Liturgy of the Hours produces a person whose day is articulated rather than amorphous. Lectio Divina produces a person capable of patient iterative reading. The Examen produces a person capable of honest self-review. The rosary produces a person capable of sustained focused attention with bodily anchoring. The Mass produces a person whose week is integrated and whose long-arc orientation is maintained. These are real properties of the practices, observable in the people who have undertaken them seriously across a lifetime. The properties are available to anyone who picks up the practices.

I want to be explicit, here at the close of Part II, about what this means for readers from different starting points, because I have been writing for a wide audience and the wide audience deserves to know what I am suggesting.

To the reader who is Catholic and is already inside the practices: the operational layer I have been describing is, I hope, a help rather than a distraction. The practices are what the Church teaches them to be, at the deepest level. They are also, observably, doing attentional work that matters now in a particular way. Both layers can be held at once. The deeper layer is what governs. The operational layer is one of many real properties of the practices that the contemporary situation has made newly visible. If anything in this chapter has helped you see what you are already doing in a slightly different light — without flattening or reducing it — then the chapter has done what I hoped.

To the reader who is religious in another tradition: the disciplines I have been describing have parallels in your tradition, and the operational argument applies equally. The Jewish tradition has the daily *Shacharit*, *Mincha*, and *Maariv* prayers, the practice of slow Torah study, the disciplined cycle of Shabbat. The Eastern Orthodox tradition has the Jesus Prayer, the *kontakia*, the long liturgical cycle of its own. The Islamic tradition has the five daily prayers, the recitation and contemplation of the Qur'an. The Buddhist tradition has its meditation lineages. Each of these is, on the analysis I have been developing, a library of attentional disciplines refined by long use. Each of them is now operationally relevant to a wider population than at any time in living memory. The book happens to be about the Catholic library because that is the one I was formed by and the one I am qualified to describe, but the point is general.

To the reader who is not religious — agnostic, atheist, post-religious, religiously curious, or simply uninterested in the religious question — the operational argument also applies, and I would like to address you directly. You may be reading this chapter as someone who is simply trying to focus better in the age of phones, who does not want to take on any metaphysical commitments, who is suspicious of religious traditions and would prefer not to be sold a faith along with the techniques. You are welcome here. The techniques work whether or not you accept the metaphysical context in which they were developed. The Liturgy of the Hours, if you adapted it as a secular daily rhythm of three short attentional resets, would still do what it does at the operational level. The Examen, if you took it as a structured end-of-day reflection without the prayer that frames it in the original, would still produce the self-regulation that is its operational fruit. Lectio Divina, if you applied it to a passage of philosophy or poetry or even technical reading, would still train the patient iterative attention that it trains in the original context. The Mass is harder to extract because the Mass is the Mass, and the structural property I described in the last chapter does not function in the same way outside its sacramental context — but the structural pattern can inform the design of other long-form attentional rhythms in your life, and many secular practitioners have, in fact, drawn on the pattern in exactly this way.

I want to add, for the secular reader, that the practices have a useful property the contemporary self-help literature on attention has not been able to replicate: they have been refined by long use in actual human populations across actual centuries, and they have been embedded in social structures that supported their continuation, and they have been tested against many varieties of human temperament and circumstance. The contemporary self-help literature on attention is mostly new. Most of the practices it suggests have been tried at scale for less than a generation. The religious traditions have been running their practices for centuries or millennia, with population sizes in the millions, across diverse cultures and historical conditions, with the practices being adjusted by long iteration. The track record is, by any reasonable comparison, vastly more extensive. The practices have earned, on operational grounds alone, the kind of seriousness that the contemporary literature on attention has not yet had time to earn.

I will close Part II with an observation that points forward to the rest of the book.

The argument I have been making across these four chapters — that the religious traditions, and Catholicism specifically, offer libraries of cognitive architecture that are operationally relevant to the AI era — is one application of a larger pattern that the rest of the book will develop. The pattern is that the Catholic tradition, across its long life, has been working on problems whose solutions turn out to be applicable, with adjustment, to problems that the contemporary world is encountering for the first time. The attentional problem is one such case. The governance problem is another. The problem of how a probabilistic distributed system can maintain coherent identity across long time horizons is another. The problem of how to balance individual conscience with institutional authority is another. The problem of how to incorporate new technologies without losing the tradition's integrity is another. In each of these cases, the Catholic tradition has been working on the problem for centuries, has accumulated a library of solutions, and has developed an institutional memory for what works and what does not.

Part III of the book takes up this larger argument. Where Part II has been about the cognitive architecture for the individual practitioner, Part III will be about the systems architecture for the larger civilizational task — what I will call, in the chapters that follow, *Catholic systems thinking*. The argument I have been making about attentional disciplines is one instance of the larger argument that Part III will develop: that the Catholic tradition, considered as a working laboratory of civilization-scale system design across two thousand years of operation, has lessons for AI governance that the engineering community has not yet fully recognized. The lessons are not religious in the sense that they require religious commitment. They are operational, in the sense that they describe how to make complex systems work over long timescales with bounded resources and probabilistic components. The Catholic tradition has, accidentally and intentionally at once, been running the experiments for two thousand years.

The reader who has followed the argument so far — who has accepted, at whatever level of commitment is comfortable, that the religious practices do real attentional work and that the work is operationally relevant to the contemporary situation — is now in a position to consider the systems-level argument. If the practices for individuals do useful work, the larger systems within which the practices are embedded may also do useful work, at a larger scale, on questions that the AI governance conversation is now starting to encounter. That is the next phase of the argument. Part II closes here. Part III begins on the next page.
