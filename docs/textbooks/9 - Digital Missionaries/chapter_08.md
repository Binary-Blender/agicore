# Chapter 8: Mass as Prompt Engineering

The title of this chapter is deliberately provocative, and I want to be precise, before the chapter begins, about what it claims and, more importantly, about what it does not claim. The Mass is what the Catechism of the Catholic Church teaches that the Mass is. It is the source and summit of the Christian life. It is the re-presentation of Christ's sacrifice on Calvary, made present to the worshipping community across time. It is the meeting of the faithful with the Real Presence in the consecrated bread and wine. None of that is what this chapter is examining. None of that is what this chapter is qualified to examine. The theological reality of the Mass is the Church's domain, taught by the Church on her own authority and timeline, and a layman writing a book on the AI substrate has no business making claims at that level. I am not making them. I will not be making them anywhere in this chapter or in the rest of the book.

What this chapter examines is one structural property of the Mass that becomes visible when we have the new vocabulary that the AI era has given us. The structural property is this: the Mass functions, among many other things, as a carefully designed sequence of semantic operations performed on the consciousness of the participants. That structural property is recognizable, from the side of the contemporary practitioner who has spent time working with large language models, as prompt engineering at the human level. The recognition is interesting. The recognition is also, emphatically, not a reduction of what the Church teaches the Mass to be. The Mass remains what the Church says it is. The chapter is observing one of its many other properties — a property that has always been present in the structure but that becomes nameable in a particular way once you have the engineering vocabulary that names similar properties in another domain.

I will say this once more, because I do not want any reader to come away misreading me: the Mass is not "merely" prompt engineering. The Mass is not "primarily" prompt engineering. The Mass is not prompt engineering at all, if "prompt engineering" is taken to be a complete account of what is happening. The Mass is what the Church says the Mass is, and what the Church says is, in the formal sense, infinitely more than what this chapter is going to discuss. What this chapter is going to discuss is one operational property, visible from a particular angle, that the tradition has always known about under different names — "formation," "the work of the liturgy on the people of God," "the school of prayer," "the hidden discipline" — and that we can now also describe, in 2026, in language that the engineering community will recognize.

With that on the table, let me develop the structural observation.

The Catholic Mass, in its current form (the Ordinary Form of the Roman Rite, as restored after the Second Vatican Council, although the underlying structure goes back, in its essentials, much further), is a sequence of distinct semantic operations performed in a fixed order. The sequence is bounded — it begins at a particular moment, runs through a particular set of movements, and ends at a particular moment. The participants enter the sequence carrying whatever interior state they happen to bring; the sequence performs its operations on whatever they bring; and they leave the sequence in a different interior state from the one they entered with. The structure of the sequence has been refined over sixteen hundred years of practice, and the refinements have been made, century after century, with explicit attention to what each part of the sequence does to the people undergoing it. The Church has, in the strict sense, been engineering this sequence for as long as engineering of any kind has existed, although she has been calling the engineering by other names.

Let me walk through the sequence, attending to the operational function of each part.

The Mass opens with what the rubrics call the *Introductory Rites*. The community gathers physically in one place. The priest, vested, enters in procession. There is an opening hymn, an opening greeting, a sign of the cross, a moment in which the priest welcomes the assembly and orients them to the day's celebration. The community then enters the *Penitential Act*, in which each person acknowledges their sinfulness and asks for mercy, usually with a short prescribed prayer. There is the *Gloria*, on most Sundays — a hymn of praise that is itself among the oldest texts in the Church, used liturgically since at least the fourth century. There is an opening prayer, the *Collect*, which gathers the assembly's particular intentions and sets the day's theme.

What the Introductory Rites do, from the structural standpoint, is *initialize the context*. The participant arrives with whatever was on their mind on the way to church — the traffic, the argument from yesterday, the worry about the week ahead, the half-formed thought about a project at work. The Introductory Rites do not pretend the participant arrives in any other state. They take the actual state and perform a series of operations on it. The gathering is a relocation: physical, but also attentional — the body is here now, with this community, not at the desk or in the car. The greeting and the sign of the cross are an orientation: the participant is reminded who they are in this room, what frame they are in, what is about to happen. The penitential act is a clearing: whatever has been accumulated since the last Mass, in the way of small failures and frictions, is acknowledged and named. The Gloria, when it is sung, lifts the mood — the assembly moves from the inwardness of the penitential moment to the outwardness of praise. The Collect focuses the attention on a specific theme. By the time the Introductory Rites are finished, perhaps ten minutes in, the participants have been brought from whatever scattered state they arrived in to a roughly common state of attention, oriented to what is about to come.

Anyone who has done serious prompt engineering will recognize the structure. Initializing the context is the first move. The model — or, in this case, the human participant — arrives with whatever previous state. The opening sequence performs a series of operations to bring the state into a defined frame. Without the initialization, what follows lands in unpredictable terrain. With the initialization, what follows lands in a state that has been deliberately shaped. The Church figured this out long before any of us did.

The Mass then enters the *Liturgy of the Word*. There are scripture readings, typically three on Sundays — one from the Old Testament, one from the New Testament epistles, and one from the Gospels. The readings are not chosen by the priest or the local community for the day. They are fixed by the universal three-year cycle, called the Lectionary, which covers most of the Bible over its full revolution. Between the readings there is a responsorial psalm and a Gospel acclamation. After the Gospel, the priest delivers the *homily*, which interprets the readings in light of the present moment of the assembly's life. The Liturgy of the Word closes with the *Profession of Faith* — usually the Nicene Creed, recited together by the assembly — and the *Prayers of the Faithful*, the intercessions in which the assembly prays for the needs of the Church, the world, the suffering, and the dead.

What the Liturgy of the Word does, structurally, is *load the long context*. The scripture readings bring into the room a sequence of texts that the participants would not, on their own, have selected for that day. The texts are not novel — most of them are texts the participants have heard, in some configuration, many times before — but the specific combination is fixed by the cycle, and the cycle ensures that across a year (and especially across the three-year revolution) the participant has been exposed to a remarkably large fraction of the biblical canon, read aloud, slowly, in a community of listeners. The homily interprets the readings in light of the present — what the texts mean *now*, for this community, on this day. The Creed reasserts the long doctrinal context — the participant remembers, in the recitation, what the larger frame is, what is being assumed about God and humanity and the world. The intercessions extend the attention outward, beyond the assembly's own concerns, to the wider human condition.

In engineering vocabulary, the Liturgy of the Word is the part of the prompt that supplies the long-horizon context — the relevant prior material, refreshed week after week, that the rest of the sequence will operate against. The participant cannot, in the long run, do without this loading. The Christian intellectual and moral life is, on the tradition's understanding, a life in which a person's thinking and acting are shaped by sustained exposure to a particular set of texts and a particular set of interpretive frames, refreshed and re-applied across the years. The Liturgy of the Word is how the Mass does that work. Each week the context is reloaded; each week the homily applies it to the present; each week the Creed reasserts what is being assumed. After ten years of weekly attendance, the participant has been brought through the full Lectionary three times, has heard hundreds of homilies, has recited the Creed five hundred times. The long context is now resident in the participant in a way that no other intervention could have produced.

The Mass then moves into the *Liturgy of the Eucharist*. The assembly brings forward the gifts of bread and wine — symbolic of the work of human hands and of all the labor of the week. The priest receives them at the altar and offers them, in the *Preparation of the Gifts*. There is the *Eucharistic Prayer*, the long central prayer of the Mass, during which the priest recalls God's saving work, recites the words of Christ at the Last Supper over the bread and wine, and offers the assembly's prayer to the Father through the Son in the Holy Spirit. The Eucharistic Prayer closes with the *Great Amen* of the assembly. Then comes the *Our Father*, the *Sign of Peace* (in which the assembly exchanges peace with each other), the *Lamb of God*, the *Communion* itself (in which the consecrated bread and wine are distributed to the assembly), and a *Prayer after Communion*. The assembly is then sent out into the world with the *Concluding Rites* — a blessing and a dismissal.

I want to be especially careful here. The Eucharistic part of the Mass is, on the Church's teaching, the heart of the sacrament. It is where the re-presentation of Christ's sacrifice happens, where the Real Presence is made present, where the faithful receive what the Church calls the source and summit of the Christian life. The theological reality of this is, again, the Church's domain. The structural observation I am about to make is, *in addition to*, not *instead of*, the sacramental reality. I want that to be on the page in this paragraph, in the strongest possible terms, before I say anything else.

What the Liturgy of the Eucharist does, structurally, is *perform the central operation*. The whole Mass has been building toward this moment. The context has been initialized; the long material has been loaded; the assembly has been brought into a shared frame, with a shared text, in a shared posture. Now the central transformation happens. The bread and wine — and, with them, the offering of the assembly's life — are brought forward and offered. The Eucharistic Prayer recalls the whole arc of salvation history and brings it to bear on the present moment. The words of consecration are spoken. The assembly responds with the Great Amen. The community exchanges peace, recognizing each other across whatever divisions they brought into the room. The consecrated species are distributed. The participant receives.

Whatever else the participant receives at this moment — and the tradition teaches that what is received is far more than the operational vocabulary I am using can describe — the participant also undergoes, at the operational level, an event of integration. The week's work, the week's failures, the week's hopes, the week's worries, have all been brought into the room. They have been gathered up in the offering. They have been placed inside the long arc of salvation that the Eucharistic Prayer rehearses. They have been transformed, in some sense the operational vocabulary does not exhaust, into something the participant can now carry forward. The participant leaves the Mass not in the state they entered. The state has been modified by the sequence. The modification is, among many other things, an act of profound recalibration of the participant's whole orientation in time.

The Concluding Rites send the participant out. The dismissal — *Ite, missa est* in the old form, "Go forth, the Mass is ended" in English — is, etymologically, what gives the entire sequence its name. The participant has been sent. The point of the Mass, the structure says, is not the Mass itself but the life that flows from it. The participant takes the recalibrated state into the next week of their life. They will return, the next Sunday, in a new state shaped by the week, and the sequence will run again.

I want to draw out, now, the structural observation that the title of this chapter points at. The Catholic Mass is, in its long-arc operation across the years and decades of a participant's life, a weekly recalibration of the participant's semantic state. The repetition is essential: each week, the same structure runs. The variations within the repetition are also essential: each week, the readings are different, the homily is different, the season is different, the participant's own life is different. The bodily memory of having done this every week for years matters: the participant's whole nervous system has been shaped by the repeated sequence. The communal embedding matters: the participant has been doing this with a community across time, and the community shapes what the practice does in the participant. The long-horizon ethical framing matters: the participant has, week after week, been brought into contact with texts and structures that orient their whole life around the dimensions of meaning, sacrifice, mercy, community, and the long arc of salvation history.

These properties are, in their operational structure, recognizable as the properties that contemporary prompt engineering tries to engineer for AI models. We try to engineer good context initialization, good long-context loading, good central-operation performance, good integration of relevant prior material, and good outputs that carry the desired state forward into the user's continued work. We do this with text and with structure and with iteration, and we are, in 2026, still mostly bad at it. The Church engineered the equivalent operation for human consciousness, accidentally and intentionally at once, across sixteen hundred years of refinement. The Church engineered it for human beings, centuries before anyone in the engineering community had encountered "engineering" as a word in this context. The structure works. The structure has been working, in the lives of the people who participate in it, for as long as there has been a Mass.

I want to say something to readers who have never been to Mass, because the chapter is for them too. You do not have to find the metaphysical claims of the sacrament convincing in order to recognize what I am pointing at. You can look at the structure of the sequence — the operations it performs, the order in which it performs them, the long-arc rhythm it produces in the lives of people who participate over years — and you can recognize that the structure is doing something real to its participants. The recognition does not require you to take a position on whether what the Church says about the Mass is true. You can hold that question open while still noticing the structural property. The structural property is interesting whether or not you take a position. The Church's teaching about what is happening at the deeper level is a separate question, on which the Church's teachers have spoken and continue to speak with authority that I do not claim.

I want to say something also to readers who have been to Mass all their lives and who may, at moments in this chapter, have been worried that I was going to flatten what they have lived inside. I hope I have not. I have tried to make it clear, more than once, that the structural observation is one layer of analysis and not the whole reality. The whole reality is what the Catechism teaches, what the saints have written about, what the priests in your life have explained to you, what you yourself know from the years of having received communion and the years of having sat in the pew and the years of having let the sequence work on you in ways that the operational vocabulary does not exhaust. The structural observation is real but partial. The reality the Church teaches is what it is. I am not in any conflict with the teaching, and I would not write this chapter if I thought I were.

What I am pointing at is this: the Mass has, among its many other properties, a structural property that is now nameable in a particular way. The naming does not change what the Mass is. The naming does give us, as practitioners of an era that has discovered prompt engineering at the level of machines, a new way to appreciate what the Church has been doing at the level of human beings for as long as the Church has existed. The Mass is the source and summit of the Christian life, in the Catechism's words. It is also, observably, one of the most refined attention-and-formation protocols ever developed by any human civilization. Both are true. They are true at different levels, and they do not compete.

Sixteen hundred years of refinement, applied to one of the highest-stakes problems of human life — how to keep a person, week after week and year after year, oriented to what matters and integrated with their community and capable of carrying their tradition forward into the next chapter of their life. The Church has been working on this problem the whole time. The work shows in the structure. The structure works.

That is what I wanted this chapter to put on the page. The next chapter takes the cognitive-architecture argument we have been building and asks what it means for the practitioner who is trying to maintain coherent attention in a contemporary environment that is, by accident or by design, structured against it. The traditions we have been examining were built for a different attention environment than the one we live in. The fact that they still work — that they do the work they have always done, even more starkly when the contrast with the surrounding environment is so sharp — is a fact worth ending Part II on.
