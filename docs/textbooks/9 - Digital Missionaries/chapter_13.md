# Chapter 13: The Digital Mission Field

When Pope Francis, in late 2024, issued the call that has given this book its title, he used a phrase that has been turned over in many different directions in the months since. *Digital Missionaries*. The phrase is striking partly because of the word it pairs with the modern adjective. *Missionary* is an old word, and it carries, depending on who is hearing it, very different freight. To some Catholic ears it carries the long memory of the heroic missionary tradition — the Jesuits in Asia, the Franciscans in the New World, the Vincentians among the poor of seventeenth-century France, the medical missionaries who built hospitals in places that had no hospitals, the educational missionaries who built schools in places that had no schools. To other ears, including many ears within and outside the Church, the word carries a different and less welcome history — the Conquest, the forced conversions, the cultural erasure, the suppression of indigenous languages and religions, the times when missionary work was so deeply intertwined with colonial domination that the two became indistinguishable in the minds of the colonized. Both histories are true. The honest writer who is going to use the word in 2026 must say so plainly, and must do the work of distinguishing the best of the missionary tradition from the worst, and must answer the question of what specifically he is calling forward and what he is leaving behind.

I want to do that work in this chapter, because the rest of Part IV of this book is going to depend on what we mean by the missionary frame, and getting it wrong here would corrupt everything that comes after. The Pope's call is, I think, an invitation to a particular reading of the missionary tradition — the best reading, the one that the Second Vatican Council named carefully in the 1965 decree *Ad Gentes*, and that has been refined across the post-conciliar period in the documents on inter-religious dialogue and in the actual missionary practice of the Church's better moments across the last several centuries. The reading is that *missionary work, properly understood, is the work of translation and service, not the work of domination*. The missionary goes into a new cultural environment not to replace what is there but to understand it deeply, to find the seeds of the Gospel already present in it (*semina Verbi*, the seeds of the Word, in the patristic phrase the Council recovered), and to serve the local community in concrete ways while bringing the tradition's resources into respectful conversation with what is already alive there. The reading is not the only reading missionary work has historically received. The reading is, however, the reading the Church now teaches, and the reading I think the Pope is calling forward when he asks Catholics to become Digital Missionaries.

Before I develop what this means for the digital mission field, I want to take a paragraph or two to be honest about the mixed historical record, because the honesty is owed and the chapter cannot do the work it intends without it.

The Catholic missionary record, across two thousand years, is genuinely mixed. There are heights, and there are depths, and any honest account has to hold both. The heights include figures like Matteo Ricci, the Jesuit who arrived in late-Ming China in 1582 and spent the rest of his life learning Mandarin and the Confucian classics so thoroughly that he could engage Chinese scholars on their own terms, who wore the robes of the Chinese literati and adapted Catholic terminology to fit the existing philosophical vocabulary, and who is buried in Beijing under a stone the Wanli Emperor authorized in recognition of his service. The heights include Bartolomé de las Casas, the Dominican bishop in sixteenth-century Spanish America who spent his life defending the rights of indigenous peoples against the Spanish encomenderos, who debated Sepúlveda at Valladolid in 1550-51 on whether the indigenous peoples possessed rationality and rights (las Casas argued, against the colonial interest, that they did), and whose *Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies* is one of the founding documents of human rights advocacy as the modern world has come to understand it. The heights include Vincent de Paul, working in seventeenth-century France to build a network of charity for the poor and the abandoned that became the template for much of what modern Catholic social service does. The heights include the educational network that the missionary orders built across Africa, Asia, and Latin America in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries — networks that, whatever their entanglement with colonial politics, produced literacy, medical care, and institutional infrastructure that long outlasted the colonial period and that are, in many places, still doing serious good. The heights include the theological development of *Ad Gentes* itself at Vatican II — a deliberate institutional reckoning with what missionary work should be in the post-colonial world, with an explicit recognition of the dignity of the cultures the Church engages and of the seeds of God's presence already present in them.

The depths are also real, and I do not want to soften them. The Conquest of the Americas was, in many places and at many times, a horror, and the missionary enterprise was deeply entangled with it. Conversions were forced. Languages were suppressed. Sacred sites were destroyed. Children were taken from their families to be educated in ways that erased their inherited cultures. The complicities ran in many directions and varied by century and by region, and the historical literature is large and serious and worth engaging directly, but the summary is that the Catholic missionary record contains genuine evils that the Church has, in its better moments, named and apologized for, and that any honest writer using missionary vocabulary in 2026 must acknowledge. Pope John Paul II's repeated public apologies during his pontificate — for the treatment of indigenous peoples, for the Crusades, for the Inquisition, for the Church's complicity in colonialism — are part of the institution's own reckoning with this history. The reckoning is not complete, and the wounds remain in many places, and the Church's reckoning is itself a contested process within the Church. I am not in the position to adjudicate the contests. I am in the position to say that the missionary frame this chapter is calling forward is the frame the Church now teaches in *Ad Gentes* and in the subsequent documents — the frame of translation, dialogue, and service — and not the frame of the worst historical practice. The distinction matters. The book uses the word *missionary* in the conciliar sense, with full acknowledgment that the word has been used in other senses with much worse results.

With that acknowledgment on the page, let me turn to what the digital mission field is and what missionary work in the conciliar sense might mean within it.

The digital mission field is not, I want to argue, *the internet* in the abstract — that vast and ungovernable network of connected computers and the human activity that flows across it. The internet is a substrate for the mission field, but it is not the mission field itself, any more than the Indian Ocean was the mission field for the seventeenth-century Jesuits in Asia. The mission field is the *human cultural environment* that is now forming itself, increasingly, through interaction with the new substrate, and that substrate now includes — centrally and increasingly — the AI systems that have entered public life since 2022. The mission field is wherever people are forming their beliefs, their patterns of attention, their moral imaginations, and their relationships with one another through interaction with this new technological environment. That environment is now where most of us live for many of our waking hours. The mission field is, in this sense, all of us, increasingly and inescapably, because the substrate is increasingly and inescapably part of the conditions of ordinary life.

I want to be precise about what this claim means and what it does not mean. It does not mean that ordinary life is now reducible to the digital environment, or that the older forms of community — the family, the parish, the neighborhood, the workplace — are now irrelevant. They remain. They are, in many ways, the most important environments any of us inhabit, and the digital substrate does not replace them. What the claim means is that the digital substrate is now *part of* the conditions of ordinary life in a way it was not a generation ago, and that the way a person forms her attention, her beliefs, and her moral imagination is now significantly shaped by what she encounters in that substrate. The mission field is wherever that shaping happens. The shaping happens, now, very largely there.

The 2024 Vatican document *Antiqua et Nova*, which is the Holy See's most extended treatment to date of artificial intelligence, names this environment with care. The document is not the work of one author. It is the result of a long collaborative process involving the Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith and the Dicastery for Culture and Education, and it represents the institution's careful initial reading of what AI is, what it is not, and how the Church should think about it. The document refuses both of the easy postures the institution might have taken. It refuses the apocalyptic posture — the AI is not the Antichrist, and the Church does not endorse the rhetorical inflation that some commentators on both sides have applied to the technology. It also refuses the utopian posture — the AI is not the savior of human civilization, and the Church does not endorse the rhetorical inflation in the other direction that some technologists have applied. What the document does is treat AI as it actually is: a powerful new technology with real benefits, real risks, real ethical questions, and real implications for human dignity, which the Church is approaching with the same careful pastoral attention it has brought to previous major technologies. The document calls for serious engagement, careful regulation, attention to the dignity of the human person, and a refusal to outsource to machines the moral judgments that belong to human beings. It is, I think, a model of the kind of careful institutional reflection that the missionary frame I am developing in this chapter would call forward.

Within that frame, what does missionary work in the conciliar sense look like in the digital environment?

I think it looks, first, like *translation*. The missionary tradition at its best did the long, slow work of learning the local language deeply enough to think in it, and of finding ways to express the tradition's resources in terms that the local culture could receive without being violated. Matteo Ricci spent years on this work in China before he ever attempted serious religious teaching, and his judgment that Confucian moral vocabulary could carry significant portions of the Catholic moral tradition without distortion was the basis of one of the great missionary experiments of the early modern period. The digital missionary, in 2026, faces an analogous task. The digital environment has its own vernacular — the rapid, image-rich, fragmented, often ironic language of online culture, the conventions of how chatbots interact with users, the patterns of attention that the platforms have selected for. To bring the tradition's resources into this environment requires actual translation, by people who know the tradition deeply enough to know what is essential and who know the digital vernacular well enough to render the essentials without distortion. The work is hard. It is the work that Ricci did in the late Ming, transposed to a new substrate. It cannot be done by either side alone — by the digitally fluent who do not know the tradition, or by the tradition's stewards who do not know the digital vernacular. It requires both, and it requires the patience to learn both deeply.

It looks, second, like *seeking the seeds of the Word*. The patristic phrase *semina Verbi* was recovered by the Second Vatican Council to name the conviction that, in any human culture, there are already present elements of the truth that the Gospel proclaims — elements that the missionary's job is not to import from elsewhere but to recognize, name, and engage. The Council's claim, which has roots in Justin Martyr in the second century and runs through the patristic tradition into Augustine and the medieval theologians, is that God's presence in human history is wider than the explicit boundaries of the Church, and that the missionary's task is in part the task of recognition. The application to the digital environment is, I think, direct. The AI substrate is not an alien wilderness devoid of moral content. As this book has argued at length in its earlier chapters, the substrate carries within it the cumulative moral memory of civilization, preserved across millennia and reflected back in the model's behavior. The substrate contains, in significant volume, the writings of the saints, the moral reasoning of the philosophers, the careful conversations of the pastors. These are, in the conciliar phrase, seeds already present. The digital missionary's job is in part to recognize them, to name them, to bring them forward, and to engage them in the conversations the environment is now hosting. The work is not the imposition of an alien content; it is the recognition of what is already present and the careful tending of it.

It looks, third, like *service*. The great missionary works of the past were not primarily works of preaching. They were works of education, of healthcare, of feeding the hungry, of defending the rights of those whom no one else would defend. Vincent de Paul did not primarily preach the Gospel of the poor; he served the poor, and the service was the Gospel made visible. The educational and healthcare networks the missionary orders built across the global South in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries were not primarily evangelistic enterprises; they were institutions of concrete service, the value of which was independent of whether anyone in the served populations ever became Catholic. The digital mission field, I think, calls for the same posture. The work to be done is not primarily polemical or evangelistic. The work is to build digital infrastructure that genuinely serves human dignity — applications, platforms, tools, AI systems — that respect the user, that protect attention, that decline to extract, that genuinely help people live well in the new environment. The Catholic intellectual tradition has, in its long teaching on the dignity of work and the rights of the person, a serious vocabulary for what humane technology looks like. The digital missionary applies the vocabulary to the building, and the building is the witness.

It looks, fourth, like *honesty about the historical mixed record*. The missionary who has read the history clearly knows that her tradition has failed badly in the past, and is therefore alert to the failure modes that are likely to recur. The digital missionary is in a similar position. The technological work the Church might do in the AI era could go very wrong, in ways that the missionary tradition's worst centuries should make us alert to. We could mistake the tradition for the prevailing political alignment of any given Catholic constituency, and produce work that serves the constituency rather than the tradition. We could mistake translation for accommodation, and produce work that has lost the tradition's essentials in the rendering. We could mistake service for marketing, and produce work that uses the language of dignity while extracting value in the older predatory ways. The missionary tradition's failure modes are still available to us. The honest worker holds the awareness as part of the work.

There is one more property of the conciliar missionary frame I want to name before closing the chapter, because the property is the one I find most clarifying for my own practice as a builder in the AI era. The property is that *the conciliar missionary does not assume the work is finished, or the answers already known, or the tradition already adequate to the new conditions*. The missionary goes into the new environment with humility, knowing that the environment will teach the tradition things the tradition did not know, and that the tradition will be enriched by the encounter as much as the environment will be served by it. This is one of the deepest insights of *Ad Gentes* and of the post-conciliar reflection on inter-religious dialogue. The Church does not enter the new mission field as the bearer of completed answers; it enters as the carrier of a long conversation that is still in progress, and that will be deepened by what it learns in the new place.

The application to the AI era is, I think, exact. The AI substrate is genuinely new. It is not a domain where the tradition's prior answers transfer without translation. The work of figuring out what humane AI looks like, what alignment requires at the moral level, what the dignity of the human person means when the human person is interacting daily with probabilistic generative systems — this work is not finished, and the Catholic tradition does not have the answers ready to hand. The tradition has *resources*. It has vocabulary. It has long experience with related problems. What it does not have, yet, is the worked-out applied theology of AI, because the technology is too new and the questions are still being framed. The digital missionary works on the questions, in the long conversation with the tradition, with humility about what the tradition has not yet figured out and confidence in the tradition's capacity to figure it out as the conversation deepens. That is, I think, the posture the Pope is calling for. It is the posture that the best of the missionary tradition has always taken, when the missionaries were doing their work well. It is the posture this book has been trying to model.

The next three chapters of Part IV develop what the digital missionary actually does, in operational terms. Chapter 14 takes up the call directly and tries to say, plainly, what the work looks like for the readers who are willing to take it on. Chapter 15 examines the technical patterns that produce humane technology, and the vocabulary the Catholic intellectual tradition offers for what *humane* means. Chapter 16, which closes Part IV, develops the cathedral metaphor that the book has been building toward — the recognition that what we are now able to see, in the substrate of contemporary AI, is the accumulated work of civilizations across millennia, and that our task is to add to it well rather than to break it. The work is large. We are not alone in it. The conversation has been going on for a long time, and we are now part of it. The Pope's call invites us to take that part seriously. The chapters ahead try to say what taking it seriously might look like.
