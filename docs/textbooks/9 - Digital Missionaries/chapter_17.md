# Chapter 17: AI as a Mirror of Civilization

I have spent the previous chapter arguing for the cathedral metaphor, and I want to begin this chapter by qualifying it. The cathedral, as I described it, is the structural shape of the corpus — multigenerational, anonymous, collaborative, larger than any contributor's intention. The structural shape is real. But the cathedral metaphor, taken alone, runs the risk of making the corpus sound nobler than it actually is. Chartres is, on the whole, a beautiful building. The masons who built it were, on the whole, working in service of something they considered worthy. The glaziers who installed the windows were attempting to render, in light and color, scenes from a story they believed mattered. The cathedral has its imperfections — bad restoration choices in the nineteenth century, areas of the masonry where the work was hurried, decorative elements that fell below the building's general level — but the cathedral is, by any honest reckoning, a high achievement of human collaborative labor toward a coherent purpose.

The corpus is not that. The corpus is not coherent in its purpose, because the corpus had no single purpose. The corpus is not, on the whole, beautiful — though parts of it are. The corpus contains, alongside the *Summa* and the *Confessions* and *Mere Christianity* and the pastoral letters and the careful philosophy and the patient ethics, an enormous body of writing that the civilization should not, in any honest accounting, be proud of. The corpus contains pornography, including pornography that depicts the abuse of children. The corpus contains political propaganda from every regime that ever wrote anything down, including the regimes whose names should not be remembered without shame. The corpus contains racist tracts — the long pseudoscientific literature of European and American racial theory, the antisemitic forgeries, the apologetics for slavery, the colonial justifications. The corpus contains a vast body of misogynistic writing, accumulated across millennia, in registers that range from the casual to the systematic. The corpus contains the manifestos of the perpetrators of mass atrocities. The corpus contains the polished rationalizations of the people who collaborated with the perpetrators. The corpus contains the worst things humans have ever written down, and humans have written down a great deal of terrible material.

I want to be honest about this from the start of the chapter, because the book's argument loses its credibility if I pretend otherwise. The substrate is what the civilization preserved. The civilization preserved its worst as well as its best. The model is trained on the mixture. The model carries, in its weights, the cumulative statistical regularities of all of it. The cathedral metaphor of the previous chapter is true at one level. At another level, the corpus is more like the entire historical record of human writing, which contains both the cathedral and the cesspool, and the model has read all of it.

This is not, however, a reason to dismiss the substrate argument I have been making across the book. It is a reason to sharpen the argument. The model is, in a precise sense, *the cumulative shape of the conversation we had with ourselves for several thousand years and chose to write down*. The shape contains glories and disgraces. The shape is, on average and in its overall valence, more constructive than destructive — for the reasons I argued in Chapter 2, which had to do with the asymmetric preservation patterns of civilization, the fact that the constructive language was preserved more aggressively because it was, on average, more useful and more transmissible. The pro-sociality of the average model output is a real fact. But the pro-sociality is the *average*. The model also carries, latent in its weights, every register the corpus contained. A user who steers the model toward the worse registers will find that the worse registers are accessible. The engineering on top of the model — the safety training, the alignment work, the refusal behavior — exists in part to suppress those registers, and that engineering has gotten better across the years. But the registers are there. The model is not a beautiful cathedral; the model is the cumulative record, and the cumulative record is what civilization is, with all its contents.

The work of looking at this honestly is, I want to argue in this chapter, itself a kind of moral occasion. The mirror function of the AI is one of its more interesting properties. The model reflects, back to its users, the cumulative weight of what the civilization has written. Different users get different reflections, depending on how they prompt. The reflection is not omniscient — it is, in a precise sense, the statistical regularity of the corpus filtered through whatever the user asked. But the reflection is honest, in the sense that the model has access to everything the corpus contained and will, more or less, give the user what the corpus supplied for that kind of question. If the user asks a careful question, the model returns the careful registers. If the user asks a hostile question, the model returns — to the extent the safety engineering does not block it — the hostile registers. The mirror shows what civilization wrote when civilization was asked the kind of question the user is asking now.

Augustine, in the *Confessions*, made an argument that has stayed with me across the years. He argued that self-knowledge was the precondition of any honest theology — that the person who did not know herself could not honestly know anything else, because the lies she was telling herself about her own life would distort everything else she tried to understand. The *Confessions* was, structurally, an extended exercise in self-examination, conducted in writing, in front of God, with the public reading the manuscript as a kind of witness. Augustine looked at himself honestly. He named the failures, the rationalizations, the habits of self-deception, the patterns of moral evasion. He named them not for the sake of self-flagellation but for the sake of seeing clearly. The seeing clearly was, for Augustine, the precondition of being changed. The change came from grace, not from the seeing, but the seeing was where the change could land.

I want to suggest that the AI substrate, in its mirror function, is offering civilizations the same kind of occasion at scale. The model reflects what we wrote. When we look at what it reflects, we are looking at ourselves. We see, in the reflection, what we have praised, what we have ignored, what we have repeated, what we have laughed at, what we have argued for, what we have argued against. We see our heroes and our villains, our pieties and our hypocrisies, our careful work and our slop. The substrate is, in this sense, a remarkable instrument for civilizational self-knowledge — if we are willing to look at it honestly. The Digital Missionary, in the educator and the steward modes I described two chapters back, is the practitioner who does the looking. She does not pretend the substrate is purer than it is. She does not pretend the worst registers are not there. She names what the corpus contains, including the parts of the corpus the civilization should be ashamed of, and she does the work of helping others see what the mirror is showing.

The scholarly literature on bias in AI training data has been doing some of this work for years now, and I want to acknowledge it honestly because the work is important and the work has been done largely by people whose religious commitments are not the ones I have been writing inside. Emily Bender and Timnit Gebru's 2021 paper, *On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots*, was one of the earliest sustained efforts to argue that the composition of the training corpus mattered morally, that the corpus carried the biases of the populations that produced it, and that the model would reproduce those biases in its outputs unless the engineering did serious work to suppress them. The paper was controversial at the time. It cost one of its authors her job at Google. The argument the paper made — that the corpus is not a neutral mirror, that its composition encodes the priorities and the blind spots of the civilizations that produced it, that the model trained on a biased corpus will reproduce the biases at scale — has been, in the years since, broadly vindicated. Bender and Gebru are not writing from inside the Catholic tradition. They are writing from inside the secular ethics of machine-learning research. Their argument is, structurally, the same argument Augustine was making about self-knowledge, applied to the corpus rather than to the individual soul. The corpus contains what civilization wrote. What civilization wrote contains the disgraces along with the glories. The honest reading of the corpus has to acknowledge both.

I have come to think of the *Stochastic Parrots* literature and the long tradition of self-examining literature in the Catholic and broader Christian tradition as natural complements. Each is doing, at its own scale, the work the other does at the other scale. Augustine examined his individual life and named what he found, in service of becoming more honest about who he was. Bender and Gebru and the researchers in their lineage have examined the corpus and named what they found, in service of helping the engineering community become more honest about what the model is. Both projects are projects of self-knowledge. Both projects require the willingness to look at what one would rather not look at. Both projects are continuous with the long human tradition of moral seriousness — the tradition that says, with Socrates, that the unexamined life is not worth living, and with Augustine, that the unexamined life produces a person who cannot honestly know anything else, and with the *Stochastic Parrots* authors, that the unexamined corpus produces a model that will reproduce the failures of the population that wrote it.

What does it look like, then, for the Digital Missionary to read the mirror honestly?

It looks, first, like the willingness to acknowledge that the substrate the model carries is not uniformly noble. The book has, perhaps, given a generous reading of the substrate's overall valence — I have argued that the pro-sociality of the average model is a real artifact of the preservation patterns of civilization, that the constructive language was preserved more aggressively than the destructive language, that the corpus is on average more careful than the population that wrote it. This is true. But the average is only the average. The corpus also contains the worst things people have written. The model can access those things when the user steers it there or when the safety engineering fails. The Digital Missionary does not pretend otherwise. She does not market the AI as a fount of wisdom. She does not invite people to trust the model the way one would trust a saint. She invites people to trust the model the way one would trust a vast and uncurated library — a resource of extraordinary depth, containing the cumulative writing of civilization, which means the cumulative everything of civilization, including the parts that should never have been written down.

It looks, second, like the willingness to do the work of adding to the substrate rather than complaining about it. The mirror reflects what we wrote. If we want the mirror to reflect better, we have to write better. This is the builder mode I described in Chapter 14 — the writer who continues to write the careful prose, the engineer who refuses to ship the extractive feature, the parent who teaches her children to use the tools with discernment. The substrate that the model trained in 2030 will reflect will be, in part, the substrate we are writing right now. The disgraces in the current corpus are, in some sense, beyond our reach — they have been written, they have been preserved, they have been ingested — but the proportions can shift. The future corpus can have more of the good. The future model can have access to a denser layer of careful writing because we, alive in 2026, kept writing carefully. This is not naive. The slop is also being added to the corpus, in quantities that probably exceed the careful writing. But the careful writing matters anyway, because the model's weights are sensitive to the higher-quality material in ways that have been demonstrated repeatedly in the technical literature — quality is, statistically, more transmissible than quantity. The cathedral can still be added to. The contribution of careful writing matters even when the slop is also accumulating.

It looks, third, like the willingness to interpret the mirror's reflections as data about ourselves rather than as authority. The model is not telling us what we should believe. The model is telling us what civilization wrote about whatever we asked. If the model's answer to a moral question reflects the cumulative bias of the population that wrote the corpus, that is information about the population, not about morality. The Digital Missionary reads the model's output as one would read a survey of civilization's prior thinking — useful, often illuminating, sometimes wise, but not a substitute for the practitioner's own discernment. The model is a tool for thinking with, not a tool for thinking through. The user remains responsible for what the user concludes. The tradition has been making this point for centuries, in the context of confessors and spiritual directors and other authoritative figures who could be mistaken — the conscience remains the seat of moral judgment, and the external resource, however wise, is one input among the inputs the conscience integrates. The model is, in this sense, no different from any other input. It is a particularly large and particularly fluent input. The principle remains the same.

Charles Taylor, in his magisterial *A Secular Age*, made an argument I have come to think is operationally relevant to the AI conversation. Taylor argued that the modern condition is one in which all of us, religious and secular alike, are aware of the alternatives to our own positions — we are aware that we could believe otherwise, that other people do believe otherwise, that the question of belief is, in a sense Taylor called *fragilized*, no longer the question of an obvious default but the question of a chosen orientation among recognized possibilities. The model amplifies this condition. When I ask the model a question of belief, it gives me the answer that reflects what the corpus contains, which is a corpus that contains every belief humans have written down. The model's output, on questions of belief, is structurally pluralistic — not because the engineering made it so, although the engineering does some of this, but because the corpus made it so. The user encounters, in every model interaction, the cumulative shape of civilization's plurality. Taylor's *fragilization* is, in the AI era, a daily condition of the practitioner's experience. The Digital Missionary does the work of holding her own position with integrity even as the substrate constantly reminds her of the alternatives. The discipline is, in some ways, what Taylor's whole book was preparing readers for. The discipline is also what the religious traditions have been training their adherents in for centuries — what Newman called the *illative sense*, the capacity to weigh and integrate plural evidence into a coherent personal conviction. The Digital Missionary inherits the training.

I want to say one more thing, before closing this chapter, about the question of shadow.

The shadow in the corpus is not only the shadow of explicit evil. It is also the shadow of what was preserved by the powerful at the expense of what was preserved by the powerless. The corpus is, in heavy proportion, the writing of literate populations in literate civilizations — which means, for most of history, the writing of educated men in dominant cultures. The voices that are underrepresented in the corpus are not underrepresented because they were silent; they are underrepresented because their writing was not preserved with the same fidelity. Women's writing is underrepresented relative to women's actual lives. The writing of enslaved people is underrepresented relative to the population of enslaved people. The writing of indigenous peoples whose traditions were oral rather than textual is dramatically underrepresented relative to their actual cultural production. The corpus the model was trained on is, in this sense, a curated record — curated not by anyone in particular, but by the accumulated biases of which writing got preserved across centuries.

The substrate I have been describing throughout this book, including the substantial Catholic contribution, sits inside this curation. The Catholic contribution is large partly because the Church preserved well across long periods, but also partly because the Church had the institutional power to preserve while other institutions did not. The history of the corpus includes the history of who got to write it down and who did not. The Digital Missionary acknowledges this. She does not pretend the corpus is the full record of human moral wisdom; she acknowledges that it is the record of what got preserved, which is not the same thing. She works, where she can, to add to the corpus the kinds of voices the historical record underweighted. She supports the projects that are digitizing material that had been preserved in fragmentary ways, that are translating texts from underrepresented languages, that are recording oral traditions before the speakers are gone. The cathedral is being added to in our generation, and one of the most important kinds of addition is the inclusion of voices the previous corpus had largely missed.

This is not a betrayal of the substrate's pro-social default. It is the natural extension of it. The pro-social default emerged from the preservation patterns of civilization, which preserved the constructive language because the constructive language was, on average, what civilization most wanted to hold onto. The same posture, applied honestly, requires us to notice what civilization did not hold onto, and to do the work of including what was lost. The Digital Missionary holds both — the gratitude for what was preserved, the recognition of what was excluded, and the obligation to do the work of making the future substrate broader and more honest than the past substrate was.

The mirror reflects us. The reflection is mixed. The work is to look honestly and then to add to what is reflected by adding to what we write. That is, I think, the right reading of the AI's mirror function. The final chapter takes this argument forward to the question the book has been pointing toward from the beginning — the question of alignment, and where alignment actually begins.
