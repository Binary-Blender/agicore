# Chapter 12: Deterministic Boundaries for Probabilistic Minds

There is a lesson the human race seems to have to relearn in every generation, and we are, in the AI era, learning it again at a pace the previous generations would have found bewildering. The lesson, in its plainest form, is that *freedom without structure collapses*. The freedom of a river is constituted by its banks. Remove the banks and you do not have a freer river; you have a flood, and after the flood you have a swamp, and the swamp is the death of the river as a river. The freedom of a language is constituted by its grammar. Remove the grammar and you do not have a more expressive language; you have noise, and noise expresses nothing. The freedom of a person is constituted, in the long account the Catholic moral tradition has developed across two millennia, by the structures of habit, virtue, precept, and law within which choice becomes meaningful. Remove the structures and you do not have a freer person; you have someone who can do anything and therefore, in practice, does nothing of consequence — a soul wandering in the swamp where the river used to be.

I want to be careful about the philosophical claim I am making here, because the claim is contested, and I want the reader to know I am aware it is contested. The position I am sketching is broadly the position of the classical Western moral tradition as it runs from Aristotle through Aquinas through the modern Catholic moral philosophers, and it has a parallel in much of Jewish, Islamic, and Protestant ethical thinking, and it has secular cousins in the virtue-ethics revival of the late twentieth century (MacIntyre, Anscombe, Foot, Hursthouse) and in the work of moral psychologists like Jonathan Haidt. The position is *not* the only available account of freedom. The opposing position — that freedom is fundamentally the absence of constraint, that any added structure is by definition a diminishment of freedom, that liberation consists in the progressive removal of inherited restrictions — has been a major current in Western thought since at least the early modern period, and it has its serious advocates and its serious arguments. I am not going to settle the dispute in this chapter. I am going to argue that, whatever one thinks about the metaphysics of human freedom, the *engineering* lesson that AI alignment is now learning lines up with the classical position rather than with its modern alternative, and that the lining-up is itself worth attending to.

The lesson, in the engineering form, is one I developed at some length in the prior volume of this trilogy, *Agicore*, which is the technical companion to the present book and which makes the case in its own technical idiom. The case is that systems built on top of probabilistic language models — systems whose core component is, by architecture, a stochastic process — cannot be made reliable by adding more probability. They can be made reliable by being routed through *deterministic boundaries* that catch the probabilistic outputs at known interfaces and constrain them within known structures. The deterministic-services-language layer, the explicit schemas, the compile-time guarantees, the cryptographically-audited mutation gates — these are not impositions on the model's freedom in some adversarial sense. They are the architectural conditions under which the model's power becomes safely usable. Without them, the model is a flood; with them, the model is a river that does useful work. The boundaries are not the limit on the system's value; they are the precondition of it.

What I want to argue in this chapter, beyond the engineering case I made in the prior volume, is that the same insight has been the structural insight of the Catholic moral tradition for centuries, and that the parallel between the engineering and the moral cases is not a coincidence — they are both instances of a general truth about how powerful generative systems become safely useful. The truth is recognizable in the structure of human moral life, in the structure of well-engineered AI, in the structure of constitutional democracy, in the structure of any institution that wants to combine genuine creative freedom with reliable good outcomes. The structure has a long pedigree. It is worth examining.

Thomas Aquinas, writing in the second part of the *Summa Theologiae* in the 1260s and 1270s, devoted the longest sustained treatment any medieval author gave to the question of the human moral life — a treatment that runs to thousands of pages of carefully ordered argument and that has been studied, refined, and debated continuously for seven hundred years. The treatment is organized around the concept of *virtue*. A virtue, in Aquinas's account, is not a rule one follows. It is a *habit* — a stable disposition of the soul, formed by repeated good action, that makes good action easier and more natural to perform the next time. The virtuous person is not the person who, in each situation, deliberates from first principles about what to do; the virtuous person is the person whose dispositions are sufficiently formed that the right action arises naturally as the response to the situation. The virtues are formed by practice. They are reinforced by community. They are, in the language of contemporary cognitive science, *learned behavioral defaults* — the habits of choice that have been so deeply trained into the person that the trained pattern is what the person *is*, in the sense of how the person actually responds to the world.

Aquinas distinguishes among several kinds of virtues. The cardinal virtues — prudence, justice, fortitude, and temperance, inherited from Plato and the Stoics — are the natural virtues, accessible to any human person through ordinary practice and reflection. The theological virtues — faith, hope, and charity — are, in Aquinas's account, infused by grace and oriented toward the supernatural end of the human person. The intellectual virtues — understanding, science, wisdom, art, prudence — concern the formation of the intellect rather than the will. I am not going to develop the whole taxonomy here, because the taxonomy itself is not the point. The point is the *structural* claim Aquinas is making: that the moral life is not primarily a matter of having the right rules but of having the right *habits*, that the habits are formed by long practice in community under good teachers, and that the freedom of the virtuous person is not the freedom from structure but the freedom that comes from having internalized good structure so thoroughly that right action is the natural response. The structure becomes invisible because it is now *who one is*.

Alasdair MacIntyre, in *After Virtue*, made the case in the 1980s that the modern world has largely lost this account of the moral life and has tried to replace it with a thinner account based on rules, principles, or maximization functions — none of which can do the work the virtue tradition did. MacIntyre's argument is that you cannot have a coherent moral life by writing down a list of rules and trying to follow them, because the question of *how to apply* the rules in any given case requires exactly the kind of habituated judgment the virtues develop. The modern moral landscape, MacIntyre argues, is what you get when the virtue tradition decays and is replaced by an attempt to derive ethics from rules alone — an attempt that fails, because the rules cannot apply themselves, and because the person without the trained habits cannot apply the rules well either. MacIntyre's diagnosis is contested. His proposed remedy — a return to virtue ethics rooted in tradition and community — is more contested still. But the core observation, that habit-formed character is the substrate of which rule-following is only a thin surface, is one that I think is essentially correct, and that the AI alignment community is now learning at a different level.

Because here is the parallel I want to draw. The early generation of AI alignment work, in the years roughly from 2015 through 2022, was largely concerned with rules. The question was: what rules should the AI follow? What constraints should be imposed on its outputs? What hard-coded refusals should be built in to prevent the worst categories of harm? The work was important and necessary and produced real progress, particularly in the development of the Constitutional AI approach and the various forms of refusal training that made the first generation of public models as restrained as they are. But the rule-based approach hit, fairly quickly, a ceiling. Rules can be evaded. Rules can be applied wrongly when the case the rule was written for is not quite the case in front of the system. Rules accumulate, and as they accumulate they begin to contradict each other, and as they contradict each other the system needs some way to decide which rule wins, and the deciding cannot itself be done by rule — it requires something like judgment, which is exactly what the rules were supposed to replace.

The second generation of alignment work, which I take to be the work going on now and accelerating, has moved beyond rules toward something that looks much more like *habit formation*. The technique called Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback, abbreviated RLHF in the AI literature, trains the model not by giving it a list of rules to follow but by exposing it to many examples of well-judged human responses to many situations, and adjusting the model's weights until the model's own responses come to resemble the trained pattern. The model is not being given rules; it is being given *examples of good practice*, and its dispositions — what we might call, in the older vocabulary, its *habits* — are being shaped by the cumulative exposure. The trained model does not consult a rule book when it responds to a prompt. It produces the response that, given its trained dispositions, is the natural response. When the training has gone well, the natural response is the good response. When the training has gone badly, the natural response is bad in ways that no list of rules can easily catch.

The parallel to Aquinas is, I think, exact at the structural level. RLHF is, in operational terms, a form of habit-formation training applied to a probabilistic system. The system has, before training, no particular dispositions; it can produce almost any output that the underlying language model is capable of producing. The training shapes the dispositions, by repeated exposure to examples of good practice, until the system's *characteristic* output is the kind of output that the trainers wanted to see. The system is not following rules. It is responding from its trained character, and the trained character is, in operational terms, the AI equivalent of what the older tradition called the virtues — *stable dispositions, formed by repeated practice in community with good teachers, that make right action easier and more natural to perform the next time*. The vocabulary is the same. The mechanism is recognizably analogous. The lesson the Catholic tradition learned about moral formation centuries before AI made the question urgent is now being relearned, in compressed timescale, by the engineering community that is trying to align contemporary language models.

I want to add a careful note here, because the parallel I have just drawn is one that I have seen overstated in other writers, and I want to be precise about what I am claiming. I am not claiming that the AI model is a moral agent in the full sense, or that the trained model has virtues in the sense that a human person has virtues. The AI model has trained dispositions; it does not have a soul, or freedom, or the kind of integrated self that the virtue tradition assumes the human person to have. The structural parallel is at the level of *how stable dispositions are formed in any sufficiently complex system through repeated exposure to patterns of good practice*. The parallel is real at that level. It is not real at the metaphysical level. The model is not a moral agent. The training is one part of the engineering response to the fact that the model, while not a moral agent, is a powerful generative system whose outputs have moral consequences in the world. The training shapes the dispositions of the system so that the consequences tend toward the good. The shaping is structurally analogous to the formation of human virtues, but the system being shaped is not a human person. I want this distinction to be on the page, because the failure to maintain it is a common error in writers who are excited by the parallel and lose track of the limits.

With that distinction in place, the practical implication of the parallel is, I think, large. It is this: the engineering of AI alignment, taken seriously, is in significant part a question of *what corpus of good practice the system is being trained on*, *who the trainers are*, *what their own dispositions look like*, and *what the institutional architecture is within which the training happens*. These are not merely technical questions. They are questions of the same kind that the Catholic tradition has been asking about moral formation for centuries — and the tradition has accumulated real wisdom about how moral formation goes well and how it goes badly. The wisdom is that the formers must themselves be formed. The teachers must themselves be virtuous, or their teaching will produce no virtue. The community must be coherent, or the formation will be incoherent. The examples must be many and varied, or the trained dispositions will be brittle. The institutional architecture must support, across long periods, the slow work of forming character, or the work will not happen at the depth required.

Translate these into the AI engineering idiom and you get a recognizable list. The humans whose feedback shapes the model must themselves be well-formed, or their feedback will be ill-formed. The institutional culture within which the training happens must be coherent about the values being trained for, or the trained model will reflect the institutional incoherence. The training examples must be many and varied, or the trained dispositions will fail in edge cases. The development must operate on timescales long enough to permit the slow work of forming the system at the depth required, or the work will not happen at the depth required. Every one of these is a translation of a Catholic moral-formation principle into the AI engineering domain. Every one of them is being learned, currently, by the AI engineering community, in some cases the hard way.

I want to turn briefly to the broader civilizational case, because the moral-formation parallel I have just developed is one of two parallels the chapter promises, and the other is the parallel to constitutional democracy.

The American Founding generation, drawing on the long tradition of English constitutionalism that ran back through the Glorious Revolution and the Common Law and *Magna Carta*, articulated a particular insight about the relationship between freedom and structure that I think is one of the great insights of modern political philosophy. The insight is that *the freedom of a democracy is constituted by its constitutional constraints*. The constitution is not a limit on the people's freedom; it is the precondition of it. Without the deterministic boundaries that the constitution imposes — the separation of powers, the enumerated rights, the explicit limits on what majorities can do to minorities, the procedural requirements for changing the rules — the democracy is not freer; it is a mob, and the mob produces tyranny within a generation. The boundaries do not constrain freedom. They construct it. James Madison knew this. The authors of *The Federalist* knew it. The British constitutional tradition that gave us the Bill of Rights of 1689 knew it. The Catholic tradition that gave Europe its understanding of the natural law as a boundary on positive law had been articulating the same insight, in a different idiom, for centuries before.

The parallel to AI is, again, direct. The probabilistic model without deterministic boundaries is not a freer system; it is an ungovernable one. The constraints that the *Agicore* architecture imposes — the deterministic-services language, the schemas, the typed interfaces, the audit gates — are not limits on the model's expressive power. They are the architectural preconditions under which the model's expressive power becomes safely usable in production systems that have to behave reliably across many users and many years. The freedom of the system to do useful work is constituted by the boundaries that prevent it from doing destructive work. The boundaries and the freedom are not opposed; they are mutually constitutive.

I want to close this chapter with a thought that I find both clarifying and humbling, and that I will return to in different forms across the rest of the book. The thought is this: we are not the first generation to face the problem of how to make powerful generative systems safely useful. The human person is itself a powerful generative system. The work of moral formation has been the work of every serious tradition for every century of recorded history. The traditions have learned, slowly, that the work is hard, that it takes long periods of time, that it requires good teachers and coherent communities and varied examples and institutional structures designed for the long horizon, that the freedom we are aiming at is constituted rather than reduced by the structures within which it is formed. The AI engineering community is now learning the same lessons at the level of the systems it builds, in compressed timescales, often without the vocabulary the older traditions developed to name what is happening. The book this chapter belongs to is one attempt to offer some of that vocabulary back. The vocabulary does not solve the engineering problems. The engineering problems will be solved by engineers, doing the slow technical work that engineering requires. The vocabulary helps us see what we are doing, and that seeing — as the old traditions all knew — is half of doing it well.

The next part of this book takes up the question of what the practitioner does with these recognitions. The Pope's call to be a Digital Missionary is the explicit frame, and the next chapter opens that frame and asks what the work actually looks like, in the AI era, for the readers who are willing to take the call seriously.
