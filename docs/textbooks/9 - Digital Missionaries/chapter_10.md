# Chapter 10: Why Catholicism Thinks in Systems

When I was first learning to build software for organizations larger than the room I was sitting in, I came across a phrase that a senior architect used so often it became a kind of refrain. He would say, looking at whatever brittle and over-centralized arrangement had been proposed for the new system, that the design did not yet *think in systems*. He meant something specific by this. He meant that the design had not yet accounted for what would happen when the people running the system moved on, when the regulations governing the system changed, when the underlying technology was replaced, when the customers wanted something the original designers had not anticipated, when the network partitioned, when the storage failed, when the team grew from six to sixty. To think in systems, in his usage, was to design for the long horizon and the distributed case and the unexpected failure — to design, in effect, *for the conditions you cannot foresee but know will arise*. The phrase has stayed with me. I think about it whenever I look at an institution that has lasted a long time and that still works.

The Catholic Church is, among the institutions I have looked at in this way, the most interesting one I know. It has lasted two thousand years. It governs, today, across every continent and most languages. It maintains a teaching tradition that began in the patristic centuries and that, through its councils and its encyclicals and its catechetical literature, still answers questions today on the same authority by which it answered them in the fourth and the thirteenth and the nineteenth. It has absorbed, across that long life, every major civilizational technology — the codex, the manuscript, the press, the broadcast, the network — without losing itself in any of them. It has navigated the relationship between its universal claim and the dense plurality of local cultures in which it must actually live. It has held doctrinal continuity in tension with theological development, and has not, on the whole, mistaken either pole for the whole. It is, in the technical sense my old architect meant, an institution that thinks in systems. It has had to.

I want to make this case in this chapter without overstating it. The Church is not the only such institution. The rabbinical tradition has carried Jewish law and learning across an even longer span, through diasporas and persecutions that the Catholic Church has never had to navigate at the same scale, and the rabbinical pattern of layered commentary — the Mishnah, the Gemara, the Rishonim, the Acharonim — is itself a magnificent piece of civilizational systems design. The Islamic legal tradition, with its four major Sunni schools and the Shia jurisprudential parallels, has carried a comparable architecture of layered interpretation across more than a millennium. The Common Law tradition of England, with its case-by-case accretion of principle through precedent, is one of the most successful long-horizon distributed decision systems any civilization has produced. The modern constitutional democracies, with their explicit balance of separated powers and their amendment procedures and their judicial review, are deliberate attempts to engineer the same properties the older religious systems achieved through long iteration. The list could be lengthened. The Catholic Church is one major contributor to civilizational systems thinking among several. I want to keep saying this because the case I am about to make, that the Catholic tradition's patterns are now operationally relevant to AI governance, is not a claim of uniqueness. It is a claim of usefulness. The patterns are there. They have been refined over a long time. The AI governance conversation can learn from them without having to convert to anything.

The first pattern I want to name is the one the Church calls *subsidiarity*. The word is awkward in English, and it conceals the cleanness of the underlying idea. Pope Leo XIII, in the 1891 encyclical *Rerum Novarum*, was trying to think clearly about the relationship between the worker, the firm, the state, and the broader social fabric, in the new conditions of industrial capitalism that the nineteenth century had produced. His successor Pius XI, in the 1931 encyclical *Quadragesimo Anno*, returned to the question forty years later and gave the principle its most often-quoted formulation. "It is an injustice and at the same time a grave evil and a disturbance of right order," he wrote, "to assign to a greater and higher association what lesser and subordinate organizations can do." That is subsidiarity in one sentence. Decisions should be made at the lowest level capable of making them well, and the higher levels should intervene only to support the lower levels, not to absorb them. The family decides what the family can decide. The neighborhood decides what the neighborhood can decide. The municipality, the region, the nation, the international body — each decides only what cannot be decided well at a lower level. The principle does not say that the higher levels are unimportant; it says that the higher levels exist *to enable*, not to replace, the lower levels. Subsidiarity is, in this sense, the Church's institutional architecture of *delegated competence*.

I cannot read the principle of subsidiarity in 2026 without seeing immediately how directly it speaks to the question of AI governance. Modern AI systems involve decisions at many levels. The model itself makes a decision, in some operational sense, about how to respond to a prompt. The application built around the model makes a decision about which prompts to allow and how to constrain the model's response. The product team makes decisions about what the application is for and how it will be used. The organization makes decisions about which products to build and which markets to serve. The regulator makes decisions about what the organization is permitted to do. The international body, if there is one, makes decisions about what whole categories of system are permitted. At each layer there are decisions that the layer is competent to make and decisions that exceed its competence. The conversation about AI governance, when it goes badly, almost always goes badly because someone is trying to make a decision at the wrong layer — pushing local decisions to a global authority that cannot see the local conditions, or pushing global decisions to a local actor who cannot see the global consequences. The principle of subsidiarity gives us a vocabulary for what *layer* a decision belongs at, and a presumption — important, this — that decisions belong at the lowest layer that can make them well, with the higher layers existing to support rather than to absorb. That presumption is not the only available one. The opposing presumption — that decisions belong as high as possible by default — is widely held and has its own arguments. But the Catholic tradition, after working on this question for more than a century, settled on the presumption it did, and the choice was the product of serious deliberation. It is worth knowing about.

The second pattern is what I will call the *encyclical tradition*, by which I mean the practice, codified across the modern papacies and reaching back through earlier forms into the patristic age, of the Pope writing major teaching letters that respond to the conditions of the moment in conversation with the prior teaching of the Church. *Rerum Novarum* in 1891 responded to industrial capitalism. *Quadragesimo Anno* in 1931 returned to the same questions in the conditions of the Depression. *Mater et Magistra* in 1961 responded to the post-war reorganization. *Populorum Progressio* in 1967 took up the conditions of the developing world. *Laborem Exercens* in 1981 returned to labor. *Centesimus Annus* in 1991 marked a hundred years of the conversation. *Caritas in Veritate* in 2009 responded to the financial crisis. *Laudato Si'* in 2015 took up ecology. *Antiqua et Nova* in 2024 took up artificial intelligence. Each of these documents is read in conversation with the prior ones. Each one engages, by name, the encyclicals that came before. Each pope reads his predecessors and is read by his successors. The result is an institutional learning trajectory that spans more than a century in the modern form and, in the broader patristic-and-conciliar form, more than fifteen centuries. The Church does not have to invent its position from scratch each time a new condition arises. It has a record of how it has previously thought about analogous conditions, and it can refine the record by writing the next chapter.

I am tempted, when I look at the encyclical tradition with my engineer's eye, to call it institutional memory in its most disciplined form. The institution does not just remember the past; it *engages* the past, in writing, on the same authority by which the past was originally written. The result is a tradition that can develop without rupturing, because development is structured by the prior conversation. Newman, in the nineteenth century, called this *development of doctrine*, and his theory of how the Church's teaching changes without ceasing to be itself remains one of the most subtle accounts I know of how a long-lived institution can grow without losing its identity. The application to AI governance is, I think, direct. We are at the beginning of what will be a long institutional conversation about how to govern systems of this kind. The conversation will need to develop across the conditions we cannot yet foresee. The conversation will need to remember itself well enough that later participants are in real engagement with earlier ones, rather than reinventing the wheel each cycle. The encyclical tradition is one model — not the only one, but one — for how such a long conversation can be structured to learn from itself.

The third pattern is the *religious orders*, by which I mean the Church's practice, beginning in the patristic centuries and elaborated through the medieval and modern periods, of organizing specialized communities of vowed religious around particular charisms and tasks. The Benedictines, founded in the sixth century on the Rule of Saint Benedict, organized stable communal life around prayer and work — the *ora et labora* that gave Europe its monasteries and, through them, much of its preserved literature, agriculture, and learning. The Dominicans, founded in the early thirteenth century, organized around preaching and the intellectual defense of doctrine — the Order of Preachers, whose members included Aquinas and Albert the Great and a long line of theologians and university faculty. The Franciscans, founded in the same century, organized around radical poverty and service to the poor. The Jesuits, founded in the sixteenth century, organized around education and missionary work and what one of their founders called *finding God in all things* — and over four hundred years they have produced schools, universities, scientific work, theological development, and a particular spirituality that has shaped the modern Church and much else. Each order has its own rule, its own training, its own internal governance, its own characteristic intellectual style. Each order operates within the broader institutional umbrella of the Catholic Church but with significant autonomy within its own domain. The pattern is recognizable, in modern terms, as a *federation of specialized teams* operating under shared principles, with each team developing the depth of expertise the shared institution could not develop if it tried to be everything at once.

The application to AI is again direct. The serious work of AI governance is going to require specialized teams — teams that develop deep expertise in safety, in alignment, in interpretability, in policy, in education, in application engineering, in the long-tail evaluation of model behavior. No single organization can develop all of this expertise at the depth required. The pattern of federated specialized teams under shared principles is one that the Catholic tradition has worked out at scale, across centuries, and the lessons it has learned about how such federations function — how they hold the shared principles while permitting genuine specialization, how they manage the inevitable tensions between the specialized communities and the central authority, how they recognize and absorb new charisms when the conditions of the world call for them — are operationally relevant. I am not suggesting we found a religious order of AI safety researchers. I am suggesting that the structural pattern of *specialized communities with characteristic depth, federated under shared principles* is one the Catholic tradition has refined to a high degree, and the AI governance conversation will need something like it.

The fourth pattern is the layered authority structure that the Catholic tradition holds among *Magisterium*, *Tradition*, and *Scripture*. Magisterium, in the Church's vocabulary, is the formal teaching authority — what the bishops in council and the Pope ex cathedra and the broader teaching office of the Church have explicitly defined and taught. Tradition is the lived practice of the Church across the centuries — the liturgical, sacramental, devotional, and moral life of the community as it has actually been carried forward. Scripture is the foundational text — the Old and New Testaments, received as inspired and authoritative. The three layers are distinct, related, and held in a structure of mutual checks. Magisterium does not contradict Scripture and is constrained by Tradition; Tradition is understood in light of Scripture and is interpreted authoritatively by Magisterium; Scripture is interpreted within the Church's lived Tradition and under the Magisterium's authority. The interlocking is deliberate. No single layer can override the others. The structure has been refined across centuries of internal debate, schism, reformation, and counter-reformation, and the current form, articulated most clearly in the documents of the Second Vatican Council, is the result of long practical experience with what happens when the layers fall out of balance.

Looking at this with the engineer's eye, I see a three-layered authority structure with explicit checks across the layers, designed to prevent any single layer from becoming a single point of failure for the truth-claims of the institution. This is, in the strict technical sense, *defense in depth*. The Catholic tradition learned, in its own bitter history, what happens when one layer is allowed to swallow the others — what happens when Magisterium operates without genuine engagement with Tradition and Scripture, what happens when Scripture is read without the Tradition that contextualizes it and the Magisterium that authoritatively interprets it, what happens when Tradition is treated as autonomous from the texts and the teaching office that should hold it accountable. The current architecture is the institutional memory of those failures. The pattern, again, is directly relevant to AI governance. We are designing systems whose authority claims will need to be checked from multiple directions — by the explicit policies the developers articulate, by the lived practice of the systems' actual operation across millions of users, by the foundational texts (the constitutions, the model cards, the training documentation) that the systems were built from. We will get this wrong, repeatedly, in the early decades. The Church's experience of getting it wrong and recovering across many centuries is a usable inheritance.

I want to mention briefly, because no honest case can be made without it, the secular counterpoint to all of this — the case that James Scott made in his book *Seeing Like a State*. Scott argues that large-scale planning institutions tend to produce what he calls *high-modernist failure* — they impose a legibility on the territory they govern that simplifies the territory in ways that destroy what made the territory valuable in the first place. The forester who replaces a complex old-growth forest with a uniform monoculture for ease of management eventually finds that the monoculture cannot sustain itself, because the original forest's resilience came from the complexity the management destroyed. Scott's case is largely a case against the kind of governance pattern I have just praised. He would argue that subsidiarity is honored more in the breach than in the practice, that the encyclical tradition is selectively read, that the religious orders are absorbed into central administration more than is admitted, that the three-layered authority structure flattens in practice into whichever layer holds the loudest microphone. I take his point. The Catholic tradition's *intended* governance patterns are, in any given decade, only imperfectly realized. The Church is also an institution composed of fallen human beings, and it has produced its share of failures. The case for the patterns is not the case that the Church always lives up to them. The case is that the patterns themselves, as articulated, are sophisticated, that they have been refined across long experience, and that they are available to be learned from by the new institutional architectures we are building for the AI era. Scott's warning is itself worth absorbing into the design — the patterns must be paired with the kind of attention to local knowledge that Scott rightly insists on, or they will calcify into the high-modernist failure he warns against.

What I take from the long Catholic experience, on balance, is something Alasdair MacIntyre named clearly in *After Virtue*: that a tradition is a living argument, sustained across generations, about what counts as the goods that constitute that tradition's practice. The Catholic tradition has sustained its argument longer than almost any other institution in the West. It has done so by developing the patterns I have described — subsidiarity, the encyclical record, the federated orders, the layered authority — and by refining them through the long experience of governing a probabilistic distributed system across two thousand years. The AI governance conversation, in 2026, is at the beginning of what may be a comparably long argument. The Catholic patterns are not the only resources available. They are not even the most directly applicable in every case. But they are available, and they have been worked on by serious people for a long time, and they reward attention from anyone who is now trying to design institutional architectures for systems we will not fully understand for some time. The Church did not have AI in mind when it developed its governance patterns. The patterns are what they are. The applicability is an accident of history. The accident is fortunate.

In the next chapter, I want to take one of these patterns down from the general to the specific, and look at what the Catholic tradition learned about the *escalation* of moral problems through the sacrament of reconciliation, and how that learning lines up, structurally, with what the Toyota Production System learned about the escalation of manufacturing defects through the Andon cord. The parallel is one of the more striking convergences I know between the religious and the engineering traditions, and it has direct implications for how we should be thinking about the architecture of AI systems that need to surface ethically uncertain situations to human reviewers. I will, when I get there, be careful about what I am claiming and what I am not. The confessional is a sacrament, and the sacrament is what the Church teaches it is. The structural parallel is one observation among others. But the observation is worth making, and the chapter that follows is where I make it.
