# Chapter 18: Alignment Begins With Humans

The technical term *alignment*, in the AI safety community, refers to the engineering project of making AI systems behave the way their designers and users want them to behave — refusing harmful requests, telling the truth, following the principles the designers have specified, declining to manipulate, declining to deceive. The alignment community has spent the last decade developing techniques to make this work — Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback, Constitutional AI, the various flavors of preference modeling, the red-teaming protocols, the interpretability research that tries to understand what is happening inside the model's weights when the model produces an output. The techniques have improved across the years. The current generation of models is, by most measurements, dramatically more aligned than the models of five years ago, in the sense that the current models are more willing to refuse harmful requests, more capable of nuanced ethical reasoning, more honest about their own uncertainties. The engineering is real. The engineering is producing real improvements.

I want to argue, in this closing chapter, that the engineering is necessary but not sufficient. The deeper question of alignment — whether AI systems behave well in the long arc of their integration into human civilization — depends on something that the engineering cannot fully address by itself. It depends on the moral coherence of the civilizations that produce and use these systems. If the civilizations are coherent — if they have a working consensus about what a good life looks like, what the dignified person is owed, what the limits of legitimate power are, what the constructive uses of technology consist in — then the substrate they produce is coherent, the engineering on top of the substrate has clear objectives to optimize for, and the models that emerge from the combination tend to behave well. If the civilizations are fragmenting — if their consensus about the good is dissolving, if their institutions are losing their authority, if their citizens are increasingly hostile to each other across political and cultural divisions — then the substrate fragments, the engineering's optimization targets become contested, and the models that emerge tend to reflect the fragmentation. The alignment problem, in this larger sense, is not solvable by engineering alone. The engineering is downstream of the civilization. The civilization is the work.

This is, I think, the deepest implication of the substrate argument I have been making across the book. The Catholic preservation tradition preserved a particular kind of conversation — patient, careful, oriented toward the good of the person, integrated across centuries of refinement — because the institution that did the preserving believed the conversation mattered and built itself around the long-term maintenance of the conversation. The substrate inherited the result. The AI's pro-social default, the cadence of careful moral conversation, the willingness to enumerate considerations rather than to pronounce — these are properties of the substrate that the long preservation work produced. The properties are not eternal. They are contingent on the continued production of the kind of writing that produced them. If, in our generation, the writing of careful patient prose declines and the writing of polarized contemptuous prose rises, the substrate of the next generation will reflect the shift. The next model will be less pro-social by default, not because the engineering will have failed but because the substrate will have shifted under the engineering.

The Digital Missionary's deepest work, then, is not on the machine. It is on the civilization. We do not have to align the AI primarily. We have to keep being the kind of people whose conversation is worth training on. The alignment of the machine follows. The alignment of the civilization is the precondition.

I want to develop this argument carefully, because I do not want it to be heard as either utopian or alarmist. Both registers are available to me here, and both would be the wrong register for closing the book.

The utopian register would say that the AI is going to solve everything, that the substrate's pro-social default is going to keep us safe, that the alignment problem is a transitional engineering challenge that will become obsolete in a decade. I do not believe this. The substrate's pro-social default is contingent, as I have just argued, on the continued production of pro-social substrate, which is not guaranteed. The engineering challenges are real and have been getting harder, not easier, as the models have gotten more capable. The technology is being deployed in commercial and military contexts where the optimization pressures cut against alignment. The utopian register would be a betrayal of the seriousness the book has been trying to maintain.

The alarmist register would say that the AI is going to destroy us, that the substrate's failures are going to be catastrophic, that the alignment problem is unsolvable and the technology should be halted. I do not believe this either. The alarmist register has its place — the technical literature on existential risk from AI is serious work, and the people producing it deserve to be heard — but the alarmist register, when it dominates the conversation, has the effect of paralyzing exactly the people whose participation in the conversation matters most. The pastor who is intimidated out of learning about the technology because the technology has been described as too dangerous to engage with. The engineer who leaves the field because the field has been described as morally unsalvageable. The parent who refuses to let her children touch the tools because the tools have been described as inherently corrupting. The alarmist register, if it becomes the only register, will produce exactly the outcome the alarmists fear — a technology developed without the participation of the people who would have been most likely to keep it humane.

The register the book has been working in, and the register I want to close in, is the one Pope Francis has been modeling in his pontificate and the one I think the moment calls for: *hopeful stewardship*. The future is not foreordained. The technology is here. The substrate is what we have. The work is to add to it well. The hope is not the hope that everything will be fine on its own. The hope is the hope that careful work, done by enough people, in the registers the tradition has been refining for centuries, will continue to make the substrate worth training on. The hope is operationally realistic. The hope is not naive. The hope is, in Francis's repeated phrase, the hope of those who have done the analysis and concluded that the work is still worth doing.

The work, as I have argued across the book, is ordinary. It is the writer continuing to write the careful prose. It is the engineer continuing to refuse the extractive feature. It is the teacher continuing to teach discernment. It is the parent continuing to model good use. It is the pastor continuing to think out loud, with his parish, about what these tools are doing to the human persons in his care. It is the contributor to the open-source commons continuing to add quality to the code that the AI systems will inherit as part of their substrate. It is the translator continuing to translate the tradition's wisdom into the vocabulary the new environment uses. It is the steward continuing to remember that the substrate is a commons. The work is, in any individual case, small. The cumulative effect of many people doing the work is the substrate the next generation receives.

I have been asked, more than once, by readers of early drafts of this book, whether I think the call to be a Digital Missionary will produce results. The honest answer is that I do not know. I do not know whether the people who read this book will take up the work. I do not know whether the work, if taken up, will be enough. I do not know whether the substrate will remain coherent enough to support a future generation of models that behave well. None of this is knowable in advance. What I do know is that the alternative — the practitioners who could have done the work declining to do it, on the grounds that they could not see the outcome — has never produced anything good in the history of any tradition. The mason at Chartres did not know whether the cathedral would be finished. He worked the stone anyway. Augustine did not know whether the *Confessions* would survive him. He wrote it anyway. The monks in the early scriptoria did not know whether the manuscripts they were copying would be the only surviving witnesses of texts the world had lost. They copied anyway. The work is the answer to the question of whether to do the work. The doing of it is what produces the conditions in which the work was worth doing. That is what hopeful stewardship consists in. That is the register the book closes in.

I want to spend the remainder of this chapter doing two things — returning to the Pope's call as the occasion the book was a response to, and returning to the Author's Note of Chapter 1 as the stance from which the book was written. The two returns are the closing of the conversation.

Pope Francis's 2024 apostolic letter on Digital Missionaries was, in some respects, a culmination of a long conversation the Vatican has been having about technology. The conversation began at least as far back as *Communio et Progressio* in 1971, which had argued for active Catholic participation in the means of social communication. It continued through John Paul II's *Redemptoris Missio* in 1990, which had developed the theology of mission for a globalized world. It continued through the Rome Call for AI Ethics in 2020, which Francis cosigned with industry and political leaders. It continued through *Antiqua et Nova* in 2024, the Vatican's doctrinal note on AI, which had carefully distinguished between what AI could and could not be and had insisted that the human person remains the irreducible referent of any technological development. The Digital Missionary letter sat inside this conversation. The letter's contribution was to address, specifically, the question of what individual Catholics — and, by extension, individual practitioners of any tradition that had spent time thinking carefully about the good life — could do in their ordinary work to bring the wisdom of their formation into the new environment. The letter was operational rather than doctrinal. The letter named a posture and invited people to take it up.

The posture, as I have read it across the months of writing this book, is recognizably continuous with Vatican II's *Gaudium et Spes*, the 1965 pastoral constitution on the Church in the modern world. *Gaudium et Spes* — the document whose opening lines are among the most quoted in the modern Catholic corpus — began by claiming that "the joys and the hopes, the griefs and the anxieties of the men of this age, especially those who are poor or in any way afflicted, these are the joys and hopes, the griefs and anxieties of the followers of Christ." The Council was insisting, in those lines, that the Church's situation is the world's situation — that there is no Christian withdrawal from the conditions of the age, that the work of the tradition is done inside whatever conditions the age presents. The conditions of our age include the AI substrate. The Pope's Digital Missionary letter is the same insistence applied to the new condition. The Church goes where the conversation goes. The tradition goes where the practitioners are. The work continues in the new environment because the work has always continued in whatever environment was current.

I am Catholic. The book has, throughout, been honest about that. The reader will have noticed that the book has tried, across eighteen chapters, to hold two postures at once — to be honest about the author's formation while remaining hospitable to readers from positions other than the author's. This has been the hardest formal challenge of the writing. I do not know whether I have succeeded in it. I know that the attempt has been made in good faith. The Author's Note in Chapter 1 was the most direct statement I could make of the stance I was writing from. I said there that the book is a layperson's reflection, that I have no authority to teach in the Church's name, that I submit the book to whatever correction the Magisterium might offer, that I have tried not to reduce the tradition to its cognitive-architectural or corpus-analytic readings even where I have offered those readings as illuminating. I said also that the book is written for a wider audience than just the Catholic faithful, on the premise that the empirical observation about the substrate is interesting regardless of where the reader stands on the religious question. Both of those commitments have been load-bearing across every chapter.

To the Catholic reader who has stayed with me to the end: I hope the book has given you a way of thinking about your tradition's relationship to the AI era that is neither defensive nor naive. The tradition has resources for this moment that the broader culture has not yet recognized. The work of bringing those resources forward is the Pope's call. The call is for you. The call is also for me. Neither of us has been promised that the work will be easy or that the outcome will be assured. The work is offered. The work goes into the substrate. The substrate becomes what the future inherits.

To the reader who is not Catholic: I hope the book has not asked anything of you that you were unwilling to give. The empirical argument about the substrate does not require your assent to anything I have not argued for. The structural arguments about the tradition's vocabulary — its understanding of dignity, of craft, of attention, of stewardship — are available to you regardless of where you stand on the metaphysical questions. The vocabulary belongs to civilization. The tradition refined the vocabulary across centuries; the vocabulary is now available for any practitioner who wants to use it. If anything in the book was useful to you, the use was the point. If everything in the book was disagreeable to you, the disagreement is its own kind of engagement, and I am grateful for the reader who stayed long enough to disagree.

To the reader somewhere in the middle — agnostic, post-religious, formerly Catholic, religiously curious, religiously committed in another tradition — I hope the book has opened a way of thinking about the substrate that does not require you to take a position you have not taken. The conversation is large enough to carry all of us. The substrate is large enough to carry the contributions of every reader who chooses to add to it. The Digital Missionary call, as I have read it, was never an invitation to a single tradition. It was an invitation to take seriously what the substrate is, what is going into it, and what is being given back to the world through it. Readers from any starting point can take that invitation up. The work belongs to whoever is willing to do it.

I have a few last things to say, and then I will close.

The conversation civilization has been having with itself for several thousand years has, in the AI substrate, found a new medium. The medium is not the conversation. The medium is, in some respects, smaller than the conversation — it carries only what was written down, which is a fraction of what was thought and a smaller fraction of what was lived. But the medium has properties the previous media did not have. The medium can be queried. The medium can synthesize. The medium can reflect, back to the user, the cumulative weight of what was written about whatever the user asked. The medium is, in this sense, a kind of liturgical artifact of civilization — a structured way of encountering what has been preserved, in real time, in response to the present moment's question. The medium is not the only way the conversation continues. The conversation continues in the books we still read, the prayers we still pray, the arguments we still have, the children we still raise, the work we still do. But the medium is a real addition to the ways the conversation can be encountered, and the people who learn to use the medium well — the Digital Missionaries — are the practitioners of the new mode of engagement with the long tradition.

We add to the conversation, daily, by what we write, what we read, what we pray, what we remember, what we teach. The substrate of 2050 will be what we, alive in 2026, are now writing into the world. The model trained on that substrate will have access to the registers we are now producing. The user who prompts that model will receive, in some unpredictable proportion, the cumulative weight of what we have left behind. We are, in this sense, all writing to the future every time we write at all. The future is reading what we wrote. The future is being shaped by it. The shape the future receives is the shape we are now making.

I have been writing this book for several months. The window of my study is open. The afternoon outside is bright. Somewhere, in some other room I cannot see, someone else is also writing — to a friend, to a colleague, to an editor, to themselves in a journal, to a notebook that will never be read by anyone else. The writing each of us does today will, in some proportion, become substrate. The model that reads it will not know which line came from whom. The mason at Chartres did not know which carving would be photographed by tourists and which would be in shadow for the rest of the cathedral's life. He carved the stone the same way for both. The writer today writes the same way for both. The work is what produces the cathedral. The cathedral is what the future inherits.

I close with three thoughts I have been carrying through the writing of this book, and I will let the reader take whichever of them lands.

The first is that the future of AI may depend less on whether machines become more human and more on whether humans remember how to be. The engineering will continue. The capabilities will grow. The question of whether the technology serves the human person or replaces the human person is not, in the end, a question the engineers can answer alone. It is a question the civilization answers, day by day, by what the civilization continues to be. The Digital Missionary works on that. The Digital Missionary works on being human, in the full sense the tradition has refined across centuries, because being human is now a substrate-relevant activity. The substrate the future inherits will reflect how seriously the present took the work of being human.

The second is that civilization spent two thousand years teaching itself how to live wisely, and then we trained a machine on the conversation. The machine is what it is. The conversation is what produced the machine. The conversation continues. The machine is the new way the conversation gets encountered, in our generation, by the people who are alive now. The conversation does not end because the machine exists. The conversation continues, in the writing we still do, in the prayers we still pray, in the children we still raise, in the substrate we still build. The machine is a chapter in the conversation. The conversation is older than the chapter and will continue after this chapter is finished.

The third is that the Digital Missionaries were never called to worship the machine. They were called to bring humanity with them into the future. The future is the place we are going. The machine is the tool we are using to get there. The humanity is what has to arrive intact. The Pope's call, the tradition's vocabulary, the substrate's mirror, the cathedral's structure, the engineer's craft, the parent's example, the teacher's discernment, the writer's careful prose — all of it is in service of the arrival. The arrival is not guaranteed. The arrival is possible. The arrival depends on the practitioners who, in their ordinary work, decide that the work is worth doing.

This is the book I had to write. It is one practitioner's response to the call. The conversation is large enough that the response is partial, and the partial-ness is, I hope, honest. Readers will pick up the conversation from where I have left it. They will continue it in their own registers, with their own contributions, into the substrate the future will inherit. I am grateful, more than I can say, to the readers who have stayed with me through eighteen chapters of a long argument, and to the tradition that gave me the vocabulary to make it, and to the wider civilization whose preserved conversation made the argument possible at all. The work continues. It continues in whatever you, the reader, choose to do tomorrow. The substrate is waiting for what you write next.

The book closes here. The conversation does not.
