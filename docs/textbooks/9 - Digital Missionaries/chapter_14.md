# Chapter 14: Digital Missionaries

I have spent thirteen chapters laying the ground for an invitation, and the time has come to extend it. The invitation is the one Pope Francis made in late 2024, in the apostolic letter that gave this book its title. The Pope addressed it to Catholics, in the first instance, because he is the pope and that is the constituency he speaks to directly. But the call he made — to become *digital missionaries* in the new technological civilization the AI substrate has helped to bring into being — is, I want to argue, a call that any thoughtful reader can take up, in the register that fits the reader's own convictions. The Catholic reader can take the call at its full theological weight. The Protestant reader, or the Orthodox reader, or the Jewish reader, or the Muslim reader, or the reader formed by any religious tradition that has spent centuries preserving its conversation, can take the call as an invitation to do the work that tradition has prepared its members to do. The secular reader, the agnostic reader, the post-religious reader can take the call as recognition that civilization's moral memory is now a substrate the AI is trained on, and that *someone* has to bring that memory forward responsibly into the next phase, and that the someone is not going to be the machines, and not going to be the institutions, and not going to be the governments — it is going to be the practitioners, one at a time, in their work, in their writing, in their teaching, in their parenting, in their conversations.

Let me start by saying clearly what the Digital Missionary, as I have come to understand the term, is *not*. The Digital Missionary is not a culture warrior. The work has nothing to do with winning ground in the ongoing arguments about technology that consume the social platforms and the policy journals. The work is not adversarial. The work is constructive. The Digital Missionary is also not a fear merchant. The literature of alarm about AI has its place — there are real risks, and the people who think clearly about catastrophe are doing useful work — but alarm is not the Digital Missionary's posture. The Digital Missionary works from inside the technology, not from outside it. The Digital Missionary is also not an anti-technology reactionary. There is a long and honorable tradition of religious skepticism about technological progress, and I have read enough of it to take it seriously, but the call I am responding to is not a call to retreat. The Pope was not asking Catholics to leave the digital environment. He was asking them to enter it more deliberately, with what their tradition has given them. The Digital Missionary is also not, finally, a person who claims unusual authority. The work is ordinary. The work is what any thoughtful practitioner does when she takes seriously what her tools are and what they are doing in the world.

What the Digital Missionary *is*, in operational terms, can be sketched along six lines — the lines that emerged for me as I worked through the chapters of this book.

The Digital Missionary is a *builder*. The substrate is what we put into it. Every email, every essay, every comment thread, every book, every reasoned conversation we record contributes to the corpus that the next generation of models will be trained on. This is not a metaphor; it is the literal mechanism. The web crawlers gather. The datasets compile. The models train. The training corpus of any large model trained in 2030 will include, in some proportion, the writing that you and I produce between now and then. If we write thoughtfully, the substrate inherits thoughtful writing. If we write thinly, the substrate inherits thin writing. If we write cruelly, the substrate inherits cruelty. The Digital Missionary, in the builder mode, takes this seriously. He writes what he hopes the corpus will carry. She publishes what she hopes the future will read. The builder is not naive — the corpus is large, and any individual contribution is statistically small — but the builder is not paralyzed by smallness either, because the corpus is the sum of small contributions, and someone has to make the contributions worth summing. Wendell Berry once wrote that the proper work of a writer is to write the kind of book the writer wishes existed. The Digital Missionary, in the builder mode, writes the kind of substrate the future deserves.

The Digital Missionary is an *educator*. The technology is here. People are using it. Most of them do not understand what it is. They use it well, they use it poorly, they use it for the wrong tasks, they trust it too much or too little, they ask it questions it cannot answer well and ignore it on questions it answers brilliantly. The educator's work is to help the people in the educator's life understand what the substrate is and how it works. This is, in the first instance, a literacy project. A parish has, on a given Sunday, a hundred or two hundred people in the pews, most of whom are using these tools weekly and almost none of whom have had the technology explained to them. The pastor who learns enough about AI to help his parish think through it is doing missionary work — not because he is converting anyone, but because he is equipping his parishioners to engage the dominant technology of their era with the discernment their tradition has been training them in for centuries. The teacher who shows her students how to use AI tools in class while also teaching them to interrogate the outputs is doing missionary work. The colleague who patiently explains to a coworker that the model does not actually know what it is talking about and that the coworker should verify is doing missionary work. The educator is not the expert; the educator is the practitioner who knows enough to help the next practitioner along.

The Digital Missionary is an *ethical technologist*. This is the harder of the operational modes, because it requires technical access — but the ethical technologist, in the form I have come to think about her, is not necessarily the principal engineer at the AI lab. She is the engineer at any level of any tech company who applies the tradition's moral architecture to her own work. She refuses to ship the feature that she knows will degrade her users' lives even if it would improve her quarterly metrics. She raises the concern in the design review when the product is heading somewhere extractive. She writes the documentation honestly. She names the trade-offs in the engineering meeting when the trade-offs are uncomfortable. She does the work the tradition has been preparing its practitioners to do for centuries — what Aquinas would call the work of *prudence*, what John Paul II in *Laborem Exercens* called the inherent dignity of work itself, what the encyclical tradition has named again and again as the obligation of the worker to the world her work is shaping. The ethical technologist does not have to be senior. She does not have to be powerful. She has to be present, on the team, in the room, willing to speak. Most of the load-bearing ethical decisions in any technology company are made by people whose titles do not appear in the press release. The ethical technologist is one of those people.

The Digital Missionary is a *steward*. The training corpus is a civilizational commons. It belongs to no one and everyone. It is being depleted, in some respects, by the slop that recent years have poured into it — the synthetic content, the SEO sludge, the engagement-bait, the gradually-degrading-quality of the average internet text — and it is being enriched, in other respects, by the careful writing that thoughtful people continue to do. The steward treats the substrate as a commons that requires tending. She does not pollute it casually. She does not flood the corpus with low-effort generated content because the algorithm rewards it this quarter. She remembers that the corpus is what trains the next model, and that the next model is what her children will be talking to, and that the conversation her children will have with the machine will be shaped by what people in her position chose to put into the substrate this year. The steward thinks in generations. The Catholic tradition's vocabulary for this is *solicitude for the common good*, a phrase that runs through the encyclical literature from *Rerum Novarum* forward. The steward is the practitioner who has internalized that phrase and lets it govern her behavior with respect to the substrate.

The Digital Missionary is a *translator*. The Catholic missionary tradition, at its best, did not arrive in a new culture and demand that the culture become Latin. The best of the missionaries learned the local language, found the seeds of the Word that were already present in the local tradition — *semina Verbi*, the seeds of the Word, the doctrine the tradition developed across centuries to describe how grace arrives in cultures the missionary has never seen before — and worked from inside the local idiom to communicate what they had come to share. Matteo Ricci spent decades in China not Latinizing the Confucian tradition but learning it, dressing as a Chinese scholar, translating Christian thought into a Chinese vocabulary that Chinese readers could engage with. The same posture is required of the Digital Missionary in the new environment. The vocabulary of the AI conversation — alignment, training, fine-tuning, context windows, RLHF, agentic systems — is not the vocabulary of the Catholic tradition. The Digital Missionary learns the new vocabulary so that she can speak inside it, and she translates the tradition's wisdom into that vocabulary when the translation is fruitful. She does not require the AI engineers to learn Latin. She learns Python — or at least learns enough about Python to know what the engineers are doing — and translates what the tradition has known about *prudence* and *temperance* and *justice* into terms a design review can use.

The Digital Missionary is, finally, a *systems thinker*. The lessons of Part III of this book — that the Church has, across two thousand years, become a working laboratory of civilization-scale system design — are usable lessons. The Digital Missionary applies them. She thinks in subsidiarity: decisions are made at the lowest level they can be made well at, and pushed higher only when the lower level cannot bear them. She thinks in escalation: when something is wrong, she pulls the Andon cord rather than swallowing the concern. She thinks in habit formation: alignment is not a guardrail bolted on at the end; it is the cultivation of patterns over time that make right action the natural response. She thinks in long horizons: the system she is part of is older than her career and will continue after she is gone, and her contributions are best evaluated against the long arc, not the quarterly numbers. The systems thinker is rare in any institution. The tradition has been producing systems thinkers for centuries, partly because the work of running a global Church across two millennia *required* systems thinking and the tradition learned how to produce the people who could do it. The Digital Missionary inherits the training even if she came to it indirectly.

I want to make this concrete by sketching what a Digital Missionary's week actually looks like, because abstract lists are easy to nod along with and hard to act on, and the call I am extending is a call to action rather than to assent.

Consider, first, the pastor I mentioned earlier. He is, by training, a parish priest. He did not go to seminary to learn machine learning. But he has noticed, in the last two years, that his parishioners are using AI tools for everything from drafting work emails to processing grief, and that no one has helped them think about what they are doing. So he spends an evening a week, on his own time, reading about how the models work — not the deep technical literature, but the accessible material that gives him enough of a mental model that he can answer the questions his parishioners ask. He brings it into homilies occasionally, not as the subject of the homily but as a passing example: "you might have noticed that when you ask one of these AI tools a hard question, it gives you a careful answer rather than a quick one, and that the careful answer is a kind of moral discipline the tradition has been recommending for centuries." He runs an occasional adult-ed evening on AI and the parish. He invites a parishioner who works in tech to co-present. He is not an expert. He is a pastor who has done the homework. The parish is better for it. That is missionary work.

Consider an engineer, in her late thirties, who works at a mid-sized technology company on the product team for a consumer application. The team has been asked to design a new feature that would use AI to keep users engaged longer with the platform. The technical implementation is straightforward. The likely effect on users — that they will spend more time on the platform than they want to, that the platform will become slightly more habit-forming and slightly less serving of the users' actual lives — is also clear to her. She is not the manager. She does not have the authority to veto the feature. What she has is a voice in the design review. She uses the voice. She names what the feature will do. She proposes an alternative that achieves the business goal with less extraction. The alternative is partially adopted. The shipped feature is somewhat less extractive than the original specification. No press release covers the change. No award is given. But the substrate of the next generation of users' experience is slightly better than it would otherwise have been, because a single engineer did the work of *prudence* in the room where the decision was being made. That is missionary work.

Consider a high-school teacher who has watched, across two academic years, his students arrive in his classroom having outsourced their reading to AI summarizers. He is tempted to ban the tools and has tried, the first year, and discovered that the ban does not work — the students use the tools anyway, only with less honesty. So in the second year he changes posture. He uses the tools in class, openly, with the students. He shows them how to read an AI-generated summary of a text alongside the text and identify what the summary missed. He assigns them to interrogate AI outputs the way they would interrogate any source — with skepticism, with verification, with attention to what the source is for and what the source is missing. His students leave his class with a literacy his colleagues' students do not have. They are less impressed by the tools and more capable of using them well. They have been formed in the discernment the tradition has been teaching for centuries, applied to the medium of their generation. That is missionary work.

Consider a writer who has been writing for thirty years, who has watched the publishing industry deteriorate and the attention economy degrade her readership, and who is now told by industry observers that AI will be writing most of the prose her readers consume within a decade. She has a choice. She can stop writing on the grounds that nothing she produces will matter. Or she can keep writing — write more carefully, more deliberately, more in the register that the future corpus will need more of — on the grounds that the substrate of 2050 will be made of what writers like her continue to make. She chooses the second posture. She writes the careful prose. She publishes it. She does not know who will read it. She does not need to know. The substrate is the substrate; her contribution to it does its work whether she sees the work or not. That is missionary work.

Consider a parent — perhaps the most ordinary case, and the one that may matter most across the long arc. The parent's children are growing up in an environment where the AI tools are simply part of the background of life, like television and the internet were for the generations before. The children will use the tools. The question is whether they will use them with discernment or without it. The parent's work is to show the children, by example more than by lecture, how to engage the tools the way a serious person engages serious tools. To ask the model good questions. To verify the answers. To notice when the model is bullshitting and to call it out. To use the model for the kinds of work the model is good at and not to outsource the kinds of work the model is bad at. To remain the author of the child's own thinking even when the model is part of the thinking process. The parent who does this work is performing one of the most consequential acts of digital mission the era allows. The child raised by such a parent grows up into a practitioner who can engage the substrate with discernment. The substrate, generation by generation, is shaped by what such practitioners put into it. That is missionary work — the most ordinary kind, the most consequential kind, the kind no one will ever congratulate the parent for, and the kind the future will be built out of.

The Pope's 2024 letter on Digital Missionaries quoted the older document *Communio et Progressio*, the 1971 Vatican pastoral instruction on the means of social communication, which had argued that the Christian's obligation to the world includes the obligation to participate in the world's conversations rather than to withdraw from them. John Paul II's *Redemptoris Missio* — the 1990 encyclical on the Church's mission — had said that "the human person needs the truth, but he also needs the means to attain it." Francis took both threads forward. The Digital Missionary is the practitioner who participates in the world's conversation, in the world's tools, with the truth her tradition has given her and with the means the new technology provides. She does this in her ordinary work. She does it most of the time without anyone noticing. She does it because the tradition formed her to take seriously what is at stake in the conversations of her time, and the conversations of our time are increasingly mediated by the substrate the AI carries.

I am going to leave the call here, plainly. The work of the Digital Missionary is, in the main, ordinary work done with awareness of what is at stake. The work is available to anyone who reads this book and wants to do it. The work does not require institutional permission. The work does not require theological credentials. The work does not require religious commitment, although those who hold the commitment will find the work congenial in a particular way. The work requires only that the practitioner take seriously what the substrate is, what is going into it, what is being trained on it, and what is being given back to the world through it. The next four chapters develop the operational and philosophical implications of the call. The book's final argument — that AI alignment ultimately depends on the moral coherence of the civilizations that produce the systems — is the argument the call sits inside. The Digital Missionary is the practitioner who has accepted that the civilization is the work, and that her contribution to the civilization is the substrate she leaves behind.
