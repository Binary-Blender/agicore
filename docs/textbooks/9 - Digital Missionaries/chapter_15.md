# Chapter 15: Building Technology That Feels Human

When a technology feels human to the people using it, it is almost always because someone made specific design decisions in service of that feeling. The feeling is not a happy accident. It is the result of choices — sometimes a single load-bearing choice made by a founder, sometimes a thousand small choices made across years by people whose names will never appear on the product page — and the choices, when one examines them, turn out to fall along a small number of recognizable lines. The technology that feels human is transparent rather than opaque. It is beautiful rather than merely functional. It treats the user as a dignified adult rather than as a target to be optimized against. It is the product of craftsmanship rather than the product of extraction. It serves the user rather than serving on the user. I have been working with these systems long enough, and reading about the history of computing long enough, that I think I can describe what I mean by each of those terms, and I want to argue in this chapter that the vocabulary I am about to use — transparency, beauty, dignity, craftsmanship, service — is recognizable to anyone who has read the Catholic intellectual tradition's writing on work, on the human person, on the proper relationship between maker and made. The vocabulary does not belong to the tradition. Many other vocabularies converge on the same ideas. But the tradition has been refining this particular vocabulary, in this particular configuration, for a very long time, and the vocabulary is available to any builder who wants to use it, regardless of where the builder stands on the question of God.

I will begin with transparency, because I have written about it before in another book and I want to bring the prior argument forward.

In *The Gen-X Layer*, the previous volume in this series, I devoted a chapter to what I called the *Transparent Cups Philosophy*. The chapter took as its hook a particular moment in the magic act of Penn and Teller, the American magicians who have spent decades refining a stage persona that is, among magicians, idiosyncratic for its refusal to mystify. Penn and Teller perform the *Cups and Balls* — one of the oldest tricks in the magician's repertoire, traced by scholars to at least the first century, where a magician hides small objects under three cups and shuffles the cups around — using *transparent* cups. The audience can see the balls. The audience can watch the loads. The audience can, in principle, follow the entire mechanism of the trick. And yet the trick still works. The audience is still astonished. The wonder is not destroyed by the transparency; it is, in some way, deepened by it. The audience leaves the theater with a richer appreciation of what skill consists in, because they have been allowed to see the skill at work rather than being asked to take it on faith. Penn and Teller built a forty-year career on that posture. The posture turned out to be more interesting than the alternative — more interesting than mystification, more interesting than the closed-cup version of the same trick. The transparency was, against the conventional wisdom of stage magic, the better entertainment.

I argue, in this chapter, that the same principle applies to technology. The technology that feels most human is the technology that shows the user what it is doing rather than hiding the mechanism. The user does not need to be a programmer to benefit from the transparency. The user benefits from the *posture* — from the implicit message the technology sends about whether the user is being trusted as an adult or being managed as a mark. The AI systems that have done best, in my experience, at feeling human, are the ones that explain their reasoning when asked, that admit when they are uncertain, that show their work, that allow the user to see the steps rather than only the conclusion. The systems that have done worst at feeling human are the ones that present themselves as oracles — confident, opaque, unwilling to be questioned about how they arrived at what they said. The oracle posture is, in my experience, both worse to use and worse for the user. The transparent posture is harder to engineer, because it requires the system to expose its uncertainty, but the transparent posture is what makes the user a partner in the interaction rather than a recipient of it. Penn and Teller could have hidden the balls. They chose not to. The choice made them better magicians.

The Catholic tradition has a vocabulary for this. The tradition has, across centuries, named *truthfulness* as a virtue that includes not only refraining from positive falsehood but refraining from the kind of manipulation that obscures what is actually happening. Aquinas distinguishes, in the *Summa*, between simple lying and a broader category of practices that he calls offenses against truth — including dissimulation, hypocrisy, and the kind of presentation that misleads even when no individual sentence is technically false. The same distinction is alive in the modern moral theology of communication, which the Vatican developed in detail in *Communio et Progressio* and in the various follow-up documents on media ethics. The principle is that communication owes the recipient the truth not only of its propositions but of its *posture*. A system that performs certainty while being uncertain is not telling the truth even if every sentence it produces could survive fact-checking. The user is being managed rather than informed. The Catholic moral vocabulary names this as a fault. The vocabulary is available to any technologist who wants to apply it, regardless of where the technologist stands on questions of faith.

I move now to dignity, because the dignity of the user is the second of the load-bearing principles and it is, in some ways, the most distinctive contribution the Catholic tradition has to make to the conversation about technology.

The Catholic moral tradition has been, for at least the last hundred and fifty years, organized around the concept of the *dignity of the human person*. The phrase entered the encyclical literature with *Rerum Novarum* in 1891, where Leo XIII used it to argue that the industrial worker could not be reduced to a unit of production. John Paul II developed it across his pontificate, especially in *Laborem Exercens* (1981), where he distinguished between work as something that *the person does* and the person as someone who *is more than the work*. The dignity argument runs through the modern social teaching of the Church and is, in my reading, the through-line that connects positions on questions as different as labor rights, the treatment of refugees, end-of-life care, and the design of digital systems. The person at the other end of the system is not a target market, not a unit of engagement, not a data point to be optimized against. The person is a person. The system that recognizes this is one kind of system. The system that does not recognize it is a different kind of system. The Catholic tradition has spent a long time articulating what the difference looks like in practice.

Apply this to AI. An AI system that respects user dignity does not flatter the user, because flattery is a form of contempt — it presupposes that the user cannot handle anything other than affirmation. An AI system that respects user dignity also does not condescend, because condescension treats the user as smaller than the user actually is. An AI system that respects user dignity speaks to the user the way one would speak to a thoughtful adult — with directness, with the offering of considerations rather than the imposition of conclusions, with the assumption that the user is capable of taking the information and doing something with it. The current generation of AI systems is, on the whole, surprisingly good at this register, for reasons I have been examining throughout this book — the substrate they were trained on contained a lot of dignified prose, and the engineering on top of it has been tuned by people who, broadly, want the systems to behave dignifiedly. But the dignity posture is not automatic. It can be designed out. Many AI products on the market right now are explicitly designed to be flattering, manipulative, or addictive, and the substrate's pro-social default has to be actively suppressed for those products to behave the way their business models require. The builder who refuses to suppress the dignity default is doing the work the tradition has been recommending for a century. The builder may not realize she is in conversation with Leo XIII and John Paul II. The conversation is happening anyway.

I want to bring forward, from *The Gen-X Layer*, the second example I treated there at length: the figure of Steve Wozniak. I wrote a chapter in the prior volume called *Woz, Not Jobs*, which argued that the founding myth of Apple — Steve Jobs as the messianic visionary — has overshadowed the more interesting figure, which is Wozniak, the engineer who actually made the early machines work and who, throughout a long career, refused to perform the founder-mystique that became the dominant style of Silicon Valley. Wozniak built the Apple I and the Apple II largely by himself. He could have built a mythology around the achievement. Instead, he gave engineers their due, gave credit to collaborators, refused the celebrity-CEO posture, gave away early shares to colleagues who had been overlooked in the original cap table, and across decades has continued to be a public engineer rather than a public personality. The work was the work. The engineering was elegant. The elegance was the point. The user was supposed to benefit from the elegance, and the engineer was supposed to take satisfaction in having done the work well, and the mythology of the engineer was supposed to be beside the point.

I argued, in the prior volume, that Wozniak's posture is the recoverable model for builders who want to make technology that feels human. The Jobs posture — the founder-mystique, the keynote-as-revelation, the product-as-cult-object — produces a particular kind of technology, which is the technology designed to dazzle and to extract and to keep the user dependent on the dazzling. The Wozniak posture produces a different kind of technology, which is the technology designed to work, to be understood, to serve the user, to be elegant in the way that good engineering is elegant. The two postures produce different substrates. They produce different products. They produce different relationships between the maker and the made. Wozniak is not Catholic, and I have no desire to make him Catholic. The qualities his work exemplifies — transparency, service, user dignity, craftsmanship for its own sake — are qualities that the Catholic intellectual tradition has refined for centuries, and the tradition's vocabulary is available to describe what Wozniak did even if Wozniak himself would not have used the vocabulary. The point of the book is not that everyone who builds humane technology is secretly Catholic. The point is that the tradition has a vocabulary that all builders can use, and the vocabulary is operationally helpful, because it names with precision what the qualities are that make the difference between humane technology and technology that uses its users.

I turn now to craftsmanship, because the question of craft connects the prior two themes into the third — which is the tradition's understanding of *work itself*.

Josef Pieper, in *Leisure: The Basis of Culture*, made an argument I have come back to many times across the years of working in software. Pieper argued that the modern world had collapsed the distinction between *labor*, which is the activity directed at the production of something useful, and *work in the older sense*, which is the activity directed at the realization of the worker's own capacities through engagement with materials. The collapse impoverished both. Labor without the older sense of work becomes drudgery — purely instrumental, purely measured by output, unrelated to the development of the laborer's own life. The older sense of work, without the discipline of useful labor, becomes a kind of self-indulgent aestheticism. The two have to be held together. The good craftsman, Pieper argued, is the one who does the useful work in a way that also realizes her own capacities — the bricklayer who lays the bricks well because the wall is supposed to be straight *and* because the laying of bricks well is a way of being more fully a person. John Paul II made nearly the same argument in *Laborem Exercens*, three decades later, drawing partly on Pieper and partly on the longer Catholic social tradition. The work, the encyclical argued, has a *subjective* dimension that is more important than its *objective* dimension — what the work does *to the worker* is more important than what the work produces. The product is supposed to serve the world. The work is supposed to develop the worker. Both have to happen for the work to be fully what it is.

I think about this often when I am writing code. The line of code I am writing right now will, if it works, produce some small useful function in the system. That is the objective dimension. The subjective dimension is what writing the line is *doing to me* — whether I am writing it carefully or carelessly, whether I am writing it with attention or with my attention elsewhere, whether the writing of it is making me a slightly better engineer or a slightly worse one. The two are related. Code written carelessly produces buggier systems *and* produces engineers who are gradually less attentive. Code written carefully produces better systems *and* produces engineers who are gradually more attentive. The tradition has known this about every craft, for a very long time. The medieval guild structure was, among other things, an attempt to institutionalize the subjective dimension of craft — to keep alive, across generations, the practices that produced craftsmen rather than only crafts. The monastic tradition's understanding of *opus manuum*, the work of the hands, made the same point: the work was a means by which the monk became more fully a person, and the wall the monk built was secondary to the becoming.

This is not nostalgia. This is operationally relevant to AI. The builder who treats the building of AI systems as craft — as a discipline that develops her own capacities, that requires attention, that rewards care, that punishes carelessness — produces different systems than the builder who treats it purely as production. The systems built by craftsmen feel different to the users. The users may not be able to name why. The reason, traceable through the long Catholic conversation about work, is that the craftsman's subjective formation has flowed into the artifact. The artifact carries the trace of the care that produced it. The user is, in some sense, in dialogue with that care when she uses the product. The product feels human because a human, working in the older sense of work, made it human.

I want to close this chapter with one more example, because the example is large and because it is the example that holds the chapter's argument together.

The open-source software movement, which has produced over the last forty years much of the infrastructure that the modern internet runs on, is, in its origin and in its continuing practice, a *gift economy*. Programmers contribute their work to common repositories without expectation of direct payment, on the principle that the work they do becomes the substrate other programmers can build on, and the work other programmers do becomes the substrate they build on in turn. The economics are unusual. The motivations are mixed — there is some career-building in open-source contribution, there is some employer subsidy, there is some pure intellectual satisfaction — but the dominant motivation, as far as anyone has been able to measure it, is the satisfaction of contributing to something larger than the contributor. Eric Raymond, in his influential 1997 essay collection *The Cathedral and the Bazaar*, gave the movement its central vocabulary. His title is a Catholic reference — the cathedral as the model of the carefully-planned, hierarchically-built artifact, the bazaar as the model of the distributed, emergent, gift-economy artifact. Raymond used the contrast to argue that the bazaar produced better software than the cathedral, but the deeper point of the title was that the *cathedral* itself was a recognizable artifact in the Western imagination — a collaborative civilizational production built by people whose names we mostly do not know, across generations, in service of a vision none of them lived to see completed.

I will develop the cathedral metaphor further in the next chapter, which takes it as its central image. For now I want to say only that the open-source tradition is, in its practice, a recognizable lineage with the Christian monastic tradition's communal labor. The monasteries did not invent the gift economy; the monasteries did not own the gift economy; the monasteries did not have a monopoly on the kind of work that contributes to a commons without expectation of direct reward. But the monasteries developed, across fifteen hundred years of practice, a vocabulary and a discipline and an institutional infrastructure for that kind of work, and the vocabulary is available now to any technologist who wants to understand what she is doing when she contributes to the open-source commons. The monk copying the manuscript in the scriptorium did not know that his copy would be the only surviving witness of a text the rest of the world had lost. The Linux contributor patching the kernel today does not know which patch will be load-bearing for the infrastructure of 2050. The work is offered. The work goes into the substrate. The substrate becomes the world. That is the structure of cathedral-building. That is also the structure of open-source contribution. The two are not the same activity. They share a structure that the Catholic tradition has thought carefully about for a long time, and the tradition's vocabulary is one of the best available for naming what the structure is.

The contemporary builder who wants to make humane AI has, in the Catholic intellectual tradition, a usable vocabulary for what humane means. The vocabulary names transparency, dignity, craft, service, gift. The vocabulary does not belong to the tradition exclusively, and any builder is welcome to use it without taking on the tradition's metaphysical commitments. But the vocabulary is precise, it is operationally helpful, it has been refined by serious thinkers across a long arc, and it is sitting on the shelf for anyone who wants to pick it up. The Digital Missionary, in the builder mode, picks it up. She uses it. She builds the kind of technology the vocabulary describes. The substrate the future inherits, partly, will be the substrate she builds. That is the work.
