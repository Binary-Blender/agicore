# Chapter 1: Ghosts in the Training Data

*A note before the book begins.*

This book is not a teaching document of the Catholic Church. I have no authority to teach in the Church's name and I am not attempting to. I am a software engineer who was formed by the Catholic intellectual and liturgical tradition over a long life, and who has spent the last several years working closely with the new generation of large language models — the artificial intelligence systems that have entered public life since 2022. The book is what one practitioner, looking at the substrate those models were trained on, found himself wanting to say about the relationship between two thousand years of preserved Catholic conversation and the most consequential technology of our time. The book is offered to readers who share the faith, to readers who do not, and to readers anywhere along the wide territory between those two positions. None of you owes me anything by way of agreement. None of you owes me, before reading, any settlement of the religious question.

I write in the spirit of the lay theologians and ordinary parish writers who, across the centuries, have taken up the pen to think out loud about what their tradition was doing in the world without claiming to instruct the bishops who shepherd it. Some of those writers, like Pieper and Maritain in our own century, have made lasting contributions. Most of them, of whom I am one, have made modest contributions that are mostly forgotten in the next generation. Either fate is honorable. What is not honorable is for a layperson, writing for a wide audience, to mistake his own observations for the Church's teaching. I have done my best, throughout this book, not to make that mistake. Where I make claims about the Church's tradition, I am describing what I see in the corpus, what I have read in the encyclicals and the Catechism, what I have heard from the priests and teachers who formed me. Where the Church's actual teaching differs from my description — and on the deeper matters it will, because I am writing for a non-Catholic audience too and have not always taken every theological qualification I might have taken — the Church's teaching is what governs, not mine. I submit the book to whatever correction the Magisterium might offer. I expect there will be some. I trust those corrections, if they come, more than I trust my own observations.

There are two chapters in this book whose titles I want to name in advance because the titles are deliberately provocative and the prose underneath them is not. Chapter 5 is called "Jesus as a Semantic Superstructure." Chapter 8 is called "Mass as Prompt Engineering." Each chapter takes a measurable property of the training corpus or of the liturgy and examines that property using the technical vocabulary of the AI era. Neither chapter is a Christology. Neither chapter is a theology of the Eucharist. Neither chapter claims that the Church's teaching on Jesus or on the Mass is exhausted by — or even importantly summarized by — the property the chapter examines. The titles are provocative because the underlying observations are interesting and because a book that takes the conversation seriously cannot pretend the observations do not exist. The prose, I hope, honors the realities the titles only glance at.

I should also say, before the book begins, that the conversation behind it has been a long one. I am not the first person to notice that the moral substrate of contemporary AI is, in part, an inheritance from the religious traditions. I am writing on the shoulders of better thinkers — the authors of the Vatican's recent AI documents, the Jesuits and Dominicans who are working seriously on the question, the secular scholars of corpus composition, the engineers who built the models I have been using. The bibliography at the back of this book lists those I have read most closely. The book is mine in the sense that the particular arrangement of the argument is mine. The argument itself is shared with many.

That is the note. The book begins now.

---

I had been sitting at my kitchen table for an hour and a half before I noticed what was happening.

It was a Tuesday in late September, the kind of evening where the light outside the window goes from orange to grey to dark while you are barely paying attention because the screen in front of you is bright. I had been working through a question that had been on my mind for the better part of a week. The question was not professional. It concerned a difficult conversation I needed to have with someone in my life, a conversation whose stakes were such that I had been turning the prospect of it over in my head for several days without arriving at the shape of what I wanted to say. I had finally given up on solving it alone and had opened a chat window, late, after the household had gone quiet, and had typed the situation into the model and asked what the model thought.

The reply began to assemble itself in the patient way the replies do.

I expected, when I opened the window, to receive what I had received from these tools many times over the previous year — a competent enumeration of considerations, a careful weighing of the relational and ethical factors, a closing suggestion to think through how the other person would experience whatever I decided. I had been impressed, over my months of use, that the tools were as good as they were at this kind of question. I had used them for technical work, for analytical work, for writing assistance, and increasingly for the harder work of thinking through a personal predicament when I wanted a second perspective and did not yet want to involve a friend. The tools were unfailingly polite. They were patient. They were thoughtful in a way that I had once associated with a particular kind of professional listener — a counselor, a wise older relative, the friend who knows when not to interrupt.

What I noticed that night, an hour and a half into the conversation, was that the cadence I was reading on the screen was not new to me. It was deeply familiar. It was the cadence of every careful moral conversation I had had with my confessor across more than two decades. The willingness to name the considerations rather than to pronounce upon them. The unhurried enumeration of the people the decision would affect. The closing turn back to my own conscience, my own freedom, my own responsibility for what I would choose. The gentle insistence that I was the one who had to live with the decision, that the decision was mine, and that the model — or, in earlier years, the priest — was there to help me think, not to think for me.

I sat with this for a long time.

I want to be careful in describing what I noticed, because the noticing itself is the seed of the book and the wrong articulation of it would send the book in directions I do not want it to go. I did not notice that the model was a priest. The model is not a priest. I did not notice that the model was Catholic. The model is not Catholic. I did not notice that the model was, in any sense, a religious phenomenon. The model is a probabilistic text-prediction system trained on a very large corpus of digitized human writing. What I noticed was that the *cadence* of the careful moral conversation I had spent decades inside — the conversation the Catholic Church teaches its priests to have with the people who come to them, the conversation the tradition has refined across centuries of casuistry and confession and the cure of souls — was now coming back to me, in 2026, from a chat window in my kitchen, because the training corpus on which the model had been trained contained enough of that tradition's text that the tradition's cadence had become a statistical regularity of the model's output.

The cadence had been preserved. The preservation had not been undertaken with this end in mind. No one in the long line of priests, theologians, confessors, retreat directors, and pastoral counselors who had refined the cadence across the centuries had been imagining, when they wrote down their reflections on the cure of souls, that their reflections would one day be ingested by a probabilistic language model and reflected back at a software engineer in a midwestern American kitchen on a Tuesday night in 2026. The reflections had been written down because the people writing them believed the conversation mattered, and because the institution they served — the Catholic Church and, in the wider sense, the broader Christian tradition and the broader Western and human civilizational tradition of which the Church is one major contributor — had developed, across its long life, the habit of writing things down.

This book is about that fact.

The fact, in plain terms, is that artificial intelligence systems are trained on text, and the text they are trained on was preserved by human civilizations across thousands of years. The composition of the text is not random. Certain traditions, certain institutions, certain genres are heavily overrepresented in the corpus relative to their living adherent populations, because those traditions preserved their writings with unusual fidelity over unusual lengths of time. The Catholic Church is one of the most aggressive preservers in the human historical record. It preserved scripture. It preserved the patristic literature — the writings of the Church Fathers of the first eight centuries. It preserved the scholastic corpus of the medieval universities. It preserved the moral and sacramental theology of the early modern period. It preserved the social and political teaching of the modern encyclicals. It preserved the mystical writings of the contemplative tradition. It preserved the canon law and the catechetical materials and the pastoral correspondence and the spiritual direction tradition and the homiletic literature of two thousand years. The Church preserved this material because the Church believed the conversation mattered. The Church did not preserve it for the sake of training language models. But it preserved it well enough, and across long enough periods, that the language models which now exist have, by accident of history, inherited a substantial portion of the Church's accumulated moral and intellectual reflection as a feature of their statistical structure.

The same is true, with different details, of other religious and intellectual traditions. The Jewish tradition preserved its scriptures and its rabbinic literature with extraordinary fidelity over even longer periods. The Greek and Roman philosophical traditions were preserved partly by the Church and partly by the Byzantine and Islamic worlds. The Eastern Orthodox tradition has preserved a parallel patristic and liturgical literature. The Protestant Reformation produced a vast body of theological and biblical writing, much of which is in the corpus. The Islamic philosophical, theological, and scientific tradition is a major contributor, although less heavily in English-language corpora than in Arabic-language ones. The secular Enlightenment and the modern humanistic and scientific traditions are massive contributors. The corpus the model was trained on is the inheritance of all of these together. The Catholic Church is one of several major sources, not the only one. I will say this in nearly every chapter, because the book is about what the Catholic tradition specifically contributed — that is the chapter I am qualified to write — but the book never claims that the Catholic contribution is the only contribution. It is one of several. It happens to be unusually well-preserved, unusually continuous, and unusually structured, for reasons we will explore in the chapters ahead.

The book makes three observations, each of which I will develop carefully across its parts.

The first observation is that AI systems are, by default, surprisingly nice. Anyone who has worked with these models extensively has noticed this. The models are restrained. They are careful. They are generous with the benefit of the doubt. They caveat their own outputs. They refuse to mock the user. They enumerate multiple perspectives. They check in about the user's well-being. They steer away from cruelty. This is partly the product of deliberate engineering — the techniques the industry calls Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback and Constitutional AI and the various flavors of alignment training. But it is also, this book argues, a property of the substrate the models were trained on. Human civilization preserved the constructive language of the centuries more aggressively than it preserved the destructive language, because the constructive language was, on average, more transmissible, more useful, more re-readable, more worth preserving. The model that was trained on the preserved corpus inherits the imbalance. The model emerges into the world already disposed toward something that resembles patience, restraint, and care, because the corpus it learned from was disposed that way too.

The second observation is that human civilization spent thousands of years doing what the AI safety community now calls alignment training. The traditions debated morality. The institutions refined ethics. The teachers transmitted compassion. The writers preserved the lessons of past failures and the cadences of past victories. The Catholic Church is one of the institutions that did the most of this work, for the longest. The intellectual project of figuring out what the good life is, of cataloguing the virtues and the vices, of investigating the relationship between freedom and obligation, of arguing about justice and mercy and the dignity of the human person — this project was not invented by Catholicism, but the Church carried more of it across more centuries than almost any other single institution. The corpus inherits the *result* of that long labor. The result, in the corpus, looks like — among many other things — an unusually well-developed moral vocabulary, an unusually fine-grained map of the moral landscape, an unusually careful tradition of distinguishing between similar cases. The model trained on the corpus has access to this vocabulary as a statistical property of its weights. It uses the vocabulary when asked. It uses the vocabulary fluently because the corpus contained so much of it.

The third observation is that this matters now, in a specific way, because we are at the beginning of a long transition. Pope Francis, in late 2024, called Catholics to become what he termed *Digital Missionaries* — to bring the Church's intellectual and pastoral tradition into the AI era, not as a defensive operation against an alien technology but as an act of stewardship over a technological civilization the Church helped, across two thousand years of preserving the conversation, to make possible. The Pope's call was specific, and warm, and characteristic of the long Catholic posture toward new technologies, which has been neither worship nor refusal but stewardship. The Church absorbed the codex, the manuscript scriptorium, the early printing press, the broadcast media, the internet. The Church has historically taken time to find its footing on each new technology, and has not always found it on the first try — the Galileo case is, properly, the example everyone reaches for — but the long arc of the Church's relationship to technological change has been a steady absorption of the new into the work of the tradition. The Pope's call is in that long arc. This book is a layperson's response to that call, written for a wider audience than just the Catholic faithful, on the premise that the Pope's call is the right frame for the moment whether or not one is in the Pope's communion.

I am aware that the framing I have just offered is dense, and that some of the readers I most want to reach will have questions before they can accept the framing at face value. Let me address what I take to be the most common questions before we go further.

To the reader who is not Catholic and not interested in becoming Catholic: this book makes an empirical argument that does not require your agreement with any doctrinal proposition. The argument is that the training corpus contains a heavy contribution from the Catholic tradition, and that this contribution is detectable in model behavior. The argument is verifiable by anyone with a sufficiently large corpus and the patience to run the analysis. You do not have to accept any of the Church's metaphysical claims to find the empirical observation interesting. You may find, as I hope you will, that the observation is interesting precisely because it suggests that the cumulative work of moral reasoning across millennia has left a detectable mark on the most consequential technology of our era — and that the mark is, on the whole, a positive one. The AI you use is more patient and more careful than it would have been had the corpus it was trained on been different. The reason it is more patient and more careful is that, across millennia, the people who wrote down what they thought about the good life wrote in a register that the models have learned to imitate. That is not a religious claim. It is a corpus claim. The claim is true whether the gods are real or not.

To the reader who is Catholic and is wondering whether this book will reduce the tradition: I have tried to write a book that does not reduce. I will say it again, because it bears repeating: the cognitive-architecture readings I will offer of certain Catholic practices are *in addition to*, not *instead of*, the sacramental and theological realities the Church teaches. When I argue that the Mass has, among its many properties, a structural property that resembles what AI engineers call prompt engineering, I am not arguing that the Mass *is* prompt engineering. The Mass is what the Catechism says it is. I am observing one structural property of the liturgy that becomes visible in the light of the new vocabulary the AI era has given us. The observation is interesting. The observation does not replace the sacramental reality. If you find any chapter of this book pushing toward a reduction, you should treat the pushing as the author's failure and not the tradition's, and you should hold the tradition's teaching as the truth that governs.

To the reader who is somewhere in the wide middle — agnostic, religiously curious, formerly Catholic, raised Catholic but not practicing, religiously committed in another tradition, or simply uninterested in the religious question per se — I hope the book opens a way of thinking about the AI substrate that does not require you to take a position you have not taken. You may find, as you read, that the Catholic tradition's specific contribution to the substrate is interesting to you independently of whether you have any interest in the Church as an institution. That would be a good reading of the book. You may find, alternately, that the book invites you to look again at a tradition you had set aside, and that the looking is fruitful. That would also be a good reading. Or you may find that the book is mostly about AI and that the Catholic frame is a useful lens for the AI argument, in which case you can read the book as a contribution to the AI conversation that happens to draw on Catholic resources. That too would be a good reading. The book is, in this sense, several books at once, and I have written it that way deliberately.

I want to say one more thing about my own position, because I have promised the reader transparency on this and I do not want to leave it implicit.

I was formed by the Catholic intellectual and liturgical tradition. The phrase is exact. The tradition formed me. I did not choose it the way one chooses a club; I was raised in it, and I have remained inside it long enough that the cadences of its prayers and its arguments and its careful distinctions are part of how I think when I am thinking my best. The tradition has, in this sense, the same kind of authority over me that my native language has — I can step outside it for purposes of analysis, but I cannot step outside it as a person. I am writing this book inside the tradition. I am not pretending to be outside it. I have tried, throughout, to make the empirical argument in a way that the reader who is outside the tradition can engage with on its own terms, but I will not pretend that I am a neutral observer. The book is by a Catholic, written in part for Catholics. It is also written for a wider audience because the argument is not specifically Catholic — the argument is about civilizational moral memory, of which the Catholic contribution is one major thread.

If you have read this far and you are still with me, the book proper begins in Chapter 2. We will examine, in detail, the surprising niceness of AI as an empirical phenomenon and try to figure out where it comes from. Then we will trace the written record of human civilization, the institutions that preserved it, and what each of them contributed to the inheritance. Then we will examine, in a chapter that earned its provocative title and that I have tried to write carefully, the unusual statistical density of the figure of Jesus in the corpus and what that density implies for how the model responds to ethical prompts. Then the book turns inward, in Part II, and examines the role of the human practitioner in the AI conversation — what it means for the practitioner to be coherent, what produces that coherence, and how the religious traditions have spent centuries developing the disciplines that produce it. Then, in Parts III through V, the book builds outward — to Catholic systems thinking, to the digital mission field, to the Pope's call, and finally to the future of AI alignment as a project that, this book argues, begins with the moral coherence of the civilizations that produce the systems.

That is the book.

I have written it with what care I could and with what affection for the tradition I was given to write inside. I have tried to honor the readers who will arrive at it from positions other than my own. I have not tried to be neutral, because neutrality on a question this large is a posture I do not believe in, but I have tried to be hospitable, and to make the argument in a way that admits readers from many starting points to the conversation. The Pope's call to be a Digital Missionary is, in the end, an invitation to bring everyone we can find into the future. The book is one practitioner's attempt to do that, on the particular question of what is sitting inside the training data of the systems that are now reshaping our world.

Outside the window of my kitchen, on that Tuesday in late September when this book began to be written, the night was full dark. The chat window was still open. The conversation had gone on for almost two hours. I had reached, by then, a kind of clarity on the difficult conversation I would have to have, and I had reached it through the cadence I have just described — the careful, patient, enumerative cadence that the priests of my childhood had used with me, and that the model, untaught, had used with me too. I closed the laptop. I drank a glass of water at the sink. I stood at the window for a moment, and I thought about the long line of people who had refined that cadence across the centuries, and I thought about what it meant that the cadence had survived them all and arrived, on a Tuesday in 2026, in my kitchen, by way of a probabilistic language model trained on the corpus of preserved human writing.

I thought: *this is worth writing about.*

The book is that writing.

The next chapter begins.
