# Chapter 7: After Hours

Dr. Petra Vance's report came back on Tuesday afternoon.

Diane picked it up at the Lincoln Elementary office at three forty PM.

She read it in the parking lot of Lincoln Elementary at three forty-six PM with the truck heater on because the late-October afternoon had turned cold.

The report was eleven pages.

The eleven pages described, in the careful neutral language Dr. Vance had been using on the Lincoln Elementary forms since 2011, that Eli scored at the seventy-third percentile on the verbal-reasoning subtest, the eighty-first percentile on the nonverbal-reasoning subtest, the ninety-fourth percentile on the spatial-pattern subtest, and at percentiles ranging from the twelfth to the twenty-eighth on each of three subtests measuring sustained attention to externally directed tasks under time pressure.

The eleven pages did not, on Diane's careful reading in the parking lot, contain the word *deficit* anywhere.

The eleven pages did, on the last page in a small paragraph titled *Observations*, contain a single sentence Dr. Vance had written in plain English: *Eli appears to be a child whose attention is internally directed at a high level of sophistication and whose cognitive resources are not, on the testing instruments currently available to the district, well measured by the time-pressured external-task paradigm that the testing instruments rely on.*

Diane read the sentence three times.

He walked out of the break room.

The first shift had cleared at four forty-five. The second shift was on the floor but the second-shift workers, on the operational reality of how second shift used the locker bay, came in through the south door, not the north door. The terminal was at the standby state.

He looked at it for about six seconds.

He reached out his right hand.

The screen responded.

The standby state slid away.

The screen showed the plant diagram on the left, the queue on the right, and at the bottom, a small text box.

The diagram was the same diagram from Frank's slide three.

The queue had, since five oh-three PM Tuesday evening when Bob had last walked past, grown by four items.

The total was twenty-seven items.

The bottom of the screen, in small grey text, read: *Welcome. Touch anywhere to begin. Touch and hold the queue items to read them in full. Touch and drag queue items onto the diagram to place them. The diagram is a map. The queue is a memory. The work is to put the memory on the map.*

Bob read the small grey text twice.

He read the third sentence three times.

The third sentence was: *The diagram is a map. The queue is a memory. The work is to put the memory on the map.*

He, on his honest internal accounting at five oh-six PM Wednesday afternoon in the corridor between the break room and the locker bay at Hartford Pump Co. in Lima, Ohio, recognized the third sentence as the same sentence he had been writing in his Mead Composition for twenty-seven years without ever having put it in those words.

The diagram was a map.

The queue was a memory.

The work was to put the memory on the map.

He touched the first item in the queue.

The terminal opened the item.

The item was the dock-door-latch item Lupe Hidalgo had added on the tenth at two seventeen AM.

The terminal said, in the same small grey text at the bottom of the screen: *Drag this to the part of the diagram where you noticed it.*

Bob put his finger on the item.

He dragged.

His finger moved the item across the screen.

He moved the item to the shipping bay on the east side of the diagram.

He let go.

The item landed.

The terminal placed a small green dot on the shipping bay.

The terminal said: *Saved. Lupe Hidalgo added this on 10/10. You placed it on the map on 10/29. Thank you.*

Bob, on his honest internal accounting, did not say anything for about four seconds.

He touched the second item.

The second item was Eugene Pell's small-pumps Line 1 casting cooling item.

He dragged it to the small-pumps Line 1 on the diagram.

The terminal placed a green dot.

The terminal said: *Saved.*

He dragged it to the paint bay on the diagram.

He let go.

The terminal placed a green dot.

The terminal said: *Saved.*

He paused.

The fourth item did not, on the standard layout of the diagram, have an obvious location. The forklifts moved around the plant. The forklifts did not, on the diagram, have a station.

He looked at the diagram for about eleven seconds.

He saw, at the bottom-right corner of the diagram, a small unlabeled rectangle.

He touched the rectangle.

The rectangle expanded into a sub-diagram of the support equipment — the forklifts, the air compressors, the wash bay, the cleaning equipment, the lighting circuits.

The sub-diagram was labeled *plant infrastructure.*

Bob dragged the Forklift 7 item to the forklift bay in the sub-diagram.

He let go.

The terminal placed a green dot.

The terminal said: *Saved.*

He continued.

He worked through items five through twenty-seven across the next thirty-one minutes.

He, in the thirty-one minutes, dragged twenty-three items.

Four of the items he could not place because he did not, on his own knowledge of the plant, know where they had happened.

Those four items he left in the queue.

By five thirty-eight PM, the diagram had twenty-three green dots scattered across it.

Bob looked at the diagram.

He looked at the green dots.

He looked at the green dots for about ninety seconds.

He, on his standing operational practice when looking at a pattern he had not yet recognized, did not say anything.

He pulled the Smith and Hawken out of his back pocket.

He clicked the pen.

He wrote, standing at the terminal:

> *5:39 PM. 23 dots on the map. Six dots at the north end. Four dots at the south end. Three dots on the small-pumps line. Three on the medium-pumps line. Two on the large-pumps line. Two on the paint bay. Two on shipping. One on the forklifts. Six dots cluster at the north end. The north end is the area with the wash-down station, the casting cleanup, and the small-pumps casting prep. Six dots is a lot of dots for one area. The six dots are not all the same problem. Some are about water. Some are about cooling. Some are about timing. But they are in the same neighborhood. The neighborhood is the thing.*

He looked at the screen.

He looked at the small text box at the bottom.

The small text box now read: *Touch the diagram to add your own observation in this area.*

Bob touched the north end of the diagram.

The terminal opened a small text-entry window.

The window said: *What did you notice in this area?*

Bob looked at the keyboard at the bottom of the screen.

He, on his standard practice with keyboards on his phone, had typed maybe a few hundred sentences in his life.

He had never, on the operational reality of working in a plant where the maintenance shop had a logbook with a pen on a string, typed at a workstation as a routine part of his job.

He looked at the keyboard.

He, on his honest internal accounting at five forty-one PM Wednesday afternoon, decided that the typing was going to be slow but the typing was going to be the typing.

He started to type.

He typed with the index finger of his right hand.

He typed, in the standard slow way of a man who had spent twenty-five years carrying a Mead Composition and a Bic pen instead of a phone:

*I noticed that 6 of the items in the queue are in this area. The items are not all the same problem. But the items are all in the same neighborhood. The neighborhood has the wash-down station. The wash-down station runs Monday at 4 AM by the night cleaning crew. I think the wash-down is putting water on machines or in places that the wash-down was not supposed to put water. I do not know yet which machines or which places. I would like to look. I am going to look this Monday morning at 4 AM if Frank says it is ok.*

He read what he had typed.

He, on the standard practice he used when reading his own writing in the notebook, did not edit it.

He hit the small button at the bottom that said *Save.*

The terminal said: *Saved. Your observation has been added to the queue. Would you like to link this observation to other observations?*

Bob touched *Yes.*

The terminal displayed the queue items he had placed in the north end of the diagram.

The items were the six items.

Bob looked at the six items.

He selected four of them — the four that, on his read, involved cooling or water or timing on a Monday morning.

He hit the button that said *Link these four to my new observation.*

The terminal said: *Linked. Would you like to see the pattern view?*

Bob touched *Yes.*

The screen changed.

The screen showed, on a timeline, the four items he had linked plus his own. The four items he had linked, on the date and time stamps, all happened on Mondays. The four items happened, in the four cases, at four eleven AM, four eighteen AM, four oh-three AM, and four twenty-six AM.

The four items happened in the window when the night cleaning crew was washing down the plant.

The four items happened in the window when the wash water was running.

The four items happened in the place where the wash water was running.

Bob, on his honest internal accounting at five forty-seven PM Wednesday afternoon, looked at the timeline.

He, on the same internal accounting, recognized that the timeline was the answer.

The answer was the answer.

The wash water was the answer.

The wash water was running into places that, on the design of the plant in 1981, the wash water was not supposed to run into. The wash water was, on the operational reality of forty-four years of slowly settling concrete and slowly degrading floor drains and slowly accumulating cleaning chemicals, finding its way into bearings, into cooling lines, into the back of the regulator on the paint bay primary cabinet, and into the casting cleanup tank where the small-pumps castings were getting cooled inconsistently on Monday mornings because the wash water was changing the temperature of the cleanup tank.

The bearing on Line Two had not been killed by Line Two.

The bearing on Line Two had been killed by the wash-down on Monday morning.

The wash water was running into the Geneva-wheel housing.

The wash water had been running into the Geneva-wheel housing for, on the standard duty cycle of the night cleaning crew, twenty-six Monday mornings since the bearing had been installed.

Twenty-six Monday mornings was about six months.

Six months and three days was about the time the bearing had run.

Bob, on his honest internal accounting, did the arithmetic in his head.

Twenty-six Monday mornings.

Six months and three days.

The bearing had not been a six-months-and-three-days bearing.

The bearing had been a twenty-six-Mondays bearing.

He pulled the Smith and Hawken back open.

He wrote, standing at the terminal at five fifty-one PM Wednesday afternoon:

> *5:51 PM. The wash water on Monday is killing bearings, drifting regulator setpoints, changing cooling tank temps, and probably leaking hydraulic seals on Forklift 7 which lives in the back corner where the wash drains. The wash water has been doing this for years. Nobody has caught it because each machine has its own maintenance guy and each maintenance guy has only seen his own machine's symptom. The terminal is the thing that lets the symptoms talk to each other. I have been pulling Timken bearings out of Line 2 every six months for two years. I never knew why. I know now. I am going to fix it. I am going to fix it by talking to the night cleaning crew. I am going to fix the drains. I am going to install splash guards. The cost is going to be small. The savings are going to be enormous. Frank needs to see this. I am going to print this screen if I can figure out how. I am going to leave it on his desk.*

The first observation was about the way the dust collector on the fabrication shop's plasma cutter had been cycling faster on humid days, which he believed indicated the filter was loading prematurely because of moisture.

The second observation was about the way the air compressor on the south side of the plant lost about three psi between Tuesday and Friday every week, which he believed indicated a slow leak in a quick-disconnect somewhere on the line.

The third observation was about the way the fluorescent lights on Line Four flickered for about twelve seconds every time the indexing motor on Line Four cycled, which he believed indicated a starting-current draw issue on the motor that was going to, in the next year or two, mean it was time to look at the motor's bearings or the contactor.

The fourth observation was about the way one specific palletizing fork on the medium-pumps line had developed a small wobble that he had measured at about a sixteenth of an inch and that he believed was going to be three-eighths of an inch by next March and would, at three-eighths of an inch, start dropping pumps.

The fifth observation was about the way Carlos Mendez's voice on the line-lead radio sounded different on Mondays after the wash-down than on the other four days of the week, which he believed indicated something about the wash-down that he had not yet been able to articulate but that he wanted to write down anyway so that, at some point, he could come back to it.
