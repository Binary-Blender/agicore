# Chapter 4: Eli's Report

Lincoln Elementary was on West Spring Street, on the west side of Lima, about a fifteen-minute drive from Bellevue Avenue at three forty PM on a Wednesday. The building had been built in 1962 and added onto in 1989 and re-roofed in 2014. The parking lot had thirty-six spaces. The flagpole at the front entrance had been donated by the Class of 1973.

Bob parked the Chevy in space number twenty-two at three forty-six PM.

He turned the engine off.

He did not, immediately, get out.

Diane did not, immediately, get out.

Eli, in the back seat, did not say anything.

Bob looked at the back seat in the rearview mirror.

Eli was looking out the back-passenger-side window at the playground.

The playground was empty.

The kids had all gone home at three fifteen.

Eli had been picked up at three fifteen by Diane in the front passenger seat of the Chevy and then driven home — for fourteen minutes — and then driven back, the three of them now, for the four PM meeting with Mrs. Marlene Hoffman.

Eli had not asked, in either of the drives, why they were going back.

Bob and Diane had not, in either of the drives, told him.

Diane had said, at the kitchen counter at three thirty-six PM while putting on the second of her two earrings: "Eli. We are going to meet with Mrs. Hoffman at four. About school. Then we will come home and have spaghetti."

Eli had said: "Am I in trouble."

Diane had said: "No."

Eli had said: "Are you in trouble."

Diane had said: "No."

Eli had said: "Is the meeting good or bad."

Diane had looked at Bob.

Bob had looked at Diane.

Diane had said: "Eli. We don't know yet. We will know after the meeting. Spaghetti is at six."

Eli had said: "OK."

Now, in the back seat at three forty-six PM, Eli was looking at the empty playground.

Bob said: "Eli."

Eli said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "Buddy."

Eli said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "Whatever Mrs. Hoffman says in this meeting, the meeting is not about who you are. The meeting is about school. Those are not the same thing. You are a good kid. You are my kid. That is true on the drive in and it is going to be true on the drive home. Do you understand."

Eli looked at him in the rearview mirror.

Eli said: "Dad."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Eli said: "OK."

Bob said: "OK."

Diane reached back between the seats with her good hand and squeezed Eli's knee.

Eli put his hand on top of his mother's hand.

They sat in the car for another forty seconds.

Then Bob got out and opened Diane's door and helped her out and the three of them walked into Lincoln Elementary.

Diane said: "Eli, you can sit in the library. We will come and get you when we are done."

Eli said: "How long."

Diane said: "Thirty minutes. Probably less."

Eli said: "OK."

Eli walked across the lobby and into the library.

He sat down on a small green plastic chair near the picture-book shelves.

He pulled the small spiral notebook out of his backpack.

He pulled the Bic pen out of the inside pocket of the backpack.

He opened the notebook.

He started writing.

Bob, walking out of the lobby toward Room 12, did not see Eli start writing.

Diane did.

Diane did not, in the walk to Room 12, mention to Bob that Eli had started writing.

She had grey hair cut short and small wire-rimmed glasses and a cardigan in the green-grey color of a winter pond. She had the face of a woman who had loved her job for twenty-eight years and who was, on this particular Wednesday, doing the part of her job that she did not love.

She said: "Mr. and Mrs. Pawlak. Thank you for coming. Please sit down. Are you both comfortable in these chairs. I can get a different chair for you, Mrs. Pawlak, if you would prefer."

He pets the class hamster the way the class hamster wants to be petted, which is not the way most of the third-grade boys pet the class hamster. He is, in every way I can describe to you, a good kid."

She paused.

She said: "I want you to hear that part first, because the rest of what I am going to share with you is not the part about who Eli is. It is the part about how Eli is doing in third grade."

Diane said: "Mrs. Hoffman."

Mrs. Hoffman said: "Diane."

Diane said: "Thank you. Tell us."

Mrs. Hoffman pulled three papers out of the folder.

The second paper was a math worksheet Eli had taken on September 24th.

The third paper was Eli's writing sample from the previous Friday.

Bob picked up the reading-comprehension assessment.

The assessment had Eli's name at the top.

Below the name, in red pen, was a circled number.

The circled number was the eighteenth percentile.

Below the circled number, in red pen, were the words: *below grade-level expectations.*

Bob looked at the test itself.

The test had been a passage about a girl named Anna who had found a kitten.

Twelve questions had followed the passage.

Eli had answered three of the twelve questions.

Two of the three had been correct.

Nine of the twelve had been left blank.

Bob set the paper down.

He picked up the math worksheet.

The math worksheet had been twenty single-digit multiplication problems.

Eli had answered seven of them.

Six of the seven had been correct.

Thirteen of the twenty had been left blank.

On the back of the worksheet, Eli had drawn, in pencil, a small diagram.

The diagram showed a stick figure with arrows pointing at its left hand and its right hand. The left hand had a circle around it. The right hand had a smaller circle around it. Below the diagram, in Eli's eight-year-old printing, was a single line.

The line read: *junie is leftpawed. otis is rightpawed. me?*

Bob looked at the line.

He read it twice.

He set the math worksheet down.

He did not, on his own standing operational practice in conversations with school authority figures, say anything yet.

He picked up the writing sample.

The writing sample had been a prompt to write three sentences about a favorite memory.

Eli had written four sentences.

The sentences read: *My favorite memory is when my dad showed me how the engine works. The car was the Chevy. It is in the driveway. My dad does not get mad when I ask why.*

He set the writing sample down on the table.

He looked at Mrs. Hoffman.

Mrs. Hoffman said: "Yes."

Mrs. Hoffman said: "All right, Mr. Pawlak. Thank you for telling me that."

Bob said: "Bob."

Mrs. Hoffman said: "Bob."

Diane said: "Mrs. Hoffman."

Mrs. Hoffman said: "Marlene."

Diane said: "Marlene. Tell us what you would like us to do."

Marlene said: "I would like, with your permission, to refer Eli to the school psychologist for an evaluation. The evaluation is non-binding. It is a screening. It is not a diagnosis. The evaluation looks at attention, processing, executive function, and whether there are any learning differences that might explain why Eli's work product is so different from the work product I would expect from a child who, in conversation with me one on one, is as bright as I find Eli to be. The evaluation takes about three hours, spread across two sessions, conducted in a small room by a kind woman named Dr. Petra Vance, who has been the district psychologist for fourteen years and who I personally trust. The evaluation produces a written report. The report is shared with you. The report is shared with me. The report is not shared with anyone else without your written permission. The report does not go in his permanent record unless you request that it does. The report's purpose is to help us understand what might be going on so that we can help Eli succeed in school."

Marlene paused.

She said: "Diane. Bob. I want to be very clear. I am not telling you Eli has a problem. I am telling you Eli is not, on the paper measures I have, performing the way I believe he could perform. The gap between what Eli does on a worksheet and what Eli does when I sit across from him at this table and ask him a question is the largest gap I have seen in twenty-eight years of teaching third grade. The gap is not small. The gap is enormous. I do not know what is causing it. The evaluation is the tool I have for finding out. I am asking you for permission to use the tool."

The room was quiet for about eight seconds.

Diane looked at Bob.

Bob looked at Diane.

Diane looked back at Marlene.

Diane said: "Marlene."

Marlene said: "Yes."

Diane said: "Can I ask you a question before I answer."

Marlene said: "Please."

Diane said: "On the math worksheet. The drawing on the back. The line about June being left-pawed and Otis being right-pawed."

Marlene said: "Yes."

Diane said: "What did you think when you saw it."

Marlene was quiet for a beat.

Marlene said: "Diane, I have been thinking about that drawing for nine days. I will tell you what I thought when I first saw it. What I first thought was that Eli was not paying attention to the worksheet. That was my first thought. That was, I will tell you, my honest first thought, and I am embarrassed by it. I am embarrassed by it because it took me four more days of looking at that drawing before I had my second thought. My second thought was that Eli was paying very close attention to something. The something was not the worksheet. The something was the question of which hand his five-year-old sister favors and which paw the family dog favors and where his own answer to that question fits. That is a real question. That is a real observation. That is a real thought from a real eight-year-old boy. The boy who drew that diagram is not, on my second thought, a boy who is failing to pay attention. The boy who drew that diagram is a boy who is paying attention to a question I did not ask him."

Marlene looked at Diane.

She said: "Diane. I have been a third-grade teacher for twenty-eight years. The Department of Education does not have a worksheet for the kind of attention the boy who drew that diagram is paying. The kind of attention the boy is paying is the kind of attention I do not have a tool for measuring. The evaluation I am requesting may give us a tool for measuring it. The evaluation may not. I do not know. I am asking you for permission to use the tool because the tool is the only tool I have. If you do not want me to use the tool I will respect that. If you want me to use the tool I will use it carefully. I am not, on the standard practice I have used for twenty-eight years, going to tell you what to do. I am telling you what I know. You will tell me what to do. I will do what you tell me to do."

Diane was quiet for a longer beat.

Diane said: "Marlene."

Marlene said: "Yes."

Diane said: "I want to think about it. Can we tell you by Monday."

Marlene said: "Monday is fine."

Diane said: "Marlene."

Marlene said: "Yes."

Diane said: "Thank you for the second thought."

Marlene said: "Diane."

Diane said: "Yes."

Marlene said: "Eli's drawing reminded me, when I had the second thought, of a student I taught in 1999 who is now a structural engineer in Cincinnati and who used to draw diagrams on the backs of his worksheets exactly the way Eli does. I did not, in 1999, have the second thought about that student. I had only the first thought. He passed third grade with a C in reading and a C in math and a note in his permanent file that said *does not stay on task.* I have, since 2015, had the second thought about him. He came back to visit the school four years ago. He told me he is a structural engineer. He told me he loved third grade. He told me I was his favorite teacher. He did not, on the conversation I had with him then, tell me about the note in his file. He did not need to. I had reread the note in his file the week before he visited. I had reread the file the week before he visited because, on the operational reality of having been a teacher long enough to want to look backward, I had been rereading old files. I had been rereading old files because I had been having second thoughts. I am telling you about that boy because Eli reminds me of him. I am not telling you Eli is him. I am telling you that I have, since 2015, been trying to have the second thought sooner."
