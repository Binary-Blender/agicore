# Chapter 8: Frank

The footsteps came up the corridor at six forty-four PM.

Bob heard them at about thirty feet out.

He did not, on his standing operational practice in a corridor at a plant he had worked at for fourteen years, turn around.

He stood at the terminal.

He, on the same standing practice, knew the footsteps were Frank's by about twenty feet out, because the Red Wing 8167 boots Frank wore had a heel cleat that hit the concrete with a particular dry click that Bob had been hearing on the catwalk and in the corridor for the past seven years.

The footsteps stopped about six feet behind him.

The footsteps did not, in the next eight seconds, say anything.

Bob stood at the terminal.

He looked at the screen.

He did not, on his standing operational practice in moments when an authority figure had walked up behind him and not yet said anything, fill the silence.

Frank, after the eight seconds, said: "Bobby."

Bob said, without turning around: "Frank."

Frank said: "How long you been at this."

Bob looked at his watch.

He said: "One hour, forty-one minutes."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "Turn around."

Bob turned around.

Frank Reyes was standing in the corridor about six feet from the terminal. He was holding two cups of coffee from the kitchenette pot. One in each hand. The cups were the same kind of paper cups the kitchenette stocked. The cups were steaming. The steam, in the cool air of the corridor, was visible.

Frank said: "I brought you a coffee."

Bob said: "Frank."

Frank said: "It's not good."

Bob said: "It's hot."

Frank said: "That's the deal."

Bob said: "That's the deal."

Frank walked the six feet.

He handed Bob a cup.

Bob took the cup.

He held it.

Frank looked at the screen.

He, on his standing operational practice when looking at a thing he had been waiting to look at for nine months, did not say anything for about twenty seconds.

Bob, on his standing operational practice in the same moment, did not say anything either.

The two men stood at the terminal.

The screen showed the diagram with thirty-one green dots and a small linked-pattern view down the right side that the terminal had brought up to show the four observations Bob had linked at five forty-six PM.

Frank read the linked-pattern view.

He read it twice.

He, on his honest internal accounting at six forty-six PM Wednesday afternoon, did the second thing he had not done in front of any other person in eleven years of being plant manager.

He smiled.

Not by himself this time.

Bob saw it.

Bob said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Am I in trouble."

Frank looked at him.

Frank said: "Bobby. Do I look like a man who is about to put you in trouble."

Bob said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "I have known you for fourteen years and I have never seen the look on your face that is on your face right now. I do not know what the look is. I have nothing to read it against."

Frank said: "Bobby. The look on my face is relief. The look on my face has been waiting to be on my face for nine months. The look on my face is, on my own honest internal accounting, the same look that was on your face a few minutes ago when the terminal told you the plant thanked you."

Bob said: "You saw that."

Frank said: "I saw that."

Bob said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "Were you watching."

Frank said: "Bobby. I have been watching you for nine months wait to walk up to this thing. I have been watching you for seven years before that watch the plant the way I have been watching it. I have been watching you, on my own honest accounting, the way a man watches a river he has been told there is a big fish in. The fish does not come out every day. The fish comes out the day the fish decides. The fish came out tonight, Bobby. I am not going to scare the fish."

Bob looked at him.

Bob said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "How did you know I was going to come down here tonight."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "Diane called me at three forty-five PM."

Bob, on his honest internal accounting at six forty-eight PM Wednesday afternoon at the terminal in the corridor at Hartford Pump Co., did not say anything for about four seconds.

Bob said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "Diane."

Frank said: "Diane."

Bob said: "She called you."

Frank said: "She called me. She has the office number. She wrote it on a piece of paper at the picnic Hartford did for the families in 2017 when she gave it to me and I gave her my home number. We exchanged numbers because she came up to me at the picnic and asked me how you were doing at the plant. I told her you were doing fine. She told me she figured. She told me she wanted my number anyway. She told me she would only ever use the number if the call was important. I told her I would only ever pick up if I thought the call was important. She has called me twice in eight years. Today was the second time."

Bob said: "What did she say."

Frank said: "She said *Frank. Bobby is coming back to the plant this evening to spend some time with the terminal in the corridor. Leave him alone for an hour. Then come down. He is going to be ready for whatever conversation you have been wanting to have with him since 2018, but he is going to be scared. Don't be scared with him. Be ready.*"

Bob said: "She said that."

Frank said: "She said that."

Bob said: "What did you say."

Frank said: "I said *I will be ready.*"

Bob looked at the cup of coffee in his hand.

He did not drink it.

He, on his honest internal accounting at six forty-nine PM Wednesday afternoon, recognized that the conversation he was in was the conversation Diane had set up for him without having told him.

He, on the same internal accounting, recognized that Diane setting it up without telling him was, on her own quiet practice of knowing what he could handle and when, the kind of grace he had been receiving from her for the eleven years of their marriage and had not yet, at age thirty-six, fully learned to thank her for.

He said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "I have to tell Diane *thank you* tonight."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "Tell her *thank you* every night. That is the deal."

Bob said: "That is the deal."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "Walk me through what's on the screen."

Bob walked Frank through what was on the screen.

He started with the first item he had placed at five oh-six PM — Lupe's dock-door-latch item in shipping. He worked through each of the twenty-three items he had placed. He told Frank where on the diagram each item was, and why. He told Frank which four items he had linked at five forty-six PM and what the linked-pattern view had told him about Monday at four AM. He told Frank about the wash-down hypothesis. He told Frank about the bearing on Line Two being a twenty-six-Monday bearing instead of a six-months-and-three-days bearing. He told Frank about the splash guards and the drain fixes and the small cost and the big savings. He told Frank about the printout sitting in Frank's office.

He told Frank about his own six observations.

He told Frank about the plasma cutter and the air compressor and the Line Four fluorescent flicker and the palletizing fork wobble and the change in Carlos's voice on Mondays.

The walking-through took eighteen minutes.

Frank did not, in the eighteen minutes, interrupt.

He sipped his coffee.

He watched the screen.

He, on his standing operational practice when listening to a worker walk him through a piece of work that mattered, did not, in the eighteen minutes, write anything down.

He, in the eighteen minutes, did one thing Bob would not, until two days later, understand the significance of.

He nodded.

He nodded thirty-one times.

He nodded once at each of the thirty-one green dots Bob had placed on the screen.

Bob, on his standing operational practice when being listened to by Frank Reyes, noticed the nods. He counted them. He counted them because the counting was, on the operational reality of his own brain, what his brain did when he had nothing else to do with the part of his attention that was watching Frank. He counted to thirty-one.

He, in his own honest internal accounting, registered that the count was thirty-one, and that thirty-one was the number of green dots on the screen, and that Frank had nodded once at each dot.

He did not, in the same internal accounting, know what that meant.

He finished the walk-through at seven oh-six PM.

He looked at Frank.

He said: "That's what I did, Frank."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "How long have you been seeing the wash-down pattern."

Bob said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "Tonight is the first time I have ever seen it."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "You have been seeing the pieces of it for fourteen years."

Bob said: "Yes."

Frank said: "The terminal put the pieces together."

Bob said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "I put the pieces together. The terminal gave me the table to put them on."

Frank, on his honest internal accounting at seven oh-seven PM Wednesday afternoon, said: "Bobby. That is exactly what I wanted to hear."

The two men stood at the terminal.

Bob did not say anything for about fifteen seconds.

Frank did not say anything either.

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "I am going to ask you something tomorrow morning. I am not going to ask it tonight. I am going to ask it tomorrow morning because, on my own honest accounting, the question is bigger than the time we have at seven oh-eight PM on a Wednesday evening in front of a terminal in a corridor. The question wants a maintenance shop in the morning with the second pot of coffee on the burner and the daylight coming in the east window."

Bob said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "OK."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "I would like, before that conversation tomorrow morning, for you to think about something tonight."

Bob said: "Tell me."

Frank said: "If you could do any kind of work at this plant for the next twenty years — any kind, any title, any pay grade, the question is not about money — what would the work be."

Bob looked at him.

He said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "I don't know yet."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "I am not asking for an answer tonight. I am asking you to spend the drive home thinking about it. You have an eleven-minute drive. Spend the eleven minutes. Tell me at seven AM tomorrow what you came up with."

Bob said: "OK."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "Go home."

Bob said: "I'm going home."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "Tell Diane *thank you* for me."

Bob said: "I will."

Frank said: "Tell her again from yourself."

Bob said: "I will."

Frank stepped back from the terminal.

He took a sip of his coffee.

He said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "The fish came out."

Bob said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "I have been the fish."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "Go home."

Bob walked away from the terminal.

He walked down the corridor.

He walked past the locker bay.

He clocked out for the second time at seven twelve PM — the time stamp printed across the second card he had punched that day, with a small note added by the time-clock software that read *late departure logged.*

He walked to the truck.

The truck was the truck.

The Chevy was the Chevy.

He started the engine.

The engine did not hesitate.

He drove out of the employee parking lot at seven fourteen PM Wednesday evening.

He turned right on Hartford Drive, left on Elida Road, and east on Bellevue.

He drove the eleven minutes.

He thought, on the eleven minutes, about Frank's question.

He thought about it the way he thought about most things — without trying to land on an answer.

He let the question sit in the front of his head and looked at it the way he would look at an unfamiliar engine from outside the hood.

He looked at it across all eleven minutes.

He did not, by the time he pulled into the driveway of 1418 Bellevue Avenue at seven twenty-five PM, have an answer.

He, on his honest internal accounting, was not yet supposed to have one.

He had until seven AM Thursday morning.

That was the deal.

He parked the Chevy.

He killed the engine.

He sat in the driver's seat for about thirty seconds.

He took the Smith and Hawken out of his back pocket.

He wrote, in the driveway, in the small careful blocky printing, with the trouble light still hanging from the steering linkage where Eli had put it on the last Tuesday of September:

> *7:25 PM. Frank just asked me what work I would want to do at this plant if I could do any work. I do not have an answer. I have until seven AM. I am going inside. Diane called Frank at 3:45 PM and set this whole thing up. Diane is the smartest person I have ever met. I have known this since 2013. I forget it most days. I am not going to forget it tonight.*

He closed the notebook.

He clipped the pen.

He got out of the truck.

He walked up the porch.

Diane was in the kitchen.

The kitchen light was on.

Eli was at the kitchen table with the spiral notebook open in front of him.

June was on the floor in the living room with Owl and Otis.

Bob walked into the kitchen.

He set his lunch pail on the counter.

He walked to Diane.

He put his arms around her.

He, on his standing operational practice in moments where he had nothing to say but a lot to mean, did not say anything for about fourteen seconds.

He, on the same standing practice, did not move.

Diane did not move either.

She, on the same standing practice, said nothing.

Diane said: "Yeah."

Diane said: "Bob."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Diane said: "You are welcome."

She said when I was a little older I would understand. I asked her how much older. She said two or three years. I said OK. I noticed that my mom was happy today.
