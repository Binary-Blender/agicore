# Chapter 6: The Jam

Line Two jammed at ten eleven AM on the Tuesday two weeks after the all-hands.

Bob heard it from the maintenance shop.

The maintenance shop was about a hundred and twenty feet from Line Two. It was a smaller distance than the paint bay had been to Line Four. The smaller distance was, on his standing accounting of why he could hear what he could hear, not what mattered. What mattered was that he had been sitting at the workbench with his back to the shop door reading the second-shift logbook when he heard the sound.

The sound was a grind.

The grind lasted about a second and a half.

It stopped.

It came back about eight seconds later.

It lasted about a second and a half again.

It stopped.

Bob set the logbook down.

He stood up.

He, on his standing operational practice, did not run.

He walked.

He covered the hundred and twenty feet in about thirty-eight seconds.

He used the thirty-eight seconds to consider, in order:

The first possibility was the indexing motor.

The second possibility was a bearing in the indexing wheel at one of the four stations.

The third possibility was the rotary union on station three.

He had, by the time he reached Line Two, settled on the second.

Line Two was a medium-pumps line — centrifugal pumps in the four-to-twelve-inch impeller range, the bread and butter of Hartford's order book, the workhorses that went to municipal water plants from Dayton to Duluth. The line had four stations. The stations indexed every forty-one seconds. The indexing was driven by a four-horsepower three-phase Baldor motor in a small cabinet at the head of the line. The motor turned a Geneva wheel. The Geneva wheel indexed the four-station table forty-one seconds at a time.

The bearings in the Geneva wheel were the bearings in the Geneva wheel.

Bob walked up to Line Two.

The line lead was a man named Dale Wisniewski who had been at Hartford for seventeen years and who was, on Bob's standing assessment, the most patient line lead in the plant.

Dale said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Dale."

Dale said: "You heard it."

Bob said: "I heard it."

Dale said: "I'm getting a grind every other index."

Bob said: "I heard it from the shop."

Dale said: "What is it."

Bob said: "I think it's the Geneva. I want to listen."

Dale said: "Listen."

Dale nodded at one of the line workers, a young woman named Renata Marrero who had been at Hartford for two years and who was on the casting-load station, and Renata raised the half-finished pump back off the fixture.

The line indexed once.

Bob put his hand flat on the cabinet of the indexing motor.

He felt the index.

He listened.

The grind happened on the index.

He moved his hand to the Geneva-wheel housing.

He felt the next index.

He listened to the next index.

The grind happened on the index.

The grind was in the Geneva-wheel housing.

He said: "Dale."

Dale said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "Shut her down. I'm going to pull the cover."

Dale said: "Shut her down."

Dale walked to the panel.

The line stopped.

Bob pulled the cover.

He looked into the housing.

He, on his own quiet operational practice at ten thirteen AM Tuesday morning at the Geneva-wheel housing of Line Two, looked at a bearing that was, on the inspection-tag he himself had stuck to the housing six months earlier, six months and three days old.

The bearing was failing.

The bearing was a Timken sealed deep-groove ball bearing. The bearing should have run for two years on the duty cycle Line Two was on. It had run for six months and three days.

Bob looked at it.

He did not say anything for about four seconds.

Dale said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Dale."

Dale said: "What."

Bob said: "This bearing is six months old."

Dale said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "It's not supposed to be."

Dale said: "I know."

Bob said: "Dale."

Dale said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "I'm going to swap it. Twenty minutes. But I want to talk to you about it after lunch."

Dale said: "Bobby. You think it's the bearing or you think it's something else."

Bob said: "I think the bearing is the bearing. I think the something else is whatever is killing the bearing."

Dale said: "OK."

Dale said: "After lunch."

Bob walked to the maintenance shop.

He pulled a new Timken deep-groove ball bearing out of the second bin from the left on the second shelf.

He walked back to Line Two.

He pulled the failing bearing.

He pressed the new bearing in.

He buttoned the housing.

He put the cover back on.

Dale started the line.

The line indexed.

The grind was not there.

Dale waited.

The line indexed twice more.

The grind was still not there.

Dale nodded.

Dale said, into his radio: "Line Two up. Back in five."

The line workers walked to the break room.

Bob walked toward the maintenance shop.

The shop was past the locker bay.

The locker bay was past the corridor with the terminal.

The corridor was the corridor.

He walked the corridor.

He passed the terminal.

He, on his own quiet operational practice that had been forming since the Thursday two weeks earlier, slowed down.

He stopped.

He turned to face the screen.

---

The screen was not at the standby state.

The screen showed the plant diagram with — on the right side, where Frank's slide-four screenshot had shown the queue — fourteen items.

Each of the fourteen items had a small chevron next to it.

The chevrons were the soft little marks that the terminal used to indicate fresh notes.

Bob, on his standing operational practice when looking at any information surface for the first time, read the items in order from the top.

The first item read: *Shipping dock door 3 latch is loose. Truckers have been slamming it. The hinge is gonna go. — anonymous, 10/10 2:17 AM.*

The second item read: *Small-pumps Line 1 casting cooling time inconsistent on Mondays. I think the night cleaner cools the line down with the wash water. — Eugene Pell, 10/15 8:43 AM.*

The third item read: *Paint bay primary cab regulator setpoint drifts about 4 psi over a shift. Have to bump it twice. — anonymous, 10/16 1:11 PM.*

The fourth item read: *Forklift 7 has a leak somewhere on the hydraulic side. I top it off Monday. By Friday it's down again. — Marcus King, 10/16 4:50 PM.*

The fifth through fourteenth items were like the first four.

The fifth through fourteenth items were observations.

The observations were the observations.

Bob, on his own honest internal accounting at ten thirty-one AM Tuesday morning of the third week of October at the terminal in the corridor between the break room and the locker bay, read the fourteen items twice.

He read them a third time.

He recognized, on the second reading, that the fourteen items were the kind of items he would have written in the Mead Composition and copied into the Smith and Hawken if he had been the one observing them.

He recognized, on the third reading, that the fourteen items were the kind of items his father Ray Pawlak would have written down in the small spiral notebook Ray had carried in the front pocket of his coveralls between 1986 and 2007.

He recognized, on the third reading, the kind of attention the fourteen items were the product of.

The kind of attention was the kind of attention Eli had described in his own spiral notebook at Lincoln Elementary at three forty-eight PM Wednesday afternoon at the small green plastic chair in the library.

The kind of attention was the kind of attention Eli's writing sample had described in the second sentence: *The car was the Chevy.*

The kind of attention was the kind of attention Cody had had on his face in Donny's garage on the Saturday afternoon of the timing on the Pontiac.

The kind of attention was the kind of attention Bob had had on his own face on the driveway in 1998 on the Saturday afternoon when Ray had handed him his first Mead Composition and said, in Ray's standard way of saying difficult things: *Bobby. Write down what you noticed. Not what you did. The world will tell you what you did. Only you can tell you what you noticed.*

The kind of attention was the thing.

The thing was on the screen.

The thing was on the screen, on the right side, in a queue of fourteen items, with small chevrons next to each one because they were all fresh, all added in the previous twelve days, all from people who had — on the operational reality of having walked up to the terminal in the corridor between the break room and the locker bay and touched it — decided to write down what they had noticed.

Bob stood in front of the terminal.

He stood in front of it for about four minutes.

He, in the four minutes, did not touch the screen.

His right hand was at his side.

He, on his honest internal accounting, knew that his right hand was at his side.

He, on the same honest internal accounting, knew that he was not, this morning, going to lift his right hand.

He was not yet ready.

He was not ready because, on the operational reality of where his attention was, half of his attention was at the school psychologist's office at Lincoln Elementary where Dr. Petra Vance was, this morning, conducting the second of two evaluation sessions on Eli, and the other half of his attention was at the Geneva-wheel bearing he had just pulled off Line Two that should have run for two years and had run for six months and three days.

The two halves of his attention were not, on his own honest accounting, available to be moved to the terminal this morning.

The terminal was the terminal.

The screen was the screen.

The fourteen items were the fourteen items.

The chevrons were the chevrons.

He, on his own quiet operational practice, did the only thing he was ready to do.

He pulled the Smith and Hawken out of his back pocket.

He clicked the pen.

He wrote, standing in the corridor at the terminal, the longest single entry he had written in either of the two notebooks since starting the Smith and Hawken on the Wednesday after the Lovejoy spider:

> *10/22. Queue has 14 items. All in last 12 days. Items are observations. Items are written by people on the floor. Items read like my notebook reads. Items read like Dad's notebook used to read in the front pocket of the coveralls. The terminal is the notebook. The terminal is the notebook for the plant. I knew this. I am writing it down now because I want to be sure I knew it before I touched the screen. The bearing on Line Two was six months old. That is not a bearing problem. That is something killing the bearing. The something is in the queue on the right side of the screen, somewhere, in one of the 14 items, or in items 15 through 47 which somebody else has not yet added but will. I am not going to touch the terminal today. I am going to wait. Eli's evaluation is today. The evaluation is going to come back next week. The bearing is going to wait a few days. The terminal is going to wait a few days. The waiting is the waiting. I will know when I am ready.*

He closed the notebook.

He clipped the pen.

He put the notebook back in his pocket.

He turned away from the terminal.

He walked to the maintenance shop.

He sat at the workbench.

He picked the second-shift logbook back up.

He read the rest of it.

He worked through the rest of his shift.

---

He clocked out at five oh-three PM.

He drove home.

The Chevy did not hesitate.

He arrived at 1418 Bellevue Avenue at five fifteen PM.

Diane was at the kitchen counter.

The evaluation report was not back yet — Dr. Vance had said it would take three to five business days — but the second evaluation session had been at Lincoln from one to two thirty PM that afternoon, and Eli had come home with Diane at three.

Diane was in the kitchen with her good hand wrapped around a coffee mug that had been refilled twice since three thirty.

Eli was on the floor of the living room with Otis.

Eli was not, on his standard practice when home from school, watching anything on the television.

Eli was, on the family-room rug, lying on his stomach with the small spiral notebook open in front of him and the Bic pen in his right hand and a small drawing he was working on in pencil that occupied most of the open page.

The drawing was a diagram.

The diagram showed, in eight-year-old printing and rough pencil lines, a circle with the word *me* in it, an arrow pointing from the circle to a square with the word *worksheet* in it, a second arrow pointing from the circle to a different square with the word *notebook* in it, and below the diagram, the line:

> *the worksheet square is smaller than the notebook square.*

Bob, on his standing operational practice when walking into the living room and finding his son lying on the floor working on a diagram, did not say anything for about six seconds.

He set his lunch pail on the floor next to the door.

He walked over to the couch.

He sat down.

Otis came over and put his head on Bob's boot.

Eli looked up.

Eli said: "Dad."

Bob said: "Eli."

Eli said: "Dr. Vance asked me a lot of questions today."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Eli said: "She was nice."

Bob said: "I'm glad."

Eli said: "She asked me what I think about when I am working on a worksheet."

Bob said: "What did you tell her."

Eli said: "I told her I think about what I would write in the notebook later."

Bob said: "What did she say to that."

Eli said: "She said *that's an interesting answer.*"

Bob said: "She is right."

Eli said: "Dad."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Eli said: "Is what I told her going to get me in trouble."

Bob said: "Eli."

Eli said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "You are not in trouble. You are never going to be in trouble for telling Dr. Vance the truth. You are not in trouble for telling Mrs. Hoffman the truth. You are not in trouble for telling Mom and me the truth. The truth is the truth. The truth is the part you write down. The truth is the part the notebook is for."

Eli said: "OK."

He went back to the drawing.

Bob watched him for about forty seconds.

Diane walked in from the kitchen with two glasses of iced tea.

She handed one to Bob.

She sat on the couch next to him.

She put her head on his shoulder.

She said: "Bob."

He said: "Diane."

She said: "How was the plant."

He said: "Line Two jammed."

She said: "You fixed it."

He said: "I fixed it."

She said: "What else."

He said: "Diane."

She said: "Yeah."

He said: "I stood in front of the terminal."

She lifted her head.

She looked at him.

She said: "Bob."

He said: "Yeah."

She said: "Did you touch it."

He said: "No."

She said: "Why not."

He said: "Diane. I wasn't ready."

She said: "Were you scared."

He said: "I was. But that isn't why I didn't touch it. I didn't touch it because half of me was at the plant and half of me was at Lincoln Elementary. The half that was at Lincoln was the half I needed to touch the terminal. I am going to wait until both halves are at the plant."

She said: "Bob."

He said: "Yeah."

She said: "That is exactly the right reason."

He said: "I know it is."

She said: "When are you going to touch it."

He said: "I don't know yet."

She said: "Bob."

He said: "Yeah."

She said: "I am not worried."

He said: "I am, a little."

She said: "I know you are."

She put her head back on his shoulder.

The two of them sat on the couch in the living room with Eli on the rug and Otis on the floor and June, who had been at her friend Mia's house since school, expected back in twenty minutes.

The afternoon went into evening.

The Tuesday went into Wednesday.

Bob did not, in the rest of the week, touch the terminal.

He, on his standing operational practice in the corridor between the break room and the locker bay, walked past the terminal twice a day, every day, Wednesday and Thursday and Friday.

He, in the four walk-pasts of those three days, read the queue.

The queue grew.

By the close of Friday afternoon at five oh-eight PM, the queue had twenty-three items.

The twenty-three items were the twenty-three items.

Bob, in the Smith and Hawken, on the Friday evening, wrote one line.

The line was:

> *10/25. Queue is at 23. Eli's eval comes back Monday or Tuesday. Both halves of my attention are going to be at the same place by next Wednesday. Wednesday is the day. I will know.*

He underlined the last sentence.

He closed the notebook.

He clipped the pen.

He went to bed.

The Wednesday was the Wednesday.

The Wednesday was the day.

He would know.

He did not yet know what.

He would know what on Wednesday.
