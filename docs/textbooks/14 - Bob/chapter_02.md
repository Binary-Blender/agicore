# Chapter 2: Line Four

Bob heard Line Four from the paint bay.

The paint bay was at the far north end of the plant. Line Four was at the south end. The two ends of the plant were separated by about three hundred and eighty feet of poured concrete floor, two rows of overhead fluorescent lights that had been replaced with LEDs in 2019, a long run of forced-air ducting, the assembly area for the small-pumps line, the long run of conveyor for the medium-pumps line which was Line Three, and the run of conveyor and finishing stations for the large-pumps line which was Line Four.

Bob was at the paint bay on a Monday morning at nine forty-two AM in late September because the air feed to the secondary cabinet had been dropping out at random intervals all weekend, and the second-shift maintenance guy, a younger man named Travis Boudreau, had left a note in the shop logbook on Sunday night that read, in Travis's handwriting: *Bob — air to paint bay secondary cab is dying every half hour or so. Not the compressor. Not the line. Not the regulator. Drives me crazy. Yours.*

Bob had read the note at six fifty-five AM.

He had been at the paint bay since seven oh-eight AM.

He had not yet found the problem.

He had narrowed it down to either a check valve in the manifold or a small piece of debris that had gotten past the inline filter and was migrating around the manifold and intermittently seating itself across the inlet to the secondary cabinet's regulator. He suspected the debris. He had not yet caught it.

He had the manifold half-disassembled on a piece of cardboard on the floor of the paint bay when he heard Line Four.

He stopped.

He listened.

He listened for about four seconds.

He set the wrench he was holding on top of the cardboard.

He stood up.

He walked out of the paint bay and across the three hundred and eighty feet of poured concrete floor toward the south end of the plant, around the small-pumps line, past Line Three, and up to Line Four.

He did not, in the walk, run.

Bob had not, in the fourteen years he had been at Hartford Pump Co., ever been seen to run on the plant floor. The reason he had not run was not the standing OSHA-compliance reason. The reason was that, in his fourteen years, he had not once gotten to a problem any faster by running. The walk was the walk. The walk was a thinking pace. The walk was the pace that, by the time Bob got to the problem, had given Bob between forty-five seconds and a minute to think about what the problem probably was.

By the time Bob reached Line Four at nine forty-three AM and twenty-eight seconds, the walk had given him fifty-eight seconds.

He had used the fifty-eight seconds to consider three possibilities.

The first possibility was the conveyor motor.

The second possibility was a bearing in the indexing wheel at station six.

The third possibility was the elastomer coupling between the conveyor motor and the drive shaft.

He had, by the time he reached Line Four, settled on the third.

He walked past the line lead, a man named Carlos Mendez who had worked at Hartford for nineteen years and was, by Bob's quiet operational accounting, one of the two or three best line leads in the plant.

Carlos saw Bob coming.

Carlos said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Carlos."

Carlos said: "You hear it from the paint bay."

Bob said: "I heard it from the paint bay."

Carlos said: "I been listening to it for ten minutes and I cannot tell what it is."

Bob said: "Spider."

Carlos said: "Spider."

Bob said: "Spider on the Lovejoy. The slap is the slap of the rubber when the conveyor changes load. It's chewed up. We get maybe an hour, maybe two. Then it shears."

Carlos thought about this for about two seconds.

Carlos said: "Shut her down."

Bob said: "Shut her down."

Carlos walked to the panel and hit the line stop.

The conveyor on Line Four slowed and came to rest with a small sigh of compressed air from the brake.

The line workers — six men and two women on this shift — looked up.

Carlos held up a hand.

Carlos said, loud enough to carry to the end of the line: "Coupling change on the main drive. Twenty minutes. Take fifteen."

The line workers walked to the break room.

Bob walked to the maintenance shop and pulled a new Lovejoy L-095 coupling and a new spider out of the bin he himself had ordered restocked in March on his own quiet operational assessment that the original installed spiders, which had been put in at the plant's last major overhaul in 2019, were going to start failing across Year Eleven on the operational duty cycle Line Four had been running since the order book picked up in 2023.

The bin had been restocked with twelve spiders in March.

The spider Bob pulled out of the bin at nine forty-five AM was the eighth one.

Bob walked back to Line Four.

He removed the guard.

He pulled the drive-shaft side of the Lovejoy off the keyway.

He looked at the spider.

The spider was, on his honest inspection, two days away from shearing.

He held the spider up to Carlos.

Carlos looked at it.

Carlos said: "You called it."

Bob said: "I called it."

Carlos said: "How."

Bob said: "The slap."

Carlos said: "I been listening to that line for nineteen years, Bobby. I never heard that slap."

Bob said: "You hear it. You just don't write it down."

Carlos said: "What."

Bob said: "Nothing."

He pulled the chewed-up spider out.

He set it on the floor next to his boot.

He pushed the new spider into place.

He slid the drive-shaft side of the Lovejoy back onto the keyway.

He torqued the setscrew.

He put the guard back on.

He stood up.

He looked at his watch.

The line had been down for twenty-one minutes.

Carlos walked to the panel.

He hit the line start.

The conveyor moved.

The motor's sound was the motor's sound.

The slap was not there.

Carlos listened for thirty seconds.

He nodded.

He said, into the small handheld radio clipped to the chest pocket of his coveralls: "Line Four up. Back in five."

The line workers walked back from the break room.

Carlos turned to Bob.

He said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Carlos."

Carlos said: "What was that you said about writing it down."

Bob said: "Nothing. Forget it."

Carlos said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Carlos said: "I been on this line for nineteen years. You been on it for fourteen. I hear it run every day. You hear it run when you walk by. You called the spider from the paint bay. I missed it standing six feet from it."

Bob said: "You had a radio in your ear and a parts shortage on the medium line and a guy out on the small line and you've been on shift for two hours and forty minutes. I had a manifold in pieces in front of me and nothing else to listen to. The line is loud when you stand next to it. The line is quieter from a hundred yards. You can hear the wrong note better from far away."

Carlos thought about this.

He said: "That's a fair answer."

Bob said: "That's the answer."

Carlos said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Carlos said: "I want you to teach me what you said about writing it down."

Bob looked at him.

He said: "Carlos."

Carlos said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "I learned it from my dad."

Carlos said: "Teach me what your dad taught you."

Bob said: "It's a notebook."

Carlos said: "A notebook."

Bob said: "Mead Composition. Ninety-nine cents at the gas station. You carry it. You write what you noticed. Not what you did. What you noticed. The slap. The funny smell. The way the bearing felt when you pulled it off the shaft. Stuff you'd forget if you didn't write it down."

Carlos said: "Just that."

Bob said: "Just that."

Carlos said: "And you read it back."

Bob said: "And I read it back. Once a week. Sunday night."

Carlos said: "Sunday night."

Bob said: "Sunday night."

Carlos said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Carlos said: "I am going to start a notebook tonight."

Bob said: "All right."

Carlos walked back to the panel.

Bob walked back to the maintenance shop.

He passed under the glass office on the catwalk.

The glass office was on the catwalk on the east wall of the plant, accessible by a short flight of metal stairs, and it had been Frank Reyes's office since 2018. The office had three walls of glass that looked down on the plant floor.

Bob did not, on his standard practice when walking under the glass office, look up.

He did not see Frank looking down at him.

Frank had been looking down at him for approximately the last seven minutes.

Frank Reyes was at his desk in the glass office with the line-monitoring dashboard up on his right monitor and his email up on his left monitor and a half-empty cup of coffee that he had poured at eight twelve AM and that was now, at nine fifty-one AM, room temperature.

The line-monitoring dashboard had registered Line Four going down at nine forty-two AM and forty-one seconds, and back up at ten oh-three AM and seventeen seconds.

Twenty-one minutes.

The dashboard had not, at any point in the twenty-one minutes, fired an unscheduled-stoppage alert, because Carlos had logged the stoppage as a *planned preventive replacement — primary drive coupling* on the line lead's interface before Bob had even reached the panel.

Frank had not, until two seconds after Carlos hit the line stop, looked away from his email.

He had looked away from his email at the sound of the conveyor sigh.

He had stood up.

He had walked to the glass.

He had watched Bob walk to the maintenance shop and walk back to Line Four with the spider in his hand.

He had watched Bob change the spider.

He had watched Carlos talk to Bob after the line came back up.

He had not been able to hear the conversation through the glass and the line noise.

He had not needed to.

He had been watching Bob change machine parts for, on his own quiet count, the past seven years of being plant manager, and on the four years before that of being assistant plant manager. He had been watching Bob change machine parts for, in cumulative total, approximately one hundred and forty distinct incidents that the plant's official line-monitoring system had logged as *unscheduled preventive replacement* and that, on Frank's own private accounting kept in a small leather notebook in the top right drawer of his desk, had been, in operational reality, Bob walking over to a line and catching something before it broke.

Frank had been keeping the leather notebook since 2018.

The leather notebook was, on the standard format Frank had developed for it, organized by employee.

The section in the notebook for Bob Pawlak ran from page eleven to page forty-six.

Page forty-seven was blank, with the heading *Pawlak (cont'd)* written at the top in Frank's neat printing.

Frank walked back to his desk.

He sat down.

He opened the top right drawer.

He took out the leather notebook.

He turned to page forty-six.

The bottom of page forty-six had two lines of writing on it from the previous Friday.

The two lines read: *9/19. Pawlak caught the dryer baffle warp on the small-pumps line before it cracked. Walked past it on his way to lunch. Walked back to the dryer with a putty knife. Tapped on the baffle three times in three spots. Heard it. Logged it as PR. Lunch was twelve minutes late.*

Frank read the two lines.

He turned to page forty-seven.

Page forty-seven, at the top, had the heading *Pawlak (cont'd)* and then the blank space below it.

Frank picked up his pen.

He wrote, in the standard format he had used for one hundred and forty entries across seven years:

> *9/29. Pawlak heard Line 4 Lovejoy spider from the paint bay. Three hundred and eighty feet. Hearing or arithmetic — I don't know which. He himself told Carlos it was hearing. I am not sure he is right. I think there is something else going on. I have been telling myself for two years there is something else going on. I am going to figure out what it is.*

He read what he had written.

He read it twice.

He closed the leather notebook.

He put it back in the top right drawer.

He picked up the room-temperature coffee.

He did not, on his own standard practice with morning coffee that had gone room-temperature, drink it.

He carried it down the stairs of the glass office and across the plant floor to the small kitchenette next to the maintenance shop, where he poured it into the sink and rinsed the cup and refilled it from the pot that the morning-shift guys had brewed at seven AM and that was now, on the operational reality of how the kitchenette coffee worked, the kind of coffee that you drank because it was hot and not because it was good.

He stood at the kitchenette holding the cup.

Bob came out of the maintenance shop.

Bob saw Frank.

Bob said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Coffee."

Frank said: "Coffee."

Bob said: "It's not good."

Frank said: "It's hot."

Bob said: "That's the deal."

Frank said: "That's the deal."

The two men stood at the kitchenette for about ten seconds without saying anything.

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "Line Four."

Bob said: "Spider on the Lovejoy."

Frank said: "From the paint bay."

Bob said: "From the paint bay."

Frank said: "Three hundred and eighty feet."

Bob shrugged.

He said: "Quiet end of the plant. Carry on the air."

Frank said: "Three hundred and eighty feet of air."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "Did you write it down."

Bob looked at him.

He said: "Wrote it down on the way back."

Frank said: "What did you write."

Bob said: "Slap on the L-095. Three-eighty out. Carry on the morning air. Eighth spider out of the March bin."

Frank said: "That's it."

Bob said: "That's it."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "Carlos asked you about the notebook."

Bob said: "He asked me."

Frank said: "What did you tell him."

Bob said: "I told him to start one."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "I have been keeping a notebook on you for seven years."

Bob looked at him.

He, on his own honest internal accounting, did not say anything for about four seconds.

He said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "What kind of notebook."

Frank said: "The kind your dad would have kept on his own crew if he had ever had a crew."

Bob was quiet for another four seconds.

He said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "Why."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "I am going to tell you in a couple of weeks. I have been telling myself it was the kind of thing I would tell you when I figured out the thing I was trying to figure out. I have not yet figured it out. I am going to tell you when I figure it out. Until then I am going to ask you one thing."

Bob said: "Ask."

Frank said: "Next time you write something in your own notebook about something on this plant floor, I would like, if you are willing, for you to copy that line into a second notebook I am going to give you on Wednesday. You keep your notebook. I keep mine. The second notebook is the one that's the same as both. You write in it twice. Once for you. Once for me. Is that something you would do."

Bob looked at him.

He said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "You want me to keep a notebook for you."

Frank said: "I want you to keep a notebook with me. The notebook is for both of us. The notebook is going to do something I have not yet figured out, but I have, on my own twenty years at this plant, learned to stop arguing with the gut. The gut said keep a notebook on Pawlak seven years ago. I kept the notebook. The notebook has been right for seven years. The notebook now says the next step is to give Pawlak his own copy of the notebook. The notebook is, on my own honest internal accounting, the smartest thing I have done as a plant manager. I am going to keep listening to it. Are you in."

Bob held his coffee.

He did not drink it.

He looked at Frank.

He said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "What's the cover look like."

Frank said: "Black. Stitched. Smith and Hawken. They are eleven dollars at the bookstore on West Market. I have been buying them since 2018. They hold up. The pages don't tear out. The binding doesn't crack. They take a Bic pen the way a Mead Composition takes a Bic pen, which is to say, fine. I would have bought you a Mead because I know that's what you use, but the second notebook is not the Mead. The second notebook is the Smith and Hawken. The point of the second notebook is that it is a different book. Different book is so that, when you open it, you know what kind of writing you are doing in it."

Bob thought about this.

He said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "Yeah. I'm in."

Frank said: "Good."

He set his coffee down on the kitchenette counter.

He said: "Wednesday. I'll have it on my desk."

Bob said: "Wednesday."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "Thank you."

Bob said: "Don't thank me yet. I haven't written in it yet."

Frank said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "You have been writing in it for fourteen years. Wednesday is just the day the cover changes."

Frank picked his coffee back up.

He walked back across the plant floor and up the stairs to the glass office.

Bob stood at the kitchenette holding his coffee.

He looked at the kitchenette window.

The kitchenette window looked out on the small employee parking lot.

The small employee parking lot held forty-one cars and two pickups, of which one of the pickups was the 1992 Chevy Silverado that Bob had driven in this morning with the new throttle position sensor he had installed on Wednesday — yesterday — afternoon, that he had driven to work this morning, and that had not, on the eleven-minute drive from Bellevue Avenue to Hartford Pump Co., hesitated once.

The TPS had been the TPS.

The notebook had been the notebook.

The slap on Line Four had been the slap on Line Four.

Frank Reyes had a black stitched Smith and Hawken notebook in his office with thirty-five pages of writing about Bob in it.

Bob had not, in the fourteen years he had worked at the plant, known.

He, on his own quiet operational accounting standing at the kitchenette window at ten oh-six AM on the last Monday of September, did not know what to make of the fact that Frank had known.

He drank his coffee.

The coffee was not good.

The coffee was hot.

The coffee was the deal.

He set the cup in the sink.

He walked back to the paint bay.

He sat back down on the cardboard.

He picked up the wrench.

He looked at the half-disassembled manifold.

He, on his standard practice when working a problem, did not, in the next forty seconds, do anything with his hands.

He looked at the manifold.

He listened to the secondary cabinet's regulator hiss faintly.

He listened to it hiss for about eleven seconds.

He heard, in the eleventh second, a very small irregular click.

He picked up the small flashlight from his belt.

He shone it into the manifold.

He saw, at the bottom of the second port, a small piece of black rubber about the size of a grain of rice.

The rubber was sitting on the inlet seat of the secondary regulator.

It was, on the irregular pulse of the supply air, lifting and reseating.

It was the debris.

It had not been a check valve.

He picked it out with a small pair of needle-nose pliers.

He held the piece of rubber up to the trouble light.

He held it in his palm.

He, on his own quiet operational accounting at ten oh-eight AM on the last Monday of September of Year Eleven, did the thing his father had taught him.

He pulled the notebook out of his back pocket.

He wrote, in his small careful blocky printing:

> *9/29. Paint bay secondary cab air drop = small piece of black rubber, rice-grain size, on seat of secondary regulator inlet, port 2. Migrated from somewhere upstream. Inline filter must have a tear. Pull and inspect filter element tomorrow. Travis was right not to think it was the regulator.*

He read what he had written.

He read it twice.

He nodded.

He put the manifold back together.

He turned the supply air back on.

The secondary cabinet held pressure.

The paint bay was the paint bay again.

He walked the cardboard out to the dumpster.

He clocked out for lunch at eleven fifty-eight AM.

He drove home for lunch on his standard half-hour Tuesday-and-Thursday and-occasionally-Monday lunch break.

He had a peanut butter sandwich on the front porch with Diane and Otis.

He did not, at lunch, tell Diane about Frank's notebook.

He, on his own quiet operational practice, did not yet know what to tell her.

He would, on Wednesday at five fifteen PM, tell her on the porch with a glass of iced tea.

For now, the peanut butter sandwich was the peanut butter sandwich.

The notebook was the notebook.

The plant was the plant.

Frank Reyes had been watching for seven years.

Bob had not, until ten oh-three AM on the last Monday of September, known he had been watched.

He drove back to the plant at twelve thirty-one PM.

He worked his shift.

He drove home at five oh-three PM.

He had a second peanut butter sandwich because the chili was the chili from last night and Diane was tired and June had a craft project and Eli had not yet come home from a friend's house on Highland Avenue.

He sat on the porch.

He read the notebook back.

He, on his own honest assessment of the last entry, recognized that the last entry — the rice-grain piece of rubber on the seat of the secondary regulator — was the kind of entry he had been writing for fourteen years and that Frank Reyes had been, on a parallel track, reading for the last seven.

The two tracks were now, on Frank's request at the kitchenette at ten oh-six AM, going to merge.

The merging was the merging.

The Wednesday was the Wednesday.

The cover of the second notebook was going to be black and stitched.

The first thing Bob was going to write in the second notebook on Wednesday afternoon at three forty-seven PM was going to be the rice-grain piece of rubber.

He did not yet know that.

He did not yet know what the second notebook was for.

He did not yet know that Frank Reyes had, in the seven years of keeping the leather notebook on him, been quietly preparing for a moment Frank could not yet have named.

He did not yet know that the moment was, on the standard operational arc of the next nine weeks, going to happen on a Thursday at seven fourteen PM in front of a terminal on the second-floor corridor of the plant.

For now the porch was the porch.

The peanut butter sandwich was the peanut butter sandwich.

The Mead Composition notebook was open on his knee.

Otis lay on the porch slab with his chin on Bob's boot.

Diane was in the kitchen.

June was in the living room.

Eli was on Highland Avenue.

The September evening was the September evening.

Bob closed the notebook.

He clipped the pen back to the cover.

He put the notebook back in the back pocket of his jeans.

He sat on the porch.

He, on his standard operational practice, did not say anything.

He listened to the cul-de-sac.

The cul-de-sac was quiet.

The cul-de-sac was Lima.

Lima was Ohio.

Ohio was Year Eleven.

The plant was the plant.

The notebook was the notebook.

The day was the day.
