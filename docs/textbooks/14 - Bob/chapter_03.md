# Chapter 3: The Cookout

Donny Pawlak's house was on a half-acre lot on the south side of Bath Township, off Slabtown Road, about a fifteen-minute drive from Bellevue Avenue. The house was a two-story vinyl-sided 1990s build with an attached garage, a fenced backyard, a small above-ground pool, and a deck Donny had built himself in 2015 that, on the family's collective informal accounting, sloped to the southeast by approximately three-quarters of an inch over its sixteen-foot run.

The slope was the slope.

The deck still held.

The cookout was on a Saturday in early October at three PM.

Bob and Diane and Eli and June arrived at three oh-seven PM. Bob carried a half-gallon Tupperware of Diane's potato salad. Diane carried nothing because Diane's hands at three oh-seven PM on a Saturday in early October were the hands Diane's hands were at three oh-seven PM on a Saturday in early October. Eli carried a small zippered backpack containing, on Bob's quiet read of the bulges, three Hot Wheels, a small spiral notebook he had bought with Bob at the Walgreens on Spring Street on Wednesday after school, a Bic pen, and a baggie of Goldfish crackers. June carried a stuffed owl named Owl.

Donny was at the grill.

Donny's wife Karen was on the deck.

Donny and Karen's son Cody was, on the family's standing operational location at any cookout since Cody had turned thirteen, somewhere not present.

Donny and Karen's daughter Brooke, age six, was in the above-ground pool in spite of it being sixty-two degrees outside.

The cookout had been called for *all the Pawlaks plus the Walerczyks plus whoever else.* There were, on Bob's count walking up the driveway, three Pawlak families (his, Donny's, and Donny's older brother Mike's), one Walerczyk family (Karen's parents), and one couple in a corner of the deck Bob did not immediately recognize and who turned out to be Karen's friend from work and her boyfriend.

The boyfriend was a man in his early forties named Pete who, on the standard practice of Pete at family events he had been brought to twice, had a beer in his hand and was talking.

Pete was talking to Donny's older brother Mike about AI.

Bob, on his standard practice at family events, walked the potato salad to the kitchen and put it in the refrigerator and walked back out to the deck and shook Donny's hand at the grill and accepted a beer he was going to drink half of and walked to a corner of the deck and sat down in the folding chair next to Diane, who had taken the only chair on the deck with a cushion, because Karen had put the cushioned chair out for Diane specifically.

Karen had also, Bob noticed, put a small footstool under the cushioned chair.

Karen had not, on her own standard discretion, said anything about the footstool.

Bob nodded to Karen across the deck.

Karen nodded back.

Eli and June walked into the backyard and stood at the edge of the pool talking to Brooke.

The cookout was the cookout.

Pete kept talking.

Pete said, in the tone of a man who had been reading the same three Substack writers for ten months: "I'm telling you, Mikey. I'm telling you. Two years. Maybe three. Anybody whose job is anything you can describe in words, gone. They are coming for the white-collar guys first. Then the supervisors. Then the blue-collar guys who they can replace with robotics. The window is closing. Get out of anything that involves a desk. Get into something with your hands. Trades. HVAC. Plumbing. Electrical. Welding. If you got a kid in college right now studying anything except nursing or engineering, pull him out. I am not joking."

Mike Pawlak was a lineman for AEP. Mike Pawlak had been a lineman for AEP for twenty-six years. Mike Pawlak was, on his own family-evening rule, not a man who got into long arguments at cookouts.

Mike said: "Pete."

Pete said: "Mikey."

Mike said: "I climb poles."

Pete said: "I know you do."

Mike said: "I am going to keep climbing poles."

Pete said: "Mikey. Robots are going to climb poles."

Mike said: "Pete."

Pete said: "Yes."

Mike said: "Robots are not going to climb the poles I climb."

Pete said: "How do you know."

Mike said: "I climb the poles in the storm at three in the morning in the back of the property in Oakwood with the dog that's gonna bite. The robot is not coming out at three in the morning in Oakwood. The robot is in a warehouse in Columbus. The robot's gonna stay in Columbus."

Pete laughed.

Pete said: "All right, Mikey. All right. You I'm not worried about."

Pete looked across the deck.

Pete said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Pete."

Pete said: "What do you do."

Bob said: "Maintenance at Hartford Pump."

Pete said: "Maintenance."

Bob said: "Maintenance."

Pete said: "Bobby, you got a phone."

Bob said: "I got a phone."

Pete said: "You got a computer."

Bob said: "No."

Pete said: "At home."

Bob said: "No."

Pete said: "Anywhere."

Bob said: "There's one at the plant. Frank uses it."

Pete said: "Frank's the boss."

Bob said: "Frank's the boss."

Pete said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Pete said: "You got two years, three at the outside. They are deploying machine vision at the plant level. They are deploying predictive maintenance models. They are running diagnostics off the PLC data and the predictive model is telling the supervisor what to do before the maintenance guy even hears it. The maintenance guy gets a tablet that tells him *go change the spider on the L-095 on Line Four, the line will be down in forty-seven minutes if you don't*. The maintenance guy used to have to know that. The maintenance guy doesn't have to know that anymore. The tablet knows. The maintenance guy is, in operational terms, the hands. The hands get cheap. The hands get a high schooler. The high schooler does the work for nineteen bucks an hour and a Coke. You with your fourteen years of experience are, on the going arithmetic of the manufacturing-AI deployment timeline, two years from being a guy who Frank's boss decides to let go because the tablet does the listening now."

Bob took a sip of his beer.

He set the beer down on the deck rail.

He looked at Pete.

He, on his own quiet operational practice in conversations with men like Pete that he had been in approximately twenty-seven times since 2020, did not say anything for about six seconds.

Pete waited.

Bob said: "Pete."

Pete said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "I think you might be right."

Pete looked at him.

Pete said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Pete said: "That's not the answer I was expecting."

Bob said: "I know it isn't."

Pete said: "Most guys argue."

Bob said: "Most guys argue because most guys don't want to be wrong. I don't mind being wrong. Wrong is fine. Wrong is most of what I do at work. Wrong is the part you write down."

Pete said: "Bobby, you don't seem worried."

Bob said: "I am worried."

Pete said: "You don't act worried."

Bob said: "I act like a guy. I don't act like a worried guy. There's a difference. Inside, I'm worried."

Pete said: "What are you going to do about it."

Bob said: "Pete."

Pete said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "I don't know yet. I am going to know in a couple of months. Right now I am writing down what I notice. The thing I have noticed in the last four weeks is that the plant is changing. The thing I have noticed in the last four days is that my boss has been watching me for seven years and he thinks I might be useful in the part of the plant that's changing. The thing I have noticed in the last four hours is that I drove from my house to my cousin's house in a truck I fixed and that the truck did not hesitate once on the way over. The thing I have not yet noticed is what comes after that. I will notice it when it shows up. Then I will write it down. Then I will figure out what to do."

Pete looked at him for a long beat.

Pete said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Pete said: "That's an answer I don't have a comeback for."

Bob said: "I'm not trying to give you one."

Pete said: "I know you're not."

Pete looked at his beer.

He drank from it.

He looked back across the deck at Mike.

He said: "Mikey. I'm gonna shut up about AI for the rest of the afternoon."

Mike said: "Thank you, Pete."

Donny, at the grill, who had not, in any of the previous nine minutes of the conversation, said anything, turned around and looked at Bob.

Donny said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Donny."

Donny said: "You see Cody when you came in."

Bob said: "No."

Donny said: "Garage."

Bob said: "What's he doing in the garage."

Donny said: "He's pretending to do something with the Pontiac. He's actually hiding from the cookout. He came out twice for a hot dog. He's been in there since one."

Bob said: "What's wrong with the Pontiac."

Donny said: "Timing. I think. Maybe. I don't know, Bobby. You know I don't know. I keep meaning to take it to the shop on Mumaugh Road. Haven't gotten to it."

Bob said: "Want me to look."

Donny said: "Bobby, I cannot pay you for that."

Bob said: "Donny. I'm not asking you to."

Donny said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Donny."

Donny said: "Go look. He'd love it."

Bob set his half-finished beer on the deck rail.

He walked down off the deck.

He walked across the small concrete pad in front of the attached garage.

He opened the side door of the garage.

---

Cody was sitting on a stool next to a 1979 Pontiac Trans Am that had been red in 1979 and that was now, in October of Year Eleven, the same red but with about thirty percent of the paint sun-bleached to a sort of orange-pink along the hood and roof.

The hood of the Pontiac was up.

Cody had a flashlight.

Cody was not, on Bob's first three seconds of watching him from the doorway, doing anything with the flashlight. He was holding it. He was looking at the engine.

Cody was fourteen.

Cody was tall for fourteen.

Cody had the standing posture of a kid who had been told, at school, every day for the past two years, that he was the wrong kind of person to be.

Bob said: "Cody."

Cody jumped about an inch.

Cody turned around.

Cody said: "Uncle Bob."

Bob said: "Your dad said you're working on the timing."

Cody said: "My dad doesn't know anything about timing."

Bob said: "I know he doesn't."

Cody said: "I am pretending to work on the timing."

Bob said: "I know you are."

Cody said: "Uncle Bob."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Cody said: "Are you going to make me go back to the cookout."

Bob said: "No."

Cody said: "Thank God."

Bob walked into the garage.

He pulled a second stool over.

He sat down next to Cody.

He looked at the engine.

He said: "You want to learn the timing."

Cody looked at him.

Cody said: "Uncle Bob. I don't know what the timing is."

Bob said: "Do you want to know what the timing is."

Cody said: "Yes."

Bob said: "OK."

Bob did not, for the next forty-five minutes, leave the garage.

He walked Cody through the timing on a 1979 Pontiac 400 V-8 from first principles.

He showed Cody what the distributor was.

He showed Cody what the rotor was.

He showed Cody what the cap was.

He showed Cody what the timing mark on the harmonic balancer was.

He showed Cody what the timing light was, after walking out to the Chevy in the driveway and getting his own timing light out of the cab.

He showed Cody how to clip the timing light to the number-one plug wire.

He showed Cody how to start the engine and aim the strobe at the harmonic balancer.

He showed Cody what the mark looked like when the timing was right.

He showed Cody what the mark looked like when the timing was off.

He showed Cody how to loosen the distributor hold-down and rotate the distributor to bring the mark to the right place.

He showed Cody how to torque the hold-down back down.

He showed Cody how to listen to the engine afterward and hear the difference.

Cody listened.

Bob said: "Cody."

Cody said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "Hear that."

Cody said: "It sounds smoother."

Bob said: "It sounds smoother."

Cody said: "Uncle Bob."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Cody said: "Can I do the next one."

Bob said: "The next what."

Cody said: "Whatever the next one is. Whatever you do next. Can I do it."

Bob looked at him.

Bob said: "Cody."

Cody said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "Do you have a notebook."

Cody said: "I have a phone."

Bob said: "Do you have a notebook."

Cody said: "Uncle Bob. I don't have a notebook."

Bob said: "Walgreens. Spring Street. Ninety-nine cents. Mead Composition. Get a Bic pen for thirty-nine. Carry both. Tonight. Before bed. Write down what you noticed today about the timing on the Pontiac. Not what we did. What you noticed. The way the engine sounded before. The way the engine sounded after. The way the distributor felt when I rotated it. The way the strobe felt when you got the mark to the line. The way your brain felt when you saw the mark move. Whatever you noticed. Write it down. Read it back next Saturday. Then come over to my house on Sunday and tell me what you noticed when you read it back."

Cody looked at him.

Cody said: "Uncle Bob."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Cody said: "You serious."

Bob said: "Cody. I am as serious as I have ever been about anything."

Cody said: "OK."

Bob said: "OK."

Cody said: "Why a notebook."

Bob said: "Because the brain forgets. The notebook doesn't. Your brain is going to forget today by Wednesday. The notebook is going to remember today on Wednesday and on the Wednesday after that and ten years from now. Your brain right now is the smartest thing in this garage. Your brain in three weeks is going to remember about eight percent of what's in your brain right now. Eight percent is fine if you wrote down the other ninety-two."

Cody said: "Did your dad teach you that."

Bob said: "He did."

Cody said: "I never met your dad."

Bob said: "I know you didn't. He died in 2007. You weren't born yet."

Cody said: "Was he like you."

Bob said: "He was better at it than me."

Cody said: "Uncle Bob."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Cody said: "Thank you."

Bob said: "Cody."

Cody said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "Get the notebook tonight."

Cody said: "I will get the notebook tonight."

Bob said: "OK."

Bob stood up.

He brushed the dust off the back of his jeans.

He picked up the timing light.

He looked at Cody.

Cody was standing next to the Pontiac with the flashlight in one hand and the timing light's case in the other, looking at the engine with an expression Bob recognized.

Bob had not, on his standard practice in conversations with his nephew, told him what the expression on his face was.

The expression was the expression Bob had seen on his own father's face in 1998 in his father's garage on Hazel Avenue in Lima when Bob was twelve and his father was forty.

The expression was the expression of a man looking at a machine he had just understood for the first time.

The expression was the look.

Bob walked out of the garage.

He walked back across the concrete pad.

He walked up onto the deck.

Donny was at the grill.

Donny said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Donny."

Donny said: "Did you fix the timing."

Bob said: "Cody fixed the timing."

Donny said: "Cody."

Bob said: "Cody."

Donny looked at the garage.

He said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Donny said: "He never does anything in there. He goes in there to hide."

Bob said: "He fixed the timing today, Donny."

Donny said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Donny said: "Is he good at it."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Donny said: "How do you know."

Bob said: "I watched his face when the strobe hit the mark."

Donny was quiet for a beat.

Donny said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Donny said: "Will you teach him."

Bob said: "I told him to get a notebook."

Donny said: "Just that."

Bob said: "Donny. That's the only thing I know to teach him to do that makes everything else I teach him stick. Get the notebook. Write what he noticed. Read it back. After that, it's just engines. He'll pick the engines up on his own if he wants to. Notebook first. Engines second. That's the order."

Donny said: "OK."

Donny said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Donny said: "You're a good uncle."

Bob said: "Donny."

Donny said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "He's a good kid."

Donny said: "He doesn't think so."

Bob said: "I know he doesn't."

Donny said: "He's gonna think so."

Bob said: "I hope so."

Donny flipped a burger.

He said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Donny said: "Pete was wrong about you."

Bob said: "Pete wasn't wrong."

Donny said: "Bobby. Pete was wrong about you."

Bob said: "Donny."

Donny said: "Yeah."

Bob said: "Pete was right about the world. The world is changing. Pete was wrong about me only if I do something about it. If I do nothing, Pete was right."

Donny said: "Bobby, you're gonna do something about it."

Bob said: "I'm going to try."

Donny said: "Bobby, I'm going to bet you a hundred dollars right now that you do."

Bob said: "Donny, don't bet a hundred dollars."

Donny said: "Bobby, I have a hundred dollars."

Bob said: "Donny, you don't."

Donny laughed.

Donny said: "Bobby, you're right, I don't. I will, though. I will when the work picks up in the spring. I will bet you then."

Bob said: "All right. In the spring."

Donny said: "In the spring."

Bob picked the half-finished beer back up off the deck rail.

He drank the rest of it.

He set the empty bottle on the table.

He walked back to Diane's chair.

He sat down on the deck floor next to her, with his back against the leg of her chair, and his hand on her ankle, and Diane's good hand on the top of his head.

The cookout went on for another two hours.

Bob and Diane and the kids left at five forty-two PM.

---

In the truck on the way home, with Diane in the passenger seat, Eli and June in the back, the Chevy not hesitating once, the small notebook open on Bob's right thigh as he drove, Bob wrote one line at a red light at the corner of Cable Road and Elida Road at five fifty-three PM.

The line read:

> *10/4. Cody got it. The look on the kid's face. Same look I had in 1998. Same look Eli had at the porch on the day with the worksheet. Same look I think I have when I figure out a Line Four spider before it goes. It's the same look. The look is the thing. Whatever the look is — that's the thing. Pete asked me what I am going to do. I do not know yet. But the look is part of the answer. Whatever I do next has to be something that helps the look show up on more faces. Kids. Cody. Eli. Carlos's grandkids someday. The look is not coming for AI to take. The look is the thing AI can't take. AI doesn't know what the look is yet. Maybe Frank does. Maybe Frank's notebook is about the look.*

The light turned green.

He drove home.

He did not, in the rest of the drive, write anything else.

The notebook lay open on his thigh until he pulled into the driveway at 1418 Bellevue Avenue at six oh-two PM.

Eli got out and ran into the house.

June got out and chased Eli.

Bob walked around the truck and opened Diane's door and helped her out.

He carried her into the house with one arm around her waist and the other under her elbow, because the four hours at the cookout had used up the day's hands and the day's hips and the day's everything.

He set her on the couch.

He covered her with the green afghan her mother had crocheted for her in 1992.

He sat down next to her.

She put her head on his shoulder.

She said: "Bob."

He said: "Diane."

She said: "Tell me about the garage."

Bob told her about the garage.

He told her about Cody.

He told her about Donny.

He told her about Pete.

He told her about the look.

She listened across the whole telling.

When he finished, she said: "Bob."

He said: "Yeah."

She said: "You're going to figure this out."

He said: "I'm going to try."

She said: "Bob."

He said: "Yeah."

She said: "I am not worried."

He said: "Diane."

She said: "Yeah."

He said: "I am, a little."

She said: "I know you are."

She put her good hand on his hand.

She said: "I am still not worried."

He said: "OK."

He sat on the couch with her.

The afghan was the afghan.

Eli came in with a Hot Wheel.

June came in with Owl.

Otis came in with himself.

The Saturday went into evening.

The cookout was the cookout.

The look was the look.

The notebook was on the kitchen counter where Bob had set it down when he walked in.

The notebook was open to the line Bob had written at the red light.

The line was not, on Bob's own quiet operational accounting that evening, an answer.

The line was a question.

The question was a question Bob was going to spend the next eight weeks of his life trying to answer.

He did not yet know it would be answered by a screen on a corridor wall.

He did not yet know that the answer was already, at six forty-one PM Eastern that same Saturday evening, sitting on its mounting bracket on the second-floor corridor of Hartford Pump Co. at the south end of the building near the locker bay, with its screen at the default standby state, with its small green power LED on, waiting.

For now the Saturday was the Saturday.

The afghan was the afghan.

Diane was warm against his shoulder.

The cookout was over.

The week was about to start.

Monday was Monday.
