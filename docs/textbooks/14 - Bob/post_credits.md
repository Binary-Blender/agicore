# Post-Credits Scene

Bob Pawlak was at the desk in the office on the catwalk at Hartford Pump Co. at six forty-three AM on a Saturday in mid-December of Year Twelve.

The Saturday was the second one Bob had spent at the desk that month.

The plant was, at six forty-three AM Saturday, closed.

The catwalk was empty.

The corridor was empty.

The plant floor was empty except for Otis, who had, on Diane's standing weekend permission, come with Bob today on the operational reality that Otis was eleven years old and that Otis had not yet been to the new office.

Otis was lying on the sage-green braided rug Eugene Pell's wife had picked out at the flea market in Year Eleven.

Bob, at the desk, had open in front of him a small Mead Composition notebook with a faded blue cover.

The notebook was the 1998 notebook.

The notebook was the first one Ray Pawlak had bought himself in 1986 and the third one he had bought himself in 1998 and the one he had been writing in across the year that he had handed his son Bob a Mead Composition of his own at the Sohio station on Cable Road.

The notebook had Ray's small careful blocky printing across one hundred and twelve pages.

Bob had, since opening Frank's wooden box at Maria Reyes's kitchen table on the eighth of November of Year Eleven, read the 1998 notebook five times.

He had read it tonight for the sixth.

He had finished the sixth reading at six forty-one AM Saturday morning.

He closed the notebook at six forty-two AM.

He sat at the desk.

He, on his honest internal accounting at six forty-three AM Saturday morning the thirteenth of December of Year Twelve, recognized that he had been waiting, since the seventh of October of Year Eleven when he had told Eli at the kitchen counter that *the kind of kid who notices things is the best thing a kid can be*, to do the next piece of work that the work had been asking him to do.

The next piece of work had not yet had a name across the fourteen months since.

The next piece of work had a name at six forty-three AM Saturday.

The name was that he was going to mail the 1998 notebook to Christopher Bender at the address on the back of the pamphlet.

He pulled out the top right drawer of the desk.

The top right drawer of Antonio Reyes Sr.'s desk, which had now been Bob Pawlak's desk for eleven months, held a small padded mailing envelope Maria had given Bob in November for any thank-you cards he might want to send and that Bob had not, in the eleven months, used.

The padded mailing envelope was the envelope.

Bob, on his standing operational practice on a Saturday morning in his office, did not, in the next four minutes, do anything quickly.

He pulled a clean piece of legal-pad paper from the bottom drawer.

He clicked the Bic pen.

He wrote, in his small careful blocky printing, the note he had been composing in his head across the past three Saturdays without yet having written it down:

> *Christopher,*

> *My father taught me what you put in the pamphlet. He died in 2007. He never knew there was a word for it. Thank you for giving him the word, twenty years late.*

> *The notebook in this envelope is his 1998 Mead Composition. 1998 was the year he handed me my first one. The notebook is the one in which he was working out, on the operational reality of teaching a twelve-year-old, how to teach a twelve-year-old. Most of the entries in the notebook are about cars. A small number of the entries are about me. I have read the notebook six times across the past fourteen months. The notebook is yours now. The notebook is yours because, on the operational reality of who I have come to understand you to be, you should have a notebook from before the framework had a name. The notebook is from 1998. The framework had no name in 1998. The notebook is the framework's grandfather. I would like the framework to know its grandfather.*

> *Diane is well. The baby is Ray Junior. Eli is doing third grade over for the second time but in a small program at the school that Marlene Hoffman started after she had her second thought about my son. The program is called Notebook Hour. There are nine kids in it. Eli loves it. June is in kindergarten. Otis is eleven. Diane sends her love and a tin of molasses cookies which is in this envelope under the notebook.*

> *The class at Rhodes State is going. Sixteen students. Halfway through. They are who I was. I am teaching them what my father taught me. I am teaching them with your pamphlet on the table next to my notebook. I am teaching them because you gave me a word. I am teaching them because, on the operational reality of having a word, I can now tell a young man named Aaron Vega at the Adrian plant what the word is and watch him write it down. He has written it down. He is going to keep writing.*

> *I am sending this note and the notebook and the cookies because, on the operational reality of what you wrote at the close of the pamphlet about your father, I would like — if your bookshelf has room — for my father to sit next to your father on the bookshelf in your workshop. They would, I think, get along.*

> *Bob Pawlak*
> *Plant Cognition Engineer*
> *Hartford Pump Co.*
> *Lima, Ohio*

He read the note.

He read it twice.

He folded it into thirds.

He, on his standing operational practice at six forty-eight AM Saturday morning, placed the folded note on top of the 1998 notebook in the padded mailing envelope.

He, on the operational reality of Diane's tin of molasses cookies — which she had baked on Thursday evening and wrapped in foil and set in his lunch pail on Friday morning with a small note that read *for Sarah Bender. tell her the recipe is on the back* — placed the tin under the notebook.

He sealed the envelope.

He addressed the envelope by hand:

*Christopher Bender*
*Christopher Bender Foundation*
*123 Maple Drive*
*Ava, Missouri 65608*

He, on his standing operational practice on Saturday mornings, drove the envelope to the Bellevue Park Post Office.

The Bellevue Park Post Office was open from seven AM to noon on Saturdays.

Bob arrived at seven oh-six AM.

He mailed the envelope at the counter with a tracking number.

The tracking number was the tracking number.

The envelope was the envelope.

He drove home.

He had pancakes with the family.

The Saturday was the Saturday.

The padded mailing envelope arrived in Ava, Missouri, on the following Friday at four eleven PM.

Christopher Bender was, at four eleven PM Friday afternoon, in the workshop in the back half of the detached garage on the property at the southwest edge of Ava, with the laptop open, with the Agicore repository's pull-request queue on the screen, with the small lamp on, with the window closed against the December cold, and with the small green plant on the corner of the desk that Sarah had moved from the kitchen to the workshop in 2026 and that had, three years later in 2029, become a small green plant that approximately filled the corner of the desk.

The package was set on the workbench inside the workshop door by Sarah at four fifteen PM after the mail carrier had handed it to her at the front porch.

Sarah carried two cups of coffee out to the workshop at four sixteen PM.

She set one cup on the workbench.

She handed the other to Christopher.

She said: "Christopher."

Christopher said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "The mail came."

Christopher said: "Anything good."

Sarah said: "One package from Lima, Ohio."

Christopher said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "Yeah."

Christopher said: "Lima, Ohio."

Sarah said: "Hartford Pump Co. Plant Cognition Engineer. Bob Pawlak. I recognize the handwriting from the letter that came in November Year Eleven."

Christopher set his coffee down.

He, on his standing operational practice when receiving something he had been waiting for without knowing he had been waiting, did not, in the next four seconds, do anything.

He walked to the workbench.

He picked up the padded mailing envelope.

He opened it.

He pulled out, in order:

The folded legal-pad note.

The 1998 Mead Composition.

The tin of molasses cookies wrapped in foil.

He read the note.

He read it at the workbench across approximately three minutes.

He, at the end of the three minutes, did not say anything.

Sarah, on her standing operational practice when watching her husband read a thing that had landed, did not say anything either.

She watched him.

He, after about a minute of silence, picked up the 1998 Mead Composition.

He opened it to a random page in the middle.

The page had Ray Pawlak's small careful blocky printing across nineteen lines.

The lines were about a 1991 Buick LeSabre that had been in for a coolant flush on a Saturday morning in August of 1998 and that, on Ray's quiet inspection, had a small weep at the heater-core seal that the customer was not yet aware of and that Ray had told the customer about with the recommendation that they get the heater core replaced before the following winter for an estimated cost of two hundred and forty dollars.

The customer had been a man named Hal McKinley, age sixty-three, retired from the Ford Lima Engine Plant in 1994, who had not, on Ray's quiet entry, been able to immediately afford the two hundred and forty dollars and who had, on Ray's quiet practice with retired men of approximately his own age and approximately his own economic margin, been told by Ray that the heater core could probably wait until the following March if Hal would, on his honest accounting, keep the coolant topped up across the winter and bring it back in for a free check on the second Saturday in March of 1999.

Hal had brought it back.

The heater core had been replaced in March of 1999 for two hundred and twenty-three dollars, Ray having found a slightly better price on the heater core itself across the intervening seven months.

Hal had paid Ray.

Hal had also brought, on the Saturday morning of the heater-core replacement, a small loaf of his wife's banana bread.

The banana bread was, on Ray's careful entry at the bottom of the page, *the best banana bread I have ever had.*

Christopher Bender read the page across about four minutes.

He read it twice.

He closed the notebook.

He, on his honest internal accounting at four twenty-three PM Friday afternoon the nineteenth of December of Year Twelve in the workshop in Ava, Missouri, said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "Christopher."

Christopher said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "Yeah."

Christopher said: "Bob Pawlak sent me his father's 1998 notebook."

Sarah said: "What's in it."

Christopher said: "Sarah. The entry I just read is about a man named Ray giving another man named Hal a price break of seventeen dollars and a seven-month payment grace on a heater-core repair in 1999 because Hal had retired from the Ford Lima Engine Plant in 1994 and was, in Ray's quiet operational accounting, the kind of man for whom seventeen dollars was the kind of money Ray could absorb and Hal could not. The entry closes with a note that Hal's wife's banana bread was the best banana bread Ray ever had. Ray wrote both pieces in the same paragraph. Ray treated the price break and the banana bread as the same kind of information. Ray's notebook is, on the page I just read, the cleanest description of operational solidarity between two men in a small American town in the year 1998 I have ever read."

Sarah said: "Christopher."

Christopher said: "Yeah."

Sarah said: "Bob Pawlak sent you that."

Christopher said: "Bob Pawlak sent me that."

Sarah said: "Is it another one."

Christopher, on his standing operational practice when classifying objects that arrived at the workshop, said: "No. This one's not from an operator. This one's from before the framework had a name. This one is the framework's grandfather. The framework's grandfather has the same handwriting as the framework's grandson. The framework's grandfather paid for the framework's grandson's trade school across nine years on the operational margin of a small-town gas station in Allen County, Ohio. The framework's grandfather did not, in any of the nine years, have a word for what he was doing. The framework's grandson did not, until I gave him a pamphlet I wrote because my father died in 1998 the same year his father started teaching him, have the word either. They have the word now. The grandfather did not live to see it. The grandfather lived inside a notebook that, on the operational reality of Bob Pawlak's quiet decision sometime in November of this year to send it to me, is now in this workshop. The notebook is the grandfather. I am going to put the notebook on the bookshelf next to my father's notebook. The two of them will be next to each other for the rest of my life. After my life they will be next to each other in whatever shelf my own son keeps them on. The shelf is the shelf. The notebooks are the notebooks. The framework had a grandfather. He was a mechanic in Lima, Ohio."

Sarah said: "Christopher."

Christopher said: "Yeah."

Sarah said: "Bring me the tin."

Christopher said: "The tin?"

Sarah said: "There's a tin in the envelope. Bob's wife's note said it's for me."

Christopher picked up the tin.

He handed it to Sarah.

Sarah opened it.

The tin held twenty-four molasses cookies.

Sarah picked one up.

She, on her standing operational practice with a cookie she had not yet tasted, smelled it before she ate it.

The smell was the smell of Sarah's own grandmother's kitchen in 1968 in a small town in southern Illinois that Sarah had not been to in fifty-one years.

Sarah, on her honest internal accounting at four twenty-seven PM Friday afternoon in the workshop in Ava, Missouri, did not say anything for about thirty seconds.

She said: "Christopher."

Christopher said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "I am going to write Bob Pawlak's wife a letter tonight."

Christopher said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "Yeah."

Christopher said: "Tell her about your grandmother."

Sarah said: "I am going to tell her about my grandmother."

Christopher said: "OK."

Sarah said: "Christopher."

Christopher said: "Yeah."

Sarah said: "Put the notebook on the shelf."

Christopher walked to the bookshelf.

He pulled out the small slot on the third shelf from the bottom where, in 1998, he had placed the small green canvas-covered notebook that had belonged to his father John Bender.

He, on his standing operational practice at the third shelf, did not, in the next eleven seconds, move his father's notebook.

He placed Ray Pawlak's 1998 Mead Composition next to it.

The two notebooks touched.

The two notebooks were, on the third shelf of the bookshelf in the workshop in the back half of the detached garage on the property at the southwest edge of the small town of Ava, Missouri, at four twenty-nine PM on Friday the nineteenth of December of Year Twelve, the two notebooks.

Christopher Bender, on his honest internal accounting at four twenty-nine PM, stepped back from the bookshelf.

He looked at the two notebooks.

He, on the same internal accounting, did the thing he had not done in the workshop in eleven years.

He cried.

He did not, in the crying, make any noise.

He did not, in the crying, hide his face.

Sarah, on her standing operational practice when her husband was crying in the workshop, walked over.

She put her good hand — which was both of her hands, because Sarah did not have rheumatoid arthritis and never would, but which on Sarah's operational accounting was the hand she always offered her husband first — on his shoulder.

She, on the same standing practice, did not say anything for about a minute.

After the minute, she said: "Christopher."

Christopher said: "Sarah."

Sarah said: "It's working."

Christopher said: "I know it is."

She turned off the lamp.

The workshop was dark.

The small green plant on the corner of the desk was still alive.

The two notebooks on the third shelf were the two notebooks.

The framework had a grandfather.

The grandfather had a name.

The name was Ray Pawlak.
