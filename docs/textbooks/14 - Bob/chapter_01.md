# Chapter 1: The Chevy

Bob was on his back on the concrete of the driveway with the trouble light hanging from the steering linkage where Eli had put it, and the wrench in his right hand, and the small notebook open beside him on the concrete with a Bic pen clipped to its cover.

The Chevy was a 1992 Silverado, half-ton, two-wheel-drive, the long bed, originally white but now the kind of white that had spent twenty years in the sun and was, on a good day in good light, more of a soft grey. The truck had belonged to Bob's father. Bob had been bringing it back since 2022. He had replaced the carburetor in the first year. He had replaced the fuel pump in the second. He had been chasing a hesitation on hard acceleration for the past two weeks.

He had not yet caught it.

Eli was sitting on the second concrete step of the front stoop holding a second trouble light that Bob hadn't asked him to bring. Eli was eight years old. He was wearing the Mickey Mouse t-shirt that had been June's and had been handed down. He had a stripe of axle grease on his left wrist that he had put there himself by reaching into the wheel well when Bob wasn't looking.

Bob had said nothing about the grease.

Otis was on the grass with June. June was five. Otis was a yellow Lab mix who had come to them in 2020 from the shelter on Cable Road and who had, on the family's collective informal accounting, been worth every dollar of his food bill and several of the dollars they did not actually have. June was throwing a rope toy. Otis was bringing it back. The rope toy had not, in the past four years, been thrown into the street.

Diane was on the porch.

The porch was a small concrete slab with two metal folding chairs and a small folding table the family had bought at a yard sale on Spring Street in 2019 for four dollars. Diane was in the chair on the left. She had a glass of iced tea on the table. The glass had not yet been touched. Her hands, this afternoon, were not the hands she had at six in the morning. Her hands at six in the morning were the hands that could open a jar. Her hands at five thirty in the afternoon, in late September, after a humid Tuesday and a low front coming in from the southwest, were the hands that could not.

She had not said anything about the hands.

Bob had not asked.

He slid out from under the truck.

He sat up.

He picked up the notebook.

He read the last two pages.

The last two pages were the past nine days of observations. They were the observations he had been keeping on the hesitation. He had logged the date, the outdoor temperature, the engine temperature when the hesitation started, the engine temperature when the hesitation stopped, the duration of the drive before the hesitation started, and one small column on the far right he had added on the third day for *what it sounded like.* The column on the far right had entries like *not the pump*, *not the filter*, *like a sneeze*, *like the engine forgot for a half-second what it was*, and on the second-to-last entry, *like a switch that doesn't know whether it's on or off.*

Bob looked at the column on the far right.

He looked at the engine-temperature column.

He had not, in the previous nine days, looked at the two columns side by side.

He looked at them side by side now.

He read down the engine-temperature column. One hundred and sixty-three. One hundred and sixty-eight. One hundred and sixty-six. One hundred and sixty-four. One hundred and sixty-seven. One hundred and sixty-five.

Six entries.

All six between one hundred and sixty-three and one hundred and sixty-eight degrees.

He turned the notebook ninety degrees and looked at the page sideways.

He looked at the two columns at the same time.

He sat on the concrete for about a minute.

He picked up the pen.

He drew a small line under the bottom of the page.

Under the line, in his small careful blocky printing, he wrote:

> *9/24. Not fuel. TPS. Heats up around 165 and gives a bad reading. Test cold tomorrow morning. If cold reading is good and hot reading is bad, that's it. $42 part. Auto Zone on Elida.*

He looked at what he had written.

He read it twice.

He nodded once.

He closed the notebook.

He set it on the concrete next to the wrench.

He looked at Eli.

Eli was watching him.

Eli said: "Dad."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Eli said: "Did you get it."

Bob said: "I think so."

Eli said: "What was it."

Bob said: "It's a sensor on the engine. It tells the computer how much gas to send. When it gets warm it lies a little. The computer believes it. The engine hiccups."

Eli said: "Did it lie all the time."

Bob said: "No. Only when it was warm."

Eli thought about this.

He said: "How'd you know."

Bob said: "I wrote down what I noticed for two weeks. Then I looked at what I wrote."

Eli said: "That's it."

Bob said: "That's it."

Eli said: "Grandpa taught you that."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Eli said: "He told you what to do."

Bob said: "He told me to write down what I noticed. Not what I did. What I noticed."

Eli said: "Why not what you did."

Bob said: "Because what you did doesn't teach you anything. You already know what you did. You did it. What teaches you is what you noticed. The things that are almost right. The things that don't fit. The hunches. The funny smells. Stuff you'd forget if you didn't write it down."

Eli looked at the notebook on the concrete.

He said: "Can I have one."

Bob looked at him.

Bob said: "Yeah."

Eli said: "When."

Bob said: "Tomorrow. After school. We'll go get you one."

Eli said: "Like yours."

Bob said: "Like mine."

Eli said: "OK."

He sat on the step.

He did not, in the next forty seconds, say anything else.

Bob stood up.

He brushed the concrete grit off the back of his jeans.

He walked to the porch.

He took the iced tea off the table.

He held the glass.

The glass was sweating.

Diane looked at him.

She said: "Bob."

He said: "Yeah."

She said: "Hold it to my wrist for a minute."

He held the cold glass to the inside of her left wrist.

He held it there.

He counted to thirty in his head.

He moved the glass to the inside of her right wrist.

He held it there.

He counted to thirty again.

He set the glass back on the table.

He sat down in the other folding chair.

He did not, in the next two minutes, say anything.

Neither did Diane.

The sun was going down over the rooftops on the west side of the street. The west side of the street was three identical small ranch houses that had been built in the same year as the Pawlak house, 1957, by the same developer. The street was a cul-de-sac. The cul-de-sac was on the east side of Lima, Ohio. Lima was a town of about thirty-five thousand people in Allen County in the northwest of the state. The Pawlak house was on the east side because the east side was the side that had not, in the last twenty years, been bought up by the kind of out-of-town investor who buys up the small ranch houses and rents them back to the people who used to own them.

Bob had bought the house in 2014 for sixty-one thousand dollars. He had paid it off in 2023. The paying-off had taken nine years. The paying-off had been, on Bob's quiet accounting at the time of the last payment, the proudest day of his adult life.

He had not told anyone except Diane.

Diane had cried.

The mortgage payment had been four hundred and forty-one dollars a month.

Without the mortgage payment, the family had been able to absorb the cost of Diane's biologic medication, which the insurance through Hartford Pump Co. covered at sixty percent and which left the family responsible for about three hundred and twenty dollars a month in coinsurance and copays.

The three hundred and twenty dollars a month was, by the family's standing operational arithmetic, the difference between Diane being able to use her hands in the morning and Diane being able to use them all day.

The three hundred and twenty dollars a month was the mortgage payment that the family no longer paid.

The arithmetic had worked out.

The arithmetic was the kind of arithmetic Bob did in his head while driving.

He did it again now, sitting in the folding chair next to Diane, watching the last of the September light on the houses across the street.

Diane said: "Did you figure it out."

Bob said: "Yeah."

Diane said: "What was it."

Bob said: "The throttle position sensor. It heats up around one sixty-five and gets sloppy. Forty-two-dollar part. I'll grab it tomorrow on lunch."

Diane said: "Two weeks."

Bob said: "Two weeks."

Diane said: "Your dad would've laughed at you."

Bob said: "He'd have laughed at me on day three."

Diane said: "He'd have written it down."

Bob said: "He'd have written it down on day one and I'd have read it on day two and I wouldn't be sitting here on day fifteen with a sore back."

Diane laughed.

The laugh was the laugh that had, in the past six months, been the laugh she let out about once a week.

Bob heard the laugh.

He, on his own quiet internal accounting, did not say anything about the laugh.

He sat next to her.

The light on the houses across the street went from gold to grey.

Eli came up the porch steps with the trouble light Bob hadn't asked him to bring.

Eli said: "Mom."

Diane said: "Eli."

Eli said: "Can I show you what I noticed today."

Diane looked at Bob.

Bob looked at Diane.

Diane said: "Yes."

Eli sat down on the porch slab cross-legged at her feet.

He pulled a folded piece of yellow legal-pad paper out of the front pocket of his jeans.

He unfolded it.

The paper had eleven lines of writing on it in Eli's eight-year-old printing. The lines were:

> *otis only catches the rope on the left side*
> *otis only catches the rope on the left side every time*
> *otis is right-pawed*
> *otis catches with the side away from his strong paw*
> *because the strong paw is for braking*
> *because the strong paw is the steering wheel*
> *no the strong paw is the brake*
> *the other paw is the steering wheel*
> *if i throw the rope to the left otis catches it faster*
> *i threw it to the left twelve times*
> *otis caught it twelve times in less than a second.*

Diane read the paper.

She read it twice.

She said: "Eli."

Eli said: "Yeah."

She said: "Did you have a notebook for this."

Eli said: "No. It was a piece of paper from school."

Diane said: "Why was it on paper from school."

Eli said: "Because Mrs. Marlene gave me a worksheet today and I finished it and there was the back."

Diane looked at the worksheet.

She turned it over.

The front of the worksheet was the worksheet. It was a third-grade reading comprehension worksheet about a girl named Anna who lost her dog and found it again at a pond. There were eight questions on the worksheet. Eli had answered four of them. Two of the four were correct. The teacher had circled the four blank questions in red.

The blank questions had been blank, on Diane's quiet read of the back of the worksheet, because the back of the worksheet was the part Eli had filled in.

Diane folded the worksheet back up.

She handed it to Eli.

She said: "Eli."

Eli said: "Yeah."

She said: "You're getting a notebook tomorrow."

Eli said: "Dad said the same thing."

Diane said: "I know he did. I am saying it again because I want you to hear it from me too. You are getting a notebook tomorrow. The notebook is for what you noticed. The questions on the worksheet are for the worksheet. Both of them are good. Both of them are important. But the notebook is yours. The worksheet belongs to Mrs. Marlene. Do you understand the difference."

Eli thought about this.

He said: "I think so."

Diane said: "Tell me the difference."

Eli said: "The notebook is mine. The worksheet is Mrs. Marlene's."

Diane said: "And."

Eli said: "And the notebook is what I noticed. The worksheet is what she asked."

Diane said: "Right."

Eli said: "OK."

He stood up.

He folded the worksheet and put it back in his front jeans pocket.

He walked into the house.

The screen door slapped shut behind him.

Diane looked at Bob.

She said: "Bob."

Bob said: "Yeah."

She said: "He's you."

Bob said: "I know."

She said: "Mrs. Marlene called me today."

Bob said: "I know."

She said: "She wants a meeting next Wednesday."

Bob said: "I know."

Diane said: "She thinks he's not paying attention."

Bob said: "He's paying attention to everything except her."

Diane said: "I know."

She picked up the iced tea.

She held it.

She did not, on the operational reality of her hands at five forty-five PM in late September with a humid Tuesday and a low front coming in from the southwest, drink from it.

She put it back on the table.

She said: "Bob."

He said: "Yeah."

She said: "How are we going to do this."

Bob said: "Do what."

Diane said: "Eli. Mrs. Marlene. The meeting. The way she said it on the phone."

Bob said: "How did she say it on the phone."

Diane said: "She said *we need to talk about Eli's trajectory.*"

Bob was quiet for a beat.

He said: "His trajectory."

Diane said: "His trajectory."

Bob said: "He's eight."

Diane said: "I know he's eight."

Bob said: "He doesn't have a trajectory. He has third grade."

Diane said: "I know."

Bob said: "Did you tell her that."

Diane said: "No. I told her we'd come in next Wednesday at four."

Bob said: "We'll go in next Wednesday at four."

Diane said: "Bob."

He said: "Yeah."

She said: "Don't be the guy at the meeting who fights the teacher."

Bob said: "I won't be."

Diane said: "You will want to be."

Bob said: "I know I will."

Diane said: "Don't."

Bob said: "I won't."

She looked at him.

He looked at her.

She said: "I love you."

He said: "I love you."

She picked the iced tea back up.

She did not, this time, set it down.

She held it in the curled fingers of her right hand. She did not raise it to her mouth. She held it.

She held it because, on her own quiet operational accounting, holding it was the part she could do this evening, and the rest of it could wait until morning when the hands worked.

Bob did not, on his own quiet operational accounting, take the glass from her.

He sat next to her on the porch.

The street went from grey to a kind of blue.

Otis came up the steps and lay down at their feet.

June came around the corner of the house with grass in her hair.

She climbed into Bob's lap.

She said: "Daddy."

Bob said: "June."

She said: "Otis is right-pawed."

Bob said: "I heard."

She said: "Eli told me."

Bob said: "Eli is right too."

She said: "I'm left-pawed."

Bob said: "Are you."

She said: "Watch."

She held up her left hand.

She wiggled the fingers.

She held up her right hand.

She wiggled the fingers.

She said: "The left ones go faster."

Bob said: "I see that."

She said: "Eli says I should write it down."

Bob said: "He's right."

She said: "I can't write yet."

Bob said: "You can draw."

She thought about this.

She said: "OK."

She climbed off his lap.

She walked into the house.

The screen door slapped shut behind her.

Bob and Diane sat on the porch in the folding chairs for the next eleven minutes without saying anything.

The streetlights came on at six oh-one PM.

The streetlights on this block of Bellevue Avenue had been installed by the city in 1962. They were the original poles. The bulbs had been replaced twice in those sixty-three years. The poles had not.

The light from the streetlights was the kind of light that was the color of a dim grocery-store sign.

Bob picked up the notebook off the concrete on his way inside.

He clipped the pen back to the cover.

He put the notebook in the back pocket of his jeans.

He held the screen door open for Diane.

Diane stood up.

She walked, on the operational reality of her hips at six oh-two PM in late September, more slowly than she had walked into the porch at five thirty.

Bob did not, on his own operational discipline, watch her walk.

He held the screen door.

She walked through.

He walked in behind her.

The screen door slapped shut.

The light over the kitchen sink was on.

The pot on the stove was the pot Diane had started at four PM.

The pot was chili.

The chili was Tuesday.

Tuesday was chili.

Tomorrow was Wednesday.

Wednesday was the day Bob would, on his lunch break, drive to the Auto Zone on Elida Road and buy a forty-two-dollar throttle position sensor and install it in the Chevy in the driveway after work.

The Wednesday after that — eight days from now — was the day Bob and Diane were going to drive Eli to Lincoln Elementary at three forty-five PM and sit in a small plastic chair across from Mrs. Marlene Hoffman and listen to a woman they had never met describe to them, with the kind of careful word choice that Diane had recognized on the phone, the trajectory of their eight-year-old son.

Bob did not, on his own operational discipline, think about the meeting.

He washed his hands at the kitchen sink.

He sat down at the table.

The chili was good.

The kids ate.

The dog lay on the linoleum under the table with his chin on Bob's left work boot.

The notebook was in the back pocket of Bob's jeans.

The Chevy was in the driveway with the trouble light still hanging from the steering linkage.

The day was the day.

The day, on Bob Pawlak's own quiet accounting at the kitchen table at six twenty-three PM on the last Tuesday of September of Year Eleven, in the kitchen of the small house at 1418 Bellevue Avenue in Lima, Ohio, had been a good day.

He did not yet know it had been the day everything had started to change.

He would know it in about six weeks.

For now the chili was the chili.

The notebook was the notebook.

The trouble light was on a sensor it would not, on tomorrow's testing, turn out to be the cause of, because Bob had already, this evening, on the concrete of the driveway, found the cause.

The cause was the cause.

The cause had been in the notebook the whole time.

Bob had not, until five forty-three PM this Tuesday, looked at the notebook the right way.

He had looked at it the right way now.

He would not, for the rest of his life, look at any of his notebooks any other way.

He did not yet know this.

The chili was the chili.

He had a second bowl.
