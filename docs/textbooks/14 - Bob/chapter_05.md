# Chapter 5: The All-Hands

The all-hands was on the second Thursday of October at twelve fifteen PM in the cafeteria.

The cafeteria was on the east side of the plant, between the office wing and the locker bay. It held, on the standard capacity Hartford had used for shift parties since 1981, about a hundred and seventy people at sit-down density and about two hundred and twenty if you took the tables out and used folding chairs.

The tables were out.

The folding chairs were in.

The folding chairs had been put out by the maintenance team at the close of first shift on Wednesday. Bob had set up forty-eight of the two hundred and twenty chairs himself. He had not, on his own quiet operational practice when setting up a room he was going to be sitting in the next day, sat in the third-row chair before he had set it up.

He sat in it now, at twelve fourteen PM Thursday, with his lunch in a brown paper bag at his feet — peanut butter, the standard — and the second notebook, the black stitched Smith and Hawken Frank had given him three weeks earlier on the Wednesday after the Line Four spider, open on his right knee with the Bic pen clipped to the cover.

The second notebook had, by this Thursday, fourteen entries in it.

The fourteen entries were the same fourteen entries as the fourteen entries in the Mead Composition notebook in Bob's back pocket, copied word for word in the small careful blocky printing.

Frank had not, in the three weeks since giving Bob the Smith and Hawken, said anything about it.

Bob had not, in the three weeks, mentioned it back.

The notebook was the notebook.

The all-hands was the all-hands.

The room was filling up.

Carlos Mendez sat down two seats to Bob's right with a turkey sandwich.

Travis Boudreau sat down on Bob's left with two slices of pepperoni from the pizza Hartford had ordered for the meeting.

Travis said: "Bobby."

Bob said: "Travis."

Travis said: "You actually read the email."

Bob said: "I read all the emails."

Travis said: "Who reads all the emails."

Bob said: "I read all the emails."

Travis laughed.

He said: "What's it about."

Bob said: "The new system."

Travis said: "Which new system."

Bob said: "The terminal in the corridor."

Travis said: "The thing nobody uses."

Bob said: "The thing nobody uses."

Travis said: "Bobby. Nobody is going to use it after the all-hands either."

Bob said: "Maybe."

Travis said: "Bobby. Trust me. We had a thing like this at GM Defiance in 2019. Nobody used it. Same thing. They put a kiosk in the corridor. Three months later they pulled it out. Plant guys don't want to touch a screen on the way to their locker. We want to touch our truck keys."

Bob said: "I hear you."

Travis said: "You hear me but you don't agree."

Bob said: "I don't know yet."

Travis said: "Bobby. You always say *I don't know yet.*"

Bob said: "Travis. That's the only honest thing a person can say most of the time."

Travis said: "Fair."

He bit into his pizza.

---

Linda Hartford walked to the front of the cafeteria at twelve sixteen PM.

Linda Hartford was sixty-four years old. She had inherited Hartford Pump Co. from her father, Walter Hartford, in 2007. She had run the company for eighteen years. She had not, in the eighteen years, ever fired anybody she could keep. She had, in the eighteen years, kept the plant in Lima when every other mid-size manufacturer in Allen County had moved or closed. She had, on the standing operational accounting of the four hundred and twenty people who currently drew a paycheck from Hartford Pump Co., made the calls that needed to be made.

She wore a navy blazer and a pair of dark jeans and the same pair of Red Wing work boots she had been wearing on the plant floor since 2012.

She did not, on her own standard practice, raise her voice to start a meeting.

She tapped a fingernail twice on the side of the small microphone clipped to the lapel of her blazer.

The room quieted in about four seconds.

Linda said: "Thank you all for being here. I am going to take five minutes and then I am going to hand it over to Frank. Five minutes for me. Twenty for Frank. Five for questions. Done by twelve forty-five. Back on the floor by one. Lunch is on us. Pizza in the back, plus whatever your wife packed if she packed something better, which in my case she did, but Brendan made the pizza order, so the pizza was inevitable."

There was a small laugh.

Linda said: "Three things."

She held up a finger.

She said: "One. Q3 numbers are in. We shipped four hundred and eleven pumps in the quarter. That's the second-best quarter in our history. The best quarter was Q2 of 2007, which my father always said was a fluke because we caught a hurricane order from a contractor in Florida. So if you don't count flukes, this was our best quarter ever. The credit goes to you. All of you. Every single one of you in this room. Thank you."

She paused for the small round of applause.

She held up a second finger.

She said: "Two. We are not closing. We are not moving. We are not selling. I get the question every year. The answer this year is the same as the answer the last seventeen years. We are not closing, we are not moving, we are not selling. Hartford Pump is going to be in Lima as long as I am alive and as long as my daughter wants it to be after that, and Margaret, who is here in the second row and waving at me, has told me she wants it to be after that. So the question is settled. We are staying."

She paused for the second round of applause, which was louder than the first.

She held up a third finger.

She said: "Three. Frank is going to talk to you in a minute about the system in the corridor. Some of you have used it. Most of you have not. Frank and I have been talking about this for nine months. I am going to say one thing about it before I hand the floor to him. The thing I am going to say is this. The system is not a productivity tool. The system is not a surveillance tool. The system is not a way for me or Frank to second-guess the work you do. The system is, on my honest understanding of what Frank put in nine months ago, a notebook. The system is a place where the plant is keeping notes on itself. Frank put it in because Frank and I both believe that the smartest thing in this plant is the four hundred and twenty of you, and the second-smartest thing is the plant itself, and the second-smartest thing has been trying to tell us things for forty years that the four hundred and twenty of you have known and that we have not had a way to write down at the level of the plant. The system is the writing-down. The system is the plant's notebook. Frank is going to tell you the rest. Frank, the floor is yours."

She unclipped the microphone and handed it to Frank.

Bob, in the third row, had — by the time Linda finished the third point — written one sentence in the Smith and Hawken on his knee.

The sentence was: *Linda just said it. The system is a notebook.*

He underlined it.

He looked up.

---

Frank stood at the front.

Frank, on Bob's standing read of Frank in front of an audience, was not in his element. Frank was a man whose element was the glass office and the plant floor at six AM and the small leather notebook in the top right drawer of his desk. Frank in front of two hundred and twenty folding chairs was a man who had prepared a slide deck.

The slide deck came up on the screen behind him.

The first slide had the Hartford Pump Co. logo and the words *Q3 Plant Intelligence Update* and a small subtitle that read *Frank Reyes, Plant Manager.*

Travis muttered: "Plant Intelligence."

Bob said nothing.

Frank advanced to slide two.

Slide two had a bullet list.

The bullets read:

- 9 months of operational telemetry
- 47 production-line constraint boundaries encoded
- 14 quality-gate checkpoints active
- 3 supply-chain triggers integrated
- 0 floor-level adoption

The fifth bullet was the one Frank had circled in red on the printed version sitting on the small podium in front of him.

Frank said: "Nine months ago we put in the system. Nine months later we have the data. The data is in the top four bullets. The fifth bullet is the bullet that brings us here today."

He clicked to slide three.

Slide three was a diagram of the plant. The diagram had boxes for each line, arrows between the boxes, and small circles at each constraint boundary.

Frank said: "What the system is doing is this. It is watching the plant. It is encoding what it sees. It is keeping a record of what is going right and what is going almost wrong. It is, in the language Linda used in her opening, taking notes on the plant. The notes are organized by line. The notes are queryable. The notes are not stored anywhere outside of this plant. The notes do not go to corporate. The notes are not connected to any external system. The notes are the plant's notes about itself. The terminal in the corridor is the way you read the notes. The terminal in the corridor is also the way you add a note. You add a note by walking up to the terminal and dragging an observation into the part of the diagram where the observation happened. The terminal will then ask you, in plain English, what you observed. You will type or talk. The terminal will write it down."

Bob, in the third row, wrote in the Smith and Hawken:

> *He just said it. You add a note by dragging it into the part of the diagram where the observation happened. Then you tell the terminal what you noticed. The terminal writes it down. The terminal is taking dictation. The terminal is doing what the Mead Composition does. The terminal is the digital notebook.*

He underlined the last sentence.

He looked up.

Frank was on slide four.

Slide four was a screenshot of the terminal interface.

The screenshot showed the plant diagram, a queue of items down the right side, and a small text box at the bottom.

Frank said: "The queue on the right is what the plant has been trying to tell us. The queue includes pattern-matched signals — things like *the air pressure on the secondary cab at the paint bay has dropped six times in the last fortnight with the same time signature*, which the system flagged before Bob Pawlak figured out what was causing it on the twenty-ninth of September. The queue also includes notes any of you have added. The queue is not a to-do list. The queue is a list of things the plant has noticed. Some of them will be useful. Some of them will be noise. The job of the people who use the system is to look at the queue and decide which is which. That is the job. That is the only job. We do not, on this rollout, have any other expectation of you besides looking at the queue and adding what you notice. Look. Add. That is the system."

He clicked to slide five.

Slide five was a list of names.

The names were the names of the seven people in the plant who had, in the previous nine months, used the terminal more than once.

The names were all from the office wing.

Bob's name was not on the list.

Carlos's name was not on the list.

Travis's name was not on the list.

There were no names on the list from the production floor.

Frank stood next to the slide.

He looked at the room.

He said, off the script that was printed on the podium in front of him, the sentence he had not, on his own quiet operational practice when writing the script the previous Sunday night, written down:

He said: "The terminal is going to tell you what the machine has been trying to tell you for forty years. Some of you already hear what the machine has been trying to tell you. The terminal is not for you. The terminal is the thing that is going to tell the rest of the plant what the few of you have been hearing all along. The terminal exists because what you hear is the most valuable thing in this building. The terminal is the way we write it down so that the next generation of you can hear it too."

He paused.

He said, returning to the script: "Q3 numbers. Adoption goal for Q4 is forty floor users adding at least one observation per month. We will check in again in January. Thank you. Questions."

The room was silent for about three seconds.

Then a hand went up at the back.

The hand belonged to a man named Eugene Pell who worked the small-pumps line and who had been at Hartford for twenty-eight years.

Eugene said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Eugene."

Eugene said: "I got a question."

Frank said: "Ask."

Eugene said: "Is this going to put any of us out of a job."

Frank said: "Eugene."

Eugene said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "On the standard practice I have run as plant manager for seven years and assistant plant manager for four years before that and the standard practice my predecessor ran for nineteen years before that, Hartford Pump Co. has not, in thirty years of being run by anybody currently alive, laid off a single worker because of a tool we installed in the plant. The tools we install in the plant are the tools we install in the plant. The workers we have in the plant are the workers we have in the plant. The two things have nothing to do with each other. The system is a tool. The system is going to make the work easier. The system is going to make the work last longer because the work is going to be more legible to the next generation than it has been to ours. Eugene, the answer to the question is no. The answer this Thursday is the same as the answer thirty years ago. The terminal is a wrench. The wrench is not going to fire you. The wrench is going to help you do the work."

Eugene was quiet for a beat.

Eugene said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Eugene said: "OK."

Eugene sat down.

A second hand went up in the front row, belonging to a woman named Lupe Hidalgo who worked in shipping.

Lupe said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Lupe."

Lupe said: "If I walk up to the terminal and I do not know how to use it, what happens."

Frank said: "Lupe."

Lupe said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "The terminal works the way your phone works on the day you buy it. You touch it. It does something. You touch something else. It does something else. You touch the screen wrong, it tells you in plain English what you meant to do. You leave. The terminal forgets. There is no way to break the terminal by touching it. Touching the terminal is the only way to learn it. Anyone who has any worry about being judged for touching the terminal wrong can do it on the third shift, when the corridor is empty. The terminal does not know who is touching it unless you tell it. You don't have to tell it. The terminal is happy to take notes from somebody named *anonymous floor worker.* Most of the office staff who use it use their own names. The floor doesn't have to. The notes are the notes. The names are optional."

Lupe was quiet for a beat.

Lupe said: "Frank."

Frank said: "Yeah."

Lupe said: "I am going to touch it tonight."

Frank said: "Lupe."

Lupe said: "Yeah."

Frank said: "Good."

A third hand did not go up.

Frank waited.

Frank said: "OK. We are done. Pizza in the back. Back to the floor by one. Thank you."

The room emptied.

Bob did not, in the leaving, stand up immediately.

He sat in the third-row folding chair for about forty seconds.

He looked at the Smith and Hawken on his knee.

He read what he had written.

The five lines read:

> *Linda just said it. The system is a notebook.*
> *He just said it. You add a note by dragging it into the part of the diagram where the observation happened. Then you tell the terminal what you noticed. The terminal writes it down. The terminal is taking dictation. The terminal is doing what the Mead Composition does. The terminal is the digital notebook.*
> *Frank off-script: the terminal is going to tell you what the machine has been trying to tell you.*
> *Eugene asked if the tool was going to fire him. Frank said the tool is a wrench. The wrench doesn't fire anybody.*
> *Lupe said she is going to touch it tonight.*

Bob looked at the five lines.

He, on his own honest internal accounting at twelve forty-three PM Thursday afternoon in the third-row folding chair in the cafeteria of Hartford Pump Co., recognized that the five lines were the five lines.

The five lines were what he had been waiting to hear without knowing he had been waiting.

He underlined the fourth line for the second time.

He clipped the pen to the cover.

He closed the notebook.

He stood up.

He folded his folding chair.

He carried it to the rack on the east wall of the cafeteria along with the eleven other chairs in his row.

He walked out of the cafeteria.

He walked down the corridor to the locker bay.

He passed the terminal on the way.

He did not, on his standard practice for the previous nine months of walking past the terminal on the way to the locker bay, walk past it without looking at it.

He looked at it for the first time.

The terminal was a forty-eight-inch touchscreen mounted at chest height on the south wall of the corridor, between the break room and the locker bay, on a steel bracket that had been bolted to the wall by a contractor in February. The screen was at the standby state. The standby state was a slowly rotating low-contrast diagram of the plant — the same diagram Frank had shown on slide three — with the line names labeled in a small white serif font.

Bob stopped.

He stood about three feet from the screen.

He looked at the diagram.

He looked at it for about fourteen seconds.

He recognized one of the shapes.

He did not, in the recognition, know how he recognized it.

He recognized it the way he had recognized the slap on the Lovejoy spider on Line Four from three hundred and eighty feet away in the paint bay.

He recognized it the way he had recognized the temperature pattern on the Chevy TPS from looking at his own notebook sideways on the driveway.

He recognized it the way he had recognized the look on Cody's face in Donny's garage.

He recognized it.

He did not, in the recognition, name what he was recognizing.

He stood in front of the terminal for another nine seconds.

He did not touch it.

He, on his own quiet operational practice at twelve forty-six PM Thursday afternoon in the corridor between the break room and the locker bay, was not yet ready to touch the terminal.

He would be ready twelve days from now.

The twelve days were the twelve days.

He walked past the terminal.

He went back to the floor.

He worked the rest of his shift.

He clocked out at five oh-two PM.

He drove home.

He had spaghetti with Diane and the kids.

He sat on the porch.

He wrote in the Mead Composition in his back pocket and in the Smith and Hawken on his lap.

The line he wrote in both notebooks was the same line.

The line was:

> *10/9. The terminal is a notebook. Frank deployed a notebook for the plant. Nobody on the floor has touched it because nobody on the floor knows it is a notebook. Linda called it a notebook today. Frank called it a notebook today. I heard it. I am not yet ready to touch it. I will be ready soon. I do not know when. I am writing this down so I can read it back next week and find out when I knew I was ready.*

He closed both notebooks.

He clipped both pens.

He sat on the porch with Diane and Otis.

The October evening was the October evening.

The terminal in the corridor at the plant was the terminal in the corridor at the plant.

Lupe Hidalgo, on the third shift at two seventeen AM Friday morning, walked up to the terminal and touched it for the first time and added one observation.

The observation was: *Shipping dock door 3 latch is loose. Truckers have been slamming it. The hinge is gonna go.*

Frank read the observation at six twelve AM Friday morning on the dashboard in the glass office.

Frank, in the glass office at six twelve AM, did the second thing he had not done in front of anyone in eleven years of being plant manager.

He smiled by himself.

Then he wrote, in the leather notebook in the top right drawer:

> *10/10. Lupe Hidalgo added an observation at 2:17 AM. Shipping dock door 3. She wrote it like she's been writing it all her life. The first floor user. Eight years and one month after I first thought of this. The thing is starting.*

The thing was starting.

The thing had not yet, on Bob Pawlak's own operational arc, started for Bob.

The thing was going to start for Bob on the Tuesday two weeks from this Friday, when Line Two would jam on the morning shift, and Bob would fix it, and he would walk past the terminal again on the way to his locker, and this time the terminal would not be at the standby state because Eugene Pell, having watched Lupe Hidalgo's note appear in the queue on the previous Wednesday at break, had added a second one of his own — *small-pumps Line 1, casting cooling time inconsistent on Mondays, I think the night cleaner cools the line down with the wash water* — and a third floor worker had added a third one on Friday, and a fourth had added a fourth on Saturday, and the queue had begun to fill, and the diagram on the screen had begun to flicker with the soft little chevrons that the terminal used to indicate fresh notes, and Bob would, on the Tuesday, see the chevrons.

The chevrons were the chevrons.

The Tuesday was the Tuesday.

For now the Thursday was the Thursday.

The all-hands was over.

The terminal in the corridor was on.

The plant was the plant.

Bob slept eight hours.

He was on the line at six AM Friday.

The line was the line.

The work was the work.
