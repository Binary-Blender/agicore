# Chapter 6: Semantic Contracts and Information Boundaries

Traditional software engineering is a discipline built on the discipline of specification. Before a component is built, its interface is defined: the types it accepts, the types it produces, the exceptions it may raise, the invariants it maintains. This specification is the contract — not a legal document, but a formal commitment about what the component does, enforced at compile time, at runtime, or both. Type systems, schema validators, interface definitions, unit tests: all are mechanisms for making contracts precise, checkable, and binding. The engineer who writes a function signature in a strongly typed language is making a contract that the compiler will enforce. The engineer who writes an OpenAPI specification is making a contract that the network stack will validate. The engineer who writes a database schema is making a contract that the storage engine will uphold. The contract is the atom of software engineering discipline, and the history of software quality improvement is largely a history of making contracts more precise, more comprehensive, and more automatically enforceable.

AI pipeline components have, in most contemporary deployments, no contracts at all. A language model is invoked with a natural language prompt, it returns a token sequence, and that token sequence is either passed directly to the next stage or — optimistically — checked against some informal notion of what a good response should look like. The inputs are untyped. The outputs are untyped. The quality criteria are unstated. The relationship between what the component receives and what it produces is governed by no specification that any system or person could check. This is not a limitation of AI technology. It is an engineering deficit. The **specification gap** — the difference between what an AI component does and what it is supposed to do — is the most consequential unresolved engineering problem in the deployment of AI systems at scale, and closing it is the primary work of the discipline of semantic contracts.

A **semantic contract** is a formal specification of an AI pipeline component that defines, at minimum, three elements. The first is the **input specification**: the format of the data the component accepts, the semantic content it expects, and the conditions under which the component's behavior is well-defined. The second is the **output specification**: the format of the data the component produces, the semantic content it guarantees to provide, and the conditions under which an output is considered well-formed. The third is the **quality specification**: the criteria by which an output is evaluated as satisfactory or unsatisfactory — criteria that are explicit, evaluable, and applied systematically rather than assumed and applied occasionally. All three elements are required for a complete contract. A component with an input specification and an output specification but no quality specification has committed only to producing outputs of a given format, not to producing outputs of a given quality — a commitment that is technically satisfiable by a component that produces well-formed garbage. A component with input and quality specifications but no output specification produces well-evaluated outputs whose downstream consequences are unpredictable. Any element missing from the contract is a dimension along which the component can fail silently.

The critical engineering property of semantic contracts — the property that distinguishes them from all prior forms of software contract — is their evaluability by both human and machine readers. Consider a quality criterion of the form: "The generated summary must accurately reflect the information present in the source document without introducing claims, attributions, or inferences not supported by the source text." This criterion is stated in natural language. It has no formal type signature. There is no compiler that can verify it at build time. And yet it is completely evaluable. A human expert who reads the summary alongside the source document can apply this criterion and render a judgment. An AI model prompted with the summary, the source, and this criterion can apply the criterion and render a judgment — not with perfect reliability, but with meaningful accuracy across a population of evaluations. The criterion is checkable by any sufficiently competent cognitive agent, human or artificial, without requiring a formal type system, a schema language, or a specialized verification tool.

This property — call it **natural language evaluability** — does not exist in traditional software engineering. A type signature is machine-checkable but not human-readable in any deep sense; a human programmer must understand the semantics of the type system to interpret it. A database schema is machine-enforced but carries no semantic content beyond structural constraints; it cannot express a criterion like "the summary must be accurate." A unit test expresses a behavioral criterion but in a form that only a programmer can author and only a test runner can evaluate. Natural language quality criteria are authored by domain practitioners without programming expertise, evaluated by human reviewers without technical training, evaluated by AI models without formal specification, and enforced by QC pipeline nodes that call evaluator models. The contract is readable by everyone who touches the pipeline and evaluable by any component of the system. This is a genuinely novel property of AI-native engineering, and it resolves a limitation that has plagued software quality practice for decades: the gap between what domain experts know about quality and what they can express in forms that systems can check.

Before proceeding to the mechanics of contract design, a taxonomy of the specification gap is useful. The gap manifests in three characteristic forms. The **format gap** is the failure to specify output structure: the downstream system expects JSON, the model produces prose; the downstream system expects a list of exactly five items, the model produces three or seven. Format gaps are the easiest to detect and the easiest to close — explicit format instructions in the prompt, combined with a schema validator on the output, eliminate them with high reliability. The **content gap** is the failure to specify semantic content: the prompt asks for a summary, but does not specify whether to include numerical data, whether to preserve the author's qualifications and hedges, whether to include the conclusion or only the body. Content gaps produce outputs that are formally correct — they are summaries — but semantically incomplete or misaligned with the downstream use case. Content gaps require more careful input specification and more semantically rich quality criteria. The **quality gap** is the failure to specify the evaluative standard: the contract defines what the output contains but not how good it must be. Quality gaps are the most consequential and the hardest to close, because quality in knowledge work is often multi-dimensional, partially tacit, and domain-specific in ways that resist simple articulation. Closing the quality gap is the deepest work of semantic contract development.

The **wire format** is the concrete representation of data as it flows between pipeline stages. A mature cognitive pipeline does not pass raw AI output from one stage to the next. It passes structured data envelopes that carry the substantive content of the AI output alongside metadata required for pipeline observability, auditability, and debugging. A well-designed wire format includes, at minimum: a transaction identifier that traces the data item from its entry point into the pipeline through every transformation to its exit; a timestamp for each transformation, enabling latency analysis and performance monitoring; a stage identifier that records which pipeline node produced this version of the data; a status field that distinguishes between outputs that have passed validation, outputs that are pending evaluation, outputs that have failed validation and are quarantined, and outputs that require human review; and the substantive content in a consistent structured format appropriate to the content type. The wire format is the engineering infrastructure that makes a pipeline a system rather than a sequence of independent invocations. Without consistent wire formats, a cognitive pipeline cannot be debugged when it fails, cannot be audited when its outputs are questioned, and cannot be monitored for quality degradation over time. A pipeline that cannot be observed is a pipeline that cannot be controlled.

**Information boundary analysis** is the systematic examination of what information crosses each boundary between pipeline stages, what is retained in the transformation, what is added by the transformation, and what is lost. Every transformation in a cognitive pipeline — whether performed by an AI model, a human reviewer, a validation node, or a format converter — is an information transformation in the Shannon sense: it takes an input carrying some information, produces an output carrying some information, and the relationship between input and output information is determined by the transformation function. Information boundary analysis asks, for each boundary: what information is present in the input? What information is present in the output? What information was in the input but is not in the output — what has been lost? What information is in the output that was not in the input — what has been added, and from what source? The answers to these questions reveal the engineering properties of each stage and the potential failure modes at each boundary.

Unanalyzed boundaries are the primary origin of defects in cognitive pipelines. An unanalyzed boundary is a point in the pipeline where the practitioner has not explicitly characterized what information passes, what is lost, and what is added. At such boundaries, the pipeline designer has made implicit assumptions about information content that may be incorrect, and that cannot be verified because the analysis has not been performed. Consider a pipeline that extracts key claims from a source document, passes those claims to an analysis stage, and produces a synthesis of the analytical conclusions. If the extraction stage is unanalyzed, the practitioner does not know whether the extraction reliably captures all key claims or systematically omits certain types of claims — those buried in subordinate clauses, those qualified with hedging language, those that depend on domain knowledge to identify as significant. If the analysis stage is unanalyzed, the practitioner does not know what information the analysis adds — is it drawing only on the extracted claims, or is it importing background knowledge from its training data that was not present in the source? If the synthesis stage is unanalyzed, the practitioner does not know what information the synthesis loses from the analysis — whether the synthesized conclusions accurately represent the analytical findings or whether the synthesis introduces a systematic bias toward certain types of conclusions. Defects that originate at any of these unanalyzed boundaries will propagate through the pipeline and be indistinguishable from correct outputs until a human reviewer with sufficient ICE performs a deep evaluation. Information boundary analysis converts these implicit assumptions into explicit claims that can be verified and monitored.

The practical method for information boundary analysis begins with enumeration: for each stage in the pipeline, list every distinct information element that the stage is expected to handle. For an extraction stage processing a scientific abstract, this enumeration might include: the claims stated in the abstract, the evidence types cited for each claim, the methodological limitations acknowledged, the confidence qualifications applied by the authors, and the citation structure. Once the expected information elements are enumerated, the analyst characterizes each element at each boundary: present and preserved, present and transformed, present in input but lost in output, absent in input and added in output. The resulting information boundary map reveals which elements are reliably propagated through the pipeline, which are vulnerable to loss, and which stages introduce information from outside the source document — the hallucination risk profile, translated into information-theoretic terms.

**Contract evolution** is the process by which semantic contracts become more precise, more demanding, and more comprehensive as the pipeline that they govern matures. This process has a natural sequence. The first stage is articulation: writing down the contracts that currently exist only in the practitioner's head. The entry-stage cognitive pipeline typically operates under implicit contracts — the practitioner knows what a good output looks like, evaluates outputs against that knowledge, and adjusts the prompt when outputs fall short. The first engineering discipline is to make this implicit knowledge explicit: to write down the input format, the expected output format, and the quality criteria that the practitioner is actually applying, even if imperfectly articulated. An imperfect written contract is better than a perfect implicit one, because the written contract can be examined, criticized, refined, and communicated to other practitioners and to the AI components that will be asked to enforce it.

The second stage of contract evolution is operationalization: converting quality criteria from natural language statements into evaluable operations that can be executed programmatically. A criterion of the form "the summary must be accurate" is a natural language statement that a human expert can apply but that a QC node cannot execute without further specification. Operationalization converts it: "An AI evaluator will receive the source document and the summary and will return a structured judgment containing a factual accuracy score from 1 to 5, a list of any claims in the summary not supported by the source, and a binary flag indicating whether the summary omits any claims that the evaluator identifies as central to the source's argument." This operationalized criterion can be executed by an AI evaluator model, its outputs can be recorded in the wire format, and its aggregate performance over time can be tracked as a quality metric. The transition from articulation to operationalization is the transition from knowing what quality means to being able to measure it.

The third stage is automation: building QC nodes that execute the operationalized criteria on every output, at full pipeline throughput, without requiring human intervention for routine evaluations. Automated QC is not a replacement for human QC — it is the mechanism that makes human QC efficient by ensuring that human reviewers see only the outputs that automated evaluation has flagged as uncertain or out-of-specification. A mature pipeline routes all outputs through automated evaluation, routes flagged outputs to human review, and routes a statistical sample of passing outputs to human spot-check evaluation — which serves both as a validation of the automated evaluation criteria and as an exercise of the practitioner's instinct that prevents ICE atrophy. This three-tier QC architecture — automated full coverage, human targeted review, human statistical sampling — is the operational expression of the first principle of CSE: determinism in systems built from non-deterministic components.

Each improvement to a contract — a more precisely articulated quality criterion, a more operationally specific evaluation rubric, an additional information element added to the boundary analysis — is a kaizen in the sense used in Toyota's production philosophy. Taiichi Ohno understood continuous improvement not as a program or a project but as a structural feature of a well-designed production system: the process of identifying the next constraint, improving it, and raising the floor permanently. In cognitive pipeline engineering, each contract improvement raises the minimum quality level that the pipeline can reliably produce. The quality floor cannot be raised by running the same pipeline harder or faster. It can only be raised by improving the specifications that govern each component and the evaluation criteria that assess each output. Contract evolution is the CSE practitioner's primary mechanism for quality floor improvement over time.

Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety applies with particular force to quality specification. The variety of possible AI outputs for any non-trivial prompt is enormous: outputs may vary in format, in factual content, in reasoning quality, in tone, in the claims they emphasize, in the errors they commit, in the information they hallucinate. A single quality criterion — however precisely articulated — can only evaluate one dimension of this variety. A criterion that checks factual accuracy cannot detect poor reasoning. A criterion that checks format compliance cannot detect factual error. A criterion that checks tone appropriateness cannot detect omission of key information. Because the variety of possible failure modes exceeds the detection capacity of any single evaluation criterion, semantic contracts must be multi-dimensional: multiple criteria, each evaluating an independent dimension of output quality, each contributing an independent verdict to the overall QC judgment. The practitioner who believes that a single, carefully worded quality criterion is sufficient to evaluate AI outputs has not reckoned with Ashby's Law. The variety of the specification must match the variety of the failure space, and the failure space of an AI component is vast.

The multi-dimensional contract also has practical implications for QC architecture. When quality is evaluated on multiple independent dimensions, failures can be localized to specific dimensions, which enables targeted remediation. A pipeline that is producing outputs with acceptable format compliance and acceptable factual accuracy but poor reasoning quality has a diagnosis — and the diagnosis points to a specific contract dimension, which points to a specific prompt element or model configuration that governs reasoning quality. Without multi-dimensional contracts, failure is holistic and undifferentiated: outputs are acceptable or unacceptable, and the engineer cannot determine which dimension of quality is failing without manual examination of individual cases. Multi-dimensional contracts are the foundation of diagnostic transparency in cognitive pipeline engineering.

The practical progression for a CSE practitioner approaching a new pipeline stage is as follows. Begin with information boundary analysis: characterize what information enters the stage, what is expected to exit, and what transformations the stage performs. Draft the input specification, output specification, and initial quality criteria, even if informally. Write the wire format schema. Identify which quality criteria can be immediately operationalized and which require further articulation. Build the first QC node, however simple. Deploy the pipeline and begin collecting outputs. Use practitioner ICE to evaluate outputs against the contract criteria, refining the criteria where evaluation produces uncertainty or disagreement. Convert refined criteria into operationalized evaluation rubrics. Automate the operationalized criteria. Establish the statistical sampling protocol. Review the allocation of sub-tasks in the stage against the formal allocation criteria. Document the evolved contract. Repeat the cycle.

This progression is not linear in practice. Contract articulation reveals information boundary assumptions that require re-analysis. Operationalization attempts expose quality criteria that were not as well-defined as they appeared. Automation exposes edge cases that manual evaluation missed. Each iteration produces a more precise contract, a higher quality floor, and a more observable pipeline. The semantic contract is not a document completed before deployment and archived thereafter. It is a living specification that evolves with the pipeline, encoding the practitioner's deepening understanding of what the pipeline does, what it should do, and how to tell the difference. That living quality — the contract as an evolving artifact of engineering learning — is what distinguishes CSE from mere AI deployment, and what makes the discipline capable of producing knowledge production systems that improve reliably over time.
