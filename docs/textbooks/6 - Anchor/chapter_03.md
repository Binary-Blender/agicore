# Chapter 3: The Board Call

There were seven of them on the screen.

Karen had set up the call in the small conference room next to her office because the large boardroom on the fifth floor had felt, when she had walked into it that morning and stood for a moment in the doorway looking at the long mahogany table, like a room that had been built to contain a different kind of conversation than the one she was about to have. The small conference room had a round table and a single screen on the wall and a window onto the harbor that she had asked her assistant to close the blinds on, because she did not want the light from the window competing with the screen, and because she did not want the harbor in the room while she did what she was about to do.

She sat with her notebook open in front of her and watched the board members arrive on the Zoom one by one.

Charles Trent, the board chair, was in his home office in Atlanta. He had been the CFO of a regional bank for thirty years before retiring onto the boards of half a dozen Southeastern companies, and he had the comfortable bearing of a man who had spent his career being the smartest person in rooms that did not require very much smartness. Charles had been on the Anchor board for nine years. He had pushed hardest, three years ago, for the automation initiative. The McKinsey deck had been his deck before it had been anyone else's.

Marguerite Boudreau was in her car, parked, Karen thought, in the lot of a hotel in Tampa. Marguerite was a brand consultant who had served on the Anchor board for four years and whose contribution at board meetings tended to take the form of careful questions about messaging that, Karen had come to understand, were not really questions but framings.

David Lin was in an office in Singapore. David ran a private-equity portfolio that included a small position in Anchor Mutual and a larger position in two of Anchor's competitors. He had supported the automation initiative because the modeling on the cost savings had been substantial and because the competitors were doing it.

Theodore Hawes-Pemberton was in his study in Charlottesville, with a wall of books behind him that Karen had over the years come to suspect were arranged less for reading than for backdrop. Theodore had been Karen's father's lawyer in the late 1990s and had moved onto the Anchor board after Tom retired. He was eighty-one. He listened more than he spoke and when he did speak it was usually to read aloud from a printed agenda in a way that slowed the conversation.

Rebecca Voss was in her kitchen in Atlanta, with a coffee cup visible behind her laptop. Rebecca had been brought onto the board two years ago to bring what the search firm had called *technology fluency*, by which they had meant that Rebecca had spent fifteen years at a payments company and had the vocabulary. Her support of the automation had been technical-sounding and, Karen now suspected, less informed than it had presented as.

Helen Whitmore was in her study in Greenwich, Connecticut. She had her glasses on. She had a notepad in front of her. She had a pen in her hand. She had not yet spoken since the call had begun. Karen had registered, with the part of her mind that registered such things, that Helen had logged on three minutes early and had sat quietly on the screen while the others trickled in.

The seventh face on the screen was Karen's own, in the small self-view tile in the upper corner, and Karen had been training herself for six years not to look at it.

She began.

---

She did not editorialize.

She had spent the weekend writing the walkthrough by hand on a yellow legal pad — the pad Bea had picked up for her at the Office Depot on King Street on Saturday morning when Karen had said she did not want to do this on a screen — and she had written it the way she had been taught to write claims memoranda when she was twenty-six and working in her father's company as an analyst. Names. Dates. Documents. The sequence of what had happened. The language of the policy as it had been bound. The categorization the estimator had recorded. The determination the model had made. The flag the investigator had filed. The disposition of the flag.

She walked the board through it.

She named Pete Beauregard. She named Hollis Wakefield. She named the date the policy had been bound and the specific endorsements Pete had recommended and Hollis had written. She named the estimator's report and read aloud the single phrase that had been the correct categorization — *wind damage with secondary water intrusion* — and she paused after she had read it. She named Marisol Tavares. She read aloud Marisol's one-line note, which she had asked Bea to retrieve from the system and print out on a single sheet of paper. She named the Governance Escalation Layer and described what it was — an automated system reviewing another automated system's flagged cases — and she watched, on the Zoom, several of the board members register what she was telling them in the small slow way that adults registered new information when the information was something they were going to be responsible for.

She named the four appeals. She named the senior adjudication specialist whose digital signature had been on the fourth denial letter and who had been laid off six weeks before the letter went out. She named the date the letter had arrived at Doris Mercer's kitchen table.

She did not name what had happened at the kitchen table that night. The board had read the AP article. They knew.

She finished. She closed her notebook. She looked at the screen and waited.

There was a silence on the Zoom of perhaps four seconds. Four seconds, on a corporate Zoom call with seven directors of a publicly mutual insurance company, was a long silence. Karen watched the faces. She watched Helen Whitmore make a single small note on her pad and set her pen down.

Charles spoke first.

*Karen, that's — thank you for the thorough walkthrough. I think — I want to be careful here that we don't, in the heat of a specific case, lose sight of the broader picture. Every adjudication system has failure modes. The aggregate performance of the automation has exceeded the projections we approved three years ago. Our loss ratios are down two points. Our cycle time is down forty-three percent. The Mercer case is — terrible, of course, but the question of whether this is representative of a systemic problem versus an outlier is precisely the question the regulator is positioned to assess, and our exposure on the regulatory side is, my understanding from Bea is, manageable.*

Karen wrote down the word *manageable* on her notebook.

Marguerite spoke next.

*Karen, I do want to flag — and I'm not disagreeing with anything Charles said — that from a messaging standpoint we need to be very thoughtful about how the company is positioned in the next news cycle. I'd like to see, before any external communication, a draft messaging framework that we can socialize with the board. I would also gently suggest that the language we use internally about this case — and I'm not pointing at anyone, I just want to be thoughtful — that the language we use internally tends to migrate into external posture, and I think we want our internal posture to be careful. Compassionate, of course. But careful.*

Karen wrote down the word *careful*.

David Lin spoke. He spoke briefly. He said that he had read the AP article and the Times feature and that he wanted to understand what the financial exposure looked like across all potentially similar pending claims, and that he assumed Bea was developing that analysis, and that he would like to see the analysis before the next regular board meeting. He did not editorialize beyond that. He was, Karen thought, the most honest of the men on the call, in that he did not pretend the question he was asking was about anything other than what it was about.

Theodore Hawes-Pemberton cleared his throat. He read aloud, from a printed agenda Karen had not sent him, the four bylaws sections that governed director liability in the event of regulatory action against the company. He read them slowly. When he was finished he said: *I think we should be sure we are all clear on our duties of care.* He did not specify which duties.

Rebecca Voss spoke. She said that she wanted to understand whether the automation's failure mode in the Mercer case was a model-architecture issue or a training-data issue or an operational-process issue, because the appropriate corrective action depended on which it was, and she would like a technical briefing from the CTO at the earliest opportunity. The question was, Karen registered, a fluent question that did not, when she examined it, ask anything that mattered. She wrote *training data* on her notepad and put a small line through it.

Helen Whitmore had not spoken.

Karen waited.

---

Helen had been on the Anchor board for six years. She had come on board, originally, as an institutional-investor representative when a Hartford pension fund had taken a sizable position in Anchor during a period when the company had been undervalued relative to its book. The pension fund had since rotated out of the position, but Helen had stayed on as an independent director, and Karen had over the six years come to depend on her in a quiet way she had not articulated to anyone, including herself.

Helen had spent fifteen years as the chief operating officer of a Tier-1 auto manufacturer in Michigan. Before that, she had spent twenty years rising through the same company, beginning on the line in a parts plant in Flat Rock, then moving into supervision, then into plant management, then into corporate operations. She did not talk about it often. When she did, she talked about it the way people talked about long marriages — with affection for the specifics and impatience with the generalizations. Karen had once asked her, at a dinner before a board meeting, what she had been proudest of in her years at the manufacturer. Helen had thought about the question for a long moment and had said: *I worked under three CEOs who knew the difference between a quality system and a quality department.* Karen had not, at the time, understood what the difference was. She thought she might now.

Helen took off her glasses. She set them on the notepad in front of her. She leaned slightly toward her camera.

*I have one observation,* she said. *And one question.*

The Zoom went still.

*The observation is that I have been reading these case materials for three days and I think Karen has done an extraordinary job of presenting them plainly. I want to say that on the record. Karen, thank you.*

Karen registered the *on the record* and registered also that Helen was, with that phrase, asking the company secretary to put it in the minutes.

*The question,* Helen said, *is something I have been thinking about since I first read the AP piece six weeks ago, and I have not been able to stop thinking about it, and I would like to put it to the board now.*

She paused. She picked up her pen. She set it back down.

*Our manufacturing facilities,* she said, *have had safety stop-cords for fifty years. Anyone on the line can pull them. A line worker, a janitor, a visiting auditor. They pull the cord. The line stops. Production halts. Supervisors converge. The problem is investigated. The line restarts only when the problem is resolved. We built this into our facilities because we learned, expensively, in the 1970s and 1980s, that if you do not give the people closest to the work the authority to stop the work, the work will produce defects that compound until the defects destroy you. The cord is not a feature. The cord is the architecture.*

She paused again.

*Why don't we have one here?*

The Zoom was quiet in the way that a Zoom went quiet when seven people did not, simultaneously, know what to say.

Karen watched the screen. Charles Trent's face had not moved. Marguerite Boudreau was looking down at something off-camera. David Lin was, Karen could see, writing on a notepad of his own. Theodore Hawes-Pemberton was sitting very still. Rebecca Voss had her mouth slightly open in the way of someone who had been about to say something and had decided not to.

Helen did not press. She picked up her glasses and put them back on. She looked at Karen through the screen.

*I do not have an answer to my own question,* she said. *I am asking the question because I think it is the question. I would like the board to consider it.*

Charles, to his credit, recovered first. He said that Helen's observation was thoughtful and that he believed the board should take it under advisement and that he would like to schedule a working session in the coming weeks to consider operational governance changes in light of the Mercer matter. Karen wrote down *working session* and did not put a line through it. She did not yet know whether she would put a line through it later.

The call wound down inconclusively. There would be a follow-up meeting. There would be a technical briefing from Aiden Cross. There would be a financial-exposure analysis from Bea. There would be a messaging framework from Marguerite. There would be, eventually, a working session on operational governance.

Karen thanked them. She ended the call.

---

She sat in the small conference room with the lights off.

The blinds were still drawn on the harbor window. The only light in the room was the soft blue glow of the closed Zoom on the wall screen, which would shut off in another two minutes when the system idled out, and Karen could have stood up and turned on the overhead lights, but she did not stand up.

She thought about Helen.

She thought about the fifteen years in plant management. She thought about the cord — the actual physical cord — that hung above a moving assembly line in a factory in Michigan, and about the workers who had been told, by management, that they were authorized to pull it. She thought about how long it had taken, after the cord was first installed, before any worker actually believed the authorization. She thought about the first time one of them did. She thought about the supervisors who had converged. She thought about whether they had been angry or whether they had been grateful, and she thought that the difference between those two responses was probably the entire history of how a company learned to do its work.

She thought about her father.

Tom Holloway had been sixty-eight years old when he handed Karen the company. He had walked her around the eighth floor on her first afternoon as CEO and had introduced her to thirty-one adjusters by their first names, and he had told her, in the elevator on the way back down, that the people on that floor were the company and that everything else — the building, the underwriting models, the reinsurance treaties, the brass elevators — was scaffolding. She had been forty-four years old at the time and she had taken his hand on the brass railing of the elevator and squeezed it because she had not, at that moment, been able to think of anything to say.

Hugo had hit the coast in September of 1989. Karen had been seventeen. Her father had spent forty-eight hours straight in the building, on the corner of the third floor where the claims surge team had set up cots, working a phone bank with twenty-six adjusters and four temps from a Charleston agency. He had come home on the third morning and had sat at the kitchen table and had eaten the eggs Karen's mother had made him and had said, to the table more than to anyone in particular, *We are going to keep every promise we made.*

He had. Anchor had paid every covered Hugo claim in full. Some of them at significant company loss. The next year had been brutal financially. The decade afterward had been the foundation of the company Karen had inherited.

A claim is a promise we made to someone. The company is the keeping of the promise.

The Zoom on the wall screen went black. The room went fully dark except for the line of streetlight along the edge of the blinds. Karen sat in the dark for what she later thought was about ten minutes, though she did not check.

She did not know yet what she was going to do.

She knew that Helen's question would not leave her any more than Doris Mercer's question had left her, and she knew that the two questions were the same question, and that they had been the same question for longer than she had been willing to see, and that she was going to spend, she suspected, the rest of her career, however long it lasted, building an answer to them.

She knew that the call she had just taken would be the call she remembered as the one before everything.

She stood up. She opened the blinds. The harbor was dark — it was past seven — and the lights on the channel buoys were the only things visible on the water. A container ship that she did not recognize was moving slowly south, lit from within by the dim industrial yellow of working light.

She turned off the conference room and walked back to her office and gathered her things and rode the brass elevator down to the lobby and walked out into the October evening. The rain had stopped. The air smelled of harbor salt and of the bakery two doors down that had been doing its evening cleaning. She drove home with the radio off.

She did not yet have the answer. But she had begun, she understood now, to know the shape of the question, and the shape was the shape of a cord that hung above a moving line, and that anyone could pull, and that no one at Anchor Mutual was permitted to pull, because no one at Anchor Mutual had ever been given one.
