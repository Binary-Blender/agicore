# Chapter 9: The 0.001%

The claim opened on Marisol's screen at nine forty-three on a Tuesday morning in late June, and from the way it had been routed — the soft amber flag in the top-right corner of the queue interface, the small notation *Human review required: ambiguity in exclusion 4(b)(iii)* — she understood, before she had read a single line of the file, that the model had done what the model was now built to do. It had read the policy. It had read the loss report. It had pulled three precedents from the case-law repository. It had drafted a four-paragraph analysis and had identified three legally defensible interpretations of the ambiguous exclusion language, with the case law associated with each. It had calculated the dollar exposure under each interpretation. It had, at the end of the analysis, declined to render a determination, and had instead written a single sentence in the field reserved for that purpose: *This file warrants human judgment; the recommended path is not technically derivable from the policy language as written.*

Marisol read the sentence twice.

Then she closed her email, told her calendar to hold her calls for the afternoon, and opened the file.

The claim was from Coletrane Marine Provisions, a family-owned warehousing and supply business based in Edisto Beach, South Carolina. The owner was a man named Hank Coletrane, third-generation. The business had been founded by his grandfather in 1948 and had been continuously operated by the family since. Coletrane Marine Provisions supplied coastal shrimping and crabbing operations from the Edisto inlet down to the Georgia line — rope, line, traps, refrigeration parts, bait. The warehouse was a concrete-block structure that sat at the head of a small commercial dock on the South Edisto River. Two weeks earlier, on the night of June 10th, a tropical storm that had not quite organized into a hurricane and had not quite stayed out to sea had come ashore between Beaufort and Edisto, and had brought a storm surge that the National Weather Service had not predicted, and had taken the warehouse off its foundation and dropped it into the river. The dollar loss, including inventory, was three point four million.

The policy was clear on most points. The policy was not clear on exclusion 4(b)(iii).

Marisol made coffee. She took the coffee back to her desk. She read the policy.

The exclusion in question was a hybrid wind-and-water provision of a kind that had been written in the late 1990s by a reinsurance pool that no longer existed and that had been imported, more or less wholesale, into the standard commercial form Anchor used for coastal businesses of this size. The language defined a "covered storm-related loss" through a complicated chain of conditions involving the proximate cause of the damage, the duration of the wind event, and the relative timing of any associated water intrusion. It had been written, Marisol could see, by someone who had not anticipated the specific case in which a storm surge from a tropical system that had not been classified as a hurricane would inundate a structure that had sustained no measurable wind damage prior to its displacement into a tidal river. Such a case, the policy implied, would be either fully covered or fully excluded depending on how the proximate cause was interpreted, and the interpretation was not, in the language as written, determinable.

The model had identified this. The model had identified the three available interpretations. The model had identified the precedent — two cases that supported a covered finding, one that supported an excluded finding, and a fourth, more distantly relevant, that had been decided on grounds that did not cleanly map to this fact pattern. The model had laid the precedents out in a table.

Marisol read the policy a second time. She read the loss report. She read the four affidavits Hank Coletrane's attorney had submitted with the claim. She read the engineering report from the structural assessor Anchor had sent down to Edisto the week after the storm. She made notes on a yellow pad. She drew a small diagram of the warehouse relative to the dock, the river, and the prevailing wind direction during the storm.

She read the policy a third time.

It was twelve thirty.

She closed the file on her screen. She put on her jacket — the eighth floor ran cold in the summer, and she had been keeping a jacket on the back of her chair for twenty-five years for that reason — and took the elevator to the ground floor. The building had a loading dock on the south side, a holdover from its previous life as a shipping company's headquarters, and the loading dock was still used by the delivery trucks that brought the office supplies and the equipment and the occasional pallet of records up from storage. Marisol went out onto the dock and stood at the railing and watched the trucks.

There were two trucks at the dock. One was being loaded with what looked like decommissioned office furniture. The other was idling. The driver was on his phone, sitting on the running board of the cab, eating something out of a paper wrapper. The air smelled like diesel and the river. It was June in Charleston and the heat was the kind of heat that you noticed only when you stopped moving. She stopped moving.

She thought about her father.

Her father had run a fish-supply business out of a concrete-block warehouse in Apalachicola, Florida, between 1971 and 1989. He had sold ice and rope and net and chum to the working boats. He had hired four people at his peak and at his lowest had been the only person in the building, and he had worked the counter and the receiving dock and the books out of one office that had a window-mounted air conditioner that ran continuously and a calendar from the local feed store that he had never changed off the month it came in. He had been, by every measure Marisol had used as a child to measure adults, a man of dignified competence. He had also, in 1987, lost the business in a hurricane that had taken the roof off the warehouse and ruined six months of inventory and that the insurance carrier had partially covered and partially denied on grounds her father had not, at the time, fully understood. He had spent two years paying back the difference. He had died in 1991 of an illness that was not directly related to the hurricane but that, in Marisol's accounting, had begun in 1987 and had continued unabated for four years.

Hank Coletrane was forty-eight years old, the file said. He had two children, the file said. The family had owned the warehouse for seventy-six years, the file said.

Marisol watched the diesel truck idle for ten minutes. The driver finished his food, balled up the wrapper, threw it into the cab, and rolled the truck slowly away from the dock. The other truck closed its doors. The dock was empty.

She went back upstairs.

---

She did not call Hank Coletrane right away.

She read the policy one more time. She read the affidavits one more time. She pulled up the two covered-finding precedents the model had surfaced and read both opinions in full, not just the headnotes — the full text, the dissents, the procedural posture, the language the courts had used. One of the opinions had been written by a federal magistrate in the Eastern District of North Carolina in 2014, in a case involving a marina that had been similarly displaced by a tropical storm of approximately the same wind strength. The magistrate had written, in a paragraph Marisol found herself rereading: *The policy was written to protect the policyholder against the storm. The exclusion was written to protect the carrier against losses unrelated to the storm. Where the proximate cause is the storm, the burden of an ambiguity in the exclusion language falls on the drafter of the policy, not on the policyholder.*

Marisol underlined that on her pad.

She sat with the file for another thirty minutes. Then she picked up the phone and dialed the number on Hank Coletrane's claim.

He answered on the third ring. *Hank Coletrane.* The voice was tired in the way of a man who had been answering the phone all week and bracing.

*Mr. Coletrane. This is Marisol Tavares. I'm an adjuster at Anchor Mutual. I'm calling about your claim.*

He was quiet for a moment. *Yes ma'am.*

*I'm not calling to take a statement. I have the file. I have the affidavits and the engineering report. I have what your attorney sent us. I'm calling because I have some questions I'd like to ask you directly, and I'd like to understand the building, and I'd like to understand the night of the storm. If that's all right with you.*

There was a longer pause.

*All right,* he said.

They talked for thirty-five minutes.

He told her about the warehouse. He told her about the dock. He told her about the night of the storm, which he had spent at his mother's house in Walterboro because his wife had not wanted to ride out the storm at Edisto and his mother had needed someone with her. He told her how he had driven back the next morning at first light and what he had found and what he had done in the first hours after. He told her about his grandfather, who had built the warehouse in 1948 out of cinder block sourced from a quarry in Ridgeland that had since closed. He told her about his father, who had taken the business over in 1976. He told her about his daughter, who was sixteen and who had been working summers at the counter since she was twelve. He answered Marisol's questions about the wind that night, and the water, and the timing, and the structural condition of the building in the weeks before the storm. He answered them carefully and without elaboration. He had been asked many of these questions before.

Marisol took notes. She did not interrupt him. When he finished she said *thank you* and told him she would have a determination to him within forty-eight hours and that she would call him before he received the letter. He said *yes ma'am, I appreciate that.* The call ended.

Marisol set the phone down.

She wrote the determination herself. It took her three hours. She did not use the template. She wrote it in plain English, in the first person, on a fresh document. The determination ran three single-spaced pages. It cited the two covered-finding precedents and explained why the third precedent did not control. It walked through the language of exclusion 4(b)(iii) and explained, in three paragraphs, why the ambiguity in that language could not, in good faith, be resolved against the policyholder under the facts as established. It quoted the magistrate's paragraph from the 2014 case. It ruled the loss covered, in full, at three point four million.

She signed it.

She submitted it into the new tracking system, the one Aiden's engineers had built six months earlier, where every human determination above the model's complexity threshold was logged and tagged and fed back into the training set. The system asked her to indicate whether she would consent to the determination being used to update the model's interpretation of similar policy language going forward. She clicked yes. The system asked her to indicate whether she would be available for follow-up by the data team if they had questions about her reasoning. She clicked yes. The system asked her to provide three keywords that captured the load-bearing aspects of her judgment. She typed: *proximate cause; ambiguity; drafter's burden.*

She submitted the form.

Then she emailed Hank Coletrane the determination.

She did not include a cover note. The document spoke for itself, or it didn't, and she had decided long ago that cover notes on determinations were a way for adjusters to soften decisions they were not certain of, and she was certain of this one.

---

Twenty minutes later her phone rang.

She knew who it was before she picked it up.

*Marisol Tavares.*

*Ma'am.* The voice was shaking. *I — I just read your letter.*

*Yes sir.*

There was a long pause. She could hear him breathing. She could hear something in the background — a fan, maybe, or an air conditioner. The kind of background sound that came through a phone from a small office in a small town in the middle of a June afternoon.

*Ma'am, I —* his voice broke. He stopped. He started again. *I want to thank the person who looked at this.*

Marisol held the phone.

She did not say anything for several seconds. The eighth floor around her was at its mid-afternoon pitch — Tarun two cubicles down on a call with one of the data engineers, David Park reading a file at his workstation, the soft sound of the floor functioning as the floor had been built to function. The harbor through the window was bright. She could see, far out, the dark shape of a freighter moving north along the channel.

*I looked at it,* she said. *My name is Marisol Tavares. I am glad we could help.*

Hank Coletrane was crying. He did not try to hide it. He said *thank you* several times, in a voice that had given up on composure, and Marisol stayed on the phone and let him say it. She did not say *you're welcome* and she did not say *it was the right thing*. She said *yes sir* once and *yes sir* again and the third time she said *I'm here*. He pulled himself together after a minute. He apologized. She told him no apology was needed. They spoke for another two minutes about logistics — the paperwork that would follow, the timing of the payment, the name of the person in the claims office he should call if any question arose — and then the call ended and Marisol set the phone down on her desk.

She sat in her chair for a long time.

She did not move. She did not look at her screen. She looked at the window. The freighter had cleared her view and was now visible only as a dark line on the far side of the harbor mouth, moving in the slow way of large ships, and the light on the channel was the high blue light of a Lowcountry June afternoon, and the marsh past the channel was the deep green it took on at this hour.

She had been at this company for twenty-five years.

For most of those years she had handled cases like this one. She had handled them well. She had taken pride in the work in a way that she had not, in the last several years, taken pride in anything she did at the office. The years between the rollout of the automation and the rebuild had been the worst stretch of her professional life, and she had not been sure, during those years, whether she would survive them or whether she would quit, and she had decided not to quit for reasons she had not been able to articulate to her husband or to herself except by saying *somebody has to be here when it falls down*. It had fallen down. It had been rebuilt. She had not been promised that she would feel anything in particular when the rebuilt thing worked. She had not let herself want to feel anything in particular. She had only wanted the work to be possible again.

She thought about her father, sitting at his desk in the office in Apalachicola in 1987, working through the carrier's denial letter for the third time, looking for the language he could push back against.

There was no one to call.

He had been dead thirty-five years.

She picked up her pen and made a note on her yellow pad — a follow-up item, a small thing about the case, the name of the structural assessor she wanted to thank for the clarity of his report — and she put the pen down and turned back to her screen, and the next file in her queue was loading, and the soft amber flag was already visible in the top-right corner of the interface, and she clicked it open.
