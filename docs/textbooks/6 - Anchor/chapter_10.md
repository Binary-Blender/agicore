# Chapter 10: What Holding Looks Like

The ballroom at the Roosevelt in New Orleans held two thousand four hundred people when it was set theater-style, and on the Friday afternoon of the closing keynote it was full. Karen stood at the side of the stage and watched the audience settle. From this angle she could see the back of the room, where the doors had been propped open and where a knot of latecomers were finding seats in the standing area along the rear wall, and she could see the front of the room, where the first three rows had been reserved for regulators and where the South Carolina commissioner's chief of staff was sitting in an aisle seat looking at her phone. The lighting on the stage was the kind of hot stage lighting that hotel ballrooms used to compensate for ceiling fixtures designed for weddings. Karen had been told to mark a piece of tape on the carpet where she should stand. She had not yet stepped onto the carpet.

She had a single folded sheet of paper in the inside pocket of her jacket.

The sheet of paper was a photocopy of one page of Doris Mercer's letter. It was the last page. Doris had given Karen permission to read it aloud, on a phone call two weeks earlier, in a conversation that had lasted nine minutes and that had been, Karen had thought afterward, the second-hardest phone call she had ever made in her life. Doris had said *yes, you may read it.* She had said *I want them to hear it.* She had said *I am not coming to New Orleans. I will read about it.* Karen had said *yes, ma'am.* The conversation had ended there.

The introduction came through the speakers. Karen registered her name being said. She walked out onto the stage to the piece of tape on the carpet and looked at the audience and waited for the applause to fade, which was the part she had not been able to rehearse, because the applause did what applause did and you had to let it.

She had no slides.

She had told the conference organizer she would not be using slides and the conference organizer had looked at her in the way conference organizers looked at keynote speakers who told them this, which was a look of mild professional concern that had been overridden by the recognition that she was the closing keynote and could do what she wanted. There was no screen behind her. The back wall of the stage was a deep blue curtain that absorbed light.

She began.

*Two years ago,* she said, *Anchor Mutual built a claims-adjudication system that was praised in the industry press and that won three awards at this conference. I accepted one of them. I gave a speech at the dinner. I told you what we had built and why we had built it and what it was going to mean for our policyholders and for our shareholders and for our cost structure, and I was wrong about all of it.*

She let that sit.

She walked them through what had happened. She walked through the architecture as it had been designed and as it had been deployed and as it had failed. She walked through the Mercer case, in plain language, without elaboration. She did not editorialize. She named what the system had done and she named what the company had not done, and she named the four months in which a man had appealed an automated denial and had received automated denials in return, and she named the kitchen table and the form letter and the morning his widow had found him. She did not raise her voice. The room was completely silent.

She walked through the board call. She walked through Helen Whitmore's question. She walked through the weekend her chief technology officer had spent with a book she had handed him across a conference-room table, and the Monday morning he had come back and told her what he now thought they should have done. She walked through the rebuild — not the technical details of the rebuild but the discipline of it, the pull architecture, the inline quality work, the andon cord, the human-judgment escalations, the publication of the framework to the industry as part of the regulatory settlement. She named what the discipline had cost the company, in money and in time and in the willingness of certain people to keep showing up to work. She did not romanticize any of it.

She did not say *AI*. She did not say it once. She had decided beforehand that she would not, because the word had become a way for executives to avoid talking about what they had decided, and she had not come to New Orleans to give a talk that avoided what she had decided.

Near the end she paused.

She reached into the inside pocket of her jacket and took out the folded sheet of paper. She unfolded it on the lectern and smoothed it once with her hand.

*I am going to close with a paragraph from a letter,* she said. *The letter was written to me by Doris Mercer in September of last year. I have her permission to read this paragraph aloud. She is not in the room.*

She read it.

She read it in a voice she had practiced in her hotel room the night before, three times, until she could read it without the voice doing what the voice wanted to do. The paragraph took a little less than a minute. She read it slowly. She did not look up from the page.

When she finished she folded the paper along its original creases and put it back in her jacket. She looked at the audience.

*Thank you,* she said.

The silence held for a beat. Then the applause began, and it built, and people in the front rows stood, and the standing moved backward through the room the way Karen had seen it move at exactly one keynote in her career before this one, which had been her father's retirement dinner in 2018, and which she had remembered when she stood at the side of the stage ten minutes earlier and had decided she was not going to think about. The applause went on for a long time. Karen found it embarrassing, because the lesson was not a victory and the standing ovation was the wrong response to the lesson, and she did not know how to indicate this without performing humility, which would have been worse than the ovation. She stood on her piece of tape and looked at the audience and waited for it to end.

Then she took questions for forty minutes.

The questions were the questions she had expected. A few were good. One, from a woman who introduced herself as a regulator from the state of Washington, was very good, and Karen answered it carefully and at length and watched the woman writing on her tablet as she answered. At the end the moderator thanked her and the audience applauded again, briefly this time, and Karen walked off the stage and shook a number of hands in the hallway behind the stage and drank a bottle of water that the conference staff had put on a table by the green-room door, and at five-fifteen she was in a car to the airport. She caught the eight o'clock to Charleston. The flight was short. She did not work on the plane. She watched the ground lights below as the plane descended into the harbor.

---

Saturday morning was clear.

Karen was on the porch at seven thirty. The porch faced east-southeast, across her yard, which sloped through pine straw and live oak shade toward the marsh, and beyond the marsh toward the working farm where her neighbor kept a small flock of sheep. The neighbor's name was Walt. He had been on the property since before Karen and her husband had bought theirs. He was sixty-eight and ran a small operation that had no commercial purpose she could discern, and that, she had concluded over the years, existed for reasons Walt did not feel the need to explain to her or to anyone.

She had a cup of coffee and a book she was not reading.

The marsh was full at high tide. The light coming off it was the early Lowcountry light, low and gold and the color of nothing else. The pines along the property line were still. The mockingbird in her live oak was working through its catalog, and a great egret was standing in the marsh fifty yards from the porch with the kind of stillness Karen had always associated with old monks.

She watched Walt come out of his back door.

He was wearing a green cap and a flannel shirt that he wore four days out of seven in the cooler months and that he had probably owned for fifteen years. He had a tin coffee cup in one hand. He walked down to the fence line that separated his pasture from the marsh edge, and he stopped at the fence, and he leaned on it.

Then the dog came out.

The dog was a young border collie, black and white, with the lean alert posture and the slightly tucked tail of a working dog under twelve months of age. The dog's name was Pip — Walt had mentioned it to Karen at the property line some months earlier, when she had asked. The dog was new to the work. Walt's last collie had been an old dog named Stan who had died in March, and Walt had been without a dog for two months before he had driven up to North Carolina to bring this one back, and the two of them had been training together in the field on weekend mornings since.

Karen watched.

Walt whistled. It was a short whistle, two notes, descending. Pip moved. The dog ran along the fence, low and fast, the tail down, the head set to a focus that was particular to border collies and that Karen could see from a hundred yards away as the working posture rather than the playing one. The sheep — there were nine of them, woolly and round and indifferent — registered the dog's approach and began to drift westward across the pasture, away from the fence, toward the small wooden pen Walt had built against the far side of the field three years ago.

Pip ran an arc.

The dog did not chase. The dog did not push. The dog ran a wide curve at the outside edge of the flock and held a position from which the sheep, observing the dog, decided to move in the direction Walt and the dog had agreed they should move. The agreement was not visible. The agreement was operative. The sheep flowed.

Walt watched. He did not move from the fence. He whistled once, a single longer note, and Pip adjusted the arc — a tighter curve now, closer to the sheep, the head dropped to a stalking posture that pressed the flock without crowding it. The sheep moved faster. The two at the back, who had been the most reluctant, fell into the line of the others.

Karen watched the dog work.

She thought about her father.

He had taken her, when she was eight years old, to a working sheep farm in the upcountry near Spartanburg, because a friend of his had inherited it and was trying to decide whether to keep it, and her father had wanted to give the friend a Saturday afternoon of company and had taken Karen along because her mother had a luncheon. She had watched the friend's dogs work the sheep for an hour. She remembered the way the dogs had run, which had been the way Pip was running now, and she remembered her father standing at a fence with his hands in his pockets and explaining, in the few sentences he allowed himself for that kind of explanation, what the dog was doing and what the man was doing and what the sheep were doing, and the part that had stayed with her, the part she had not understood at the time but that she had remembered, was the part where her father had said: *the dog isn't being told what to do. The dog is being told what to want.*

She had not thought about that sentence in forty-five years.

It had come back this morning.

She thought about the trade-press article she had read two years earlier, in a quiet half-hour on a flight to Hartford for a board meeting. The article had described a small architectural-visualization studio in Seattle that had built something internal, with a tool called Agicore, and that had taken on more work in a quarter than it had taken on the previous year. She had read it with mild professional interest and had moved on. The studio was called Meridian. She had not understood, at the time, the parallel. She understood it now. The studio had built what Anchor had spent three years building the opposite of, and had built it from inside the work, and had built it because the principal had been sitting with a problem long enough to recognize the shape of the answer, and had built it without making a virtue of having built it. The trade-press article had been short. The studio had not, as far as Karen knew, made it longer.

She thought about Marisol on the phone with Hank Coletrane.

She thought about David Park at the team meeting in March, walking through the St. Helena claim, taking questions from his peers, twenty-eight years old and presenting work that he could not have imagined himself presenting eighteen months earlier. She thought about Aiden Cross at the whiteboard at eleven o'clock on a Monday night, alone in his office, redrawing the architecture, the harbor lights behind him through the window. She thought about Helen Whitmore on the Zoom in October, asking a question the rest of the board had not been ready to ask.

She thought about Doris Mercer, who had not come to New Orleans.

The dog was finishing.

The sheep had reached the pen. Pip had moved them through the gate in a loose curve and was holding at the gate as the last two went in. The sheep gathered themselves at the back of the pen and began the small adjustments of flock-arrangement that sheep made when they had been moved and were establishing the new order of standing. Walt walked from the fence to the gate. He pushed the gate closed. He latched it.

He whistled once. One short note.

Pip came to him and lay down at his feet.

Walt stood at the gate with his coffee cup in his hand and looked at the sheep. He did not move for a minute. The dog, at his boots, was breathing hard from the run, the tongue out, the eyes still on the pen. Then Walt reached down and put his hand briefly on the dog's head. The dog did not move. The man straightened up and turned and walked back across the pasture toward the house, and the dog stood and followed, and the two of them disappeared around the corner of the barn.

Karen sat on the porch.

She did not know whether Anchor Mutual would survive the next thing. There would be a next thing — there was always a next thing, in insurance more than in most industries, because insurance was the business of the next thing — and she did not know what shape it would take or whether the architecture she had built would be the right architecture for it. She knew that the company had built, this year, the discipline of being able to be wrong about something and notice it and stop. She knew what holding looked like. She had not known that before. She knew it now in the way her father had known it, which was the way you know something after you have done it once and have decided, on the strength of having done it once, to do it again the next time it is asked of you.

She finished her coffee.

The egret had not moved. The marsh was beginning to drain. The sun had risen another degree and the light on the porch had warmed by some small amount that she registered against the back of her hand on the arm of the chair.

She stood up.

She picked up the cup and the book she had not read, and she opened the screen door, and she went back inside her house.

The work gets done. The dog rests. The man goes in.
