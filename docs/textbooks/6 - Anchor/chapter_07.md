# Chapter 7: The Junior Adjuster

The coffee was getting cold and David was not getting up to refresh it.

He had noticed, twenty minutes earlier, that the cup was beyond the temperature at which he ordinarily would have walked to the break room. He had registered the noticing and then continued reading. The file on his screen — open in the new dual-pane viewer that Aiden's team had rolled out in October, the policy on the left and the claim narrative on the right and the model's surfaced annotations in a thin gray column between them — was not a complicated file by Anchor's volume standards. A water-damage claim from a townhouse in West Ashley, fifty-eight-thousand-dollar exposure, the policyholder a retired schoolteacher named Mrs. Calhoun who had been with Anchor for nineteen years. The model had handled the routine work. The model had flagged one thing for his attention: a discrepancy between the date of loss the policyholder had reported and the date of the weather event the policyholder had cited as the cause.

A year ago David would have looked at the discrepancy, would have noted the model's confidence score on the *non-covered cause* classification, would have clicked through to the denial template, would have signed the denial, would have moved on. He had done this several thousand times. The clicks had blurred together into a single click he had been performing for three years, the way the gas pedal of a long commute blurs into a single act of pressing, and he had stopped, at some point during his second year, being able to remember individual cases by their detail. He had been able to remember them only by their volume.

He was reading the file now in a different way.

The model had flagged the date discrepancy. The model had not concluded anything from the discrepancy. The model had surfaced it with a single line of context: *Reported date of loss precedes the cited weather event by approximately thirty-six hours; consider whether policyholder may be reporting the date of discovery rather than the date of occurrence.* The note was in the gray column, in eleven-point type, with a small flag icon next to it.

David had read the note. He had read the policy. He had read the claim narrative twice.

The note was correct. The policyholder, in her narrative, had described going down to the basement *the day after the storm came through* and finding water on the floor. She had given the date of that discovery as the date of loss on the claim form. The form did not ask her to distinguish. The form had been designed, three years earlier, by people who had assumed that the model would handle the distinction automatically.

He picked up the phone.

He had spent the morning making phone calls. This was a thing he had started doing in October and was still getting used to. He had not, until October, used the phone on his desk for outgoing calls in any volume. The phone had been there. The phone had received the occasional internal transfer. He had been told, in his first year at Anchor, that calling policyholders was an old practice and that the new architecture preferred written correspondence because written correspondence was logged and the model could parse it. He had taken this at face value. He had not, until he started working with Marisol in October, thought to question it.

Marisol had said, in their second mentoring session: *You learn things on the phone you cannot learn on paper. The thing they say first. The thing they say after a pause. Who is in the room with them. Whether they have been waiting for you to call.*

He dialed Mrs. Calhoun's number.

She picked up on the third ring. Her voice was the voice of an older woman who had been expecting a call from a doctor's office. She rearranged it, audibly, when he identified himself and the carrier, and David recognized in the rearrangement the small additional alertness with which policyholders received calls from their insurance company, the alertness that anticipated bad news.

*Mrs. Calhoun,* he said, *I'm working on your claim and I wanted to ask you about the date you reported. You wrote down October the seventeenth. Can you walk me through what happened on the seventeenth?*

There was a pause.

*Well,* she said. *That's when I found it. The water. I went down to do laundry and the carpet was wet under the washer.*

*And the storm,* he said. *The one you mentioned. Do you remember when that came through?*

*Oh, that was the night before. The fifteenth, I think. Maybe the sixteenth. It was the one that woke me up around two.*

David made a note. He asked, gently, whether there was any chance the water might have been there before the storm. There was not, she said — she had done laundry the previous Sunday and the carpet had been dry. He asked when she had last had someone look at the roof. She said she had a roofer who came every spring and had been out in April and had said the roof was sound. He thanked her. He told her he was going to do a little more work on the file and that he would call her back by the end of the week.

He hung up.

He sat with the file for a moment.

The model had been right to flag it. The model had been wrong, by his old habit, to be read as denying it. The discrepancy was the discrepancy between *date of loss* and *date of discovery,* and the policy did not, when he read it carefully, require the two to coincide. The policy required that the date of loss be the date of the covered event. The covered event had been the storm of October fifteenth-sixteenth. The discovery had been on the seventeenth. The claim was covered.

He drafted the determination. He attached the policy citation. He noted the date reconciliation. He sent the file to the quality review queue, which was no longer the automated Governance Escalation Layer but a working queue staffed by a rotating pair of senior adjusters — Marisol was on it Tuesdays and Thursdays — and went to the break room and poured out the cold coffee and made a fresh cup.

It was eleven-forty in the morning. He had been at his desk for three hours. He had handled one claim.

A year ago he would have handled twenty.

He stood in the break room with the fresh coffee and watched the rain against the harbor-side window and thought about how he was going to feel about that ratio at the end of the day, and he found, to his surprise, that he felt good about it. He had handled one claim and the claim had been handled correctly. The policyholder was going to receive the determination by Friday. The roof was sound, the laundry had been dry on Sunday, the storm had been the storm, the water had been the water. Mrs. Calhoun was going to get her basement repaired before Christmas. He had been a part of that, in the small, particular way an adjuster was a part of it — not heroically, not visibly, just by reading the file and picking up the phone and asking the question and getting it right.

He drank the coffee. He walked back to his desk.

---

He had been at Anchor three years and four months. He had been planning, in September, to leave the industry.

He had not told anyone this. His parents, who lived in Los Angeles and had not entirely understood why he had taken a job in insurance in the first place, would not have been surprised. His college roommate, who was three years into a software job in Seattle and made twice what David made, had been hinting for two years that David should come west and that the hinting was not really hinting. David had been listening more than he had let on. He had updated his résumé in August. He had gotten as far as drafting a goodbye email to his manager when the Mercer story broke and the building changed pace.

He had not sent the email.

He had waited, instead, the way the rest of the building had waited, through the six weeks of silence that followed the regulator's announcement, through the board call that he had heard about secondhand, through the period in October when the engineers had started appearing on the eighth floor and the conversation in the break room had changed character. He had waited because he had not been sure, in October, what he was waiting to see. By the end of October he had begun to understand. By the second week of November he had taken the résumé off his desktop and put it in a folder he did not open.

The work was not the same work he had been doing. That was the first thing.

The second thing was harder to name. The second thing was that he was, for the first time since college, learning. He had been hired three years ago with the expectation that he would learn — he had been told, in the recruiter's pitch and again by the hiring manager, that the job would involve *complex edge cases that required human judgment,* and he had taken the job partly because that sentence had described the work he wanted to do. The sentence had not described the work he had actually done. The work he had actually done had been the work of clicking through screens. He had not learned from clicking through screens. He had not been able to remember the cases he had clicked through. He had not, in three years, developed in any direction that resembled professional development.

He had developed now in three months.

Marisol had been the instrument of the development. Marisol sat with him on Thursday afternoons for an hour. The session was on his calendar as *adjudication practice review,* which was a flat title that did not describe what the sessions were. The sessions were Marisol reading his files. The sessions were Marisol asking him, in her careful and slightly tired voice, why he had asked the question he had asked the policyholder. The sessions were Marisol pointing to a paragraph in a policy and asking him what the paragraph would have read like to the man who signed it in 1991. The sessions were Marisol, occasionally, telling him a story about a claim she had worked in 2008 or 1999 or 2003, the story unfolding the way an experienced practitioner's stories unfolded — with attention to the detail that turned out to matter and disregard for the detail that did not.

He was getting better. He could tell he was getting better. The files he closed in November were not the files he had closed in June. He read them differently. He held more in his head while he read them. He could see, looking back at June files, the things he had missed because he had been reading at the speed the old system had demanded of him. The old speed had not been a speed at which a person could see what an adjuster was supposed to see. He had not understood, until November, that this was the case. He had assumed the speed was the speed of the work. The speed had been the speed of the system.

He drank the rest of his coffee and pulled up the next file in his queue.

---

The team meeting was at three.

The team meeting on Friday afternoon was a new ritual that Aiden had started in October and that had, by the second meeting, become the kind of meeting people actually came to. It was held in the small conference room on the eighth floor, the one with the round table that Karen had used for Aiden's three-hour meeting in September, which had been moved out to make room for fourteen folding chairs. The format was simple. One adjuster presented one case. The case was usually a case that had gone non-routine. The presenter walked the room through the file, through the model's surfacing, through the decisions they had made, through the outcome. The room asked questions. Marisol, who chaired, occasionally added a note. The meeting was thirty minutes. The meeting had a hard stop.

David had presented twice before, both times on routine cases that had become non-routine in small particular ways. He had been nervous the first time and less nervous the second time. He was, today, presenting the St. Helena case.

The St. Helena case was the one Marisol and he had worked together two weeks earlier. A hurricane-damage claim on a vacation cottage on St. Helena Island, owned by a couple from Atlanta who had bought the property in 2019 and rebuilt the roof in 2022. The damage was substantial — the roof had partially failed during the November storm and rainwater had taken out the upstairs ceiling and a portion of the main floor below it. The model had flagged the case for the wind-vs-flood determination, which on the South Carolina coast was the determination that had become, after Carl Mercer, the determination Anchor's adjusters were trained to treat with the most care.

The model had surfaced a binding detail David had not initially understood.

He walked the room through it.

He had pulled up the slide he had prepared on the projector. The slide was a single screenshot of the policy binding document, with a clause highlighted in yellow. The clause was the *named windstorm endorsement* that had been added to the policy at renewal in 2023, after the rebuild. The clause was three sentences long. The clause was the kind of clause that, in the old system, would have been flagged as routine and applied without comment.

David explained that the clause had a sub-clause that referenced the structural improvements made at the 2022 rebuild. The sub-clause modified the windstorm coverage in a way that the model had flagged but had been unable to interpret, because the modification depended on a separate document — the contractor's certification of the rebuild's compliance with the post-2018 wind-uplift code — that was not in the binding file itself. The model had surfaced the dependency. The model had not been able to resolve it.

He and Marisol had pulled the contractor's certification from the underwriter's archive. The certification had been signed by a contractor in Beaufort whose certification number checked out. The certification had documented compliance with the post-2018 code. The compliance triggered the modification. The modification meant the windstorm endorsement covered the loss in full, irrespective of the wind-vs-flood determination on the rainwater intrusion, because the structural failure had been a windstorm failure under the modified endorsement and the rainwater intrusion was a consequence of the structural failure rather than a separate flood event.

The case went the policyholders' way. The payout was four-hundred-seventy-eight thousand dollars.

David walked the room through each step. He did not editorialize. He noted, near the end, that the model's flagging of the dependency had been the thing that had made the resolution possible — had the model not surfaced the binding-detail dependency, the case would have routed through the wind-vs-flood determination as a contested case and would probably have ended up in litigation, and the litigation would have gone the carrier's way on a strict reading of the wind-vs-flood language because the rainwater intrusion would have been the central fact. The structural endorsement would not have been read, because no one would have known to read it.

The model had known to read it. The model had not known how to interpret it. The model had handed it to a human. The human, with Marisol's help, had interpreted it.

David finished. He set down the laser pointer. The room asked questions for ten minutes — good questions, the kind of questions that came from adjusters who had worked similar cases and wanted to understand the specifics — and David answered them.

Then the questions slowed and Marisol thanked him and the meeting ended.

He gathered his notes. He did not, at first, see Karen.

She had come into the room sometime during his presentation and had stood in the back, near the door, with her arms crossed in the way she crossed them when she was listening rather than speaking. He noticed her when most of the room had cleared and she had not yet moved. She caught his eye briefly. She did not say anything. She gave him a small nod — the kind of nod that was a registration rather than a compliment — and then she walked out.

He sat at his desk for a long time afterward. He was writing the follow-up emails — the contractor needed a copy of the determination for his records, the underwriting team needed to be looped on the precedent for future renewals on similar structures, the Atlanta couple needed the cover letter and the payment schedule. He worked through the emails in a slow careful way that would have been impossible a year ago because a year ago he had not had time for slow careful and a year ago he had not had the kind of mental space to draft a cover letter to a policyholder that read as if a person had written it.

He finished the last email at five-forty-five.

He looked up.

Marisol was at her own desk, a few rows over, packing her bag. She looked across at him. He registered, the way he had registered Karen's nod, that Marisol was watching him with an expression he did not entirely have a word for — not pride, because pride would have been too large, but something in the family of pride, something that resembled the face his grandmother had worn when he had brought home a school project she had thought was real work.

She smiled at him. A small smile. She picked up her bag. She walked to the elevator without saying anything.

He sat at his desk. The rain on the eighth-floor window had started during the meeting and was steady now, a vertical rain on the harbor, the kind that came in November and would continue, off and on, until the first hard cold of December.

He thought, briefly, about the goodbye email he had not sent.

He shut down his computer. He took his coat from the hook by the elevator. He walked out into the rain and across East Bay Street to his car, and he drove home through the late Charleston afternoon with the wipers on intermittent and the harbor on his left, and he did not, for the first time in three years, think about leaving the industry.
