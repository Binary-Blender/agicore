# Chapter 4: Priya's Substack

The kettle had been making the sound kettles made before they began to whistle, the small interior boil that preceded the steam, for what Karen now realized had been at least four minutes.

She was standing at the kitchen sink. The water in the sink was running because she had begun to rinse a wineglass and had stopped halfway through to look at her phone, and the wineglass was in her hand, half-rinsed, the water still going. The kitchen light over the island was the one she and her husband had argued about three years ago when they had renovated, because Karen had wanted a warmer bulb and he had wanted a brighter one, and they had compromised on a bulb that was neither and that they had both, separately, come to resent. The clock on the microwave read 10:14. The house was quiet. Doug had gone to bed an hour earlier. The dog was asleep on the rug by the back door.

Karen looked at the phone.

She had been, for the last twelve days, reading.

She had read first the regulatory filings — the South Carolina Department of Insurance's preliminary report, the federal notice of inquiry from the Treasury's insurance office, the state attorney general's letter requesting voluntary document production. She had read these the way she had been trained to read regulatory documents, which was carefully and twice. Then she had read the AP article again, and the Times feature, and three subsequent pieces in trade publications, and had begun to read the comment threads under those pieces because she had wanted to know what other people in the industry were saying, and then she had begun to follow the names of the commenters back to whatever else they had written, and she had found, in this way, the larger landscape of how AI was being deployed in insurance and how some carriers had begun to talk about that deployment and how a smaller number had begun to talk about it differently than the rest.

She had read for ten nights running, two hours at a time, on her phone in the kitchen after Doug had gone to bed. She had read on the phone because she did not want to do it at her desk, and she had done it in the kitchen because the kitchen was where, in the geometry of the house, she felt least like a CEO.

The link to the Substack had appeared in a LinkedIn comment thread under a trade-press piece she had not particularly wanted to read in the first place. A commenter she did not recognize had written, beneath a long thread of carrier-defensive responses, *If anyone is interested in a different way to think about this, P. Kanth's piece on Adjustment last month is the clearest thing I've read all year.* The commenter had linked to it. Karen, on her phone at her kitchen sink with a half-rinsed wineglass in her hand and a kettle behind her four minutes into its preheat, had tapped the link.

The Substack was called *Adjustment*. The author was P. Kanth. There was no biography, no headshot, no list of credentials. There was a single epigraph at the top of the masthead, in italics: *On insurance, and other forms of attention.*

The post Karen had tapped through to was titled, in its full form: *What if we built AI to help adjusters do better adjudication instead of to replace them?*

The post was fourteen hundred words. Karen read it.

---

She read it standing at the sink. She read it once with the water still running and then she turned the water off and dried her hands and read it again, this time more carefully. The kettle behind her went from preheat to boil to whistle and she registered the whistle and did not turn it off for another forty seconds.

The post did not name Anchor Mutual. It did not name any specific carrier. It did not name the Mercer case, though Karen suspected, in the careful way the post described what it called *the recent failures of fully-automated coastal adjudication*, that the case had been one of several the writer had been thinking about. The post had been published seventeen days before the AP article had broken, which meant — Karen registered this with a small interior recalibration — that the writer had been thinking about these failures before they had become a story.

The post made an argument. The argument was structural and not rhetorical. The writer had read, the writer said, a book over the previous summer called *TAO: The Way of AI Orchestration*, which had been written by people who had spent decades in manufacturing operations and which had attempted to translate the operational discipline of the Toyota Production System into the discipline of building AI-augmented organizations. The writer was, the writer admitted, not in operations. The writer was a customer service representative at a mid-sized insurance company that had recently completed an automation initiative. The writer had begun the book skeptical because the manufacturing analogies had felt forced. The writer had finished the book convinced that the analogies had been not forced but precise.

The post walked through what it called the eight principles. It did not number them. It did not use the language of a textbook. It described them the way a person who had been reading about something at lunch breaks for several months described what she had been reading — with the small idiosyncratic emphases of someone who had been forming her own opinions about what mattered and what did not. The writer was particularly interested, she said, in the third and the sixth, which she described as *built-in quality* and *respect for the human*, and which she said were the two principles that the insurance industry's current deployments of AI had most thoroughly inverted.

Then the post made its central move.

The writer wrote: *We have been asking the wrong question. The question we have been asking is: how do we get the AI to do what an adjuster does? This is the wrong question because the AI is not, and is not going to become, an adjuster. The right question is the inverse. The right question is: what is the AI already good at, and how do we build adjudication around what it is already good at?*

The writer continued: *The AI is good at reading the policy language and surfacing the relevant clauses. The AI is good at organizing precedent. The AI is good at flagging the patterns in a claim narrative that warrant attention. The AI is good at the things a junior adjuster spends three years learning to do quickly. The AI is not good at the things a senior adjuster has spent twenty-five years learning to do at all — the call to the policyholder where you listen for what is not being said, the recognition that a policy's language and a policy's intent can diverge, the willingness to rule against the literal reading when the literal reading produces an injustice the policy was never meant to cause.*

The writer wrote: *Don't figure out how to get AI to do what you want. Figure out what AI is already good at and build with that.*

Karen read the sentence. She read it a second time. She did not yet say anything aloud, but the part of her mind that had begun, over the previous two weeks, to organize itself around a question she did not yet have words for, registered the sentence as the words.

The post closed with a short paragraph about what the writer thought a TAO-aligned claims architecture might look like in practice. A pull-based workflow rather than a push-based one. Human adjudicators in the position of conductors of an AI-assisted analytic pipeline rather than in the position of rubber stamps at the end of a model's classification. A quality system rather than a quality department. A halt mechanism — and here the writer used a phrase Karen had heard for the first time three days earlier from Helen Whitmore — *anyone on the line can pull the cord.*

The writer signed off with a single sentence: *I work at a company that did the other thing. I would like to be part of doing this thing instead.*

The post was dated three weeks earlier. It had, Karen could see by the small subscriber count visible to the writer but not to readers, seventy-eight subscribers. There were eleven comments. Two of them were from other insurance professionals saying versions of *thank you.* The rest were from people Karen did not recognize.

She put the kettle off.

She stood at the sink for what was probably a minute and probably four. She did not pour the water into the cup she had set on the counter. She looked at the byline.

*P. Kanth.*

She picked up her phone and opened the Anchor Mutual company directory app, which she had not used in perhaps a year and which required her to authenticate twice before it loaded. She typed *Kanth* in the search field.

There was one result.

Priya Kanth. Customer Service Representative. Hire date three years and four months ago. Floor: eighth. Department: Direct Customer Communications. Extension: 4-8821. Employee photograph: a young woman with dark hair pulled back, a small careful smile, the kind of professional photograph that had been taken on a Wednesday by an HR contractor and that Priya had probably had no input on.

Karen stood at the sink and looked at the photograph.

---

She wrote the email on her phone.

She wrote it three times. The first draft had been long and had attempted to explain the context — that she had been reading, that she had come across the post by accident, that she had not initially known the author was an Anchor employee. She had deleted that draft because she had decided, halfway through writing it, that the context was not the point. The second draft had been shorter and had asked for a meeting and had included a sentence about what the meeting might be for. She had deleted that as well. The third draft was three sentences.

*Priya — I read your post on Adjustment tonight. I'd like to come talk with you. Tomorrow morning if you're available.*

She read it twice. She added: *Karen Holloway.* She had not, when she had been about to send it, been certain whether the signature was necessary. Priya would see the email address. But the signature mattered, she thought, because the signature made it clear that the message had not been sent by an assistant.

She sent it at 10:47 PM.

She put the phone down and finally made the cup of tea she had been working toward for half an hour, and she sat at the kitchen island with the tea and looked at the dark window over the sink, which reflected her own face back at her in a way she had been avoiding for several days.

The phone, on the island next to her, lit up at 11:03.

She picked it up.

The reply was three lines.

*Mrs. Holloway — yes. I'm available any time you can come by. I'm on the eighth floor, cubicle E-14. I usually get in by 8:30. Thank you for reading the post.*

Karen read the reply. The phrasing of the last sentence stayed with her — the *thank you for reading the post*. She thought about what it might mean to write a Substack with seventy-eight subscribers in the lunch breaks of a job at a company that had just laid off most of the people whose work you were writing about, and to find, at 11 PM on a weeknight, an email from the company's CEO saying she had read it.

She did not yet know what she was going to do. But she knew that what she was going to do next, which was go upstairs and try to sleep, was going to be done by a person who had, an hour earlier, been a different person.

She put the cup in the sink. She did not rinse it.

---

She rode the elevator to the eighth floor at 8:42 the next morning.

She had been to the eighth floor twice in her tenure as CEO. The first time had been with her father, on the day she took the job. The second time had been the previous Thursday morning, when she had walked the floor briefly after Marisol had delivered the file, to see for herself what the floor looked like now. The walk had been short and she had not spoken to anyone. She had registered the chairs and the half-lit aisles and had gone back upstairs.

This morning she walked the floor with a destination.

Cubicle E-14 was in the central cluster of the floor, on the east side, in a row of cubicles that had been built for customer service representatives rather than for adjusters, and which were therefore smaller and were arranged more closely together, in groups of four sharing common acoustic dividers. Three of the four cubicles in Priya's cluster were empty. The fourth, on the diagonal opposite from Priya's, was occupied by a man in his fifties whom Karen did not recognize and who, when he saw Karen coming down the aisle, registered her with the small involuntary stiffening of someone who had recognized the company's CEO walking toward him at a quarter to nine in the morning. He looked back at his screen. He pretended he had not seen her.

Karen reached E-14.

Priya was at her desk. She was wearing a cardigan over a blue blouse and she had a coffee mug from the airport gift shop in Chicago O'Hare next to her keyboard. She was twenty-six or twenty-seven. She stood up when she saw Karen, the small physical reflex of a person who had been raised to stand up when an elder entered a room, and she said *Mrs. Holloway* in a voice that was steady but was working at being steady.

Karen said: *Please sit down. Karen is fine. May I take this?*

There was a folding chair leaning against the side of the cubicle. Karen took it without waiting for an answer and unfolded it and sat down so that she was at right angles to Priya rather than across from her, because she had decided in the elevator that she did not want to be sitting across from Priya as though this were an interview.

She set her notebook on the corner of Priya's desk.

*I read the post twice,* she said. *I would like you to talk. I would like to listen. I have one question at the end. Take whatever time you need.*

Priya looked at her.

She did not, for the first two minutes, speak. She picked up her coffee mug. She set it down. She looked at her keyboard. She looked at the cubicle wall, which had a printed quotation pinned to it that Karen did not, from her angle, immediately read but later identified as a line from Wendell Berry.

Then she began.

She was terrified, Karen could see, for the first ten minutes. She apologized too much. She qualified everything. She prefaced statements with *this is just my opinion* in the way of people who had been trained, by previous workplaces, that opinions were dangerous things to be observed having. Karen did not interrupt the qualifications. She let them happen. She watched Priya's hands, which had begun the conversation clasped together in her lap and had migrated, over the first fifteen minutes, to her keyboard and then to the edge of her desk.

By the twenty-minute mark, Priya was talking.

She talked about the call center. She had been hired as a customer service representative three years earlier, on a team that handled inbound calls from policyholders. She had taken something on the order of nine thousand calls over those three years. She had, in the first eighteen months, taken calls from policyholders who were calling about claims they had filed; in the second eighteen months, after the automation had gone live, she had taken calls from policyholders who were calling about claims that had been denied. The proportion had not been gradual. The proportion had shifted, in her recollection, over a period of about six weeks in the spring of the second year. There had been a Monday morning when she had come in and her queue had been entirely denial-related. She had not, that morning, taken a single call that was not a person asking why.

She had told no one. She had not had anyone, at the level of the company she occupied, to tell.

She had begun reading.

She had bought *TAO: The Way of AI Orchestration* in paperback at a customer-service-management conference in Atlanta the previous October, on her own dime because Anchor had not approved the conference budget that year, from a small table in the back of the exhibit hall where two of the book's authors had been signing copies. She had read the book in pieces, on lunch breaks and on the bus to work, over six months. She had read it twice. She had started, in the past three months, writing the Substack.

She had not told anyone at the company about the Substack. She had used her personal email to register it. She had been careful, in the posts, to avoid naming Anchor or any specific case or any specific colleague, because she had not been certain — she still was not certain, she said — what the company's policy was on writing publicly about the industry, and because she had not wanted to be fired before she had figured out what she actually wanted to say.

She had not, she said, ever expected anyone at the company to read it.

She paused. She picked up the coffee mug. She put it down.

She said: *I am sorry if I should have said something sooner.*

Karen had been writing on her notepad. She set the pen down.

*Priya,* she said. *You said something in the post. You wrote: I work at a company that did the other thing. I would like to be part of doing this thing instead.*

Priya looked at the notepad.

*Yes,* she said.

*I read that sentence,* Karen said, *three times.*

She did not say what she had been thinking when she read it. She did not need to.

The forty minutes had become forty-two and then forty-five. Karen had not noticed. The cubicle aisle outside had filled slightly — there were more sounds now, a phone ringing somewhere, two voices in conversation by the kitchenette — and the eighth floor had begun, in the muted way it now began, its working day.

Karen stood up. She closed the notepad.

She looked at Priya, who had not yet stood up.

*Priya,* she said. *I want to ask you one thing.*

Priya nodded.

*The book,* Karen said. *Could I borrow your copy?*

Priya looked at her for a moment. Then she pulled open the bottom drawer of her desk pedestal, and she reached in, and she lifted out a paperback whose spine had been broken in several places and whose cover had the small soft fraying at the corners that paperbacks acquired when they had been carried in bags. There were five or six small Post-it tabs of different colors sticking out of the page edges. The book had been bookmarked, Karen could see, with a folded receipt from a Subway sandwich shop.

Priya handed it to her.

*I have written in it,* she said. *In pencil. In the margins. I — please ignore that. I can give you another copy if you would prefer one that —*

*No,* Karen said. *I would like to read yours.*

She took the book. She held it in her hand and felt the weight of it. She looked once more at Priya, who was now standing, and who was looking at her with an expression Karen recognized, because she had worn it herself, a long time ago, when she had been twenty-six years old and her father had once asked her, at the kitchen table, what she thought about a particular case he had been working on.

Karen thanked her. She walked back down the aisle past the empty cubicles and past the man who had pretended not to see her and to the elevator, and she pressed the button for the tenth floor.

In the elevator she opened the book.

The Post-it tabs marked, she could see, the chapters Priya had returned to most often. The margins were dense with pencil — questions, small underlines, the occasional one-word annotation in a careful handwriting. On the inside front cover, in the same pencil, Priya had written her name and the date she had purchased the book and, underneath both, in slightly smaller letters, a single sentence she had not erased: *Start reading like you mean it.*

Karen rode the elevator up. She did not, on the ride, close the book. She read the first paragraph of the introduction with her free hand on the brass rail, and the elevator went on rising, and the rain on the harbor began again outside the windows of the lobby below, and Karen Holloway, who at fifty-three had run her father's company for six years and had until two weeks ago believed herself to be doing it well, began at last to read the thing she should have read three years ago.
