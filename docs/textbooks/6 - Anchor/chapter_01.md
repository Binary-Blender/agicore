# Chapter 1: The Letter

The harbor was slate-gray under the kind of low cloud that meant rain by noon. From the tenth floor Karen could see a container ship moving slowly north along the channel, its deck stacked four containers high, the colors of the containers — Maersk blue, China Shipping red, a single rust-orange Hapag-Lloyd in the middle — readable even at this distance because there was nothing in the sky to compete with them. The ship was the *Adriana*, she knew, because she had made a habit of checking the harbor traffic on her phone in the mornings. The *Adriana* moved past the office every Tuesday at around quarter past nine.

She turned from the window and sat down at her desk and opened the mail.

The mail came in two stacks. The first stack, which the assistant brought up every morning in a black mesh tray, was the printed correspondence — quarterly reports from carriers Anchor reinsured with, board materials for the upcoming meeting, a few personal notes from people who knew her father in the industry and still wrote, in cursive, on Crane stationery, even though they could have emailed. The second stack was the legal and regulatory traffic, which came in a separate cream-colored folder, handled by Bea Aldredge in the general counsel's office and only forwarded to Karen when Bea wanted her eyes on it. Karen always opened the cream folder first.

The cream folder was thin this morning. A notice from the South Carolina Department of Insurance, expected. A standing-order update from the federal terrorism backstop reauthorization, expected. A status memo on the Mercer investigation. Karen registered the third item and set it aside to read carefully after coffee. She had been reading those memos carefully for six weeks.

The first stack — the printed mail — had a letter in it that she did not expect.

It was an ordinary envelope, white, business-size, addressed by hand in blue ballpoint to her by name. The return address was written in the same hand on the back flap. McClellanville, South Carolina. The sender's name was D. Mercer.

Karen looked at the envelope for a long moment before she opened it.

She knew who D. Mercer was. Everyone in the building knew who D. Mercer was. The story had broken in early August, six weeks after the funeral, in a regional AP piece that had run in twenty-three papers before a *New York Times* feature picked it up and made it briefly national. A small-business owner in a coastal South Carolina village had spent the last four months of his life appealing a hurricane-damage claim that Anchor Mutual's automated adjudication system had denied as a flood event rather than a wind event. He had appealed four times. The first three appeals had been handled by the automation. The fourth, finally reaching a human, had been denied on the recommendation of the model's risk assessment. He had been reading the fourth denial letter at his kitchen table when his heart stopped. He was sixty-seven. His widow had found him the next morning. The widow's name was Doris.

Karen opened the envelope with the butter knife she kept in her desk drawer for opening envelopes.

There were six pages.

They were torn from a yellow legal pad, the kind sold at any drugstore, and they were written in the same blue ballpoint as the envelope. The handwriting was a woman's handwriting from a generation that had been taught penmanship in school. The lines were straight without being ruled. The paragraphs were indented. There were no crossings-out.

Karen read.

*Dear Mrs. Holloway,*

*I want to begin by saying that I do not write this letter in anger. I am writing it because I have read your company's mission statement, which is posted on your website, and I would like you to know what it looked like from where I was standing.*

*My husband's name was Carl Mercer. He owned Mercer Marine Supply in McClellanville, South Carolina, which he inherited from his father in 1981, and which he ran continuously for forty-three years. He sold rope and tackle and boat parts. He cut nylon line to length while shrimpers waited at his counter. He knew the names of three generations of fishermen on this coast. He was the kind of person other people called when they needed something.*

The letter went on. Karen read it as one reads a letter from a stranger that one has been waiting for without knowing it. The widow walked through the timeline of the appeal. The first denial, three days after the claim was filed. The first appeal, denied seventeen days later by what the widow now understood to have been an automated system, though at the time she had assumed there was a person involved. The second appeal, denied twenty-three days after that, also automated. The third appeal, denied after going to "external review," which she had also assumed meant a person and which she had later learned meant a different automated system maintained by a vendor. The fourth appeal, the final one, finally reaching what the form letter called a *senior adjudication specialist*. The widow noted, parenthetically, that she had since learned this person had ninety-seven other cases on her desk that week. The denial had cited the model's risk-classification score. The denial had run to seven sentences.

The widow described the four months of Carl's appeal in five paragraphs. They were the most carefully restrained paragraphs Karen had ever read in her life. The widow did not describe Carl's mood. She did not describe his health. She did not describe what watching her husband fight an automated denial system had done to him or to her. She described, instead, the routines. Carl had a folder on the kitchen table where he kept the correspondence. He read each letter twice when it came. He drafted his responses by hand and then typed them on the computer their grandson had set up for them. He stopped going to the marina coffee in the mornings because he did not want to talk about it. He started waking up at four. He started smoking again after twenty years.

The kitchen table was the table where he had his heart attack.

*The form letter that the senior adjudication specialist signed* — the widow had typed this part on the computer because she did not trust her hand for it — *informed us that the determination was final and that no further administrative appeal was available. The form letter recommended that we consult an attorney if we believed the determination to be in error. I would like you to know that the form letter arrived on a Wednesday, that Carl read it that night at the kitchen table, that he was reading it when his heart stopped, and that I found him in his chair the next morning still holding it.*

Karen put the letter down.

The container ship had cleared her window and was now visible only as a dark shape moving past the bridge. The light in the office had not changed. The sound of the building had not changed. Her hand was steady on the desk.

She picked the letter back up and read the last paragraph.

*Mrs. Holloway, I am eighty-one years old. I am not writing this letter because I want money. I have received settlement offers from two attorneys and I have declined them both, and I will continue to decline them. I am writing this letter because I want to ask you a question. I have asked it of myself a thousand times in the four months since Carl died, and I cannot stop asking it. The question is this. Was there ever a person who looked at this? In all the months Carl spent appealing, in all the letters he wrote, in all the documents he submitted, was there ever a person at your company who looked at our case and said, in their own mind, this is wrong? I would like to know. If the answer is yes, I would like to know why that person did not stop it. If the answer is no, I would like you to know that I think it should have been yes.*

*Sincerely, Doris Mercer.*

Karen folded the letter back along its original creases and slid it into the envelope and set the envelope on her desk.

She looked at the harbor.

The container ship was gone now. A small fishing trawler was visible far out toward Fort Sumter, moving south, the kind of boat that worked the inshore waters and came home every night. There was rain on the channel buoys. The light had not yet shifted in the office and would not shift for another hour, and Karen knew this because she had sat at this desk every Tuesday morning for six years and had watched the light shift every day, and she had not, until this morning, particularly noticed.

She thought about the board presentation three years ago.

Aiden Cross had been six months into his role. He had stood in the boardroom on the fifth floor with a projector display behind him and walked the board through the architecture. *Claims adjudication is a high-volume low-judgment workflow*, he had said. He had said it twice, in fact, separated by ten minutes — once at the beginning of the presentation and once near the end. Karen, sitting at the head of the conference table next to Helen Whitmore, had registered the phrase the second time and had thought, briefly, that it was a strange way to describe what her adjusters did, but she had not said anything because the consultants on the call from McKinsey had said something similar in their preparatory deck, and because the savings projections were substantial, and because she had been newly enough in the CEO chair that she was still calibrating which of her instincts to trust and which to mistrust as legacy reflex.

*Was there ever a person who looked at this.*

The question would not move. It sat in Karen's mind the way certain phrases did when the brain had decided they were important and had not yet decided what to do about it.

She thought about her father.

Tom Holloway had run Anchor Mutual through the storms of the 1980s. Hugo in '89 had nearly broken the company; her father had spent forty-eight hours straight in the building, sleeping on the couch in his office between phone calls, making decisions about which claims to fast-track and which to investigate and how to staff the surge in adjusters they had hired from the agency. He had said something to her once, when she was thirteen, that she had not understood at the time. *A claim is a promise we made to someone. The company is the keeping of the promise.* She had asked him what happened if Anchor could not keep a promise. He had said, *Then we say so. We don't pretend.*

The widow's letter was a record of pretending.

Karen reached for the phone and called her chief of staff.

Bea Aldredge answered on the second ring. *Karen.*

*I need to see the original claim file on the Mercer case. The original. Not the automation summary. The full underwriter's report, the original policy binding, all the case notes from the four months. I want it on my desk by Thursday.*

Bea did not say anything for a moment. Karen could hear her thinking. Bea had been general counsel at Anchor for nineteen years, and her job, much of the time, was to assess whether the requests she received from the CEO would survive contact with the legal and operational reality of the company.

*Karen, I'm not sure we still have the original. The case was migrated to the automation system three years ago. The original paper files were archived at the storage facility in North Charleston. They may have been digitized into the new system, in which case the binder is gone. They may not have been digitized, in which case the binder is still at the storage facility but I will need to send someone to find it. There are also case notes that existed only as written annotations on the underwriter's report and were not migrated to the digital system because they were classified as exploratory.*

Karen registered the word *exploratory*.

*Find it,* she said.

*Yes.*

*All of it, Bea. Whatever exists. I want to see what we had when the case was first filed. I want to see what was in the binder. I want to see what the adjuster who first looked at it wrote down. If there was an adjuster who first looked at it.*

*I'll get it to you by Thursday. Karen, can I ask what's prompted this?*

Karen looked at the envelope on her desk.

*I received a letter,* she said. *From Doris Mercer.*

Bea was quiet for several seconds.

*I'll get it to you by Thursday,* she said again. *I'll go to North Charleston myself if I have to.*

Karen thanked her and hung up.

She turned back to the harbor. The rain had begun on the window. It was the small, steady, vertical rain that came in October on the South Carolina coast and that, in her father's generation, the older men in the marine-supply trade had called widow's-walk weather, because it was the rain that came when the storms were past and there was nothing left to do but watch the channel and wait.

The widow had used a yellow legal pad and a blue ballpoint pen, and she had asked the only question that mattered.

Karen sat at her desk in the office her father had occupied before her, and she did not know yet what she was going to do, but she knew that the question had become her question now, and that she would not have peace from it until she had an answer she could give back.

She slid the envelope into the upper-left drawer of her desk and locked it. She did this for reasons she did not interrogate. She turned to the regulatory memos in the cream folder and began to read them, this time, properly.

The rain on the window thickened. The light in the office, for the first time, began to shift.
