# Chapter 8: The Regulator

Interstate 26 ran inland from Charleston in a long flat line under a high May sky, and from the passenger seat Karen watched the marshland give way to pine, the pine give way to scrub, the scrub give way to the low industrial sprawl that announced the approach of Columbia. The morning had been clear when they left and would be clear all day, the kind of South Carolina day that arrived in May and stayed for a week and made you forget that the summer would be unsurvivable. Bea Aldredge was driving. Bea preferred to drive on these trips because Bea preferred to be the one who knew where the next exit was, and Karen, who had been driven on this road by other general counsels before Bea and who would be driven on it by other general counsels after, had long ago stopped negotiating it.

They did not speak for the first hour.

Karen had a manila folder in her lap. The folder was thin. Inside it were six pages, printed single-sided, double-spaced, that she had drafted in her office over the past three weeks and revised seven times. The document was titled, plainly, *Adjudication Architecture: Anchor Mutual Casualty, Post-Restoration*. It had no executive summary. It had no appendices. It had no logos. Bea had read it twice and had asked, the first time, whether Karen wanted to add a section on metrics. Karen had said no. The second time Bea had read it she had said nothing and had handed it back. That had been the version Karen brought.

The car passed the exit for Orangeburg.

*The thing I want her to feel,* Karen said, more to herself than to Bea, *is that we are not here to make this go away.*

*She'll feel it,* Bea said.

*She has heard a lot of corporate apologies.*

*She has,* Bea said. *That's why she won't have to hear another one.*

Karen looked at the folder.

She had not slept well the night before. She had sat at her kitchen table at two in the morning with a cup of tea she had not drunk and had read the document one more time, looking for the sentence she would want to take back if Esther Vargas asked her to read it aloud. She had not found one. That was both a relief and not a relief, because there were sentences she would have preferred to soften and had decided not to soften, and the decision was load-bearing in a way she had only recognized at the kitchen table at two in the morning.

The car turned onto Senate Street. The Department of Insurance occupied a building of the kind state governments built in the 1970s, square and beige and faintly indifferent, with a parking lot that held thirty cars and that today held nineteen. Bea parked in a visitor space near the entrance. Karen did not get out immediately.

*Ready,* Bea said. It was not a question.

*Ready,* Karen said.

---

Esther Vargas was sixty-three years old and had spent twenty-eight of those years as a plaintiff's trial attorney before taking the commissioner's seat, and Karen had been told by three separate people in the industry that Vargas was a woman who had heard every variant of every corporate explanation that had ever been offered for any insurance failure in the state of South Carolina, and that she received them all with the same expression, which was a kind of polite professional patience that did not soften and did not harden and did not particularly indicate what she was thinking. The expression was sitting across the conference table from Karen now. Vargas had a yellow legal pad in front of her and a black ballpoint pen, and she had not yet uncapped the pen.

Two staff attorneys from the commissioner's office sat to Vargas's left. Bea sat to Karen's right. The conference room had one window, facing a parking lot that included a state-issued Chevrolet and a mature live oak that had been growing in the median since before the building was built.

*Mrs. Holloway,* Vargas said. *I understand you have a document.*

*I do.*

*Would you like to walk me through it.*

*Yes,* Karen said. *If you'll allow me to walk through it without apologies, I think it will be a better use of your time.*

Vargas's expression did not change. *I will allow that.*

Karen opened the folder.

She did not read from the document. She walked Vargas through it from memory, because she had written it from memory and the act of reading it aloud would have been a kind of distancing she did not want. She began with what the system had been — the push architecture, the volume-optimized adjudication pipeline, the appeals automation, the absence of any mechanism by which a person, anywhere in the company, could stop a denial that had been generated by the model. She named these things without ornament. She did not say *we believed at the time* or *with the benefit of hindsight*. She said *this is what we built, and this is what it did, and this is what we are doing about it.*

Then she walked Vargas through the new architecture.

She used the language her own people had developed in the months of the rebuild. She described the pull workflow, in which claims arrived in human queues at points the model itself surfaced rather than at points the model had concluded. She described the inline quality verification, the small persistent checks built into every step of the process, the way an adjuster's judgment was now captured and fed back into the model's training set instead of being overridden by a downstream governance layer. She described the andon cord — she used the phrase, and she watched Vargas register it — the mechanism by which any adjuster on the floor could halt a determination at any point, for any reason, without needing to escalate, without needing to justify, and without consequence to their performance metrics. She described the escalation rules. She described the points in the workflow at which a human judgment was required by design rather than by exception. She described, finally, the public dashboard the company had built to monitor its own adherence to the architecture, and named the four metrics it tracked, and named the threshold at which the system would force a leadership review.

Vargas had uncapped the pen by then. She was writing.

*Who designed this,* Vargas said, when Karen paused.

*Aiden Cross. He's my chief technology officer. He designed the original automation. He has rebuilt it.*

*The same person.*

*The same person.*

Vargas looked at her. *Why.*

*Because the original strategy was the failure, not the engineer. He understood what he had built once he understood what it was for. I would not have asked someone else to redesign it. He owed it to the company. He has paid it.*

Vargas wrote that down.

*And the policyholder.* Vargas put down the pen. *In your architecture. Where is the policyholder.*

Karen had expected the question. She had prepared an answer. The prepared answer fell away as she heard the question spoken, because Vargas had asked it the way a trial attorney asked a question to which she already knew the answer she wanted to hear, and Karen understood that the only answer worth giving was the one that came from underneath the prepared one.

*The policyholder is the reason the company exists,* Karen said. *We forgot that for three years. We are remembering it now. Operationally that means a human being looks at every claim above a defined threshold of complexity, and a human being is available to talk to every policyholder who requests one, and the determination on every claim is signed by a person whose name the policyholder can see and to whom the policyholder can speak. There is no automation between the policyholder and the person responsible for the determination. That is non-negotiable in the new architecture. It will be non-negotiable in any future architecture we build.*

*Respect for the human,* Vargas said, and it was not a question.

*Yes.*

Vargas wrote that down too.

The meeting went for two hours and forty minutes. Vargas asked questions in the patient unhurried way of someone who was not in a rush to get to the end. The questions probed the architecture at every point where Karen had expected it to be probed, and at three points where she had not. Twice Karen answered honestly that she did not know the answer and would have her team send it to Vargas's office by the end of the week. Bea wrote those down. Vargas accepted the answers without comment.

Near the end Vargas asked the question Karen had been waiting for.

*Mrs. Holloway. If we enter into a structural settlement here, the framework you have just described becomes part of the public record. Other carriers will read it. Your competitors will read it. Some of them will adopt parts of it. You understand this.*

*I do.*

*Are you prepared to have built it for the industry rather than for your shareholders.*

Karen looked at the live oak through the window. It moved very slightly in a wind she could not hear.

*We will publish the framework,* she said. *We are not the only carrier that needs it. The architecture is not a competitive advantage. The work of operating it is the competitive advantage. Anybody can read what we built. Not everybody will do what we have done to build it.*

Vargas closed her legal pad.

*Counsel will draft language,* she said. *We will have it to you by Friday. I am inclined toward a moderated fine and a publication requirement and a two-year compliance monitoring schedule. I am not inclined toward criminal referral. I will retain the option, and I will revisit it if anything in your subsequent conduct gives me reason to.*

*Understood,* Karen said.

*Mrs. Holloway.*

*Yes.*

*Thank you for not bringing me an apology.*

Karen did not know how to respond to that, so she did not respond. She stood. Bea stood. They shook hands across the conference table — Vargas's hand was small and cool and firm — and Karen and Bea walked out of the conference room and down the hallway and out the front doors of the building into the South Carolina afternoon.

---

The parking lot was warm. The pavement gave off the kind of mid-spring heat that promised more heat. The live oak stood at the median.

Karen did not get into the car.

She stood with her hand on the door handle for a long moment and looked at the building she had just walked out of. Bea stood on the driver's side and did not press her. There was no one else in the parking lot. A grackle was calling from the live oak. The sound of traffic from Senate Street came through faintly, the way traffic always came through in state-capital neighborhoods that had been built before the highways were widened.

*Bea,* Karen said.

*Yes.*

*Doris Mercer should hear this from me directly. Before the press release. Before anyone else.*

Bea looked at her across the roof of the car.

*That's right,* Bea said.

*I have her number in my phone. I have had it since the letter.*

*Do you want to call from here, or from the road.*

Karen thought about it. *From the road. I want a few miles first.*

They got into the car. Bea pulled out of the parking lot and headed back toward the interstate, and Karen sat with her phone in her hand and the manila folder on the floor at her feet, and she did not yet make the call. She watched the strip malls give way to scrub and the scrub give way to pine. The radio was off. The air conditioning was on at low. Bea did not speak. They passed the exit for St. Matthews. Karen unlocked her phone and scrolled to the contact, which she had entered, eight months ago, the day after the regulatory investigation had been formally opened, against the possibility that this moment would come. The contact was titled *Mercer, Doris*.

She pressed the button.

The phone rang four times.

A woman's voice answered. It was a voice from a generation that still said *hello* when it picked up a phone, and that did not say it as a question.

*Hello.*

*Mrs. Mercer,* Karen said. *This is Karen Holloway. I'm the chief executive officer at Anchor Mutual. I'm calling because I wanted you to hear from me before you heard it from anyone else.*

There was a pause on the other end.

*Go ahead,* Doris Mercer said.

The call lasted twelve minutes.

Bea did not listen, or rather she did the thing a good general counsel did, which was listen without listening, which was hold a kind of attentive silence that registered presence without registering content. Karen kept her voice low and steady. She told Doris what had happened in Columbia. She told her what the settlement terms would be. She told her that the architecture Anchor had rebuilt would be made public, and that Anchor would name what it had gotten wrong, and that the company would carry the weight of that naming for as long as the company existed. She did not ask Doris for forgiveness. Doris did not offer any. Doris asked two questions. Karen answered both of them. There was a long pause near the end of the call during which Karen thought Doris had set the phone down, but Doris had not set the phone down. Doris had only been thinking.

At the end Doris said one thing.

Karen said *yes, ma'am*. She said it twice. The first time was reflex; the second time was meaning. Then she said *thank you* and Doris said *all right* and the call ended.

Karen lowered the phone into her lap.

She looked out the window. They were passing the exit for Holly Hill. The pines were doing what pines did at this latitude in the middle of the afternoon — standing very still, holding the heat, suggesting nothing.

Bea, after a long time, said: *Are you all right.*

Karen did not answer immediately. She was watching the road.

*I am going to be,* she said.

Bea nodded and kept driving. The car moved south through the long flat coastal plain, and the marshland began again where the pine ended, and the light on the marsh in the late afternoon was the particular gold-green light that the Lowcountry produced and that nothing else produced, and Karen sat with the phone in her lap and the folder at her feet and watched it until they reached the bridge.
