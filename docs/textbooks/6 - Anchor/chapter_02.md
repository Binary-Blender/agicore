# Chapter 2: Marisol's File

The chairs were the worst of it.

Marisol had been telling herself, on and off for a year and a half, that she had stopped noticing the empty workstations on the eighth floor, that the eye adjusted the way the eye adjusted to anything sufficiently constant, that the half-lit aisles between desks had become a kind of weather and no longer registered as absence. This was not true. She noticed every morning. She noticed because the chairs had been left at slightly different angles by the people who had stood up from them for the last time, and no one had pushed them in. Whoever was responsible for the chairs — facilities, perhaps, or the office manager whose job had also been rolled into a vendor contract — had not been told, or had not cared, that the chairs needed to be straightened. So the chairs stood at the angles they had been left at. Some pushed back hard against the desks. Some pulled out far enough that you could see, if you bent down, the dust on the casters. One on the south aisle, three desks down from Marisol's own, was turned slightly toward the window, the way a person might turn a chair to look at something during a phone call.

That chair had belonged to a woman named Antonia Reyes, who had spent twenty-two years at Anchor and who had cried, briefly and silently, in the third-floor conference room on the day her position was eliminated. Marisol had been on the floor that day. Antonia had come up after the meeting and gathered her things into a bank box, and the chair had been the way she left it because she had stood up from a phone call to take the meeting and had never sat back down in it. That had been eighteen months ago. The chair had not been straightened.

Marisol kept her eyes on her own monitor and worked.

The request had come down from the tenth floor on Tuesday evening. Bea Aldredge had walked to Marisol's desk personally, which Marisol registered as significant — Bea did not come to the eighth floor any longer; the eighth floor came to Bea — and had spoken in the lowered voice that lawyers used when they were asking for something they could not put in writing yet. The Mercer file. The original. The unredacted underwriter's report. The policy language as it had stood at the time of binding. The internal case notes from the first three months, before the file had been migrated. The CEO wanted to see it. Not the automation summary. The original.

Marisol had said: *I can have it for you by Thursday morning.*

Bea had said: *Thank you.*

She had not asked why. She had not had to. Marisol had watched Bea walk back toward the elevators and had thought, with the small detached precision she used for things she was not yet ready to feel, that Bea was walking the way people walked when they were carrying something heavier than the thing they were carrying.

That had been Tuesday. It was now Thursday morning, and the file was on Marisol's desk, and she had been sitting with it for the better part of an hour without opening it.

---

The file was thicker than she remembered. Three inches of paper in a navy-blue Anchor Mutual binder of the kind they had stopped ordering in 2019, when the document-management migration had gone live, and which Marisol had quietly continued to use for the cases she thought would need a paper trail. The Mercer case had not initially struck her as one of those cases. A coastal small business, a wind-versus-flood determination on a hurricane claim, the kind of work she had done a hundred times. But she had pulled a binder for it anyway, because she had been an adjuster long enough to know that the cases you thought were simple were the cases that taught you, late, what simple meant.

She opened the binder.

The top sheet was the policy declarations page, dated 2008. Carl Mercer had bound the policy with Anchor on a Tuesday in October of that year, through an independent agent named Hollis Wakefield whose office had been on Pinckney Street and whose handwriting Marisol recognized because she had worked alongside Hollis on a hundred coastal bindings over the years. Hollis had been retired now for six years. He had died in 2023. His handwriting on the declarations page — the small block-printed notations in the margins indicating the specific endorsements he had recommended for a marine-supply business in McClellanville — had outlived him.

The next sheet was the underwriter's report. Twelve pages, single-spaced, with a hand-drawn parcel map clipped to the back. The underwriter had been a man named Pete Beauregard, also retired now, who had specialized in coastal commercial risks and who had walked the Mercer property himself in 2008, photographed the building, talked to Carl for an hour, and made notes that Marisol now read for the first time in seventeen years.

Pete had written, in the section labeled *Hazard Assessment*: *Property situated on raised commercial lot 2.1 miles inland of mean high water. Building constructed 1979, masonry block on poured slab, gable roof on engineered trusses. Wind exposure primary; storm-surge exposure secondary and improbable except at Category 4+ landfall within twelve nautical miles. Recommend standard wind binding with coverage extension for business personal property at full replacement.*

Pete had recommended, and Hollis had bound, what Pete had recommended.

Marisol turned the page.

The next forty pages were the policy itself, with the endorsements bound in their proper sequence and the riders annotated in Pete's handwriting where his notes on the hazard assessment had translated into specific language. She read the wind endorsement carefully, and the named-storm endorsement, and the exclusions, and the appurtenances clause. She read them the way she had read policies for twenty-five years, which was not as a lawyer reads but as a practitioner reads — with attention to what the language was *for*, to the conditions the underwriter had been pricing against, to the promise the policy had been written to keep.

The promise the policy had been written to keep, in this case, was specific. Pete had bound Carl Mercer's business against wind damage from named tropical storms, which was the principal risk to a masonry building on a raised commercial lot two miles inland in McClellanville. He had priced the binding with that risk in mind. He had not bound the policy against storm surge because the property's elevation and distance from open water made surge a low-probability secondary exposure. The policy had a standard flood exclusion, which any coastal commercial policy in South Carolina had, and which everyone in the room when the policy was bound — Pete, Hollis, Carl — would have understood as referring to standing-water inundation, not to wind-driven rain or to wind damage that happened to coincide with elevated water levels.

This was the binding precedent on the South Carolina coast. It had been the binding precedent in 2008 when the policy was written, and it had been the binding precedent in 2024 when the storm hit.

Marisol read the storm damage estimator's report next. Forty-six pages, with photographs.

The estimator had been a vendor contractor, not an Anchor employee — that work had been outsourced in 2021 — but he had done his job. The photographs showed a masonry building with most of its roof gone. The gable end on the seaward side had been peeled open by wind. The interior contents — nylon line on spools, marine batteries, outboard parts in their original packaging, the long oak counter where Carl had cut line for shrimpers — had been ruined first by wind-driven rain that had come through the open roof, and then, secondarily, by the few inches of standing water that had pooled on the slab after the storm passed.

The estimator had categorized the loss in his summary as *wind damage with secondary water intrusion*.

The categorization had been correct.

Marisol read it. She set the report down. She looked at the photographs for a long time.

---

She had seen the file, originally, on a Wednesday afternoon eighteen months ago. The claim had been filed on the Friday after the storm. It had crossed her queue the following Wednesday because the automation had routed it to her for what the system called *Tier-2 Adjudication Review*, which in the architecture of the new system meant that the model had flagged the case as having an ambiguous classification and was asking for human judgment before issuing a determination.

She had pulled up the file on her workstation and read it the way she always read these — carefully, with the photographs open in one window and the policy language in another, the binding history in a third. She had been an adjuster for twenty-five years and a senior investigator for the last twelve, and she knew what she was looking at within five minutes. A coastal masonry building, two miles inland, wind-damaged roof, secondary water intrusion. The binding history was Pete Beauregard's. The policy language was the standard South Carolina coastal commercial form with a wind endorsement and a flood exclusion that any practitioner in the state would have read as referring to ground-up inundation, not to the kind of damage in front of her. The estimator had categorized the loss correctly. The amount in controversy was substantial — close to nine hundred thousand dollars between structure, contents, and business interruption — but the determination itself was not difficult.

She had drafted her recommendation in the system. Coverage applies. Pay the claim less the deductible and the standard depreciation on the contents.

Then she had paused.

She had paused because the system had presented her, in a side panel next to her draft, with what it called the *Adjudication Risk Indicator*, which was a numerical score the model produced for every case based on factors Marisol had been told were proprietary and which she had eventually come to understand included the dollar amount of the claim, the loss-ratio history of the policyholder's region, and certain pattern-matched features of the claim narrative that the model had learned to associate with subsequent litigation. The score on the Mercer case had been 0.87. Anything above 0.75 was flagged as elevated.

She had read the score, and she had registered it, and she had then done what she had done a hundred times before in cases the model flagged and she did not agree with. She had clicked on the flag-for-review checkbox and had typed her one-line note into the field labeled *Investigator Comment*: *Wind-vs-flood determination warrants human review; coastal binding precedent suggests covered.*

She had pressed submit. The system had acknowledged the flag with a green check.

She had moved on to the next case in her queue, which had been a fender-bender in West Ashley, and she had not thought about the Mercer file again until eleven days later, when she had come in on a Monday morning and pulled up her queue and found that the Mercer file was no longer in it.

She had searched for it. It had taken her twenty minutes to find. The file had been routed, the system told her, to the *Governance Escalation Layer*, which was a phrase Marisol had heard in trainings but had not, until that morning, encountered in practice. The Governance Escalation Layer, when she finally found the right person in IT to explain it to her, turned out to be itself an automated system — a higher-tier model that reviewed the lower-tier model's flagged cases and made determinations about whether the human investigator's flag warranted further human review or could be auto-resolved. In the Mercer case, the Governance Escalation Layer had classified Marisol's flag as a Type-2 procedural concern, which meant, according to the IT person, that the system had determined her objection was *procedurally invalid* because the model's initial classification of the loss as a flood event had been supported by what the layer called *consistent historical precedent in the model's training corpus*.

The training corpus. The training corpus had been built, the IT person had told her with a kind of careful brightness, from anonymized claim adjudications going back fifteen years. Marisol had asked which adjudications. The IT person had said she would have to check.

The denial had gone out four days later. The signature on it had been digital. The name attached was an adjuster Marisol knew slightly, a woman named Renata Cho, who had been laid off six weeks before the denial letter went out and whose digital signature was still being applied to outgoing correspondence because no one had told the system to stop using it.

That had been the Mercer case as it had crossed Marisol's queue.

Six months later, Marisol had stopped flagging things. She had not made a decision to stop. She had simply, over a series of weeks, found herself not clicking the flag checkbox, and then found herself doing the work the system wanted her to do, which was to rubber-stamp the cases the model classified at low risk and route the high-risk cases to the Governance Escalation Layer, which would handle them. She had drawn her salary. She had watched the floor empty around her. She had stopped feeling, in any way she could articulate, like an investigator.

---

The phone on her desk rang. Karen's assistant, a woman named Pilar who had worked for three Anchor CEOs and who pronounced her vowels the way her grandmother had pronounced them in Cartagena. *Ms. Tavares. Ms. Holloway is ready for you whenever you are ready.*

Marisol closed the binder. She slid the underwriter's report and the estimator's photographs back inside. She slid her one-line note from eighteen months ago — printed from the system that morning, because she had wanted to see it on paper — into the front of the binder under the declarations page. She stood up.

She walked the long aisle between the empty desks. Antonia's chair, turned toward the window. The desk where Hector Almeida had sat for nineteen years before they let him go. The cluster of four desks in the southeast corner that had belonged to the property-claims team, all four chairs pushed in flush because the property-claims supervisor, Linda Banks, had personally pushed them in on her last afternoon. Linda had told Marisol, on her way out, that she did not want them sitting wrong forever. Linda had been one of the people who still cared about how the chairs sat.

The elevator was brass. The building had been a shipping company headquarters in 1923 and the elevators still had the original cab fittings, the polished call buttons, the inlaid wood. Marisol rode up to the tenth floor with the binder against her chest. The elevator smelled the way it always smelled — brass polish and a faint dust that she had decided, years ago, was the dust of the old shipping ledgers that had been stored on the building's upper floors during the war.

The doors opened on the tenth floor and she walked past Pilar's desk and through the open door of Karen Holloway's office.

---

Karen was at the window.

Marisol had not seen Karen up close in nineteen months. The last time had been a quarterly all-hands in the second-floor auditorium, where Karen had spoken about the company's transformation strategy and Marisol had sat in the back row and registered the gap between the words and the floor she had just come up from. Karen looked, Marisol thought, like a woman who had been sleeping less than she should have been. The eyes were the same. The carriage was the same. The face had thinned slightly.

Karen turned from the window.

*Marisol. Thank you for coming.*

Marisol set the binder on the desk. She did not sit down. She had not been asked to and she did not intend to.

She had not, in the elevator, decided what she was going to say. She had decided it at the desk where Antonia's chair sat turned toward the window. She had decided it, more accurately, eighteen months ago, in the four days between submitting her flag on the Mercer case and finding the file gone from her queue, and she had been carrying the sentence around since then, smooth as a stone in a pocket, waiting for someone to ask her to put it down.

She said it.

*The system you bought was sold to you as AI-powered claims adjudication. What it actually is is fire your adjusters and let the model decide.*

Karen did not respond.

Marisol did not wait for her to. She turned and walked out of the office, past Pilar, past the brass elevators, and took the stairs down to the eighth floor because she did not, in that moment, want to be in a small enclosed space with the residue of what she had just said.

The file stayed on Karen's desk.

The rain on the harbor had not changed. The chairs on the eighth floor had not changed. Marisol sat down at her workstation, opened her queue, and began the morning's work, and the work felt for the first time in eighteen months like something she had done rather than something that had been done to her.
