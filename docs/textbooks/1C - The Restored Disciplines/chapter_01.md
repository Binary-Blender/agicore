In 1976 Michael Fagan published a paper at IBM Kingston describing a peer-review practice he called software inspections. The practice required four hours of careful reading at a rate of two hundred lines of code per hour, performed by a small group whose roles — moderator, reader, recorder, author, inspectors — were specified in detail, with entry and exit criteria for each inspection event. Fagan reported a defect-detection rate of sixty-seven percent at the inspection stage, removing two-thirds of the defects in a unit of code before any testing was performed. Subsequent replications at Hewlett-Packard, Bell Laboratories, NASA Goddard, and elsewhere produced rates in the sixty-to-eighty-percent range across hundreds of inspections. Fagan inspections became the most empirically validated software-engineering practice in the history of the field. Almost nobody practices them.

In 1979 Allan Albrecht published the function-point method at an IBM internal conference and subsequently in public. Calibrated function-point counts produced effort estimates within fifteen percent of actuals on calibrated environments. The International Function Point Users Group standardized the practice in 1986. The discipline reached technical maturity by 1995, was adopted across IBM, was required for certain government contracts in the United States and Europe, and held a dominant position in software-cost estimation through the late 1990s. Almost nobody practices function-point analysis today.

In 1984 Donald Knuth published *Literate Programming*, a method for writing software as continuous prose interleaving explanation and code. The TeX implementation is the canonical demonstration: every page is a piece of typeset prose that contains, where the prose calls for it, the relevant Pascal code; reading the resulting book is reading the system. TeX has been in production for forty-two years with documented defect rates near zero across that span. The discipline that produced TeX is taught in no major undergraduate curriculum, is supported by almost no mainstream toolchain, and is practiced by approximately the same population of developers who practiced it in 1990. Knuth's method did not fail. The method shipped TeX. The method's adoption failed.

In 1986 Brad Cox published *Object-Oriented Programming: An Evolutionary Approach* and named the Software IC — the vision of software components as interchangeable integrated circuits, assembled rather than written. Cox's argument was economic: the field's productivity improvements would not come from better languages or better engineers but from the emergence of a *components industry* analogous to the semiconductor industry that had revolutionized hardware. Cox saw the vision; the vision did not arrive; package ecosystems (npm, PyPI, crates.io) became a partial fulfillment forty years later but never closed the loop on the discovery, adaptation, and trust problems Cox named. The Software IC is a partially-vacated category.

In 1989 Bertrand Meyer published the second edition of *Object-Oriented Software Construction*, in which Design by Contract appeared as a first-class feature of the Eiffel language. Contracts — preconditions, postconditions, class invariants — produced documented reliability improvements in Eiffel-disciplined projects and in subsequent research at Microsoft (Spec#) and elsewhere. The discipline never escaped its language community. Most production codebases in 2026 contain no formal contracts.

Five disciplines; five empirically validated track records; five examples of practices that worked when applied and were abandoned anyway. The list extends. Cleanroom Software Engineering produced certified reliability at NASA Goddard and IBM Federal Systems and is not currently practiced outside a small number of research and aerospace organizations. Operational profiles produced documented reliability improvements at AT&T and are not practiced at scale in commercial software despite the modern availability of observability data that would make profile construction nearly free. The Capability Maturity Model's Level 5 — statistical process control applied to software development — was achieved by a small number of organizations who used it to justify their global reputations and is approximately extinct outside its native habitats. Architecture Decision Records, named by Michael Nygard in 2011, have been broadly recognized as useful and are unevenly maintained because writing them after a decision is the moment in the workflow nobody wants to perform. Behavior-Driven Development with maintained Gherkin specifications has been advocated for two decades and is almost never sustained in production codebases because the .feature files drift from code behavior faster than humans can update them.

This is the graveyard. The disciplines in it are not failures of engineering reasoning. They are casualties of a specific economic mechanism that this book names and examines: the *effort tax*.

The **effort tax**, as a formal concept, is the recurring cost in human attention, calendar time, specialized training, and sustained discipline required to maintain a software engineering practice in working order. The tax is *recurring* — one-time setup costs (training a new hire, installing a tool, writing an initial document) are not the tax. The tax is the cost of *sustaining* the practice once it is established, day after day, release after release, year after year. A discipline whose one-time cost is high and whose recurring cost is low can survive at small scale on the founder's energy. A discipline whose recurring cost is high cannot survive even at organizations that initially invested heavily, because the recurring cost outlasts the organizational memory of why the investment was made.

The tax is distinct from two adjacent concepts that the field has named. It is not Philip Crosby's *cost of quality*, although the two are related. Crosby's framework distinguishes the cost of conformance (preventive activities) from the cost of nonconformance (defects, rework, failures); his argument is that the former is always smaller than the latter and that preventive investment is therefore economically rational at the system level. Crosby was correct, and the effort-tax framework is consistent with his argument, but the effort tax names a different question: not whether preventive investment is rational in aggregate, but whether any individual preventive discipline can survive the resource auction at the team or organization level given the specific *kind* of effort it demands. Crosby addresses the budget; the effort tax addresses the attention. The two failures are distinguishable, and a discipline can be defensible by Crosby's accounting while remaining unfunded by the effort-tax accounting.

The effort tax is also not Ward Cunningham's *technical debt*. Technical debt names a specific class of deferred work — corners cut, design compromises taken, refactorings postponed — that accumulate against the future maintainability of the code. Effort-killed disciplines are typically *not* in the technical-debt category because they were never adopted in the first place; the debt model assumes a prior baseline of disciplined practice from which deviation occurs. The disciplines in this book's graveyard were never the baseline. Their absence is not a debt against an established standard; it is the *failure to establish the standard*, which is a different economic situation with different remediation strategies.

What killed these disciplines, in each case, was the convergence of three economic facts. The first fact is that the *marginal cost of disciplined human attention* is high. A certified function-point counter requires training; a Fagan-inspection moderator requires authority and skill; a Cleanroom box-structure designer requires mathematical maturity; a literate-programming author requires prose discipline at parity with code discipline. None of these competences is widely distributed in any software organization. The labor is rare; the labor is expensive when it can be located; the labor is unavailable when it is most needed. The second fact is that the *yield-per-attention* of disciplined practices is variable and often invisible in the short term. Fagan inspections find defects that would otherwise be found in testing or production; the inspection moderator does not see the defects that the inspection caught from never reaching testing, only the defects the inspection logged. The discipline is paying down a tax on future failure that the organization may not perceive. The third fact is that *competing activities* with higher visible yield-per-attention exist in any team's daily work. Shipping a feature is visible; preventing a defect that never occurs is invisible. The resource auction within a team, conducted implicitly through what activities people actually do with their working hours, systematically favors visible activities over invisible ones even when the invisible ones are economically rational in expected-value terms.

The convergence is fatal. The discipline cannot win the auction even when it could win the comparison. The discipline is correct on the engineering merits and unfunded on the attention budget. The discipline dies — not in a single event, but across years, as the certified counters retire and are not replaced, as the inspection meetings are deprioritized and then cancelled, as the contract clauses are stripped from new methods, as the next cohort of engineers learns the alternative practices and never encounters the original. The death is structural. It does not require any individual to make a mistake.

The fashionable explanation for the death of these disciplines, common in agile and post-agile literature, is that the disciplines were *outmoded* — that they belonged to a waterfall era and were rightly displaced by faster, lighter, more iterative practices. The explanation is not entirely wrong: some of the displaced disciplines, particularly the heavyweight upfront ones, were genuinely poorly matched to fast-iteration software development. But the explanation conflates two different things. The waterfall era produced *both* heavyweight disciplines that were rightly displaced *and* lightweight disciplines that were displaced as collateral damage. Fagan inspections do not require waterfall; they are a unit-level activity that operates on any code, in any process, at any cadence. Function-point analysis does not require waterfall; an estimate can be produced from a story, a sprint backlog, or an MVP specification. Literate programming does not require waterfall; the prose is interleaved with code at the function level and works in any process model. The disciplines that were lost are not all the same kind. The fashion explanation paints with too wide a brush.

What AI changes about this picture is specific and mechanistic and worth stating precisely, because vague claims about AI's transformative effect do not advance the argument and tend to provoke a defensive response from readers who have heard the vague version too many times. AI does not eliminate essential complexity. AI does not understand a domain that has never been articulated. AI does not replace the human cognitive contribution to engineering work. AI does *one specific thing* that bears on the effort tax: AI reduces the marginal cost of a unit of disciplined attention by an order of magnitude or more, in the specific cases where the attention is *clerical* — that is, where the discipline involves the faithful application of explicit rules to a body of artifacts, the maintenance of representations under change, the inventory of elements against a checklist, the comparison of an artifact against a specification, or any combination of these. These are precisely the kinds of attention that the disciplines in this book's graveyard required.

A certified function-point counter does clerical work: they enumerate transactions, apply the IFPUG complexity rules, fill out the GSC matrix, and compute the totals. The judgment in the work is real but bounded; the rules are codified; the inventory is exhaustive. AI does this work faithfully, at a fraction of the time, with an audit trail that exceeds what a human counter produces. A Fagan-inspection inspector does clerical work in the dominant component of their effort: they read code at a specified rate against a specified checklist, classify findings by severity, and log them. The expert judgment that improves an inspection beyond the checklist is real, but the bulk of an inspection's effort is the checklist application, and AI applies checklists without drift. A literate-programming author does the clerical work of writing prose that explains code, maintaining the prose as the code changes, and producing the weaved output. AI writes the prose; the human reviews and revises. The pattern is consistent across the graveyard: the disciplines that died were disciplines whose effort was *predominantly clerical*, and the clerical effort is exactly what AI eliminates.

This is the central claim of the book, and it is falsifiable. If AI cannot, in fact, reduce the effort tax of any of the named disciplines to a point that makes the discipline economically viable in 2026 conditions, the chapter on that discipline fails. The book commits to specifying, in each chapter, the precise effort-tax components and the precise mechanism by which AI reduces each. The reader can evaluate the claim chapter by chapter and reject any individual restoration on its merits without rejecting the framework. This is how a research program in the Lakatosian sense ought to behave: the core thesis is general; the individual instances are particular; failure of any single instance does not collapse the program but refines the bounds of its claim.

The bounds of the claim deserve their own statement. The effort-tax framework applies to disciplines whose effort was *clerical*. It does not apply to disciplines whose effort was *judgmental*. Architectural taste, organizational politics, executive trade-off discipline, the resolution of cross-team incentive conflicts, the design of compensation structures that align engineering and product priorities — all of these have effort taxes, often heavy ones, but the effort is not clerical and AI's contribution to it is bounded. A book about restoring these disciplines is a different book, and a useful one, but it is not this book. This book is about the disciplines whose tax was clerical, because the case for restoration is strongest there and the mechanism for restoration is clearest there.

A first taxonomy of clerical-effort components is useful at this point in the argument, because subsequent chapters will name them, and naming them now establishes the vocabulary. The taxonomy distinguishes five components of clerical effort, each of which AI reduces by a different mechanism. The first is **inventory effort** — the enumeration of elements against a structured space (transactions in a function-point count, classes in a CRC deck, requirements in a traceability matrix). AI inventories exhaustively and quickly; the human attention saved is the time the inventory would have consumed. The second is **rule-application effort** — the application of codified rules to elements (IFPUG complexity weights, inspection checklists, contract derivation rules). AI applies rules consistently across large bodies of work; the human attention saved is the discipline that prevents drift. The third is **maintenance-under-change effort** — the updating of representations as their underlying subjects change (ubiquitous-language glossaries, ADR repositories, Gherkin scenarios, CRC cards). AI maintains representations continuously against the code; the human attention saved is the recurring update cost that doomed every prior attempt at these practices. The fourth is **synthesis effort** — the construction of summary artifacts from raw materials (ADRs synthesized from PR discussions, archaeological reports synthesized from commit history, literate-programming prose synthesized from code and intent). AI synthesizes faithfully when the source material exists; the human attention saved is the activation energy of looking back at what has already been done. The fifth is **deep-reading effort** — the careful, comprehensive examination of a body of work against a standard (Fagan inspections, conceptual-integrity surveys, contract audits). AI reads at any depth without fatigue; the human attention saved is the four hours per two hundred lines that the disciplines used to require.

These five components — inventory, rule application, maintenance under change, synthesis, deep reading — cover the dominant clerical effort in the disciplines this book restores. Subsequent chapters identify which components apply to which discipline and quantify the mechanism by which AI reduces each.

A note on what the framework does not promise. The framework does not promise that AI does the work *as well as* a human expert at the peak of their discipline. A senior Fagan inspector with twenty years of experience reading code in a specific domain may find defects that AI does not find; a certified function-point counter at the apex of their judgment may produce a count more defensible than AI's; a literate-programming author writing prose for their own code may communicate intent more faithfully than AI's synthesis. The framework does not contest these possibilities. The framework contests a different claim: that the *typical* practitioner, in the *typical* organization, equipped with AI as an instrument, can practice these disciplines at a level that produces the documented benefits — sixty-percent defect detection, fifteen-percent estimation accuracy, readable maintainable code, accurate ubiquitous-language glossaries, traceable requirements — at a level the typical practitioner has not been able to achieve since the disciplines were abandoned. The restoration is not a return to the peak. The restoration is a return to the practice.

The chapters that follow develop the recovery argument in three movements. Chapter 2 engages Fred Brooks's *No Silver Bullet* essay directly, on its own terms, and articulates the inversion that gives the book its intellectual frame. Chapter 3 establishes the chapter-by-chapter recovery pattern as a methodological apparatus, applicable to the disciplines in this book and to disciplines that warrant the same treatment but were not included. Chapters 4 through 26 are the restorations themselves, sequenced by domain (specification and estimation, quality and verification, knowledge and documentation, architecture and reuse), each following the six-element pattern of original formulation, empirical evidence, effort-tax mechanism, AI's change, the operational prompt, and the worked restoration. Chapters 27 and 28 close the volume with the synthesis of the restored practitioner's workflow and the trajectory of restorations beyond the present volume.

The recovery program is not modest in scope, but it is bounded in claim. The disciplines named here are not all of software engineering. They are a specific class of practices that produced documented results and were abandoned for reasons that no longer apply. They are worth restoring. The restoration is now affordable. The work begins.
