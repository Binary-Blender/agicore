The twenty-three restoration chapters of this volume do not exhaust the catalog of effort-killed software engineering disciplines that warrant the recovery treatment. The catalog is longer than this book; the book has selected the disciplines whose restoration cases are strongest, whose empirical records are most established, and whose AI-assisted versions are most directly demonstrable on the Agicore worked restorations the book has anchored throughout. A larger catalog remains. This closing chapter names a portion of the residual catalog, applies the chapter-three pattern to the candidates briefly enough that the reader can judge each candidate's restoration prospects, articulates the bounds of the framework, and frames the methodological apparatus as the contribution that travels beyond the specific restorations.

The first cluster of residual candidates is in the *process and methodology* area. Watts Humphrey's Personal Software Process and Team Software Process (Chapter 14 surveyed Humphrey's broader work but did not develop the PSP/TSP separately) are direct candidates: the personal effort-tracking burden that killed PSP adoption is the kind of clerical tax AI directly addresses, and the PSP's documented effectiveness in producing better engineers is empirically established. A separate chapter on the PSP would extend Chapter 14's measurement-and-analysis frame to individual-engineer development. Jackson System Development (Michael Jackson, not the other one, 1983) and the broader structured-analysis-and-design tradition of the 1970s and 1980s are candidates with mixed empirical records; the strongest portions (Jackson's data-driven program design) warrant the restoration treatment. The IEEE/EIA software-engineering standards — particularly the IEEE 1471 architectural-description standard and its successors — are candidates whose adoption has been concentrated in regulated industries for the reasons Chapter 18 articulated; the broader applicability with AI assistance is real.

The second cluster is in *measurement and management*. Earned Value Management for software (the worked diagnostic in Chapter 3) is a partial-restoration candidate that warrants its own treatment: the clerical taxes are recoverable, the earning-rule design tax is not, and a chapter would honestly bound the claim. Quality Function Deployment's *House of Quality* — the matrix that connects customer requirements to engineering characteristics — is a candidate whose construction tax has historically been prohibitive and whose maintenance tax has been continuous. AI's table-construction capability addresses both. The risk-based management practices of the SEI Software Engineering Body of Knowledge are candidates whose framework documentation is extensive and whose adoption has lagged behind the documentation.

The third cluster is in *verification and validation* beyond the disciplines Part III restored. Mutation testing — the practice of introducing systematic small changes to code to verify that the test suite detects each change — has been advocated since the 1970s and has been confined to research and specialized contexts because the mutation generation and the test-suite execution against mutants is computationally expensive and the analysis of survived mutants is interpretively demanding. AI can perform both the mutation generation and the survived-mutant analysis at scales that have not previously been practical. Property-based testing (Hughes and Claessen's QuickCheck and its descendants) is a partial candidate: the framework is well-supported in some language communities and underused elsewhere; AI assistance with property generation and shrinking-failure analysis could expand the practice's reach. Fuzz testing, in its modern coverage-guided form, has matured substantially through American Fuzzy Lop and its successors; the discipline of *integrating fuzzing results into the engineering workflow* — turning fuzz-discovered defects into structured improvements to the test suite — is a partial restoration candidate.

The fourth cluster is in *human factors and organizational practice*. The Athena/Andromeda tradition of carefully designed human-computer interfaces in the 1980s is a candidate whose interface-design discipline produced uncommonly usable systems and was abandoned when product cycles compressed; AI's capacity to apply usability heuristics consistently is a real restoration mechanism. The Cognitive Walkthrough methodology for usability evaluation (Wharton, Rieman, Lewis, and Polson, 1994) is a candidate whose application has been limited by the time cost of careful walkthrough work; AI can perform walkthroughs at scales humans cannot. The DACS Software Engineering Body of Knowledge from the Data and Analysis Center for Software is a candidate whose comprehensiveness has historically been a barrier to use; AI-mediated access to the body of knowledge could make the corpus operational.

The fifth cluster is in *educational and professional development*. The Software Engineering Institute's curriculum recommendations for graduate education in software engineering produced rigorous program designs that few universities have implemented at scale because the curriculum's depth required investment that few programs could justify. The recommendations remain available; AI-mediated learning environments could implement portions of the curriculum that human-mediated education has not been able to sustain. The professional-engineering licensure tradition for software (the IEEE-CS / NSPE Software Engineering PE exam, introduced in 2013 and discontinued in 2019 for lack of candidates) is a partial candidate whose discontinuation reflected adoption barriers that AI's training-and-preparation capabilities could address.

The pattern across the residual catalog is consistent with the pattern in this book's twenty-three chapters: disciplines that produced documented results and were abandoned for effort-tax reasons that AI now addresses. The restoration program's logic extends to each. The reader equipped with Chapter 3's methodological apparatus can evaluate each candidate's restoration prospects on its specific terms, applying the six-element pattern to determine whether the discipline is a full-restoration candidate, a partial-restoration candidate, or a non-candidate, and producing the operational prompt that would restore the discipline if the verdict is positive. The work of this book is the demonstration that the program works on twenty-three specific instances; the work of the residual catalog is the application of the demonstrated program to the next instances. The author invites the reader to perform that work.

A statement of the framework's bounds is appropriate before closing. The book's central claim — that AI restores effort-killed software engineering disciplines — applies specifically to disciplines whose effort tax was *clerical* in the senses Chapter 1 enumerated: inventory effort, rule-application effort, maintenance-under-change effort, synthesis effort, deep-reading effort. The book does not claim that AI restores disciplines whose effort tax was *judgmental* — architectural taste, organizational politics, executive trade-off discipline, cultural-formation work, leadership consistency. These have effort taxes too, often heavy ones, but the effort is not clerical and AI's contribution to it is bounded. The disciplines whose abandonment was caused by judgmental-effort taxes remain abandoned for the same reasons; AI's contribution to their restoration is real but limited; the restoration program's leverage is concentrated where the taxes were clerical.

A related bound concerns the *organizational* component of the restorations. The book's chapters have consistently noted that AI lowers the *economic* cost of practicing the restored disciplines; the *cultural* and *organizational* commitments that the disciplines require remain real. A team that does not want to practice Cleanroom Software Engineering cannot be moved to do so by AI alone; a team that has adopted move-fast-and-break-things as its operating stance cannot be persuaded into Zero Defects by the cost curve change alone. The economic argument creates conditions under which cultural change becomes available; the cultural change is the variable that the book's framework does not address. Engineering leaders who want to practice the restored disciplines now have the means; engineering leaders who do not want to practice them are not moved by the means' availability.

A final bound concerns the *epistemological* status of the worked restorations. The Agicore-anchored worked restorations throughout the book are concrete demonstrations rather than controlled experimental validations. The disciplines are restored in the sense that the operational prompts produce artifacts that practitioners with discipline experience recognize as legitimate applications of the discipline. The disciplines are *not* restored in the sense of controlled studies comparing AI-restored practice against historical practice with statistical confidence intervals on the outcomes. The book's argument is that the empirical case for the original disciplines is strong (Chapter 2 surveys the records) and that the AI restorations produce comparable outputs to the original disciplines on the worked examples (Chapters 4 through 26 demonstrate each restoration). The combination supports the book's claim with reasonable confidence; the combination does not constitute the kind of large-N controlled study that would be needed to make claims about industry-wide productivity effects with high statistical confidence. Such studies remain to be performed. The book is the substrate against which they can be conducted.

A statement of the *methodological apparatus* as the volume's broader contribution is the closing argument. The six-element chapter pattern — Original Formulation, Empirical Evidence, Effort-Tax Mechanism, What AI Changes, Operational Prompt, Worked Restoration — is itself a contribution beyond the specific restorations. The pattern provides a structured way for the reader to assess any abandoned software-engineering discipline as a restoration candidate, to identify the effort-tax components that determined its abandonment, to evaluate AI's specific contribution to each component, to produce the operational prompt that would restore the discipline, and to demonstrate the restoration on a worked instance. The pattern is teachable, applicable, and falsifiable. The reader who has worked through this volume has, in effect, learned the pattern by example; the reader can now apply it to the residual catalog this chapter named, to disciplines this chapter did not name, and to disciplines that have not yet been articulated as candidates for restoration. The pattern travels.

The recovery program is open. The book has restored twenty-three disciplines and identified more candidates than it has restored. The field's accumulated graveyard of effort-killed practices is large; the affordability transition that AI enables is general; the disciplines that warrant restoration extend beyond what this book has covered and beyond what its author can anticipate. The program's continuation depends on the reader. Engineering teams that adopt the restored disciplines and contribute their own restorations to the broader conversation are the population that carries the program forward. The author invites the reader's participation.

A closing note on the relationship between this volume and the other volumes of the AI Computer Science series. Volume 1A — *Cognition Systems Engineering* — describes how AI-native organizations should be engineered as cognitive production systems, with attention to the new disciplines that emerge because AI is here. Volume 1B — *Semantic Systems Engineering* — describes the semantic substrate that makes cognitive production work coherent. The present Volume 1C — *The Restored Disciplines of Software Engineering* — describes how the working engineering team applies the lessons of the field's historical discipline to its daily practice with AI as the enabling infrastructure. The three volumes are complementary projections of a single broader project: the application of engineering rigor to software development in the AI era. A team that practices all three has, in effect, a complete software-engineering practice for the AI era — new disciplines invented where the AI capability requires them, semantic infrastructure built where coherence needs it, historical disciplines restored where their value justifies the practice. The complete practice is not the work of any single volume; the complete practice is the work of integrating them.

Brooks was correct that no single technology of any decade would lower the essential-complexity ceiling of software development. The ceiling is fixed. The field has been operating below it. The disciplines that would have closed the gap were unfunded for forty years. They are now affordable. The disciplines are restored. The field rises toward the ceiling.

The work continues.
