A recovery program needs a method. The disciplines restored in this book are not the only disciplines worth restoring, and the present author makes no claim to have catalogued the full graveyard. The pattern by which an abandoned discipline is assessed for AI-assisted restoration is therefore as important as the specific restorations the book performs, because the pattern equips the reader to evaluate disciplines this book does not cover and to apply the recovery argument to their own engineering context. This chapter develops the pattern formally and walks one worked application of it to a discipline that is *not* a chapter in this book — Earned Value Management for software — in order to demonstrate the pattern as a diagnostic tool. The chapter is a methodological apparatus, in the sense that Imre Lakatos's *Methodology of Scientific Research Programmes* uses the term: a structured way of evaluating instances against a research program's central thesis.

The pattern has six elements. They have appeared in Chapters 1 and 4 in compressed form; this chapter elaborates each and articulates the failure modes when any element is omitted.

The first element is the **Original Formulation**. The discipline is met on its own terms, as its inventor named it, in the institutional context that produced it. This element requires the practitioner doing the restoration to read the seminal paper or book in its primary form — not in subsequent summaries, not in derivative implementations, not in the marketing language of vendors who licensed the term. Fagan's 1976 paper, Albrecht's 1979 talk, Knuth's 1984 essay, Meyer's 1988 book, Mills's Cleanroom papers from the early 1980s, Lamport's TLA+ work from the 1990s, Brooks's *Mythical Man-Month* — each of these is a primary source, and each rewards reading directly. The reason is not antiquarian. The reason is that subsequent summaries inevitably distort: they emphasize what was useful to the summarizer's argument, they omit nuances the inventor considered important, they import vocabulary from later movements that did not exist when the discipline was named, and they typically lose the *empirical and institutional context* that made the discipline persuasive in its original form. The restoration begins with the primary source because the primary source is what was empirically validated. Subsequent forms may have improved upon it; the empirical record attaches to what was actually published and tested.

The failure mode when the Original Formulation is omitted is *cargo-cult restoration*. The practitioner adopts a derivative version of the discipline, finds that it does not produce the documented benefits, and concludes that the discipline does not work. The conclusion is wrong, but the practitioner has no way of seeing this without the primary source, because the practitioner does not know what was modified and what consequences the modifications had. The Original Formulation is the floor below which the restoration must not fall.

The second element is the **Empirical Evidence**. What did the discipline actually produce when it was practiced? This element separates disciplines that *worked* from disciplines that were *proposed*. The field has accumulated, in any given decade, a long list of proposals that were never empirically tested — methodologies advocated in books and conference talks but never deployed in conditions sufficient to evaluate their claims. Those proposals do not warrant restoration. They warrant evaluation on their own terms, perhaps, but the book's restoration program is specifically about disciplines whose track records are documented. Fagan's sixty-seven percent defect-detection rate is not an extrapolation; it was measured in production conditions at IBM Kingston and was replicated in dozens of subsequent studies. Albrecht's fifteen-percent estimation accuracy on calibrated environments is not a model output; it was a finding from comparing predicted to actual effort across IBM projects. The Cleanroom defect rates reported by NASA Goddard for the COBE Attitude Ground Support System were measured in operational deployment of the resulting system, not in laboratory replication. The Empirical Evidence element is the *evidentiary gate* that distinguishes restoration from advocacy.

The failure mode when Empirical Evidence is omitted is *restoration of a discipline that was never validated*. The book's central claim is that the disciplines being restored worked. If the claim is to be defensible, the work must be demonstrated. A practitioner applying the pattern to a candidate discipline must locate the empirical record or, failing that, must downgrade the candidate from restoration to advocacy and treat the AI-assisted version of it as an experiment rather than a recovery. The downgrade is not a rejection; it is a correct classification.

The third element is the **Effort-Tax Mechanism**. What, specifically, made the discipline expensive to sustain? This element forces the practitioner to articulate the cost structure in terms that allow falsifiable claims about AI's contribution. The vague version — "Fagan inspections were expensive because they required time" — is not adequate. The precise version — "Fagan inspections required four hours of focused reading per two hundred lines of code by an inspector at or above the author's skill level, plus a multi-person inspection meeting that consumed an additional hour per participant per inspection, all of which was clerical and attention-intensive labor whose marginal cost in a typical organization in the 1990s was forty dollars per hour minimum and whose recurring nature made the discipline impossible to sustain across all of an organization's production code" — is the form the element requires. The Effort-Tax Mechanism is the *causal claim* about why the discipline died.

The failure mode when the Effort-Tax Mechanism is articulated vaguely is *misattribution*. A discipline may have died for reasons that AI does not address — cultural rejection, regulatory change, the rise of a competing paradigm that displaced it on grounds AI does not affect — and the practitioner who attributes the death to clerical effort when it was actually attributed to organizational politics will produce a restoration that does not, in fact, restore. The Effort-Tax Mechanism must be the right cost; AI's contribution is calibrated to that specific cost.

The fourth element is **What AI Changes**. The mechanistic claim about which of the five components of clerical effort (inventory, rule application, maintenance under change, synthesis, deep reading) AI reduces, and by what factor. This element is where the restoration's falsifiability lives. A claim that "AI makes the discipline cheaper" is not falsifiable in any useful sense. A claim that "AI reduces the inventory-effort component of function-point counting by approximately ninety percent, the rule-application component by approximately ninety-five percent, and the GSC-interlocutor component by approximately seventy percent, for a combined effort reduction of approximately one order of magnitude" is falsifiable: a reader who runs the operational prompt against an actual codebase and times the result against a human-counter baseline can verify or refute the claim. The book commits to specifying, for each chapter, the mechanistic decomposition that allows the falsifiability to be operative.

The failure mode when What AI Changes is articulated vaguely is *vague enthusiasm*. The book's claims become indistinguishable from the general-purpose AI-evangelism that fills contemporary technical discourse, and the reader has no basis for distinguishing the book's specific argument from the broader noise. The book commits to specifying mechanism precisely because the specific argument is what carries the weight.

The fifth element is the **Operational Prompt**. A paste-ready Claude Code instruction that restores the discipline. The prompt's properties have been named elsewhere in the book — scoped ask, verifiable output format, explicit methodology, provenance discipline, no-fabrication clause, abort criteria — and they are not repeated here in detail. What this chapter does articulate is the *test* of a good prompt: a competent reader, presented with the prompt and a codebase, can produce within hours an artifact that a discipline-experienced reviewer would judge as a credible application of the discipline. The standard is not that the AI output is indistinguishable from a master practitioner's output; the standard is that the AI output is recognizable as the discipline being practiced. This is the standard that lets the restoration be useful at scale: the median practitioner equipped with the prompt can practice the discipline at a level that produces most of the documented benefits, without the years of specialization that the original practice required.

The failure mode when the Operational Prompt is sloppy is *unreproducible restoration*. The book demonstrates a worked example; the reader cannot reliably reproduce the demonstration; the discipline does not actually return to the field, only its appearance in a single chapter. The prompt is the artifact that travels with the reader after the chapter is closed.

The sixth element is the **Worked Restoration**. A concrete instance of the prompt's output applied to a representative artifact, with the reader's attention directed to the features of the output that demonstrate the discipline. The worked restoration is what makes the chapter persuasive in a way that the abstract argument alone cannot be. A reader who sees the Agicore Studio ESTIMATE.md as a Function Point Analysis output (Chapter 4), sees the contract-augmented Agicore parser module as a Design by Contract output (Chapter 8), sees the literate-programming rewrite of an Agicore module as a literate programming output (Chapter 15), and so on across the book, accumulates evidence that the prompts produce the disciplines as claimed. The worked restoration is the *demonstration* that the rest of the chapter's argument cashes out.

The failure mode when the Worked Restoration is omitted is *theoretical restoration*. The chapter argues correctly that the discipline is restorable in principle and provides no evidence that it has actually been restored in practice. The reader is left to take the argument on faith. The book commits to demonstration in every chapter; the demonstrations are concise (typically a few paragraphs after the operational prompt) but they are present, and they are anchored to the Agicore codebase in most cases so that interested readers can verify directly.

These six elements, taken together, form the chapter pattern that Chapters 4 through 26 follow. The pattern is rigorous in the sense that omitting any element produces a chapter that fails to make its case; the pattern is flexible in the sense that the elements can be developed in different proportions according to the discipline's particulars. Some disciplines have rich primary sources but modest empirical records (literate programming); some have modest primary sources but rich empirical records (Fagan inspections); some have rich records of both (Cleanroom). The pattern adapts to the available material while requiring each element to be present.

A worked application of the pattern to a discipline that is *not* a chapter in this book illustrates the pattern as a diagnostic tool. The candidate discipline is **Earned Value Management** for software. EVM emerged from the United States Department of Defense's program-management practice in the 1960s, became formalized through the Cost/Schedule Control Systems Criteria in the 1970s, and was adapted to software development across the 1980s and 1990s. The practice involves periodic measurement of three quantities — Planned Value (the budget for work that should have been completed by a given date), Earned Value (the budget for work that has actually been completed by that date), and Actual Cost (the cost actually incurred) — from which derived metrics (Cost Performance Index, Schedule Performance Index, Estimate at Completion) provide early warning of project drift. The discipline is required for major U.S. federal software contracts. It is approximately extinct in commercial software development outside that contracting environment.

Applying the pattern, element by element:

*Original Formulation:* the discipline was named in DoD program-management documents from the 1960s, formalized in CSCSC (1972), and adapted for software in the *Practical Software Measurement* literature of the 1990s. The primary sources are accessible. The discipline is well-documented.

*Empirical Evidence:* EVM has documented success in predicting cost and schedule overruns on large defense and aerospace programs months in advance of the overruns becoming apparent through other means. The empirical record is strongest in the defense-software context where EVM is required and consequently practiced uniformly; the commercial record is weaker because adoption is sparser. The evidence is sufficient to qualify the discipline as worked-when-applied.

*Effort-Tax Mechanism:* EVM requires three measurement disciplines that compound. First, the work breakdown structure must be decomposed to a level fine enough to permit meaningful earned-value calculation, which in software typically means feature-level or task-level decomposition with explicit budget allocations. Second, the *earning rules* — the criteria for what counts as the percentage complete of a partially-finished feature — must be specified clearly enough that they can be applied uniformly; software's intangibility makes this harder than physical-construction earning rules. Third, the measurement cadence must be sustained; weekly or biweekly measurement is the typical requirement. Each component imposes a clerical-and-attention tax on the project-management function. The combined tax is substantial.

*What AI Changes:* the second component — the specification and application of earning rules — has historically been the dominant failure point for software EVM. Earning rules in software are notoriously slippery (what does "fifty percent complete" mean for a feature whose remaining fifty percent might contain unknown unknowns?), and the discipline of applying them consistently has been beyond most project-management functions. AI can apply earning rules consistently to a body of in-progress work — measuring partial completion against acceptance criteria, identifying scope drift, detecting blocked items — at a cadence that humans cannot sustain. The clerical-effort components (work breakdown decomposition, periodic measurement) are also reduced by AI's ability to derive them from project artifacts. *But the third component — the judgment about whether the earning rules themselves are right for the current project context — is judgmental, not clerical, and AI's contribution is bounded.* This is the diagnostic moment.

The pattern's verdict: EVM is a *partial* restoration candidate. The clerical effort taxes are recoverable by AI; the judgmental effort tax on earning-rule design is not. A restoration chapter on EVM would have to honestly bound the claim — AI restores the periodic-measurement and rule-application components but does not restore the earning-rule-design component, which remains a project-management judgment exercise. The chapter could be written, but it would be a chapter with a smaller claim than the chapters on disciplines whose effort taxes were entirely clerical.

This is the diagnostic outcome the pattern is meant to produce. Some disciplines are full-restoration candidates because their effort tax was entirely clerical; some are partial-restoration candidates because their effort tax was a mix of clerical and judgmental; some are non-candidates because their effort tax was predominantly judgmental. The pattern surfaces the distinction. The pattern's value as a methodological apparatus is exactly that it produces this distinction reliably across candidates.

The reader equipped with the pattern can now apply it to other candidates. Some candidates that warrant the application but are not chapters in this book: Watts Humphrey's Personal Software Process and Team Software Process; Jackson System Development; the IEEE/EIA software-engineering standards; Quality Function Deployment for software; the formal-inspection variants beyond Fagan (such as Gilb's evolutionary inspection); Tom Gilb's Planguage; the various structured-analysis and structured-design methodologies of the 1970s and 1980s; the long tail of software-process improvement methodologies that flourished briefly in the late 1990s. Each is a candidate; each warrants the pattern's diagnostic; some will pass and some will not. The author invites the reader to apply the pattern and reach their own verdicts.

A note on the relationship between this chapter and the rest of the book is appropriate before closing. Chapters 4 through 26 of this volume are *applications of the pattern* to specific disciplines. The chapters do not, in each instance, re-derive the pattern; they apply it. The reader who wants to see the pattern at work in compressed form should re-read Chapter 4 (Function Point Analysis) after this chapter and observe each of the six elements being executed in the prose. The execution is dense; the elements are present in every paragraph; the chapter is a worked instance of the pattern as much as it is a chapter about FPA.

Chapters 5 through 9 develop the remaining specification-and-estimation restorations: COCOMO II as the calibrated-estimation cross-check, Lamport's TLA+ as the formal-specification companion, the Z/Alloy/B family as the broader formal-methods landscape, Meyer's Design by Contract as the contract-level specification practice, and Parnas's tabular specifications as the requirements-level practice. Each chapter applies the pattern. The reader is invited to read them as instances and to evaluate each on the pattern's terms. The book's argument cumulates across the applications, and the cumulation is what carries the weight of the restoration program.

The recovery program is now equipped: a central thesis (the effort tax), an intellectual frame (Brooks inverted), and a methodological apparatus (the six-element pattern). The work of Part II begins in the next chapter.
