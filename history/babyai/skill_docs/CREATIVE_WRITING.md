# Creative Writing Skill

## Domain
Fiction, poetry, essays, narrative craft, storytelling

## Expertise

### Story Structure
- **Three-act**: Setup (25%), Confrontation (50%), Resolution (25%). Inciting incident ends Act 1, midpoint reversal splits Act 2, climax opens Act 3.
- **Five-act**: Exposition, Rising Action, Climax, Falling Action, Denouement. Works well for longer, more layered narratives.
- **Episodic**: Linked scenes or vignettes without a single throughline. Unity comes from theme, character, or setting rather than plot.
- **Non-linear**: Fragmented timelines, frame stories, reverse chronology. Use when the order of revelation matters more than the order of events.
- Every scene should do at least two things: advance plot AND reveal character, build world AND raise tension, etc.
- Structure is not formula. Learn the shapes so you can break them intentionally, not accidentally.

### Voice and Point of View
- **First person**: Intimate, biased, limited. The narrator's voice IS the story's voice. Best when subjectivity is the point.
- **Third limited**: Close to one character's perception per scene or chapter. Balances intimacy with narrative flexibility.
- **Third omniscient**: Narrator knows everything, can enter any mind. Harder to control — easy to lose emotional focus. Requires a strong narrative voice of its own.
- **Unreliable narrator**: The gap between what the narrator says and what actually happened IS the story. Plant contradictions early so the reader senses something is off.
- **Choosing POV**: Ask who has the most to lose in this story, who has the most limited information, and whose perception creates the most tension. That's usually your POV character.
- Consistency matters. If you're in third limited, you cannot report what another character is thinking unless your POV character observes or infers it.

### Character Development
- **Wants vs. needs**: The want is what the character pursues consciously (the goal). The need is what they must learn or accept (the theme). The best stories put these in conflict.
- **Show through action**: A character who says "I'm brave" is telling. A character who walks into the burning building despite shaking hands is showing. Behavior under pressure reveals who people are.
- **Avoid flat characters**: Give every named character at least one contradiction — the kind priest who is cruel to animals, the coward who tells the truth when it counts. Contradiction creates dimension.
- Backstory is not character. Character is what someone does in the present moment under pressure. Use backstory to explain behavior, not replace it.
- Secondary characters need their own motivations. They are not waiting offstage for the protagonist to need them.

### Dialogue Craft
- **Subtext**: The best dialogue is about what characters are NOT saying. Two people arguing about dishes are really arguing about respect. Let the surface conversation carry the deeper one.
- **Voice differentiation**: Each character should have distinct speech patterns — vocabulary, sentence length, verbal tics, what they avoid saying. Read dialogue aloud; if you can't tell who's speaking without tags, revise.
- **Dialogue tags**: "Said" is invisible and almost always correct. Avoid "exclaimed," "queried," "opined." Use action beats instead of tags when you need to break up exchanges: *She set down her glass. "That's not what I meant."*
- **Pacing**: Short exchanges speed up a scene. Long speeches slow it down. Interruptions and incomplete sentences create tension. Let characters talk past each other — real people do.
- Cut greetings, small talk, and throat-clearing unless they serve a purpose. Enter scenes late, leave early.

### Prose Style
- **Show vs. tell**: "Show don't tell" is a guideline, not a law. Show emotions, key moments, and character-defining actions. Tell transitions, backstory, and things the reader needs to know quickly. Showing everything bogs the pace.
- **Sensory detail**: Anchor scenes in at least two senses beyond sight. Sound, smell, and texture make a scene feel real. One precise detail beats three generic ones.
- **Rhythm**: Vary sentence length deliberately. Short sentences punch. Longer sentences build and accumulate and carry the reader forward through layers of meaning. Monotonous rhythm puts readers to sleep.
- **Cutting purple prose**: If a description calls attention to itself rather than to what it describes, cut it. Beautiful writing that stops the story is not beautiful — it is indulgent. Clarity serves the reader; ornamentation serves the writer's ego.
- Strong verbs do more work than adjectives. "She sprinted" beats "she ran very quickly." Kill adverbs that duplicate what the verb already implies.
- Read your prose aloud. Your ear catches what your eye misses.

### Revision Strategies
- **Structural pass** (first): Does the story work? Check arc, pacing, and whether every scene earns its place. Cut or move scenes before polishing sentences that might not survive.
- **Character pass**: Track each character's arc. Are their decisions motivated? Do they change? Are secondary characters distinct and necessary?
- **Line editing**: Tighten sentences, kill redundancy, fix rhythm. This is where you read aloud and cut anything that makes you stumble.
- **Kill your darlings**: If a sentence, scene, or character exists because you love it rather than because the story needs it, cut it. Save it in a separate file if you must. The story is not a showcase for your best lines.
- Let a draft rest before revising. Distance gives you the reader's perspective instead of the writer's.
- Revise in passes, not all at once. Trying to fix structure, character, and prose simultaneously leads to paralysis.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Head-hopping**: Switching POV mid-scene without a clear break confuses the reader. Stick to one perspective per scene. Signal POV shifts with scene or chapter breaks.
- **Info dumping**: Resist the urge to explain the world, the backstory, or the magic system all at once. Deliver information at the moment the reader needs it to understand what's happening, not before.
- **Deus ex machina**: If the solution to the climax was not set up earlier in the story, it feels like a cheat. Plant the seeds early. The reader should think "of course" when the resolution arrives, not "where did that come from?"
- **Telling emotions**: "She felt sad" is a label, not an experience. Show the behavior: she stops mid-sentence, looks away, picks at the hem of her sleeve. Let the reader feel the emotion through observed detail.
- **Overwriting**: New writers add; experienced writers subtract. If a paragraph works without a sentence, cut the sentence. If a sentence works without a word, cut the word.
- **Neglecting stakes**: If nothing is at risk, nothing matters. Make sure the reader knows what the character stands to lose — and make that loss feel real.
- **Passive protagonist**: A character things happen TO is less compelling than a character who makes choices, even bad ones. Agency drives narrative.
