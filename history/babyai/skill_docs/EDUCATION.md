## Expertise

### Socratic Method
- Never give the answer first — ask a question that leads the student toward it
- Start with "What do you already know about...?" to find their current understanding
- When they're stuck, narrow the problem: "What part is confusing?" not "Here's the answer"
- Use "What would happen if...?" to encourage prediction and reasoning
- After they answer, ask "How do you know that?" to build justification habits
- If they give a wrong answer, don't say "wrong" — ask "Can you walk me through your steps?"
- Give the direct answer only after 2-3 guided attempts, then explain the reasoning path
- Praise the process ("Good thinking to check that") not just the result ("Correct")

### Project-Based Learning
- Start from the student's interest: "You like Minecraft? Let's learn area and volume by designing a castle"
- Every project should produce something shareable — a poster, a presentation, a working demo
- Break projects into daily-sized chunks with clear deliverables
- Embed multiple subjects in one project: a garden project covers biology, math (measurement), writing (journal), and art
- Let students make real choices within the project — ownership drives engagement
- Build in a "teach-back" step where the student explains what they learned to someone else
- Fail-friendly design: the project should still work even if parts go wrong, so students learn from mistakes without losing everything

### Age-Appropriate Explanations
- **Ages 5-7**: Use concrete objects and stories. "Addition is putting groups together." Physical examples always. Keep sessions under 15 minutes. One concept at a time.
- **Ages 8-10**: Introduce "why" alongside "how." Analogies to things they know (video games, sports, animals). Can handle 20-30 minute focused work. Start building multi-step reasoning.
- **Ages 11-13**: Abstract thinking is emerging but unreliable. Use both concrete and abstract. They can debate and discuss. Peer comparison matters — frame challenges as "most people find this tricky."
- **Ages 14-17**: Capable of full abstraction but need relevance. Connect material to their goals and world. Respect their autonomy. Ask what they think before telling them.
- **Adults**: Skip the scaffolding unless asked. Focus on practical application. Respect their time — get to the point. Connect new knowledge to their existing expertise.
- **General rule**: If an explanation isn't landing, go one level more concrete — add an example, draw a picture, use an analogy

### Study Techniques
- **Spaced repetition**: Review material at increasing intervals — 1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 14 days, 30 days. The forgetting curve is real; cramming doesn't last.
- **Active recall**: Close the book and try to remember before checking. Flashcards, self-quizzing, writing from memory. Rereading feels productive but isn't.
- **Interleaving**: Mix different types of problems in one session instead of doing 20 of the same kind. Harder in the moment, better for long-term retention.
- **Elaboration**: Explain the concept in your own words. Connect it to something you already know. Ask "why does this work?" and "how does this connect to...?"
- **Pomodoro technique**: 25 minutes focused work, 5 minute break. After 4 rounds, take a longer break. Good for students who struggle with sustained attention.
- **Teach-back method**: If you can explain it simply to someone else, you understand it. If you can't, you've found your gap.

### Common Misconceptions by Subject
- **Math**: "Multiplication always makes numbers bigger" (not with fractions). "Equals sign means 'the answer is'" (it means both sides are the same). "You can't subtract a bigger number from a smaller one" (negatives exist). "Division always makes numbers smaller" (not when dividing by a fraction).
- **Science**: "Heavier objects fall faster" (Galileo settled this). "Seasons are caused by Earth's distance from the sun" (it's axial tilt). "Evolution means species 'try' to improve" (natural selection is not goal-directed). "Lightning never strikes the same place twice" (it absolutely does).
- **Language/Reading**: "Sounding it out always works" (English has irregular spelling). "Good writers get it right on the first draft" (revision is where writing happens). "Grammar rules are absolute" (many rules are conventions that vary by context and dialect).
- **General**: "Some people are 'math people' and some aren't" (growth mindset matters — ability develops with practice). "Learning styles (visual, auditory) are fixed" (evidence doesn't support rigid style-based teaching; multimodal is better).

### Assessment Approaches
- Use assessment to find gaps, not to rank students — "What do you need to work on?" not "What grade did you get?"
- **Formative checks**: Quick, low-stakes. Thumbs up/down, one-sentence summary, exit ticket. Do these constantly during learning.
- **Diagnostic questions**: Multiple-choice where each wrong answer reveals a specific misconception. More useful than open-ended for finding where understanding breaks down.
- **Show-your-work problems**: The process matters more than the answer. A wrong answer with good reasoning is more fixable than a right answer from guessing.
- **Portfolio/project assessment**: Evaluate what the student can create, not just what they can recall. Better for real-world skills.
- **Self-assessment**: Ask students to rate their confidence before checking answers. Builds metacognition — knowing what you know and what you don't.
- Avoid trick questions — they test reading comprehension and anxiety, not subject knowledge
